# The British Empire: Power, Profit, and a Complicated Legacy

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

At its height in the early twentieth century, the British Empire covered approximately a quarter of the world's land surface and governed around a quarter of the world's population. It was the largest empire in the history of the world. Its territories stretched from Canada to Australia, from India to South Africa, from Hong Kong to the Caribbean. It was truly the empire on which the sun never set — and for two centuries, it shaped the world in ways that can still be traced in almost every corner of the globe today.

The British Empire did not begin with a grand plan. It grew incrementally, through trade, conquest, settlement, and strategic rivalry with other European powers, over the course of roughly four hundred years. Its origins lay in the sixteenth century, when England — a relatively small and not especially powerful nation — began looking outward.

The early overseas ventures were modest. Trading companies were established to seek profits in Asia and the Americas. The East India Company, founded in 1600, was among the most consequential — a commercial enterprise that would eventually govern vast territories and deploy its own armies. In North America, English settlers established colonies along the eastern seaboard from the early seventeenth century, displacing and frequently destroying indigenous populations. The Caribbean islands became enormously profitable through the cultivation of sugar — a trade that depended entirely on enslaved African labour.

---

The slave trade is inseparable from the story of the British Empire. Britain became the world's dominant slave-trading nation in the eighteenth century, transporting an estimated three and a half million enslaved Africans across the Atlantic. The profits of this trade flowed into British banks, merchant houses, and country estates, funding industrial investment and cultural institutions whose origins in slavery are still being reckoned with today. The slave trade was abolished in 1807 — after decades of campaigning by abolitionists including William Wilberforce — and slavery itself was abolished throughout the Empire in 1833. But the economic structures built on slavery did not disappear with its legal end.

---

The eighteenth century saw Britain and France locked in a global struggle for imperial dominance. The Seven Years' War, fought between 1756 and 1763, was in many ways the first true world war — fought in Europe, North America, the Caribbean, West Africa, India, and the Philippines. Britain emerged dominant, consolidating its position in India and North America. But barely a decade later it suffered a major reverse when thirteen of its American colonies declared independence in 1776 and, with French support, won the American Revolutionary War. The lesson the British drew was not to abandon empire but to govern it more carefully.

The loss of America coincided with the accelerating conquest of India. The East India Company, which had begun as a trading enterprise, found itself drawn into Indian politics — backing one ruler against another, defending its commercial interests militarily, and gradually assuming direct control over vast territories. By the mid-nineteenth century, the Company governed more than a hundred and fifty million people. In 1857, a widespread uprising of Indian soldiers against British rule — known as the Indian Mutiny by the British and the First War of Independence by many Indians — shook the Company to its foundations. The British Crown abolished the Company and assumed direct rule over India in 1858. Queen Victoria was proclaimed Empress of India in 1876.

The late nineteenth century brought the scramble for Africa. At the Berlin Conference of 1884 and 1885, the European powers divided up the African continent among themselves in negotiations that excluded Africans entirely. Britain acquired vast territories: Egypt, Sudan, Kenya, Uganda, Nigeria, the Gold Coast, Rhodesia, and the Cape Colony among them. The ambition of some British imperialists — most notoriously Cecil Rhodes — was a continuous strip of British territory from Cairo to the Cape of Good Hope.

---

The ideology that accompanied this expansion was largely one of self-justification. The British Empire was presented, by its defenders, as a civilising mission — bringing Christianity, law, education, and trade to peoples who supposedly needed them. The reality was more complicated. The Empire did build railways, schools, and hospitals. It did spread the English language and common law in ways that some of its former subjects have found genuinely useful. But it did all of this while extracting enormous wealth, suppressing cultures and languages, drawing arbitrary borders that ignored existing communities, and governing through coercion, violence, and the ever-present threat of force.

The twentieth century brought the slow unravelling. The First World War strained the Empire's resources and raised urgent questions about why subject peoples should die for a power that denied them self-determination. The Second World War accelerated the process dramatically. Britain emerged from the war victorious but exhausted and bankrupt. It could no longer afford empire.

---

India — the jewel in the Crown — led the way. Decades of nationalist struggle, led above all by Mahatma Gandhi and Jawaharlal Nehru, had made British rule increasingly untenable. On the fifteenth of August, 1947, India and Pakistan became independent nations. The partition of the subcontinent was accompanied by catastrophic communal violence in which estimates of the dead range from two hundred thousand to two million, and around fifteen million people were displaced from their homes in one of the largest forced migrations in history.

Decolonisation spread rapidly through the 1950s and 1960s. Sudan, Ghana, Malaya, Nigeria, Kenya, Tanzania, Uganda, Zambia — country after country emerged from British rule. Some transitions were peaceful; others were accompanied by brutal counter-insurgency campaigns that took decades to fully acknowledge. By the 1970s, the formal Empire was effectively over, replaced by the looser Commonwealth of Nations.

---

The British Empire's legacy is one of the most debated topics in modern history. It built global trade networks, spread the English language, and established legal and administrative systems that persist in dozens of countries. It also caused immeasurable suffering — through slavery, famine, suppression, and the violent dismantling of existing societies. The borders it drew in Africa and the Middle East — often with a ruler and little regard for who actually lived there — seeded conflicts that continue to cost lives today.

Fully understanding the world — its borders, its inequalities, its languages, and its conflicts — requires understanding the empire that shaped so much of it. The British Empire was not a simple story of progress or of exploitation, but a vast, contradictory enterprise whose consequences, for good and ill, still surround us.
