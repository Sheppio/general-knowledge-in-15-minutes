# Africa Before Colonialism: Kingdoms, Trade, and a Continent Misunderstood

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Africa is the second largest continent on Earth, home to one-fifth of the world's land surface and an extraordinary diversity of environments, peoples, and cultures. It is also the continent most thoroughly distorted by the history of European colonialism — not just in the damage the colonial project inflicted, but in the stories it told about what Africa had been before Europeans arrived. The nineteenth-century ideology that justified colonialism rested on a particular vision: that Africa was a continent without history, without civilisation, without the complex social and political organisations that Europeans associated with progress. This vision was always false. Africa was home to some of the ancient world's most sophisticated civilisations, to powerful kingdoms and empires that traded across continents, to cities of learning and commerce, to agricultural and technological achievements that rivalled anything produced elsewhere. The history of Africa before colonialism is the story of a continent that the world did not know as well as it imagined it did.

We should begin with what "before colonialism" means in this context. European colonialism in sub-Saharan Africa arrived in earnest in the second half of the nineteenth century, reaching its most intensive phase in the scramble for Africa that divided most of the continent among European powers between roughly eighteen eighty-one and nineteen fourteen. What came before — across thousands of years of African history — was a story of immense variety and complexity. There is no single African story, any more than there is a single European or Asian one. Africa contains the entire range of human social organisation, from hunter-gatherer societies that have maintained ways of life going back tens of thousands of years to large literate empires with bureaucracies, armies, and trade networks spanning thousands of miles.

---

The continent's oldest and most famous civilisation needs no lengthy introduction: ancient Egypt, which flourished on the banks of the Nile for three thousand years, was African in geography and much of its culture, even if its relationship to the rest of the continent has been complicated by later scholarly debates about race and identity. Less often told is the story of the kingdoms that flourished to Egypt's south. Nubia — the region known in antiquity as Kush, occupying what is now Sudan — was a civilisation of comparable sophistication to Egypt, with which it had a relationship of rivalry, trade, and at times conquest that lasted for millennia. Around seven fifty BC, Nubian kings of the Twenty-fifth Dynasty actually conquered Egypt and ruled as pharaohs for nearly a century. The ruins of Nubian pyramids still stand in the Sudanese desert — smaller and steeper than their Egyptian counterparts, but unmistakably related — evidence of a civilisation that most people have never heard of.

Further south, along the highlands of what is now Ethiopia and Eritrea, the kingdom of Aksum flourished from approximately the first to the seventh century AD. Aksum was one of the world's great early trading states, sitting astride the sea routes between the Roman Mediterranean and the Indian Ocean, exporting ivory, gold, and slaves, and importing silk, spices, and glass. It was wealthy enough to mint its own gold coins — one of only a handful of states in the ancient world to do so. In the fourth century, Aksum became one of the first states in the world to officially adopt Christianity, before Islam had even been founded. Ethiopia's continuity with the Aksumite tradition — it is often cited as one of the world's oldest continuous civilisations, and was never fully colonised by European powers — is a reminder that African history is not a story that began with European contact.

---

In West Africa, the most dramatic story is the sequence of great empires that dominated the savannah and desert-edge regions — the Sahel — between approximately three hundred and sixteen hundred AD. The first of these was the Ghana Empire, centred in what is now southeastern Mauritania and western Mali. Despite its name, it had no geographical relationship to the modern nation of Ghana. The Ghana Empire flourished from perhaps the eighth to the eleventh century, growing wealthy by controlling the trade in gold and salt that crossed the Sahara — gold from the forests to the south, salt from mines in the desert, both essential commodities for the peoples on either side of the great desert. Arab geographers who described the Ghana Empire in the eleventh century reported a sophisticated court with a complex administrative structure and formidable military capacity.

When the Ghana Empire declined, it was succeeded by the Mali Empire, which rose to dominance in the thirteenth century. At its height in the fourteenth century, the Mali Empire was one of the largest and wealthiest states in the world. It encompassed a territory larger than western Europe, incorporated gold and salt deposits of enormous value, and maintained a thriving urban culture centred on cities that were also centres of Islamic learning. The most famous of these cities was Timbuktu, which by the fourteenth century housed several large mosques and a university — the Sankore Madrasah — that attracted scholars from across the Muslim world.

The Mali Empire's most celebrated ruler was Mansa Musa, who reigned from approximately thirteen twelve to thirteen thirty-seven. In thirteen twenty-four and thirteen twenty-five he performed the hajj — the Muslim pilgrimage to Mecca — and his procession across North Africa and the Middle East became legendary. He reportedly travelled with an entourage of tens of thousands of people and an enormous quantity of gold, distributing gifts so lavishly in Cairo and elsewhere that he disrupted local gold markets and caused inflation that persisted for years. His visit put Mali on the map — literally: European cartographers began depicting him on their maps of Africa, seated on a throne holding a gold sceptre, a figure of almost mythical wealth.

---

The Songhai Empire succeeded Mali as the dominant power in the western Sahel, reaching its peak under Askia the Great in the late fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries. At its height, the Songhai Empire was the largest in African history, stretching from the Atlantic coast to what is now northern Nigeria. It maintained a professional bureaucracy, a standing army, and an elaborate system of provincial administration. Timbuktu under the Songhai was at the height of its intellectual life: the city's libraries held hundreds of thousands of manuscripts on theology, mathematics, astronomy, history, and literature. The empire was destroyed not from within but from without — by a Moroccan army equipped with firearms that crossed the Sahara in fifteen ninety-one and shattered Songhai military power at the Battle of Tondibi. The manuscripts survived, many of them, and are still being catalogued and conserved today.

In central and southern Africa, the most remarkable monument to pre-colonial civilisation is Great Zimbabwe — a complex of stone enclosures built without mortar, the largest of which encloses an area of nearly ten thousand square metres. Great Zimbabwe was the capital of a powerful kingdom that controlled the gold trade between the interior of southern Africa and the Indian Ocean coast, flourishing from approximately the eleventh to the fifteenth centuries. When European explorers first encountered the ruins in the nineteenth century, many refused to believe they had been built by Africans, attributing them instead to Phoenicians, Arabs, or any other people rather than the indigenous Shona. The archaeological evidence is unambiguous: Great Zimbabwe was built and inhabited by the ancestors of the Shona people of modern Zimbabwe.

---

Along Africa's coasts, a different kind of civilisation flourished. The Swahili city-states of the East African coast — Kilwa, Mombasa, Zanzibar, Malindi, and others — were trading cities that had developed from at least the ninth century, connecting the African interior to the Indian Ocean trade network that linked Arabia, Persia, India, and Southeast Asia. These were cosmopolitan places: Muslim in religion, Swahili-speaking but drawing on Arabic, Persian, and Indian cultural influences, exporting gold, ivory, and enslaved people and importing porcelain, glass, and textiles. Chinese porcelain recovered from archaeological sites along the Swahili coast testifies to the reach of these trading connections. When the Portuguese arrived at the end of the fifteenth century they found sophisticated urban societies that were already deeply integrated into world trade.

In West and Central Africa, the period before colonialism was also the period of some of Africa's most powerful kingdoms in purely political terms. The Kingdom of Benin, in what is now southern Nigeria, flourished from approximately the thirteenth century onward and is famous for the bronze sculptures — the Benin Bronzes — produced by its court artists from at least the sixteenth century. These works, which depict the Oba (king) and his court in extraordinary detail, are among the masterpieces of world art. Many of them were looted by a British punitive expedition in eighteen ninety-seven and ended up in European museums, where debates about their repatriation continue today. The Kingdom of Kongo, in what is now the Democratic Republic of Congo, Angola, and surrounding areas, had a complex political structure, a sophisticated legal system, and an elaborate system of currency and trade before European contact. When Portuguese sailors arrived in the fourteen eighties, they encountered a state capable of engaging with them as diplomatic equals — at least for a time.

---

None of this is to romanticise pre-colonial Africa or suggest that the continent's history was one of uninterrupted harmony. African kingdoms made war on one another, enslaved their enemies, and sometimes treated their own people with great cruelty. The trans-Saharan slave trade, which predated the Atlantic trade by centuries, moved millions of Africans northward across the desert to the Mediterranean world and the Middle East. African political structures varied enormously: some were sophisticated states with legal systems and bureaucracies; others were organised on the scale of the village or the kinship group, with no centralised authority. The diversity of the continent's societies and their histories defies generalisation in either the positive or the negative direction.

What the history of pre-colonial Africa does challenge is the colonial myth that nothing of consequence existed there before Europeans arrived. The Gold Coast, the Slave Coast, the Ivory Coast — the very names Europeans gave to Africa's regions reflect what they came to take, and obscure what was already there. African history did not begin with European contact. It had been underway, in extraordinary variety and complexity, for as long as human history itself — which makes sense, since Africa is where human history began.
