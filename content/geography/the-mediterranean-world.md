# The Mediterranean World: The Sea That Made the West

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

The Mediterranean Sea covers about two and a half million square kilometres — less than one percent of the world's ocean surface. It is almost entirely enclosed, connected to the Atlantic only through the narrow Strait of Gibraltar, and to the Black Sea through the Bosphorus. It is flanked by three continents: Europe to the north, Africa to the south, Asia to the east. Twenty-one modern countries have Mediterranean coastlines. The sea itself is a relatively calm body of water, predictably seasonal in its winds and weather, and almost without tides — features that made it ideally suited to the ancient sailing technologies that enabled long-distance maritime trade and communication. For roughly three thousand years, from the Bronze Age to the early modern period, it was the most important body of water on Earth: the medium through which the civilisations of Europe, the Middle East, and North Africa interacted, competed, traded, and crossed one another's ideas and genes. In that sense, the Mediterranean did not merely border civilisation — it was civilisation, or at least the kind of civilisation whose legacy most directly shapes the modern world.

The history of the Mediterranean world begins long before the Greeks or the Romans. The Bronze Age was already marked by extensive maritime networks connecting Mycenaean Greece, Minoan Crete, Cyprus, Egypt, the Levant, and Anatolia in webs of trade and cultural exchange. The collapse of these Bronze Age civilisations around twelve hundred BC — sudden, dramatic, and still imperfectly understood — created a rupture in Mediterranean development that lasted several centuries. What rose from it, from roughly the ninth and eighth centuries BC onward, were the distinctive cultures that would shape the next three millennia.

---

The Phoenicians were among the most consequential of these early Mediterranean peoples. Based in the coastal cities of the Levant — above all Tyre, Sidon, and Byblos, in what is now Lebanon — the Phoenicians were master sailors and traders who established colonies and trading posts the length of the Mediterranean, from Cyprus in the east to the Iberian Peninsula in the west. Carthage, founded in what is now Tunisia in the ninth century BC, grew from a Phoenician colony into one of the great powers of the ancient Mediterranean world. The Phoenicians' most enduring contribution was not military or commercial but cultural: they developed or transmitted the alphabet that the Greeks adapted and that forms the basis of every alphabetic writing system in use today, including the one you are reading now. The very word "alphabet" derives from the first two letters of the Greek version — alpha and beta — which were themselves adaptations of Phoenician letters.

The Greeks transformed the Mediterranean world in the centuries following their emergence from the Dark Ages. From approximately eight hundred BC, Greek city-states established colonies across the sea in a great arc: from Massalia — modern Marseille — in the west, through Sicily and southern Italy, around the shores of the Black Sea, and along the North African coast. By the fifth century BC, the Aegean world had developed the institutions and ideas that would prove most lastingly influential: democracy, first practised in Athens; the beginnings of systematic philosophy, science, and history; theatre; epic poetry; the Olympic Games. The extraordinary cultural efflorescence of fifth-century BC Athens — the age of Pericles, Socrates, Sophocles, Thucydides, and the Parthenon — was built on the wealth and networks that Mediterranean trade and empire provided.

---

The spread of Greek culture across the wider Mediterranean and Near Eastern world was the work of Alexander the Great, who between three thirty-four and three twenty-three BC conquered an empire stretching from Greece to Egypt to India, founding dozens of cities — many called Alexandria — that became centres of Greek culture in non-Greek lands. Alexander died at thirty-two, and his empire was divided among his generals, who founded the Hellenistic kingdoms — Egypt under the Ptolemies, the Near East under the Seleucids, Macedon under the Antigonids — that spread Greek language, art, philosophy, and urban life across an enormous territory. Greek became the common language of educated people across the entire eastern Mediterranean, the medium of commerce, philosophy, science, and eventually, Christianity's scripture.

The Romans absorbed and extended this Greek cultural inheritance while adding their own: a genius for law, administration, engineering, and military organisation. Beginning as a small city-state in central Italy, Rome expanded through the Italian peninsula and then, in a series of wars that lasted from the third to the first century BC, defeated Carthage, conquered Greece, absorbed Egypt, and extended Roman power over the entire Mediterranean basin. Augustus Caesar, the first Roman emperor, described the Mediterranean as Mare Nostrum — "our sea" — and for roughly four centuries it was precisely that: a Roman lake, free of the piracy that had previously disrupted trade, administered through a unified legal system, connected by an extraordinary network of roads, aqueducts, and harbours. The pax romana — the Roman peace — allowed the movement of goods, people, and ideas across the Mediterranean world at a scale and speed that would not be seen again for well over a millennium.

---

The fracturing of Roman unity from the third century AD onward, culminating in the formal division of the empire into eastern and western halves in three ninety-five, began a long transformation of the Mediterranean world. The Western Roman Empire collapsed in the fifth century under the pressure of internal weakness and external incursions; the Eastern Roman Empire, based at Constantinople — the city founded by the Emperor Constantine in three thirty, on the site of the Greek city Byzantium — continued for another thousand years as the Byzantine Empire, preserving Roman law, Greek culture, and Orthodox Christianity until the fall of Constantinople to the Ottoman Turks on the twenty-ninth of May, fourteen fifty-three.

The other great transformation of the Mediterranean world was the rise of Islam in the seventh century. The Arab conquests that followed the death of the Prophet Muhammad in six thirty-two spread with astonishing speed across the Middle East, Persia, North Africa, and the Iberian Peninsula, bringing large portions of the Mediterranean world under Islamic rule within a century. For several centuries, the Muslim world — more literate, more scientifically advanced, and more commercially dynamic than the fragmented Christian kingdoms of Western Europe — was the dominant civilisation of the Mediterranean basin. The House of Wisdom in Baghdad, the great mosques and libraries of Cairo and Córdoba, the sophisticated cities of Islamic Spain — all were expressions of a civilisation that preserved and extended the Greek scientific and philosophical tradition while European learning went through its long post-Roman contraction.

---

The medieval Mediterranean was a space of conflict and coexistence between Christian Europe and the Islamic world — the Crusades, launched in ten ninety-six, were its most dramatic expression of conflict; the trading cities of Venice, Genoa, and Pisa, which prospered by acting as commercial intermediaries between Islamic and Christian worlds, were its most characteristic expression of coexistence. The wealth generated by Mediterranean trade financed the Italian Renaissance of the fourteenth and fifteenth centuries — the great cultural rebirth in which Europe recovered, transformed, and extended the classical inheritance that the Mediterranean had transmitted. The humanism of Petrarch and Boccaccio, the painting of Giotto and Botticelli, the architecture of Brunelleschi and Bramante, the thought of Machiavelli and Leonardo — all were expressions of a culture that was simultaneously local and Mediterranean, rooted in Florence or Venice or Rome and in three thousand years of civilisational inheritance.

The opening of the Atlantic world from the late fifteenth century onward gradually shifted the centre of gravity of European economic and political life from the Mediterranean to the Atlantic coast — from Venice and Genoa to Lisbon, Seville, Amsterdam, and London. The Mediterranean remained important, but it was no longer the centre of the world. The Ottoman Empire, which controlled much of the eastern Mediterranean and North Africa from the sixteenth century onward, provided a degree of order and continuity in the region, but the dynamism of Western Europe increasingly lay elsewhere.

---

Today the Mediterranean world is a region of extraordinary diversity and deep historical connections. Its coasts are home to Arab, Berber, Turkish, Greek, Italian, Spanish, French, and many other peoples, speaking dozens of languages and practising three world religions. It is also a region of sharp inequality: the wealthy European north and the poorer Arab and African south are separated by a body of water across which, in recent decades, hundreds of thousands of migrants and refugees have attempted the dangerous crossing in search of better lives.

The sea itself is threatened. It is nearly enclosed, its waters warming faster than the global average as the climate changes, its biodiversity depleted by overfishing, its coastlines crowded and polluted. The same qualities that made it the cradle of Western civilisation — its accessibility, its connectivity, its central position between three continents — make it one of the world's most crowded and contested maritime spaces.

What does not change is the depth of what the Mediterranean has deposited in human culture. The alphabet you are reading, the law that governs most Western legal systems, the philosophy that underlies much of Western thought, the religious traditions that shape the beliefs of billions — all were formed, exchanged, contested, and transmitted in the cities and across the waters of this small, enclosed, improbable sea.
