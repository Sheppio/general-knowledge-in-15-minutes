# The History of India: A Civilisation of Extraordinary Depth

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

India is not a country in the ordinary sense — it is a civilisation. The world's most populous nation today, it is also the inheritor of one of the oldest continuous traditions of human culture: a tradition that stretches back more than four thousand years to the cities of the Indus Valley, runs through the Vedic texts that gave birth to Hinduism, the forests where the Buddha found enlightenment, the courts where Sanskrit poetry was written and mathematical discoveries made, the mosques built by Mughal emperors, the railways laid by British colonisers, and the constitution written by B. R. Ambedkar for the republic that emerged in 1947. To understand India is to understand a vast, complex, and frequently turbulent conversation between these layers — one that has never been resolved into simple unity and has never entirely fragmented into chaos.

That India exists at all as a single political entity is, in some ways, remarkable. The subcontinent is home to more than a billion and a half people who speak hundreds of distinct languages, practise every major world religion, belong to thousands of caste groups, and hold political views ranging across the full spectrum of human possibility. What holds it together — and what has always distinguished it from mere geography — is something harder to define than territory: a deep sense of shared civilisational heritage, contested and argued over but never entirely abandoned.

---

The story begins at the Indus Valley. Around 3300 BC, in the river plains of what is now Pakistan and northwest India, one of the world's first urban civilisations emerged. The cities of Mohenjo-daro and Harappa had populations of tens of thousands, sophisticated drainage systems, standardised weights and measures, and a script that has never been deciphered. The Indus Valley Civilisation — also called the Harappan Civilisation — flourished for roughly two thousand years before declining around 1300 BC, probably due to a combination of climate change and shifting river courses. Its origins and language remain mysterious; its cities show no evidence of palaces or temples, no clear ruling class, no monuments to kings.

Around the same period as the Harappan decline, the Vedic culture was developing in northern India — named for the Vedas, the oldest scriptures of Hinduism, composed in Sanskrit and transmitted orally for centuries. The Vedic period produced the foundations of Hindu philosophy, ritual, and social organisation, including the caste system that would shape Indian society for millennia. From the Upanishads — philosophical texts composed from roughly 800 BC onward — came some of the most sophisticated metaphysical thinking in human history: the concepts of Brahman (the universal soul), Atman (the individual soul), karma, and the cycle of rebirth.

---

The sixth century BC saw an extraordinary flowering of religious and philosophical thought. The Gangetic plains of northern India produced not only the Upanishadic tradition but also the founders of two new religions that would go on to reshape Asia: Mahavira, who systematised Jainism, and Siddhartha Gautama, who became the Buddha. The Buddha — "the Awakened One" — taught a path of ethical conduct, meditation, and wisdom as the route to liberation from suffering. His teachings spread first through India and then, over subsequent centuries, across Central Asia, China, Japan, and Southeast Asia, becoming one of the world's major religions.

The first empire to unify most of the Indian subcontinent was the Mauryan Empire, founded in 322 BC by Chandragupta Maurya after the withdrawal of Alexander the Great's forces from the northwest. Its greatest ruler was Chandragupta's grandson Ashoka, who came to the throne around 268 BC and initially pursued a policy of military expansion that culminated in the bloody conquest of Kalinga. Horrified by the suffering he had caused, Ashoka converted to Buddhism and devoted the rest of his reign to a programme of moral governance: he had edicts inscribed on rock faces and pillars across the empire, advocating tolerance, non-violence, and the welfare of all people regardless of religion. He is remembered as one of the most enlightened rulers in world history.

The centuries that followed the Mauryan decline saw the subcontinent divided among competing kingdoms and dynasties. The Gupta Empire, which flourished from roughly 320 to 550 AD, is often called the golden age of ancient India — a period of extraordinary achievement in mathematics (including the decimal system and the concept of zero, developed by Indian mathematicians), astronomy, medicine, literature, and art. It was Gupta-era India that produced the playwright Kalidasa, often compared to Shakespeare, and made advances in surgery and metallurgy that would not reach Europe for centuries.

---

The arrival of Islam transformed the political landscape of the subcontinent. Arab traders had long visited India's western coast; Islam began to spread into the northwest from the eighth century AD, carried initially by conquest. The Delhi Sultanate, established in 1206, made Delhi the capital of a Muslim state that ruled northern India for more than three centuries — a period that saw the construction of some of India's most magnificent Islamic architecture and the synthesis of Indo-Islamic culture that would characterise the subcontinent for centuries.

The Mughal Empire, founded in 1526 when Babur — a descendant of both Timur and Genghis Khan — defeated the sultan of Delhi at the First Battle of Panipat, was the most powerful state in Indian history before the British. At its peak, it governed more than a hundred million people — roughly a quarter of the world's population. The Mughal emperors presided over a court of extraordinary sophistication: Akbar, Babur's grandson, pursued a policy of religious toleration remarkable for his time, extending membership in his court to Hindus, Christians, and Jains as well as Muslims. Shah Jahan, who ruled in the seventeenth century, built the Taj Mahal as a mausoleum for his wife Mumtaz Mahal — one of the most beautiful buildings in the world. The empire began to decline under Aurangzeb, whose religious intolerance and endless military campaigns exhausted its resources and alienated its Hindu subjects.

---

Into the declining Mughal world came the British. The English East India Company had established its first trading post in India in 1613, with permission from the Mughal emperor. Over the following century and a half, the Company accumulated power — trading rights, territorial concessions, and eventually military strength. The Battle of Plassey in 1757, in which the Company's forces under Robert Clive defeated the Nawab of Bengal with the help of conspiracy and bribery, is often marked as the moment Britain's Indian empire effectively began. The Company became a governing as well as a trading body, collecting taxes, administering justice, and fighting wars.

The 1857 Rebellion — known in British accounts as the Indian Mutiny and in Indian nationalist accounts as the First War of Independence — began as a mutiny among Indian soldiers (sepoys) in the Company's army and spread into a widespread uprising across northern India. It was suppressed with great violence by British forces. Its aftermath transferred power from the East India Company to the British Crown: in 1858, Queen Victoria was proclaimed sovereign of India, and the British Raj — direct Crown rule — began. In 1877, she was additionally proclaimed Empress of India.

The Raj built railways, codified laws, and educated a generation of English-speaking Indian administrators — and extracted enormous wealth from the subcontinent, de-industrialised its textile industry, and imposed an alien bureaucracy on an ancient civilisation. The Bengal Famine of 1943, in which an estimated two to three million people died partly as a result of British wartime policies, stands as the darkest episode of the colonial period.

---

The independence movement had been building for decades when Mahatma Gandhi, returning from South Africa in 1915, transformed it. Gandhi's strategy of non-violent civil disobedience — satyagraha — gave the Congress movement a moral power and a mass base it had lacked. The Salt March of March to April 1930, in which Gandhi led a two-hundred-and-forty-mile walk to the sea to make salt in defiance of the British salt monopoly, became one of the defining acts of civil resistance in history. The Quit India Movement of 1942 demanded immediate independence. By the end of the Second World War, British will and British resources to maintain the empire had been exhausted.

India achieved independence on the fifteenth of August, 1947 — but independence came simultaneously with partition. The Muslim-majority regions of the northwest and northeast became the new nation of Pakistan; the rest became India. The partition was accompanied by one of the largest forced migrations in human history, as an estimated ten to twenty million people crossed the new borders in both directions, and by communal violence that killed somewhere between two hundred thousand and two million people. Gandhi, who had opposed partition and spent the final weeks of British rule fasting to stop the killing, was assassinated by a Hindu nationalist on the thirtieth of January, 1948.

Jawaharlal Nehru, India's first prime minister, spoke on the eve of independence of India's "tryst with destiny." The republic that emerged was the world's largest democracy — impoverished, diverse, and improbable. It has remained a democracy, through all its contradictions and crises, for more than three quarters of a century: itself one of history's more remarkable achievements.
