# The History of China: Five Thousand Years of Civilisation

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

China is the world's oldest continuous civilisation, and it is also the world's most populous nation and, by some measures, its largest economy. These two facts — the depth of its history and the scale of its contemporary power — are not unrelated. China's sense of itself, the assumptions that have shaped its political culture for thousands of years, the cycles of unity and fragmentation, expansion and contraction, openness and closure that characterise its long history, are all essential to understanding it today. To discuss Chinese history in the space of fifteen minutes is an act of radical compression: we are dealing with a tradition that stretches back more than four thousand years, encompasses some of the greatest intellectual and artistic achievements in human history, and involves hundreds of millions of people across an enormous and geographically varied territory. What follows is the outline of that story — its major phases, its recurring themes, and its extraordinary continuity.

The earliest Chinese states emerged in the Yellow River valley in what is now northern China, where the fertile soil and reliable water supply could support dense agricultural settlement. The Shang dynasty, which flourished from roughly sixteen hundred to ten forty-six BC, is the earliest dynasty attested by contemporary written records: the oracle bones — tortoise shells and ox bones used for divination, inscribed with a script directly ancestral to modern Chinese writing — are among the most remarkable archaeological discoveries of the twentieth century. The Shang was succeeded by the Zhou dynasty, which lasted from ten forty-six to two fifty-six BC — the longest dynasty in Chinese history — and during which many of the ideas that would define Chinese civilisation were first articulated. Confucius, Laozi, Mencius, Zhuangzi, and the other thinkers of the "Hundred Schools of Thought" that flourished in the chaotic later Zhou period created a body of philosophy, ethics, and political theory that has shaped Chinese life ever since.

---

China was first unified under a single imperial government by Qin Shi Huang, the ruler of the Qin state, who conquered the last of his rivals in two twenty-one BC. The Qin dynasty was brief — it collapsed in two oh six BC, just fifteen years after unification — but its consequences were permanent. Qin Shi Huang standardised the writing system, the currency, and the system of weights and measures across the newly unified territory. He connected existing defensive walls in the north into what would eventually become the Great Wall. He established the centralised imperial administrative system — with the emperor at the top, served by a bureaucracy of officials selected on the basis of ability rather than birth — that would define Chinese governance for more than two thousand years. He was also a ruler of extraordinary cruelty, who burned books and buried scholars alive and drove his people into the ground through forced labour on his construction projects. Both aspects of his legacy — the unifier and the tyrant — have been debated in China ever since.

The Han dynasty, which ruled from two oh six BC to two twenty AD, consolidated the institutions of imperial China and gave them their enduring character. Han China extended Chinese territory significantly, sending armies and envoys into Central Asia and opening the trade routes that would become the Silk Road. Paper was invented during the Han period. The civil service examination system that would select Chinese officials for the next two thousand years was begun. The population of the Han empire was roughly sixty million people — comparable to the Roman Empire at its height — and the two civilisations were each barely aware of the other's existence, separated by the vast distances of the Eurasian landmass. When the Han dynasty collapsed in two twenty AD, China fragmented into competing states for four centuries — a period of disunion that Chinese history has always regarded as a catastrophe, and the restoration of unity as the natural and desirable order of things.

---

Unity was restored by the Sui dynasty in five eighty-nine, but the Sui was short-lived. It was the Tang dynasty, which ruled from six eighteen to nine oh seven, that represented the next great flowering of Chinese civilisation. Tang China was cosmopolitan and confident: its capital Chang'an, in what is now Shaanxi province, was the largest city in the world, home to merchants, scholars, diplomats, and artists from across the known world. Persian traders, Japanese students, Central Asian musicians, Nestorian Christian monks — all were present in Tang Chang'an, which was the eastern terminus of the Silk Road and one of the great meeting places of cultures. Chinese poetry reached its classical heights in the Tang, in the work of Li Bai and Du Fu; Chinese Buddhism flourished; Chinese art, ceramics, and music achieved forms that would influence the region for centuries.

When the Tang fragmented in the early tenth century, China went through a brief period of division before being reunified under the Song dynasty, which ruled from nine sixty to twelve seventy-nine. Song China was technologically innovative in ways that would eventually transform the world: printing with movable type, gunpowder weapons, the magnetic compass, and advanced iron and steel production all flourished during the Song period. Song China also developed a sophisticated commercial economy with paper money and a network of trade that connected it to Southeast Asia, India, and the Persian Gulf. The Song dynasty was eventually conquered not by internal rebellion but by the Mongols, who under Kublai Khan completed their conquest of China in twelve seventy-nine and established the Yuan dynasty. Marco Polo visited Kublai Khan's court and returned to Venice with accounts of a China so wealthy and so advanced that many European readers found them impossible to believe.

---

The Mongol Yuan dynasty was in turn overthrown by a Chinese rebellion led by Zhu Yuanzhang, a former Buddhist monk of peasant origin, who founded the Ming dynasty in thirteen sixty-eight. The Ming is famous for several things: the reconstruction and expansion of the Great Wall, largely in its present form; the construction of the Forbidden City in Beijing, begun in fourteen oh seven; and the extraordinary voyages of the admiral Zheng He, who between fourteen oh five and fourteen thirty-three led a series of expeditions across Southeast Asia, India, Arabia, and down the East African coast, in fleets of ships far larger than anything European navies possessed at the time. These voyages are fascinating partly for what they were — the greatest maritime projection of power the world had yet seen — and partly for what happened afterwards: the Ming court, responding to internal political pressures and Confucian suspicion of maritime trade, essentially abandoned the project, withdrew from the seas, and turned inward. Within a generation of Zheng He's death, China had retreated from a position in which it might have become the dominant maritime and trading power in the world.

The Qing dynasty, which replaced the Ming in sixteen forty-four, was founded by the Manchu people from northeast of China rather than by ethnic Han Chinese, and it expanded Chinese territory to its greatest historical extent, incorporating Tibet, Xinjiang, and Mongolia. In the eighteenth century, Qing China was by many measures the world's largest economy, accounting for a substantial proportion of global output. But the nineteenth century brought catastrophe. The Opium Wars with Britain — the first from eighteen thirty-nine to eighteen forty-two, the second from eighteen fifty-six to eighteen sixty — opened China to foreign trade on terms it had not chosen and could not resist, and the unequal treaties that followed gave foreign powers extraordinary privileges on Chinese soil. The Taiping Rebellion of eighteen fifty to eighteen sixty-four — one of the deadliest civil wars in history, killing tens of millions of people — further weakened the dynasty. Japan's victory over China in the war of eighteen ninety-four to eighteen ninety-five was a profound humiliation, demonstrating that a modernised Asian neighbour that had learned from the West could defeat the ancient empire.

---

The Qing dynasty collapsed in nineteen eleven and twelve, and the Republic of China proclaimed on the first of January, nineteen twelve, was unable to impose stable governance on the vast country. The warlord era, the nationalist government of Chiang Kai-shek, the Japanese invasion that began in earnest in nineteen thirty-seven and became total war after nineteen forty-one, the civil war between Nationalists and Communists that resumed after Japan's defeat — all of these produced decades of violence and instability that China still calls the "century of humiliation," running from the Opium Wars to the communist victory in nineteen forty-nine. On the first of October nineteen forty-nine, standing at Tiananmen Gate in Beijing, Mao Zedong proclaimed the establishment of the People's Republic of China, and the civil war effectively ended, with the Nationalist government retreating to Taiwan.

The history of the People's Republic — the Great Leap Forward, the famine that killed tens of millions, the Cultural Revolution, the reform era launched by Deng Xiaoping in nineteen seventy-eight, the extraordinary economic transformation of the following four decades, the rise of China to its current position as the world's second-largest economy and a power with global reach — belongs to a different script. What the longer history provides is context: a sense of the antiquity and continuity of Chinese civilisation, of the recurring themes — the value of unity, the Confucian emphasis on order and hierarchy, the tradition of centralised imperial authority, the complex relationship between China and the outside world — that shape how China's government and its people understand their country and its place in the world.
