# The Amazon: Earth's Greatest Rainforest

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

The Amazon rainforest is the largest tropical rainforest on Earth — a fact that is easy to state and almost impossible to truly comprehend. It covers approximately five and a half million square kilometres of South America, an area roughly the size of the entire European Union. It contains an estimated three hundred and ninety billion individual trees, belonging to around sixteen thousand different species. It is home to at least forty thousand plant species, two thousand two hundred species of fish, one thousand two hundred and ninety-four species of birds, four hundred and twenty-seven species of mammals, and a number of insect species so large that estimates run to the millions. One in ten of all species on Earth lives in the Amazon basin. The river that runs through it — the Amazon River — carries more freshwater than any other river system in the world, discharging roughly twenty percent of all the freshwater that flows into the world's oceans. The Amazon is not merely a forest; it is a biome so vast and so complex that scientists are still discovering species within it at a rate of hundreds per year.

This immensity is also a political and ecological fact of the first importance. The Amazon generates its own rainfall through a process known as the "flying rivers": trees release water vapour through their leaves, and this moisture cycles through the atmosphere to fall as rain — not only over the forest itself, but over large areas of South America well beyond its boundaries. Scientists estimate that the Amazon produces around twenty billion tonnes of water vapour per day. The agricultural productivity of much of southern Brazil, Paraguay, and Argentina depends on rainfall patterns that the Amazon forest helps to regulate. Destroy enough of the forest and you alter the hydrology not just of the Amazon basin but of a continent.

---

The human history of the Amazon begins long before European contact, and it was more complex and more populous than the landscape Europeans encountered in the sixteenth century suggested. The Amazon basin was home to diverse indigenous societies — some nomadic, some settled, some organised into chiefdoms and polities of considerable scale and sophistication. The anthropologist Anna Roosevelt's excavations at Marajó Island, near the mouth of the Amazon, revealed a society of more than a hundred thousand people that had existed there for at least a thousand years. Archaeological evidence across the basin has revealed traces of large settlements, earthworks, and what researchers call "dark earth" — a form of highly fertile, human-made soil called terra preta — distributed across enormous areas of the forest, suggesting that pre-Columbian Amazonian populations may have been in the millions and that they actively managed the landscape, shaping the distribution of useful plant species across vast areas.

The first European to travel the length of the Amazon was the Spanish conquistador Francisco de Orellana, who set out from what is now Ecuador in fifteen forty-one and reached the mouth of the Amazon River on the twenty-fourth of August, fifteen forty-two. Orellana and his chronicler, the friar Gaspar de Carvajal, reported encountering large, densely settled populations along the river, with towns stretching for miles along the banks and trade networks extending deep into the interior. For centuries, these accounts were dismissed as fantasy or exaggeration — the forest appeared so impenetrable and the soils so poor that large civilisations seemed impossible. The archaeological evidence emerging from the late twentieth century onward has substantially vindicated Orellana's reports.

---

The devastation that European contact brought to Amazonian indigenous populations was catastrophic. Diseases — above all smallpox, but also measles, typhus, and influenza — swept through communities with no prior exposure and no immunity, killing enormous proportions of the population. Estimates of pre-contact Amazonian populations and of the death toll from disease are necessarily speculative, but some scholars suggest that populations may have fallen by eighty or ninety percent in the century or two after contact. The forest that later Europeans encountered — apparently pristine, apparently empty — was in significant part the product of this demographic catastrophe: a landscape that had been cleared and managed for millennia reverting to forest after the people who had shaped it were gone.

The Amazon was drawn into the global economy most dramatically during the rubber boom of the late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries. Rubber — derived from the latex of the Hevea brasiliensis tree, native to the Amazon basin — became one of the most valuable commodities in the world as the industrial revolution created an insatiable demand for tyres, gaskets, and other rubber goods. The cities of Manaus and Belém grew rapidly, and the rubber barons who controlled the trade became fabulously wealthy. The rubber boom also produced one of the most brutal labour exploitation regimes in history: indigenous Amazonian peoples were enslaved, tortured, mutilated, and murdered on an enormous scale to force them to collect rubber. The Peruvian Amazon Company, whose atrocities were exposed by the British consul Roger Casement in nineteen ten and eleven, became an international scandal comparable to the Congo Free State abuses of King Leopold of Belgium.

The boom ended abruptly when the British, who had smuggled Hevea seeds out of the Amazon, established plantation-grown rubber in Southeast Asia, where it could be produced far more cheaply than in the scattered trees of the wild forest. Manaus, which had built an opera house of extraordinary grandeur at the height of the boom, subsided into provincial obscurity. The Amazon returned to relative economic marginalisation for half a century.

---

The modern threat to the Amazon began in earnest in the nineteen-sixties and seventies, when Brazil's military government launched a series of development programmes aimed at opening the Amazon to settlement, agriculture, and resource extraction. Road-building was the mechanism: without roads, the forest was inaccessible; with them, settlers, loggers, and ranchers could follow. The Trans-Amazonian Highway and other projects opened corridors of destruction that spread outward as settlement followed the roads. Between nineteen seventy and twenty sixteen, the Brazilian Amazon lost roughly three hundred thousand square kilometres of forest cover — an area larger than the United Kingdom. Between May two thousand and August two thousand and six alone, Brazil lost nearly a hundred and fifty thousand square kilometres of forest, an area larger than Greece.

The drivers of deforestation have been extensively documented. Cattle ranching is the single largest cause — vast areas of forest are cleared to create pasture for beef production, much of it destined for export to European and North American markets. Soy cultivation — also primarily for export, as animal feed for livestock in China, Europe, and elsewhere — is another major driver. Logging, both legal and illegal, removes timber and opens the forest to subsequent agricultural conversion. Gold mining, particularly illegal small-scale mining known as garimpo, continues to poison rivers with mercury and destroy forest at a significant scale. Behind all of these immediate causes is the political economy of land tenure, agricultural subsidies, commodity prices, and governance — the incentive structures that make forest destruction profitable and forest conservation difficult to enforce.

---

The ecological stakes are extreme. The Amazon stores an enormous quantity of carbon — estimates suggest the basin holds somewhere between eighty and one hundred and fifty billion tonnes of carbon in its trees and soil. Deforestation releases this carbon as carbon dioxide and contributes significantly to global climate change, even as climate change itself threatens the forest: higher temperatures and altered rainfall patterns push parts of the Amazon toward drier conditions that the existing forest did not evolve to handle. Scientists have warned of the possibility of a "dieback" — a tipping point beyond which the loss of enough forest creates conditions that prevent its regeneration, triggering a cascade that converts vast areas of rainforest into drier savannah. A study published in twenty twenty-two found evidence that more than three-quarters of the Amazon has already experienced a decline in resilience since the early two-thousands, suggesting the system is being destabilised.

Against this stands the extraordinary fact of what is still there. The Amazon remains the most biodiverse place on Earth, home to species that have not yet been named, chemical compounds that have not yet been tested for pharmaceutical properties, ecological relationships that have not yet been described. More than thirty million people, including over three hundred and fifty different ethnic groups and more than a million indigenous people living in traditional ways, depend on it for their livelihoods and their cultures. Several countries — Brazil, Peru, Colombia, Venezuela, Ecuador, Bolivia, and others — share jurisdiction over the basin, and the governance of what happens there involves some of the most contentious questions in international environmental politics: the conflict between national sovereignty over natural resources and the global interest in maintaining a functioning biosphere.

The Amazon is not merely a natural wonder. It is a life-support system — for the species it contains, for the continent it waters, and in a meaningful sense for the planet whose climate it helps to regulate. Understanding it requires understanding biology, hydrology, history, politics, and economics simultaneously. Protecting it requires all of those things, and political will beside.
