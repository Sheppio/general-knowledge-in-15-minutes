# The Discovery of DNA: The Secret of Life

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

In the spring of 1953, two young scientists working in a cluttered laboratory at Cambridge University proposed a structure for a molecule that most people had barely heard of. Their paper, published in the journal Nature on the twenty-fifth of April, 1953, was just over a thousand words long. It contained one diagram. It ended with what must rank as one of the great understatements in the history of science: "It has not escaped our notice that the specific pairing we have postulated immediately suggests a possible copying mechanism for the genetic material."

In other words: we think we have just explained how life reproduces itself.

The scientists were James Watson and Francis Crick. The molecule was deoxyribonucleic acid — DNA. And their discovery of its double helix structure did not just solve a problem in chemistry. It opened an entirely new era in the history of biology, medicine, and our understanding of what we are.

---

To understand why the discovery mattered so much, it helps to understand what was already known. Since the mid-nineteenth century, biologists had understood that traits were passed from parent to offspring through some kind of physical material. Gregor Mendel had worked out the mathematical rules of inheritance through his famous experiments with pea plants in the 1860s. But what was the physical substance that carried these instructions? For a long time, proteins were the leading candidate — they are complex, varied, and seemed capable of encoding the vast amount of information that heredity requires. DNA was known to exist in the nucleus of every cell, but it was thought to be too simple and repetitive for the job.

That changed in 1944, when the Canadian-American scientist Oswald Avery published careful experiments demonstrating that DNA, not protein, was the substance that carried genetic information from one generation of bacteria to another. Avery's work was transformative but controversial, and it took years to be fully accepted. By the early 1950s, the scientific community had largely concluded that DNA was the carrier of genetic information. The urgent question was: what did it look like, and how did it work?

---

The answer required both chemistry and physics. X-ray crystallography — firing X-rays at crystals of a substance and analysing the diffraction patterns they produced — was the key technique for revealing molecular structure. The best crystallographic work on DNA was being done in London, at King's College, by Rosalind Franklin and her colleague Maurice Wilkins. Franklin was a meticulous and gifted scientist, and her X-ray images of DNA were the clearest produced to that point. One image in particular — taken on the second of May, 1952, and known as Photo 51 — captured the helical structure of the molecule with exceptional clarity.

Watson and Crick, working at the Cavendish Laboratory in Cambridge, were approaching the problem differently — through model-building, constructing physical models of molecules that obeyed the known rules of chemistry. In January 1953, Wilkins showed Watson Photo 51 without Franklin's knowledge or permission. Watson later wrote that the moment he saw it, his mouth fell open and his pulse began to race. The image immediately revealed the key features of the structure.

Armed with Franklin's data and their own model-building intuition, Watson and Crick worked rapidly. Within weeks they had the answer.

---

DNA is a double helix — two strands wound around each other like a twisted ladder, with the rungs formed by pairs of chemical bases. Each base on one strand pairs specifically with one base on the other: adenine always pairs with thymine, cytosine always pairs with guanine. This pairing is not just elegant. It is the mechanism of life itself. When a cell divides, the two strands of the helix unzip, and each serves as a template for building a new partner strand. In this way, the genetic information encoded in the sequence of bases is copied faithfully every time a cell reproduces.

Franklin herself, working independently from her crystallographic data, was close to the same conclusion and would likely have arrived at it shortly. She died of ovarian cancer in 1958, at the age of thirty-seven, never fully knowing how central her work had been to the discovery. In 1962, Watson, Crick, and Wilkins shared the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine. The Nobel is not awarded posthumously, and Franklin's name was not on it. The question of how much credit she deserved — and how much was taken without acknowledgement — has been debated ever since, and her contribution is now more widely recognised than it was in her lifetime.

---

The implications of the discovery unfolded over the following decades. Once the structure was known, scientists could begin to read the molecule. The genetic code — the way sequences of bases encode the instructions for building proteins — was cracked through the 1960s. By the 1970s, techniques had been developed to cut and recombine DNA from different organisms, launching the era of genetic engineering. The Human Genome Project, completed in 2003, mapped the entire sequence of the human genome — three billion base pairs encoding the instructions for a human being.

Today, DNA testing identifies criminals and exonerates the wrongly convicted. It diagnoses inherited diseases before they manifest. It traces the migrations of ancient human populations across tens of thousands of years. The mRNA vaccines developed to fight COVID-19 — designed and tested in months rather than years — were possible only because of the decades of molecular biology that Watson and Crick's discovery set in motion.

All of it traces back to that one diagram, and those thousand words, and two scientists at a Cambridge bench in the spring of 1953 — and to the woman whose X-ray image showed them what to look for.
