# The History of Medicine — From Galen to Germ Theory

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Imagine you are ill in the ancient world. Your head is pounding, your skin burns, and your vision swims with fever. You go to the physician, and he looks at your eyes, listens to you breathe, and then tells you that the problem is simple: your blood and your bile are out of balance. He might recommend rest, a change in diet, perhaps a mild purge, or if the case seems serious, a controlled removal of blood from your arm. This treatment is not superstition. It is, by the standards of its time, rigorous scientific medicine. And it will dominate the way human beings treat the sick for the next two thousand years.

To understand how medicine got from there to here — from guesswork wrapped in philosophy to the precise identification of bacteria under a microscope — you have to start on a small Greek island in the fifth century before the common era, with a man named Hippocrates.

Hippocrates was born around four hundred and sixty BC on the island of Cos, in the Aegean Sea. We know surprisingly little about him personally; he is more legend than biography. But what we know with confidence is that he founded a tradition of medical thought that turned illness from a divine punishment into a natural phenomenon. Before Hippocrates, disease was largely understood as the work of gods or demons. The sick prayed, sacrificed animals, and visited temples. Hippocrates and the school he founded on Cos argued instead that the body obeyed its own laws, and that careful observation of symptoms could reveal what had gone wrong.

The framework he and his followers developed was called humoral theory. The body, they believed, contained four vital fluids — blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile. Health was a state of balance among these four humours. Illness was an imbalance. Each humour corresponded to a season, an element, and a temperament. Blood made you sanguine and warm. Black bile made you melancholic. The goal of medicine was to restore equilibrium: through diet, through rest, through purging, or through bloodletting. It was a beautifully organised system, logical within its own premises, and the fact that none of it was true would not become apparent for a very long time.

Hippocrates gave medicine something else that outlasted his theory of disease, however. He gave it a set of ethical commitments. The Hippocratic tradition insisted that physicians observe their patients carefully, keep detailed records, and do their best to cause no harm. The famous oath attributed to him — do no harm, maintain confidentiality, serve the patient — remains the symbolic foundation of medical ethics to this day. He may have got the biology wrong, but he got the vocation right.

---

Into this tradition stepped the man who would carry it forward for over a thousand years. Galen of Pergamon was born around one hundred and twenty-nine AD in what is now western Turkey, in a prosperous city that contained a famous shrine to the healing god Asclepius. He studied medicine at the great library city of Alexandria in Egypt, returned home to serve as physician to a school of gladiators — a job that gave him unparalleled experience treating traumatic wounds — and eventually arrived in Rome, where his public demonstrations of anatomy and his extraordinary clinical success made him the most famous physician in the empire. He became personal physician to the Emperor Marcus Aurelius.

Galen was brilliant, prolific, and almost entirely wrong about the interior of the human body. He had a fundamental problem: in Roman society, it was not acceptable to dissect human corpses. He therefore based his anatomical conclusions on dissections of animals — pigs, goats, and monkeys — then applied what he found to humans, producing descriptions that were sometimes accurate and sometimes wildly mistaken. He believed blood was continuously manufactured by the liver and consumed by the organs, rather than circulating. He described holes in the cardiac septum that do not exist.

None of this mattered for the next thirteen hundred years, because nobody checked. Galen's authority became so total that questioning him was considered professionally reckless. Medical students were taught to read Galen and then explain why a cadaver seemed to differ from what he had described — the assumption always being that the cadaver was the anomaly. For more than a millennium, the study of medicine was, in large part, the study of one man's works.

---

It would be a mistake, though, to imagine those thirteen hundred years as a dark age of stagnation. In the Islamic world, from roughly the eighth through the thirteenth centuries, scholars were not merely preserving ancient Greek knowledge — they were extending and refining it. Hospitals were built in Baghdad and Cairo that were sophisticated institutions of care and learning, with separate wards for different illnesses, apothecary departments, and formal libraries. Greek texts were translated into Arabic and studied with serious critical attention.

The most towering figure of Islamic medicine was a Persian physician and philosopher named Ibn Sina, known in the West as Avicenna. He was born around nine hundred and eighty AD, and he completed his masterwork, The Canon of Medicine, in ten twenty-five. It was a staggering achievement: five dense volumes that synthesised Greek, Roman, Persian, Indian, and Chinese medical knowledge into a single comprehensive system. It described hundreds of drug preparations and their properties. It offered clinical insights still worth reading today — including a remarkably early discussion of the possibility that diseases could be transmitted through water and soil and that the body might be protected through what we would now call quarantine.

The Canon was translated into Latin in the twelfth century and adopted as the standard medical textbook at European universities including Paris, Padua, and Oxford. In the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries alone it was printed more than thirty-five times. Ibn Sina remained, alongside Galen, one of the two pillars of European medical education well into the seventeenth century. He was working largely within the humoral framework he had inherited, and he accepted much of Galen's anatomy, but he brought to that tradition a rigour, a curiosity, and a willingness to observe that kept medicine alive when it might otherwise have simply ossified.

---

What finally cracked the edifice was not philosophy. It was bodies.

Andreas Vesalius was born in Brussels on the last day of fifteen fourteen. He was the son of a court pharmacist, trained in the finest medical faculties in Europe — Louvain and Paris — and appointed, at the astonishingly young age of twenty-three, to the chair of anatomy and surgery at the University of Padua. From his very first lectures, he did something scandalous: he performed his own dissections, standing beside the table himself, rather than reading from Galen while a barber-surgeon did the cutting below. And as he cut, he looked. He looked carefully, patiently, and without assumption.

What he saw contradicted Galen at almost every turn. The jaw was not structured the way Galen described it. The breastbone did not have seven segments, as Galen claimed. The liver did not have five lobes. The vaunted holes in the cardiac septum were nowhere to be found. Vesalius understood what this meant: Galen had been dissecting animals and calling the results human anatomy.

In fifteen forty-three — the same year Copernicus published his argument that the Earth orbits the Sun — Vesalius published De Humani Corporis Fabrica, "On the Fabric of the Human Body." Seven volumes, illustrated with extraordinary precision by artists from the workshop of the great Venetian painter Titian, showing the human body as it actually was: its muscles, bones, organs, vessels, and nerves rendered with a fidelity no previous work had approached. It was the most accurate anatomical description ever made, and it was a direct challenge to thirteen centuries of Galenic certainty.

The medical establishment did not take this quietly. Vesalius was attacked by former teachers and colleagues who accused him of arrogance and error. But the evidence was in the book, and the book was in libraries across Europe, and slowly, over the following decades, a generation of anatomists began doing what Vesalius had done: looking for themselves.

One of the things they looked at was the heart.

William Harvey was an English physician born in fifteen seventy-eight who had studied at Padua — where Vesalius's tradition was still very much alive — and returned to England to practice medicine and pursue his anatomical investigations. The question he focused on was blood: how much of it was there, and where did it go? Galen had taught that the liver produced blood continuously and that it was consumed by the tissues. Harvey calculated the volume of blood ejected from the heart with each beat, multiplied by the heart rate, and arrived at a number that made the Galenic model impossible. The liver could not manufacture blood fast enough. There had to be circulation.

In sixteen twenty-eight, Harvey published his findings in a short book, Exercitatio Anatomica de Motu Cordis et Sanguinis in Animalibus — "An Anatomical Exercise on the Motion of the Heart and Blood in Living Beings." He demonstrated with careful experiments on living animals and human cadavers that blood moves in a single closed circuit, pumped by the heart through the arteries and returned through the veins. The heart was not, as many had imagined, a kind of furnace heating the blood; it was a pump. Blood circulated. It did not get used up.

Harvey was careful and courteous in his challenge to tradition, but the implications were enormous. The body's internal economy was not what two thousand years of medicine had assumed. And if the basic mechanics of blood could be so thoroughly misunderstood for so long, what else might be wrong?

---

The answer, it turned out, was almost everything about what caused disease in the first place.

Through the seventeenth, eighteenth, and well into the nineteenth century, the dominant theory of disease was miasma — the idea that illness arose from bad air, from the rotting vapours of swamps and sewers, from the general atmospheric corruption of dirty, crowded places. There was nothing foolish about this belief. Cities that stank did tend to have more disease. Draining swamps did reduce malaria. The miasmatic framework produced real public health action, even if its explanation was entirely wrong.

The alternative theory — that disease was caused by tiny living organisms — had a long prehistory. A sixteenth-century Italian physician named Girolamo Fracastoro had speculated about seeds of disease passing between people. Antonie van Leeuwenhoek, the Dutch lens-grinder who invented the microscope in the seventeenth century, had actually seen bacteria, though he did not connect them to disease. But through the early nineteenth century, germ theory remained a minority position, plausible to some, unproven, and easily dismissed.

Then came a Hungarian physician working in Vienna named Ignaz Semmelweis, and the result was one of the saddest episodes in the history of medicine.

In eighteen forty-seven, Semmelweis was working in the maternity wards of the Vienna General Hospital. There were two wards. In the first, staffed by medical students who also performed autopsies, the mortality rate from childbed fever — puerperal fever — was catastrophically high, sometimes reaching eighteen percent. In the second ward, staffed by midwives who did not handle cadavers, the rate was a fraction of that. Semmelweis noticed this disparity, connected it to the cadaver work, and instituted a simple rule: everyone entering the maternity ward had to wash their hands with a chlorinated lime solution before touching a patient.

The results were immediate and spectacular. Mortality in his ward dropped to barely one percent. He had, without knowing the mechanism, identified the first practical antiseptic protocol in clinical medicine.

What happened next is a warning about how science and medicine actually function, as opposed to how we might wish they did. Semmelweis could not explain why handwashing worked in terms his colleagues would accept. He had no theory of germs. Germ theory was not yet established. The medical establishment found his implication — that doctors were killing their own patients by carrying contamination from the morgue — professionally offensive and theoretically unsupported. He was dismissed. He was ridiculed. He grew increasingly erratic in his arguments and his behaviour, and in eighteen sixty-five he died in an asylum, likely from the very kind of infection he had spent his career trying to prevent.

In that same year, a French chemist named Louis Pasteur was well into the experiments that would provide the explanation Semmelweis never had.

Pasteur was not a physician. He was a chemist who had become fascinated by fermentation — the process by which grape juice becomes wine, milk becomes sour. He showed in the eighteen fifties that fermentation was caused by living microorganisms, not by chemical reactions alone. In eighteen sixty-one, he performed his famous swan-neck flask experiments, in which broth was sterilised and then exposed to air through a long, curved neck that allowed air to enter but trapped dust particles carrying microorganisms. The broth stayed clear. When the neck was broken off and microorganisms could reach the broth directly, it clouded with growth. Spontaneous generation — the ancient idea that life could arise from nothing in decaying matter — was disproved. Life came from life, and the microorganisms in the air were the real agents of putrefaction.

From there, the leap to disease was not long. In eighteen seventy-eight, Pasteur presented to the French Academy of Sciences his germ theory of disease: specific microorganisms caused specific illnesses. He went on to develop vaccines for chicken cholera, anthrax, and — in a dramatic and celebrated demonstration in eighteen eighty-five — rabies. He had transformed chemistry and biology and, in the process, redirected all of medicine.

But it was a German bacteriologist who supplied the methodological rigour that turned germ theory into a settled science. Robert Koch identified the anthrax bacillus in eighteen seventy-six, establishing with full experimental proof that a specific microorganism caused a specific disease. He developed techniques for growing pure bacterial cultures and for proving causation through what became known as Koch's postulates — a procedure that made germ theory testable and falsifiable.

On the twenty-fourth of March, eighteen eighty-two, Koch announced that he had identified the bacterium responsible for tuberculosis — Mycobacterium tuberculosis — the disease that was killing one in seven people across Europe. Koch went on to identify the cholera bacterium in eighteen eighty-three and was awarded the Nobel Prize in nineteen oh five.

Meanwhile, a surgeon in Glasgow named Joseph Lister had been reading Pasteur's work and thinking about his operating theatre. Surgery in the eighteen sixties was a matter of speed and skill — and death. Between eighteen sixty-one and eighteen sixty-five, nearly half the patients in Lister's amputation ward died from post-operative infection. Surgeons of the era operated in street clothes, used unsterilised instruments, and sewed wounds with silk thread they kept tucked behind their lapels for convenience. Nobody thought to wash their hands.

Lister read Pasteur, understood immediately what the implications were, and in eighteen sixty-five began applying carbolic acid — phenol — as an antiseptic to wounds, to surgical instruments, to sutures, and to the surrounding air via spray. The results paralleled Semmelweis's: mortality in his ward fell dramatically, from somewhere near fifty percent to around fifteen percent. In eighteen sixty-seven he published his findings in the Lancet, and antiseptic surgery began its spread through the medical world. By the end of the nineteenth century, the operating theatre had been transformed from a chamber of near-certain infection into a controlled, sterile environment where survival was the expected outcome.

---

The cumulative effect of these revolutions — anatomical, physiological, microbiological, surgical — was something without precedent in human history: medicine that actually worked.

Two thousand years of humoral theory had produced practitioners who were often kind, occasionally effective by accident, and mostly powerless against the biological realities of infection and epidemic disease. The century between roughly eighteen forty and nineteen forty-five replaced that powerlessness with genuine understanding. Once you knew cholera spread through contaminated water rather than bad air, you could build sewers and save millions without touching a patient. John Snow's mapping of the Broad Street cholera outbreak in London in eighteen fifty-four — tracing deaths back to a single contaminated pump — is one of the founding acts of modern epidemiology.

From germ theory flowed bacteriology, immunology, vaccines, and antibiotics. Alexander Fleming's observation of penicillin mould killing bacteria in nineteen twenty-eight, and penicillin's development as a therapeutic drug in the nineteen forties, gave medicine its first reliable weapon against bacterial infection.

Hippocrates was right that disease has natural causes and that observation matters. Galen was right that anatomy could be studied systematically. Ibn Sina was right that knowledge should be gathered from everywhere and organised with rigour. Vesalius was right that you have to look at the evidence even when it contradicts authority. Harvey was right to trust mathematics and experiment over ancient texts. Semmelweis was right, tragically right, before anyone was ready to believe him. Pasteur and Koch gave the world both the explanation and the method. Lister turned that knowledge into a practice that saved lives in real time.

The history of medicine is not a story of steady progress. It is a story of long periods of confident error, interrupted by individuals willing to look carefully at the world and report what they actually see, even when what they see overturns everything that came before. That is, in the end, what makes it a human story — and one very much worth knowing.
