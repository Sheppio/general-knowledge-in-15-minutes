# The Big Bang Theory: The Origin of Everything

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

The greatest question in all of science is also the simplest to state: where did everything come from? For most of human history, the answer was theological — the universe was created by a god or gods, whose motivations might be understood through myth or scripture but whose methods were beyond investigation. In the twentieth century, for the first time, science developed tools powerful enough to begin answering the question empirically, through observation, mathematics, and inference from physical evidence. What those tools revealed was stranger and more astonishing than any creation myth: a universe that had a definite beginning in a state of almost incomprehensible density and heat, some thirteen point eight billion years ago, from which everything we know — every atom in every star in every galaxy — ultimately emerged.

The theory that describes this beginning is called the Big Bang. It is the most thoroughly tested and most widely accepted account of the origin of the universe in the history of human thought. It is supported by multiple independent lines of evidence, makes predictions that have been confirmed to extraordinary precision, and has survived every challenge that observation and experiment have posed to it. It is also, in important ways, deeply counterintuitive — because it does not describe the universe beginning inside something else; it describes space itself, and time itself, beginning. There was no "before" the Big Bang, and the question of what exists "outside" the universe has no meaning in ordinary physics. These are not evasions; they are what the mathematics and the evidence actually say.

---

The story of how humans arrived at this understanding unfolded over less than a century, through a series of theoretical insights and observational discoveries that repeatedly overturned what everyone thought they knew.

At the start of the twentieth century, the standard view among scientists was that the universe was static and eternal — that it had always existed, in roughly its current form, without beginning or end. This view was so deeply embedded that even Albert Einstein, when he developed his theory of general relativity in 1915 — a theory that described gravity as the curvature of spacetime — found that his equations predicted a universe that was either expanding or contracting. This seemed wrong to him. To force his equations to describe a static universe, he added an extra term — a "cosmological constant" — which he would later call the greatest blunder of his career.

The first person to take seriously what Einstein's equations actually implied was a Russian mathematician named Alexander Friedmann, who in 1922 derived solutions to Einstein's equations that described a universe either expanding or contracting over time. Friedmann's work was largely ignored. In 1927, a Belgian physicist and Roman Catholic priest named Georges Lemaître independently arrived at the same mathematical solutions, and went a step further: he proposed that the expansion of the universe implied it had been smaller in the past, and might have originated from a single initial state of enormous density. In 1931, he published a paper proposing that the universe had expanded from what he called a "primeval atom" — a single quantum of energy from which everything else had grown.

---

The observational evidence for an expanding universe arrived in 1929, when the American astronomer Edwin Hubble published measurements showing that distant galaxies were moving away from us — and that the further away a galaxy was, the faster it was receding. This relationship, now known as Hubble's Law, was exactly what an expanding universe would produce: if the universe is getting bigger, then every point in it is moving away from every other point, and the further apart two points are, the faster they separate. Traced backwards, the expansion implied a time when everything had been in the same place.

The term "Big Bang" itself was coined not by a supporter of the theory but by one of its fiercest critics. Fred Hoyle, a brilliant British astronomer, advocated an alternative model called the Steady State theory, which proposed that the universe had always existed in roughly its current form, with matter continuously being created to fill the space opened up by expansion. In a 1950 BBC radio broadcast, Hoyle used the phrase "Big Bang" to describe what he saw as an absurd competing idea — intending it as mockery. The name stuck, which must have irritated him greatly.

The decisive evidence for the Big Bang came in 1964, when two American radio astronomers working at Bell Telephone Laboratories in New Jersey made one of the great accidental discoveries in the history of science. Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson were trying to build a very precise microwave antenna and kept detecting a persistent background noise they could not explain — they even checked whether pigeons nesting in the antenna were responsible, and removed them, and the noise remained. The noise turned out to be the cosmic microwave background: a faint glow of radiation filling the entire universe uniformly in every direction. It was exactly what theorists had predicted would exist if the universe had once been an extremely hot, dense plasma that had cooled as it expanded. The cosmic microwave background was the afterglow of the Big Bang itself, still echoing across the universe nearly fourteen billion years after the event. Penzias and Wilson were awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1978.

---

The discovery of the cosmic microwave background transformed the Big Bang from a mathematical speculation into an empirically confirmed theory. It was not the only confirmation. The theory also predicted that the early universe, during the first few minutes after the Big Bang, would have been hot and dense enough for nuclear fusion to occur, producing hydrogen and helium in specific proportions. When astronomers measured the amounts of hydrogen and helium in the oldest parts of the universe, the proportions matched the Big Bang's predictions almost exactly. This process — Big Bang nucleosynthesis — explains why the universe is made of roughly seventy-five percent hydrogen and twenty-five percent helium by mass, with only tiny traces of heavier elements. Heavier elements — carbon, oxygen, iron, gold — were forged later in the cores of stars and scattered into space when those stars died.

Further confirmation came from increasingly precise measurements of the cosmic microwave background itself. The COBE satellite, launched in 1989, found tiny variations in the temperature of the background radiation — fluctuations of about one part in a hundred thousand — that matched theoretical predictions about the seeds from which galaxies would later form. The WMAP satellite, launched in 2001, and the Planck satellite, launched in 2009, measured these fluctuations in extraordinary detail. From them, cosmologists derived what is now known as the standard model of cosmology: a universe thirteen point eight billion years old, consisting of roughly five percent ordinary matter, twenty-seven percent dark matter, and sixty-eight percent dark energy.

Dark matter and dark energy are among the deepest mysteries in modern physics. Dark matter is an invisible substance that does not interact with light but does exert gravitational pull; its existence is inferred from the way galaxies rotate and the way light bends around massive clusters of galaxies, but its physical nature is unknown. Dark energy is even more mysterious: it is the name given to whatever is causing the expansion of the universe to accelerate rather than slow down, and its nature is entirely unknown. The universe, it turns out, is made mostly of things we cannot see and do not understand.

---

The very beginning of the universe remains, in some respects, a frontier. The Big Bang theory describes what happened from a tiny fraction of a second after the beginning — when the universe was already a hot plasma undergoing rapid expansion — but it does not, in its standard form, describe the very moment of the beginning itself. The mathematics breaks down at what physicists call the singularity: a point of infinite density and temperature where the known laws of physics cease to apply. What happened at or before that moment, whether the Big Bang was truly the absolute beginning or an event within some larger framework, remains one of the deepest open questions in physics.

Theories of cosmic inflation — the idea that the universe underwent an extraordinary burst of near-instantaneous expansion in the first tiny fraction of a second — explain several otherwise puzzling features of the universe's structure, including why it looks the same in every direction and why its large-scale geometry appears to be flat. But inflation itself raises further questions: what caused it, how it ended, and whether our universe is one of many produced by ongoing inflationary processes in some larger framework that physicists call the multiverse. These are questions that may or may not be answerable with the tools of observation and experiment.

What is settled is the broad picture: a universe that began hot and dense thirteen point eight billion years ago; expanded, cooled, and complexified; produced atoms, stars, galaxies, planets, and eventually the minds capable of asking where everything came from. The answer to that question — as far as science has taken us — is more ancient, more vast, and more astonishing than our ancestors imagined. And the tools we used to find it — mathematics, careful observation, and the willingness to follow evidence wherever it leads, even into counterintuition — are among the greatest achievements of the human mind.
