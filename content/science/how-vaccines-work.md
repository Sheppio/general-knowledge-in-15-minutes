# How Vaccines Work: The Science That Saved Billions of Lives

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Vaccines are the most effective public health intervention in human history, with the possible exception of clean water. They have eradicated one disease entirely, brought several others to the brink of extinction, and prevented an estimated two to three million deaths every year. The COVID-19 pandemic, in which vaccines were developed with unprecedented speed and deployed at unprecedented scale, demonstrated both the power of the underlying science and the depth of the political and social challenges involved in using it. Understanding how vaccines work is understanding one of the most important ideas in all of medicine — and one of the clearest demonstrations of science's capacity to improve human life.

The core idea is elegant. The human immune system is capable of learning from encounters with pathogens. When it encounters a new virus or bacterium, it mounts a response — producing antibodies and activating immune cells to attack the invader — and it remembers that encounter. If the same pathogen is encountered again, the immune system responds faster and more powerfully, often defeating the infection before it causes serious illness. Vaccination exploits this memory: it exposes the immune system to something that resembles a pathogen — but cannot cause disease — allowing it to learn and remember without the risks of actual infection. When the real pathogen arrives, the prepared immune system can destroy it.

---

The practice of deliberately inducing immunity against disease predates modern immunology by centuries. In China and the Ottoman Empire, a practice known as variolation — inoculating people with material taken from smallpox pustules — was used to protect against the disease. Variolation reduced the risk of dying from smallpox substantially, but it was not without danger: some people who were variolated developed the full disease and died.

The person who transformed this practice into modern vaccination was Edward Jenner, an English country physician in Gloucestershire. Jenner had noticed — as had many in rural England — that milkmaids who contracted cowpox, a relatively mild disease affecting cattle, seemed to be protected against smallpox, one of the most feared killers of the age. In 1796, he tested the observation directly: he took material from a cowpox pustule on the hand of a milkmaid named Sarah Nelmes and inoculated an eight-year-old boy, James Phipps, with it. The boy developed a mild illness and recovered. Jenner then inoculated the boy with actual smallpox material — and the boy did not develop the disease. The experiment was a success.

Jenner named the procedure vaccination, from the Latin vacca, meaning cow — a word that has given us every subsequent vaccine, no matter what animal or substance it involves. He published his findings in 1798. Within years, vaccination against smallpox was spreading across Europe and the Americas, and smallpox mortality began its long decline. The principle had been demonstrated: a safe exposure to one pathogen could confer immunity against a related, more dangerous one.

---

The scientific understanding of why vaccination worked came later, with Louis Pasteur's development of germ theory in the second half of the nineteenth century. Pasteur demonstrated that infectious diseases were caused by specific microorganisms — bacteria and, later understood, viruses — and that the immune system responded specifically to those organisms. In 1881, to honour Jenner's contribution and to distinguish his new procedures from Jenner's original work, Pasteur proposed extending the term "vaccine" to cover all protective inoculations. He had himself developed vaccines against chicken cholera and anthrax through a process of attenuation — weakening the pathogen so that it could stimulate an immune response without causing disease. His dramatic public demonstration of his anthrax vaccine on a flock of sheep — the vaccinated animals surviving exposure to anthrax while the unvaccinated ones died — was one of the most persuasive scientific demonstrations in history. He went on to develop a vaccine against rabies, used to save the life of a boy named Joseph Meister who had been mauled by a rabid dog in 1885.

The immune system, as twentieth-century science revealed in increasing detail, is extraordinarily complex. When the body encounters a pathogen, it deploys several overlapping lines of defence. B cells produce antibodies — proteins specifically shaped to bind to antigens, the molecular markers on the pathogen's surface. Antibodies can neutralise pathogens directly or mark them for destruction by other immune cells. T cells coordinate the immune response and can directly kill infected cells. The crucial element for vaccination is the memory: both B cells and T cells can persist in the body for years or decades after an initial exposure, ready to mount a rapid response if the same pathogen is encountered again. This immunological memory is what vaccines create.

---

Different types of vaccines work through different mechanisms, reflecting the accumulated ingenuity of more than a century of vaccinology. Live attenuated vaccines use a weakened form of the pathogen itself: because it can still replicate inside the body, it produces a very strong immune response, typically requiring only one or two doses. The vaccines against measles, mumps, rubella, chickenpox, and yellow fever are of this type. Inactivated vaccines use killed pathogens: they cannot replicate, so the immune response is weaker and booster doses are typically required. Polio (injected), influenza, and hepatitis A vaccines are of this type.

Subunit vaccines use only specific proteins from the pathogen — the antigens that the immune system needs to recognise — rather than the whole pathogen. Because they contain no genetic material from the pathogen, they cannot cause disease under any circumstances. The vaccines against hepatitis B and HPV are subunit vaccines. Toxoid vaccines target the toxins produced by bacteria rather than the bacteria themselves: the tetanus and diphtheria vaccines are of this type.

The most recent category — messenger RNA vaccines, which achieved global prominence during the COVID-19 pandemic — works differently again. Instead of introducing an antigen or an attenuated pathogen directly, they deliver instructions (in the form of mRNA) that tell the body's own cells to produce a specific viral protein. The immune system then recognises and responds to that protein, building memory. Moderna and Pfizer-BioNTech deployed this technology at astonishing speed in 2020 and 2021, producing highly effective vaccines against COVID-19 within a year of the virus's identification — a feat that would have seemed impossible to previous generations of vaccine scientists.

---

The history of vaccination is a history of hard-won victories against diseases that had shaped human life for millennia. The eradication of smallpox — the only human disease ever to be deliberately eliminated — stands as the greatest triumph. The World Health Organization launched a global eradication campaign in 1958; through an intensive programme of vaccination and containment over the following two decades, the last naturally occurring case of smallpox was recorded in Somalia in October 1977. On the eighth of May, 1980, the World Health Assembly formally declared smallpox eradicated. A disease that had killed hundreds of millions of people over thousands of years — that had shaped the history of civilisations, that had devastated indigenous populations in the Americas when Europeans carried it there — was gone.

Polio has been reduced from roughly three hundred and fifty thousand cases per year globally in 1988 to fewer than a hundred in recent years, largely through the oral vaccine developed by Albert Sabin and the injected vaccine developed by Jonas Salk. Measles, which killed millions of children annually before vaccination, has been dramatically reduced in countries with high vaccination coverage. Diseases like diphtheria, whooping cough, and tetanus, which once routinely killed children in wealthy countries as well as poor ones, have become rare.

---

The effectiveness of vaccines depends not only on the vaccine itself but on the proportion of the population that is vaccinated. Herd immunity — the indirect protection afforded to unvaccinated individuals when enough of those around them are immune — is crucial to controlling infectious disease. The threshold for herd immunity varies by pathogen: for the highly contagious measles virus, roughly ninety-five percent of the population needs to be immune to prevent sustained transmission. When vaccination rates fall below that threshold — as they have in various countries at various times, partly due to anti-vaccine movements — previously controlled diseases return.

The modern anti-vaccination movement traces its recent acceleration to a 1998 paper by British physician Andrew Wakefield, published in The Lancet, which claimed a link between the MMR vaccine and autism. The paper was fraudulent — Wakefield had undisclosed financial conflicts of interest and had manipulated data — and was formally retracted by The Lancet in 2010 after an investigation. Wakefield's medical licence was revoked. But the damage had been done: the false claim spread far faster than the correction, and vaccination rates fell in several countries, leading to measles outbreaks in populations that had previously controlled the disease.

The science of vaccination is among the clearest and most consequential bodies of knowledge humanity has assembled. The diseases it has controlled or eliminated caused suffering on a scale that is difficult to comprehend today precisely because vaccination has made that scale invisible. The challenge is not scientific but social: ensuring that the trust, access, and political will exist to use the tools that are available.
