# The History of Astronomy

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Welcome to General Knowledge in fifteen minutes. Today, we are going on one of the longest journeys the human mind has ever taken — from the very first moment someone looked up at the night sky and started asking questions, all the way to the cutting edge of modern science, where we are peering back to the first light of the universe itself. This is the history of astronomy.

It begins, as so many stories do, with ordinary people doing extraordinary things simply by paying attention.

Thousands of years before the invention of writing, human beings were already tracking the movements of the Sun, the Moon, and the visible planets. They noticed that the sky was not random. It was rhythmic. The Moon swelled and shrank on a predictable schedule. The Sun rose earlier in summer and later in winter. Certain stars appeared on the horizon just before the summer floods came. In a world without calendars, without clocks, without any of the tools we take for granted, the sky was the most reliable timekeeper available. And so watching it was not merely philosophical — it was practical. It was survival.

Of all the ancient civilisations who turned this watching into something systematic, the Babylonians stand apart. Living in what is now Iraq, they kept meticulous records of celestial events for centuries. By around seven hundred BC, Babylonian astronomers had identified the five planets visible to the naked eye — Mercury, Venus, Mars, Jupiter, and Saturn — and had tracked their movements with impressive accuracy. They developed arithmetical methods for predicting lunar eclipses. They invented the zodiac, dividing the sky into twelve equal sections. And crucially, they built up vast archives of observations — clay tablets inscribed with numbers, dates, and positions, accumulated over generations. These records would later prove invaluable to the Greeks.

---

Greek astronomy inherited the Babylonian data and transformed it into something new: a theoretical framework. The Greeks did not just want to predict where the planets would be. They wanted to understand why they moved the way they did. This ambition produced some of the most durable — and some of the most wrong — ideas in the history of science.

Among the greatest Greek astronomers was Hipparchus, who worked on the island of Rhodes in the second century BC, living from approximately one hundred and ninety to one hundred and twenty BC. Hipparchus was remarkable for his rigour. He compiled a catalogue of over eight hundred and fifty stars, recording their positions and brightness with greater accuracy than anyone before him. He noticed, by comparing his observations with records made roughly one hundred and sixty years earlier, that the stars seemed to have shifted very slightly against the backdrop of the sky. This was not an observational error. It was a real phenomenon: the slow wobble of Earth's rotational axis, a process that takes about twenty-six thousand years to complete one full cycle. Hipparchus had discovered the precession of the equinoxes, an insight so subtle and so technically demanding that it astonishes astronomers even today.

But the figure whose ideas would shape Western thought for the next fourteen centuries was not Hipparchus. It was Claudius Ptolemy, a Greek astronomer working in Alexandria, Egypt, in the second century AD. Around the year one hundred and fifty AD, Ptolemy produced a monumental work known by its Arabic name, the Almagest — meaning, roughly, "the greatest." In it, he presented a complete mathematical model of the universe, one in which the Earth sat motionless at the centre, and the Sun, Moon, planets, and stars all moved around it in elaborate circular paths. To explain the complex looping movements that planets appeared to make in the sky — what we now call retrograde motion — Ptolemy introduced the idea of epicycles: small circles upon circles, wheels within wheels, each planet tracing a kind of spirograph pattern as it orbited the Earth.

It was extraordinarily clever. And for over a thousand years, in Europe, in the Islamic world, and across North Africa, the Almagest was the standard astronomical authority. Ptolemy's geocentric model was not just widely accepted — it was, for most of that period, almost unquestioned.

---

The challenge to that model, when it finally came, arrived from an unlikely place: a Polish canon and mathematician named Nicolaus Copernicus, who had spent decades quietly working on a radical alternative. In fifteen forty-three, the year of his death, Copernicus published a book called De Revolutionibus Orbium Coelestium — On the Revolutions of the Heavenly Spheres. According to legend, a copy was placed in his hands on the very day he died.

The book proposed something that contradicted centuries of received wisdom: that the Earth was not the centre of the universe. Instead, the Earth and all the other planets moved around the Sun. Copernicus had flipped the cosmic order. But he had done so with considerable caution. He still believed the planets moved in perfect circles. And he still relied on some mathematical tricks not entirely unlike Ptolemy's. His model was simpler in some respects, but it was not dramatically more accurate at predicting planetary positions than the Ptolemaic system. What it offered was a different vision of our place in the cosmos — a vision that, for many, was profoundly unsettling.

The man who brought the precision to test this vision was Tycho Brahe, a Danish nobleman who was perhaps the most meticulous naked-eye observer in history. Working from his island observatory in Denmark in the late sixteenth century, Tycho spent twenty years recording the positions of planets and stars with extraordinary care. His measurements were far more accurate than anything Copernicus had had access to. Tycho himself could not bring himself to fully accept the Copernican model. He proposed instead a compromise system in which the Sun and Moon orbited the Earth, while the other planets orbited the Sun. But his real contribution was the data — and when he died in sixteen oh one, he passed those records to his young assistant. That assistant was Johannes Kepler.

Kepler was a German mathematician and mystic with a genius for pattern. He inherited Tycho's observations and spent years wrestling with them, trying to make the numbers fit a model of circular planetary orbits. They would not fit — not quite. There was always a small discrepancy. And Kepler, to his great credit, trusted the data over the theory. In sixteen oh nine, he published his first two laws of planetary motion in a work called Astronomia Nova. The first law stated that planets do not move in circles at all, but in ellipses — oval paths, with the Sun at one of the two focal points. The second law described how a planet moves faster when it is closer to the Sun and slower when it is farther away. Ten years later, in sixteen nineteen, in a book called Harmonices Mundi, Kepler published his third law, which revealed a precise mathematical relationship between the time a planet takes to orbit the Sun and its average distance from it. Together, these three laws replaced the ancient machinery of circles and epicycles with something cleaner, more accurate, and more beautiful.

---

While Kepler worked with mathematics, another scientist was working with glass. In sixteen oh nine, the Italian physicist Galileo Galilei heard about a new device recently invented in the Netherlands — a spyglass that made distant objects appear closer. He built his own improved version, turned it towards the sky, and changed astronomy forever.

What Galileo saw through his telescope was incompatible with the old order. The Moon was not the perfect, unblemished sphere that classical philosophy required — it was mountainous and pocked with craters. The Sun had spots. And Jupiter, to Galileo's astonishment, had four small objects orbiting it — moons, revolving around a body that was decidedly not the Earth. In March sixteen ten, Galileo published his findings in a short book called Sidereus Nuncius, or Starry Messenger. He also later observed that Venus went through phases, just like the Moon — phases that only made sense if Venus was orbiting the Sun, not the Earth. Each of these discoveries chipped away at the Ptolemaic cosmos. Together, they were devastating.

Galileo became an outspoken advocate for the Copernican system. This put him on a collision course with the Catholic Church, which had come to regard geocentrism as aligned with scripture. In sixteen thirty-three, the Roman Inquisition put Galileo on trial. He was found vehemently suspect of heresy, forced to recant his support for the heliocentric model, and condemned to house arrest for the remaining nine years of his life. He died in sixteen forty-two, confined to his villa near Florence. But the ideas he championed could not be confined. They were already spreading across Europe, gathering force.

The person who would give them their most powerful mathematical foundation was born in England the very year Galileo died. Isaac Newton arrived at the University of Cambridge in the sixteen sixties and showed early signs of extraordinary ability. When plague closed the university in sixteen sixty-five, Newton retreated to his family farm in Lincolnshire — and it was there, in a remarkable burst of isolated creativity, that he began working out the ideas that would reshape physics and astronomy alike. The famous story of an apple falling from a tree may be simplified, but the insight it represents is real: Newton began thinking about whether the force that pulled objects to the Earth might be the same force that kept the Moon in its orbit.

It took him another two decades to develop the mathematics fully, but in sixteen eighty-seven, Newton published Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica — Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy. The Principia laid out his three laws of motion and, at its crowning achievement, his law of universal gravitation: the idea that every object in the universe attracts every other object with a force proportional to their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. With this single law, Newton could derive Kepler's three laws of planetary motion from first principles. He could explain why the planets moved the way they did. The universe, it turned out, ran on mathematics — precise, predictable, universal mathematics.

---

Newton had explained how the heavens moved. The next great task was to understand how large they were, and where we sat within them.

The first major expansion of our cosmic address book came from a German-born British musician turned astronomer named William Herschel. On the thirteenth of March, seventeen eighty-one, while conducting a systematic survey of the sky from his back garden in Bath, England, Herschel noticed an unusual object in the constellation of Gemini. It moved. Over the following weeks, observations confirmed it was not a comet but a planet — the seventh planet from the Sun, later named Uranus. It was the first planet discovered since antiquity, and Herschel became famous overnight.

But Herschel's ambitions were larger than a single planet. He built some of the most powerful telescopes of his age and set about trying to map the structure of the Milky Way by counting stars in different directions across the sky. His surveys suggested that our galaxy was a flattened disc of stars, roughly five times wider than it was thick — and, based on those counts, Herschel concluded that the Sun sat near the centre of this disc. He was right about the shape. He was wrong about our position. But the very fact that he was asking the question was itself revolutionary.

The correction to Herschel's picture came from an American astronomer named Harlow Shapley. In nineteen eighteen, Shapley was studying the distribution of globular clusters — dense, spherical collections of hundreds of thousands of stars scattered throughout the galaxy. He noted that they were not spread evenly across the sky; they were concentrated in the direction of the constellation Sagittarius. Using variable stars called Cepheids to measure distances, Shapley concluded that the globular clusters formed a sphere centred not on the Sun but on a point far away in the direction of Sagittarius. The Sun, it emerged, was not at the centre of the Milky Way at all. It was out in the suburbs, some thirty thousand light-years from the galactic centre. Copernicus had displaced the Earth from the centre of the solar system. Shapley had now displaced the Sun from the centre of the galaxy.

And the displacements were not finished.

At the time, many astronomers assumed that the Milky Way was essentially the entire universe. The hazy smudges visible through telescopes — called nebulae — were assumed to be gas clouds within our own galaxy. But a young American astronomer named Edwin Hubble suspected otherwise. Using the hundred-inch Hooker Telescope at Mount Wilson Observatory in California, the most powerful telescope in the world at that time, Hubble identified a Cepheid variable star in the Andromeda Nebula in nineteen twenty-three. By measuring its pulsation period, he could calculate its distance. The answer was staggering: nearly nine hundred thousand light-years away — far beyond the boundaries of the Milky Way. Hubble presented his results at a meeting of the American Astronomical Society at the very start of nineteen twenty-five. The Andromeda Nebula was not a gas cloud within our galaxy. It was a galaxy in its own right, comparable in size to the Milky Way. The universe was, in an instant, incomprehensibly larger than anyone had believed.

And then Hubble made it larger still. By measuring the light from dozens of distant galaxies, he and his colleague Milton Humason noticed something systematic: the more distant a galaxy was, the faster it appeared to be receding from us. Its light was stretched to longer, redder wavelengths — a Doppler effect caused by motion away from the observer. In nineteen twenty-nine, Hubble published this relationship, which became known as Hubble's law: the universe was not static. It was expanding. Every galaxy was rushing away from every other galaxy, like raisins in a rising loaf of bread.

The implications were immediate and profound. If the universe was expanding now, then winding the clock backwards, everything must once have been much closer together — compressed, ultimately, into an unimaginably dense and hot single point. The Belgian priest and physicist Georges Lemaître had already proposed something like this in nineteen twenty-seven and nineteen thirty-one, calling the initial state the primeval atom. The idea was met with scepticism — the astronomer Fred Hoyle dismissively called it the Big Bang in a radio broadcast, intending mockery, but the name stuck. The clinching observational evidence came in nineteen sixty-five, when two radio engineers at Bell Laboratories, Arno Penzias and Robert Wilson, detected a faint, uniform glow of microwave radiation coming from all directions in the sky. This was the cosmic microwave background: the afterglow of the Big Bang itself, cooled over thirteen point eight billion years to just a few degrees above absolute zero. Lemaître, who died the following year, lived just long enough to learn of this confirmation.

The story of modern astronomy is in many ways the story of building ever-larger eyes to look ever-farther into the darkness. Ground-based observatories gave way to space-based ones, free from the blurring effects of Earth's atmosphere. The Hubble Space Telescope, launched in nineteen ninety, sent back images of astonishing beauty and precision — deep field photographs in which virtually every point of light was a galaxy, each containing hundreds of billions of stars, stretching back to within a billion years of the Big Bang. Hubble observations helped confirm the accelerating expansion of the universe, driven by something astronomers call dark energy — a discovery that won the Nobel Prize in Physics in two thousand and eleven.

In December twenty twenty-one, the James Webb Space Telescope launched, deploying its gold-plated mirrors one and a half million kilometres from Earth, at a gravitational balance point called the second Lagrange point. Webb sees in infrared, allowing it to peer through dust clouds and see the earliest galaxies ever observed, formed only a few hundred million years after the Big Bang. It has shown us newborn stars, the atmospheres of distant worlds, the cosmic web of structure that connects all things.

We began with someone lying in the dark, watching the sky, trying to understand when to plant crops or when floods might come. We have ended, in the space of a few thousand years, with a civilisation that has placed its instruments at the edge of the inner solar system, capturing light that left its source when the universe was in its infancy. The questions have only grown larger. What is dark energy? What is dark matter? Are we alone? What happened before the Big Bang? Each answer opens a new horizon.

And so humanity does what it has always done: it looks up.
