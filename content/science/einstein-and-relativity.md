# Albert Einstein and the Theory of Relativity

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

There is a particular kind of genius that does not just answer a question but reveals that the question itself was wrong. Albert Einstein had that kind of genius. When he sat down, in 1905, as a twenty-six-year-old patent clerk in Bern with no academic position and no laboratory, and rethought the nature of space, time, and energy, he did not just solve problems in physics. He dissolved assumptions that had stood for two centuries and replaced them with a new picture of reality so strange and so beautiful that the world has not stopped marvelling at it since.

Einstein was born on the fourteenth of March, 1879, in Ulm, in what is now Germany. He showed an early fascination with mathematics and physics, and a lifelong suspicion of rote learning and authority that made him an awkward student. He graduated from the Swiss Federal Polytechnic in Zurich in 1900 but struggled to find an academic position. A friend's father helped him secure a job at the Swiss Patent Office in Bern, where he spent his days evaluating patent applications and his spare hours thinking about physics.

---

In 1905 — a year that physicists now call his miracle year — Einstein published four papers, each of which would individually have made a lesser scientist famous for life. One explained Brownian motion, the random jiggling of small particles in a fluid, providing powerful evidence for the existence of atoms. One proposed that light travels in discrete packets of energy called photons, explaining the photoelectric effect — the way light striking certain metals produces an electric current. This was the paper for which he would be awarded the Nobel Prize in Physics in 1921. A third paper established that matter and energy are equivalent, expressed in the most famous equation in history: E equals mc squared, meaning that a tiny amount of mass can be converted into an enormous amount of energy.

And the fourth paper introduced the Special Theory of Relativity, which overturned the Newtonian picture of the universe that had stood for more than two hundred years.

The core of special relativity is deceptively simple: the laws of physics are the same for all observers moving at constant speeds, and the speed of light is constant for all observers regardless of their motion. These two premises seem reasonable. Their consequences are not. If the speed of light is constant for all observers, then space and time cannot be. Two people moving at different speeds will measure the same event as happening at different times and in different places. Time runs slower for someone moving faster. A metre rod moving past you is shorter than one at rest. These are not illusions or approximations — they are the actual behaviour of the universe.

---

Einstein spent the next decade extending his theory to include gravity. The result, published in 1915, was the General Theory of Relativity. Here the central insight is equally profound: gravity is not a force acting across empty space, as Newton had supposed. It is a curvature of space-time itself caused by mass. The Earth does not orbit the Sun because the Sun pulls it. It follows a curved path through curved space-time, and that path is what we call a gravitational orbit.

The confirmation came dramatically in May 1919. The British astronomer Arthur Eddington led two expeditions — one to Sobral in Brazil, one to the island of Príncipe off the coast of West Africa — to photograph a solar eclipse. Einstein's theory predicted that light passing close to the Sun would be bent by its gravitational field. When the eclipse blocked the Sun's glare, Eddington could photograph stars whose light grazed the Sun's edge and compare their apparent positions to where they should have been. When the results were announced in November 1919, they confirmed Einstein's prediction precisely. He became an overnight international celebrity — the first scientist since Newton to capture the world's imagination.

The timing helped. The world, shattered by four years of devastating war, was hungry for something transcendent. Here was a Jewish German scientist proving that reality was stranger and richer than anyone had imagined — and doing so with pure thought, a pencil, and paper.

---

Einstein's fame proved both a gift and a burden. He became a symbol — of genius, of the liberating power of pure thought, and, as a Jew in an increasingly antisemitic Europe, of cosmopolitan culture under threat. When Hitler came to power in Germany in 1933, Einstein was visiting the United States. He did not go back. He accepted a position at the Institute for Advanced Study in Princeton, New Jersey, where he would spend the rest of his life.

In 1939, deeply concerned that Nazi Germany might be developing an atomic bomb, Einstein signed a letter to President Roosevelt warning of the danger and urging American research into nuclear weapons. The Manhattan Project that resulted was the most direct application of his E equals mc squared equation — though Einstein himself took no part in the project and was devastated by the destruction at Hiroshima and Nagasaki.

Einstein died on the eighteenth of April, 1955, in Princeton. He had spent his final decades searching, unsuccessfully, for a unified field theory that would bring gravity and electromagnetism into a single framework — a quest that continues in physics to this day.

---

What he left behind was a revolution that is still unfolding. General relativity underlies the GPS system that guides navigation on every smartphone — without accounting for relativistic effects, GPS would drift by kilometres each day. It predicted the existence of black holes and gravitational waves, both confirmed decades after his death. His description of the photoelectric effect underpins the semiconductor technology in every electronic device. And his equation E equals mc squared made possible both nuclear energy and nuclear weapons — the most transformative and most terrifying applications of pure scientific thought in history.

Einstein once said he had no special talent, only passionate curiosity. The record suggests otherwise. He looked at the universe, saw that the rules as understood were wrong, and patiently worked out what they actually were. That is not curiosity. That is one of the most extraordinary intellectual achievements in human history.
