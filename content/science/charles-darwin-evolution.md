# Charles Darwin and the Theory of Evolution

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

In 1859, a quiet, anxious English naturalist published a book that would change humanity's understanding of itself more profoundly than almost anything ever written. He had been sitting on his discovery for more than twenty years, terrified of the controversy it would provoke. When he finally published, he was right to have been afraid. But he was also right about the science, and that is why his name — Charles Darwin — is still spoken with reverence more than a century and a half later.

Darwin was born on the twelfth of February, 1809, in Shrewsbury, England, into a prosperous family with strong scientific traditions. His grandfather, Erasmus Darwin, had already speculated that all life might share common ancestors. But it was Charles who would find the mechanism that explained how one species could give rise to another.

As a young man, Darwin showed more enthusiasm for collecting beetles and observing nature than for studying medicine, which his father had hoped he would pursue. He transferred to Cambridge, ostensibly to study theology and become a country clergyman, but spent most of his time pursuing natural history. It was here that he caught the attention of the botanist John Stevens Henslow, who recommended him for a position that would change his life.

---

In December 1831, at the age of twenty-two, Darwin boarded HMS Beagle as the ship's naturalist for a voyage of scientific exploration. The journey lasted nearly five years, taking him around the coast of South America, to the Galápagos Islands in the Pacific, to Australia, and around the Cape of Good Hope. Darwin observed an astonishing variety of life, collected thousands of specimens, and filled notebooks with meticulous observations.

The Galápagos Islands proved particularly important. Darwin noticed that finches on different islands had beaks of markedly different shapes — each perfectly adapted to the food sources available on that particular island. He saw similar patterns across other animals. A single original population, he began to suspect, had gradually adapted to different environments over many generations until they were different enough to be considered separate species.

---

Back in England, Darwin spent years refining his thinking, corresponding with breeders of domestic animals, studying geology, and struggling to understand the mechanism by which such change could occur. The answer came from an unlikely source: he read Thomas Malthus's essay on population, which argued that populations always tend to grow faster than their food supply, leading to competition and struggle. Darwin realised that the same logic applied to nature. In any population, individuals vary slightly from one another. Some variations make an individual better suited to survive and reproduce in its environment. Those individuals pass their advantageous traits on to their offspring. Over many generations, the population changes. Darwin called this process natural selection.

He wrote out a long essay on the theory in 1844 and shared it with only a tiny circle of trusted friends, including the geologist Charles Lyell and the botanist Joseph Hooker. He continued to gather evidence, planning an enormous multi-volume work. And then, in June 1858, Darwin received a letter from Alfred Russel Wallace, a naturalist working in the Malay Archipelago, who had independently arrived at almost exactly the same theory of natural selection. Darwin was devastated. He had spent decades building his case, and someone else had beaten him to it.

---

In a gentlemanly arrangement, Lyell and Hooker organised for papers by both Darwin and Wallace to be presented together at a scientific meeting in London in July 1858. Then Darwin abandoned his plan for a massive multi-volume work and rapidly compressed his ideas into a single readable book. On the twenty-fourth of November, 1859, On the Origin of Species by Means of Natural Selection was published. Its entire first printing of twelve hundred and fifty copies sold out on the first day.

The reaction was everything Darwin had feared — and more. Religious critics were outraged by the implication that humanity was not separately created by God but had descended from earlier forms of life. The most famous confrontation came at a meeting of the British Association for the Advancement of Science in Oxford in June 1860, where Bishop Samuel Wilberforce faced Darwin's great defender, the biologist Thomas Henry Huxley. According to widely reported accounts, Wilberforce sarcastically asked whether Huxley was descended from an ape on his grandfather's or grandmother's side. Huxley reportedly replied that he would rather be descended from an ape than from a man who used his intelligence to obscure the truth.

But the controversy could not stop the science. Darwin continued working, publishing further books — The Descent of Man in 1871, which explicitly extended his theory to human origins — and gradually, the scientific community accepted his ideas. The evidence from fossils, from the comparative anatomy of different species, from the study of embryology, all pointed in the same direction.

Darwin died on the nineteenth of April, 1882, at his home in Downe, Kent. He was buried in Westminster Abbey, near Isaac Newton — a recognition that his contribution to human understanding was of the same order as the greatest scientific minds in history.

---

The theory of evolution by natural selection has since been confirmed and extended in ways Darwin could not have imagined. The discovery of DNA in the twentieth century provided the molecular mechanism he had lacked — the means by which traits are inherited and variation arises. Every advance in genetics since has deepened the evidence for Darwin's central insight: that all life on Earth is connected by descent from common ancestors, and that the extraordinary diversity of living things — from bacteria to blue whales — is the product of billions of years of variation and selection.

Darwin's idea is the foundation of modern biology. It explains antibiotic resistance, the development of vaccines, the diversity of crops, and the evolutionary history of every living thing on this planet. It is one of the most powerful and well-supported ideas in the history of science, and it all began with a young man noticing the beaks of finches on a set of remote Pacific islands.
