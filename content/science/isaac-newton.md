# Isaac Newton: The Man Who Explained the Universe

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

There is a story — probably true in its essentials, though much embellished — that Isaac Newton was sitting under an apple tree in his mother's garden in the summer of 1666 when he saw an apple fall. He was not struck on the head, as the popular version insists. But watching the apple fall did set him thinking, and what he thought led eventually to one of the greatest intellectual achievements in human history: the realisation that the same force pulling the apple to the ground was pulling the Moon toward the Earth, and that this force governed the motion of every planet in the solar system. He had found the mechanism that held the universe together.

Newton was born on the fourth of January, 1643, in Woolsthorpe, a small manor in Lincolnshire, England. He arrived prematurely — so small, it was said, that he could fit in a quart pot — and his father had died before his birth. When his mother remarried and moved away, Isaac was left to be raised by his grandmother, an arrangement he resented deeply. He was not a cheerful child. He was solitary, obsessive, and possessed of a ferocious, almost inhuman capacity for concentration. He would later say that if he had achieved anything it was "by thinking about it always."

He went to Cambridge University in 1661, enrolling at Trinity College, where he read voraciously and developed the habit of writing dense, cryptic notes to himself in which he worked out problems of mathematics and natural philosophy. Cambridge at the time taught primarily the classical curriculum of Aristotle. Newton worked through it quickly and then moved beyond it, reading the new philosophy of Descartes and the new science of Galileo. He was already teaching himself.

---

In June 1665, the plague arrived at Cambridge. The university closed and Newton returned to Woolsthorpe, where he would remain for roughly two years. The enforced idleness — if that is quite the right word for what he did with the time — proved to be the most productive period in the history of science. Working alone, in a farmhouse in Lincolnshire, at the age of twenty-three, Newton invented the mathematics of calculus, formulated the theory of universal gravitation, and discovered that white light was composed of all the colours of the spectrum. Any one of these achievements would have secured his reputation for centuries. He accomplished all three simultaneously, in notebooks he kept almost entirely to himself for the next two decades.

He later described this period himself: "In those days I was in the prime of my age for invention, and minded mathematics and philosophy more than at any time since." What he was too modest to say was that no human being before or since has used a period of enforced isolation quite so productively.

The mathematics he invented were what he called "the method of fluxions" — what we now call calculus, the mathematical language for describing change and motion. In the same months, he was working out that the force of gravity decreased with the square of the distance from its source, and that this law of inverse squares could account for the orbit of the Moon precisely as Kepler had described it. The motions of the planets, which had seemed to require either divine intervention or mysterious invisible mechanisms, turned out to follow from a single simple law: every body in the universe attracts every other body with a force proportional to the product of their masses and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them.

---

Newton returned to Cambridge when the plague subsided and resumed his academic career with a peculiar combination of intensity and secrecy. He would not publish his mathematical discoveries for years. He was pathologically reluctant to share his work, knowing from the experience of others that publication invited criticism, and criticism was something he could not bear. He was convinced of his own correctness with a certainty that made challenge feel like personal assault.

In 1687, after being effectively badgered into publication by the astronomer Edmond Halley — who paid for the printing out of his own pocket — Newton published the Philosophiae Naturalis Principia Mathematica: the Mathematical Principles of Natural Philosophy. It is perhaps the most influential scientific book ever written. In it, Newton laid out his three laws of motion and the law of universal gravitation, and used them to explain, with mathematical precision, an extraordinary range of phenomena: the motions of the planets, the behaviour of comets, the action of the tides, the shape of the Earth, and the behaviour of objects moving through fluids.

His three laws of motion were as follows: first, that an object remains at rest or continues moving in a straight line unless acted upon by an external force; second, that the acceleration of an object is proportional to the force applied to it and inversely proportional to its mass; third, that for every action there is an equal and opposite reaction. These statements, so simple they can be memorised in minutes, provided the foundation for all of classical physics and engineering. They explained why cannon balls follow the arcs they do, why the planets orbit the Sun in ellipses, why a rocket rises by pushing gas downward. They remained the unchallenged framework of physics for over two hundred years, until Einstein showed that they broke down at very high velocities and very strong gravitational fields.

---

Newton also transformed the understanding of light. Through experiments with prisms — inserting them into gaps in the curtains of a darkened room and watching the colours spread across the wall — he showed that white light was not a pure substance but a mixture, and that each colour was bent differently by the prism. This overturned centuries of assumption. He built the first reflecting telescope, using a curved mirror rather than a lens, and presented it to the Royal Society of London, which elected him a Fellow in 1671.

His other great creation was the calculus — and here his life became bitter. A German mathematician, Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz, had independently developed essentially the same mathematical tools during the 1670s and published them in 1684, before Newton published his own version. An enormous controversy erupted over who had invented the calculus first, a controversy in which Newton behaved badly: he anonymously chaired the Royal Society committee that investigated the dispute and then published the committee's report finding in his own favour. The feud poisoned the relationship between British and Continental mathematics for a generation.

In later life, Newton became Warden and then Master of the Royal Mint, where he oversaw a major recoinage of the British currency with characteristic thoroughness, and personally pursued — with something approaching relish — the counterfeiters who had been undermining it. He was elected President of the Royal Society in 1703 and was knighted by Queen Anne in 1705. But he also spent an extraordinary amount of time on what we would now consider non-scientific pursuits: theology, biblical chronology, and alchemy. He left more words on alchemy than on physics. This side of Newton was suppressed by later admirers who wanted a rational hero; the economist John Maynard Keynes, who bought many of Newton's unpublished papers, described him as "not the first of the age of reason" but "the last of the magicians."

---

Newton died on the thirty-first of March, 1727, at the age of eighty-four. He never married. He had few close friends and many bitter enemies. He was, by most accounts, not a pleasant man. But it is possible to be the most important scientist in history without being a pleasant man, and Newton was precisely that.

When Einstein was asked whether he had stood on the shoulders of Newton, he replied with a certain diplomacy: without Newton's work, none of Einstein's would have been possible. The universe Newton described — governed by precise mathematical laws, the same everywhere, operating without exceptions — is the universe of the scientific revolution, the universe of modernity. He did not explain everything. But he showed that explanation was possible, that the cosmos was orderly and knowable, and that the human mind could — through mathematics and careful observation — read the laws written into the fabric of reality. Few ideas have been more consequential.
