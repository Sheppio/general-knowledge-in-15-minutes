# Stephen Hawking and Black Holes

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Imagine standing at the edge of the sea on a moonless night. You cannot see the water, but you can feel its pull, hear it moving, sense the vast darkness stretching out beyond the reach of any light. Now imagine something far more extreme: a region of the universe so dense, so gravitationally ferocious, that not even light itself can escape. Not a dimming, not a fading — a total and absolute disappearance. Whatever crosses a certain invisible boundary is gone from the observable universe forever.

This is the idea at the heart of one of the most extraordinary concepts in all of physics. And the story of how humanity came to understand it is inseparable from the life of one remarkable man — a man who, against every conceivable physical and medical odd, spent more than five decades reshaping our picture of the cosmos from a motorised wheelchair.

But before we get to him, we need to go back. A long way back.

---

The year is seventeen eighty-three. The place is a quiet English rectory. A Church of England clergyman named John Michell, largely forgotten today but one of the most original scientific minds of his age, is working through a thought experiment. Newton's theory of gravity has been established for nearly a century. Light, it is believed, has a finite speed. Michell asks a deceptively simple question: what would happen if a star were so massive that gravity at its surface exceeded the speed of light? He reasons that if a sphere of the same density as our Sun were made five hundred times larger in diameter, any light emitted from its surface would be dragged back by gravity before it could escape. The star would be invisible. He calls it a "dark star." He sends his findings to the Royal Society, where they are read aloud on the twenty-seventh of November, seventeen eighty-three, and then, for over a century, the idea is essentially forgotten.

It takes Albert Einstein to bring it back. In nineteen fifteen, Einstein publishes his general theory of relativity, fundamentally rethinking gravity not as a force pulling objects together but as the curvature of spacetime caused by mass. Within a year, the German physicist Karl Schwarzschild, working his calculations from the trenches of the First World War, finds an exact solution to Einstein's equations. His solution describes the geometry of spacetime around a perfectly spherical, non-rotating mass — and it contains something strange: a critical radius, later called the Schwarzschild radius, at which the mathematics appears to break down entirely. Below that radius, nothing — not matter, not radiation, not information of any kind — can escape.

Einstein himself was troubled by this. He wrote a paper in nineteen thirty-nine arguing that such objects could never actually form in nature. He was wrong. But the concept remained theoretical, lurking at the edges of physics like an unsolved equation on a forgotten chalkboard.

Then, in nineteen sixty-seven, an American physicist named John Wheeler gave the object a name. Speaking at a conference in New York, Wheeler used two words that would reshape the public imagination of the cosmos. He called these collapsed regions of spacetime "black holes." The name stuck immediately, spreading through both scientific literature and popular culture with remarkable speed. A black hole. The very phrase conjures the right sensation — that terrifying combination of nothingness and absolute power.

---

On the eighth of January, nineteen forty-two — three hundred years to the day after the death of Galileo Galilei, a coincidence Hawking himself enjoyed — a boy named Stephen William Hawking was born in Oxford, England. His father was a medical researcher, his mother a committed political activist, and the family home was intellectually lively if financially modest. They moved to St Albans, north of London, where young Stephen attended the local school and showed an unusual talent for mathematics and abstract reasoning, though his handwriting was reportedly dreadful and his teachers initially found him unremarkable.

In October nineteen fifty-nine, aged seventeen, Hawking arrived at University College, Oxford, to read physics. He found the work almost laughably easy and, by his own admission, devoted perhaps a thousand hours in total to his studies over three years — roughly an hour a day. He was sociable, witty, and joined the college boat club as a cox, steering the rowing crews along the river with what his coach described as a cheerfully reckless disregard for the safety of the boats. He graduated in nineteen sixty-two with a first-class honours degree and moved to Trinity Hall, Cambridge, to begin a doctorate in cosmology.

It was there, in the autumn of nineteen sixty-two, that the first signs appeared.

Hawking was twenty-one when he noticed his movements becoming clumsy. He was stumbling. His speech was becoming slurred. Early in nineteen sixty-three, his father took him to a specialist, and the diagnosis arrived with the force of a physical blow: motor neurone disease, the progressive degeneration of the nerve cells controlling voluntary movement. In the United States, it is called ALS — amyotrophic lateral sclerosis, or Lou Gehrig's disease. Doctors estimated he had perhaps two years to live.

What Hawking later described as his reaction to this diagnosis is one of the most striking things about him. There was, he said, a period of genuine despair — vivid dreams, a sense of futility. But something shifted. He had work to do. He had mathematics. And he had met, at a party in Cambridge, a young woman named Jane Wilde, a modern languages student, full of intelligence and determination. They fell in love. The engagement, Hawking would write, gave him "something to live for." They married on the fourteenth of July, nineteen sixty-five, and would go on to have three children: Robert, born in nineteen sixty-seven; Lucy, born in nineteen seventy; and Timothy, born in nineteen seventy-nine.

The two years became five, and then ten, and then a lifetime.

---

The disease progressed, but so did the physics. Hawking completed his PhD at Cambridge in March nineteen sixty-six — his dissertation, on the properties of an expanding universe, was a foretaste of what was coming. By this time he had encountered Roger Penrose, a brilliant Oxford mathematician who in nineteen sixty-five had proved a landmark result about black holes: that once a star collapses past a certain point under its own gravity, the formation of a singularity — a point of infinite density where the known laws of physics simply cease to function — is mathematically inevitable.

Hawking immediately grasped the power of Penrose's methods and turned them around. If singularities were unavoidable at the endpoint of gravitational collapse, what about the beginning of the universe itself? Working through the late nineteen sixties, Hawking applied and extended Penrose's techniques to cosmology. Together, Hawking and Penrose published what became known as the Penrose-Hawking singularity theorems, culminating in a definitive joint paper in nineteen seventy. Their work demonstrated that, under the conditions described by general relativity, the Big Bang itself must have begun in a singularity — a moment of infinite density and zero volume from which all of spacetime emerged. The universe had a beginning, and the mathematics demanded it.

This was a profound result. It told physicists that the origin of everything lay at a place where their best theory of gravity stopped working. General relativity, for all its power, was incomplete. Something else was needed — a way of thinking about gravity at the scale of the very small, a quantum theory of gravity. That quest would occupy Hawking for the rest of his life.

His next major contribution arrived in nineteen seventy-four, and it was, by any measure, one of the most unexpected results in the history of physics.

Black holes, everyone had assumed, were dark. By definition. The whole point was that nothing — nothing — could escape them. But Hawking, applying the mathematics of quantum field theory to the curved spacetime near a black hole's event horizon, discovered something astonishing: black holes are not entirely dark after all. They glow.

The mechanism involves quantum physics, which describes the behaviour of matter at the smallest scales. According to quantum theory, empty space is never truly empty. It seethes with pairs of virtual particles and their antiparticles, constantly popping into existence and almost immediately annihilating each other. Normally this process is invisible and leaves no trace. But at the edge of a black hole, right at the event horizon, Hawking showed that something different can happen. One particle of a virtual pair can fall inward across the horizon, while the other escapes outward into space. From the perspective of a distant observer, the black hole appears to be emitting radiation. This is what became known as Hawking radiation.

The consequences were staggering. Because the escaping particles carry energy away from the black hole — energy drawn, in effect, from the black hole's own mass — the black hole slowly loses mass over time. Given long enough, it evaporates. Completely. A black hole is not eternal. It has a temperature, it radiates, and it dies.

Hawking published his result in the journal Nature in nineteen seventy-four, in a paper titled "Black Hole Explosions?" The question mark in the title was deliberate: Hawking knew the result was so counter-intuitive that it would be met with scepticism. It was. Some physicists initially refused to believe it. But the mathematics held, and within a relatively short time, Hawking radiation had become one of the foundational results of theoretical physics — the point at which general relativity and quantum mechanics first truly touched.

The discovery also deepened one of the most tantalising puzzles in modern science: the black hole information paradox. If a black hole can swallow anything — a book, a star, a human being — and then slowly evaporate into a bath of thermal radiation that contains no trace of what fell in, then information about the initial state of that matter appears to be permanently destroyed. But quantum mechanics insists that information cannot be lost. The two principles, both well-tested and fundamental, are in direct contradiction. This paradox remains, more than fifty years later, one of the great unsolved problems in physics. Hawking himself changed his view on it over the decades, ultimately conceding in two thousand and four that information is probably preserved — though exactly how remains unresolved.

---

By the late nineteen seventies, Hawking's body was failing in increasingly severe ways. He could no longer walk; he had been using a wheelchair since the early nineteen seventies. His speech was deteriorating, until only those who knew him well could understand what he was saying. In nineteen seventy-nine, the University of Cambridge appointed him Lucasian Professor of Mathematics — the same chair once held by Isaac Newton — a recognition of his extraordinary contributions to the field.

Then, in nineteen eighty-five, came a medical crisis that changed his life irreversibly. Visiting CERN in Switzerland, Hawking contracted pneumonia. He was placed on a ventilator and his family was advised that he might not survive. He did survive, but the treatment required a tracheotomy — an incision in his throat — that permanently destroyed what remained of his voice. From that point forward, Stephen Hawking could not speak a single word.

A California programmer named Walt Woltosz sent Hawking a software system called the Equalizer, which allowed him to select words on a screen and pass them to a speech synthesiser. The voice that emerged — American in accent, slightly mechanical in cadence — became, over the following decades, one of the most recognisable voices in the world. Later, as his control deteriorated, Hawking operated the system through a sensor on his glasses activated by movements of his right cheek muscle.

It was with this voice, produced letter by painstaking letter, that he wrote one of the best-selling science books ever published.

A Brief History of Time appeared in April nineteen eighty-eight, published by Bantam Books. Hawking had originally proposed the subtitle "From the Big Bang to Black Holes" — the editor Peter Guzzardi suggested the change to A Brief History of Time, which Hawking later said was a stroke of genius. The book set out to explain to a general audience the fundamental questions of cosmology: the Big Bang, the nature of time, black holes, and the search for a unified theory of everything. It assumed, famously, almost no prior scientific knowledge in its readers.

The response was extraordinary. The book spent two hundred and thirty-seven weeks on the London Sunday Times bestseller list — a record at the time — and ultimately sold more than twenty-five million copies worldwide. Hawking became something that physicists rarely become: a genuine global celebrity. His face was recognised everywhere. His voice was parodied on television. He appeared in cameo roles in Star Trek and The Simpsons. He became not just a scientist but a symbol — of human intellectual ambition, of survival against impossible odds, and of the austere beauty of the quest to understand the universe.

His personal life during these years was complicated. His marriage to Jane, tested enormously by the demands of his care and the weight of his global fame, ended in divorce in nineteen ninety-five. His daughter Lucy would later collaborate with him on a series of children's science fiction novels.

He continued to work, to travel, to lecture, to provoke. He held strong views on the dangers of artificial intelligence and the long-term necessity of human space colonisation. He warned publicly about the risks of contact with extraterrestrial civilisations. He flew in zero gravity aboard a modified aircraft in two thousand and seven, floating weightless for a few extraordinary seconds with an expression of pure, uncomplicated joy on his face. He remained Lucasian Professor until two thousand and nine, when he stepped down and became Director of Research at the Centre for Theoretical Cosmology in Cambridge.

---

On the fourteenth of March, two thousand and eighteen — a date that also happened to be the birthday of Albert Einstein — Stephen William Hawking died peacefully at his home in Cambridge. He was seventy-six years old. The man who had been given two years to live at twenty-one had survived fifty-five more. He left behind a body of scientific work that had genuinely changed our understanding of the cosmos, and a public legacy that had made physics thrilling and accessible to millions of people who had never previously given a thought to the nature of spacetime.

Less than a year after his death, on the tenth of April, two thousand and nineteen, the Event Horizon Telescope collaboration released something that would have delighted him profoundly: the first direct image of a black hole. The telescope array, which linked radio observatories across four continents to create an effective telescope the size of the Earth, captured an image of the supermassive black hole at the centre of the galaxy Messier eighty-seven, some fifty-five million light-years away. The image showed a ring of glowing orange light — superheated gas and dust swirling at the boundary of the event horizon — with a dark shadow at its centre. The shadow of a black hole. The very thing that John Michell had imagined as a "dark star" in seventeen eighty-three, that Schwarzschild had described mathematically in nineteen sixteen, that Wheeler had named in nineteen sixty-seven, that Hawking had spent a lifetime probing and theorising about — finally, unmistakably, visible.

What makes Stephen Hawking's story so enduringly powerful is not merely the science, though the science is remarkable. It is the sheer improbability of the life. A young man sitting in a hospital at twenty-one, told that his body was systematically shutting down, found a way to redirect the entire force of his mind toward the grandest questions human beings have ever asked. He did not cure his disease. He could not stop the paralysis, the silence, the slow physical diminishment. But he used what he had — an extraordinary mathematical mind, an indefatigable curiosity, and a dry, playful wit that never left him — to explore the most extreme environments in the universe from the confines of a wheelchair in Cambridge.

Black holes, in Hawking's hands, were never just objects of terror or mystery. They were windows. Windows into the nature of time, into the birth of the universe, into the relationship between gravity and the quantum world. Every paper he wrote, every lecture he gave, every book he produced was an act of insistence: that the universe is knowable, that the human mind is capable of reaching across billions of light-years and comprehending what it finds there, and that the pursuit of that comprehension is worth every effort it demands.

The darkness at the centre of M87, surrounded by its ring of fire, is in some sense Stephen Hawking's photograph — taken a year after he died, but bearing his intellectual fingerprints nonetheless.
