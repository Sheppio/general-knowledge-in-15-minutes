# Climate Change: The Science of a Warming World

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

The warming of the Earth's climate is the defining challenge of the twenty-first century. It is also, among established scientific facts, one of the most thoroughly evidenced and the most politically contested — a strange combination that tells you something important about the gap between what science knows and what societies are willing to act on. The core physical mechanism behind climate change was understood in the nineteenth century. The role of human fossil fuel burning in accelerating warming was established in the twentieth. The consequences now being observed — rising seas, melting ice sheets, more intense heat waves, more destructive storms — were predicted decades before they arrived. The science is not new. The crisis is.

Understanding climate change requires understanding a deceptively simple piece of physics: the greenhouse effect. The Earth receives energy from the sun in the form of visible light and ultraviolet radiation. Some of this energy is reflected back into space by clouds and the Earth's surface, but most of it is absorbed and re-emitted as infrared radiation — heat. Certain gases in the atmosphere, including water vapour, carbon dioxide, methane, and nitrous oxide, absorb this outgoing infrared radiation and re-emit it in all directions, including back toward the surface. This keeps the Earth considerably warmer than it would otherwise be. Without any greenhouse effect, the average surface temperature of the Earth would be roughly minus eighteen degrees Celsius — far too cold to support most life as we know it. The greenhouse effect is not a problem; it is the condition of our existence. The problem arises when the concentration of greenhouse gases increases beyond the natural range, trapping more heat than usual and pushing global temperatures upward.

---

The scientific understanding of this mechanism developed over the course of the nineteenth century. In 1824, the French mathematician Joseph Fourier was the first to propose that the atmosphere acts like a blanket, trapping heat near the surface. In 1856, the American scientist Eunice Newton Foote — one of the most important and least celebrated figures in the history of climate science — demonstrated experimentally that carbon dioxide absorbs more heat from sunlight than ordinary air, and that a CO2-rich atmosphere would therefore produce a warmer planet. Her work was largely overlooked at the time. Three years later, the Irish physicist John Tyndall conducted more detailed experiments measuring the infrared absorption of various gases, confirming that water vapour and carbon dioxide were the primary agents of the greenhouse effect.

The crucial quantitative step came in 1896, when the Swedish chemist Svante Arrhenius — working by hand, before computers existed — calculated what would happen to global surface temperature if the concentration of atmospheric carbon dioxide were doubled. He estimated an increase of around five to six degrees Celsius. This was, in retrospect, a remarkable achievement of calculation and physical intuition. Arrhenius understood that human burning of coal was adding carbon dioxide to the atmosphere, and he even speculated that this might eventually cause significant warming — though he did not consider this a danger, expecting it would take thousands of years to accumulate. He was wrong about the timescale.

---

Through the early twentieth century, most scientists believed that the oceans would absorb any extra carbon dioxide produced by industry, neutralising the potential for warming. This assumption began to collapse in the late 1950s, when an American geochemist named Charles David Keeling started making continuous, precise measurements of atmospheric carbon dioxide concentration at the Mauna Loa Observatory in Hawaii — chosen for its remoteness from industrial contamination. When Keeling began his measurements in March 1958, atmospheric CO2 stood at three hundred and thirteen parts per million. What he then observed, year after year, was a relentless upward trend — with a characteristic seasonal fluctuation as Northern Hemisphere vegetation breathed in CO2 in summer and exhaled it in winter. The resulting graph became known as the Keeling Curve, and it remains one of the most important scientific records in history. By 2024, the annual average CO2 concentration had surpassed four hundred and twenty parts per million — the highest level in at least three million years, when sea levels were many metres higher than today and global temperatures were substantially warmer.

The Keeling Curve provided definitive evidence that human activity was fundamentally altering the composition of the atmosphere. Ice cores drilled from Antarctica and Greenland confirmed the picture from the other direction: by analysing ancient air bubbles trapped in the ice, scientists could reconstruct atmospheric CO2 levels going back hundreds of thousands of years. For the previous eight hundred thousand years, CO2 had oscillated naturally between about a hundred and eighty and two hundred and eighty parts per million, cycling through ice ages and interglacial warm periods. The current level of over four hundred and twenty parts per million is far outside this natural range, and the speed of increase is unprecedented in the geological record.

---

The body of scientific evidence connecting rising CO2 to rising temperatures is vast, independent, and mutually reinforcing. Temperature records stretching back to the mid-nineteenth century show that the global average surface temperature has risen by approximately one point one to one point two degrees Celsius since pre-industrial times, with the majority of that warming occurring since the nineteen-eighties. The ten warmest years on record have all occurred since 2010. Arctic ice extent has declined dramatically. The Greenland and West Antarctic ice sheets are losing mass. Mountain glaciers around the world are retreating. Sea levels have risen by roughly twenty centimetres over the past century and are rising at an accelerating rate.

These changes are not merely measured from instruments. They are visible in the fossil and archaeological record. They are observed in the timing of plant flowering and bird migration. They are embedded in the changing ranges of insects and fish and mammals, as species track the shifting boundaries of their habitable climate zones. The signal of human-caused climate change is now present in every component of the Earth system that scientists can measure.

The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change — the IPCC — was established in 1988 to synthesise and assess the scientific literature on climate change. Its first report, published in 1990, found clear evidence of past warming and projected future warming with increasing confidence. Each subsequent assessment report — there have been six — has drawn on an ever-larger body of evidence, refined the projections, and increased scientific certainty. The IPCC's sixth assessment report, published in 2021, stated with "unequivocal" certainty that human influence has warmed the atmosphere, ocean, and land.

---

The political response to the science has been far slower than the science itself. The United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change was signed at the Earth Summit in Rio de Janeiro in 1992, establishing the principle that countries should stabilise greenhouse gas concentrations at safe levels. The Kyoto Protocol of 1997 set binding emissions reduction targets for developed countries, but the United States — the world's largest emitter at the time — never ratified it. The Paris Agreement of 2015, signed by nearly two hundred countries, set a goal of limiting warming to well below two degrees Celsius above pre-industrial levels, and of pursuing efforts to limit it to one point five degrees. Whether current emissions trajectories are compatible with those goals is a question the IPCC has answered, clearly and repeatedly: they are not. On current policies, the world is on track for warming of around two point five to three degrees by the end of this century, with severe consequences for billions of people.

The consequences of warming vary dramatically by region and by degree of change. At one point five degrees, coral reef systems are projected to decline by seventy to ninety percent; at two degrees, nearly all reef systems would be lost. Every additional increment of warming increases the intensity and frequency of extreme heat events, the probability of severe droughts, the severity of tropical cyclones, and the rate of sea level rise. The regions most vulnerable to these impacts — low-lying coastal nations, sub-Saharan Africa, South Asia — have contributed the least to the emissions causing them.

---

The physical basis of climate change is not in serious scientific dispute. The uncertainties that remain in climate science are not about whether warming is happening, or whether humans are causing it, but about the precise magnitude and timing of future changes, the behaviour of feedback mechanisms — particularly the potential release of carbon from permafrost as the Arctic warms — and the detailed regional patterns of impact. These are real uncertainties; climate is a complex system, and science cannot predict the future with exactitude. But they are uncertainties within a range, not challenges to the underlying physics.

What is needed to limit warming is also well understood: the rapid phase-out of fossil fuels, the transition to renewable energy, changes in land use and agriculture, and the deployment of technologies that can remove carbon dioxide from the atmosphere. The technology for most of this exists. The cost of renewable energy has fallen so dramatically over the past decade that solar and wind power are now the cheapest sources of new electricity generation in most of the world. What is lacking is not knowledge or technology but political will and the speed of transformation.

The history of climate science is the history of a slow accumulation of evidence pointing to the same conclusion, from Fourier's first theoretical proposal to Arrhenius's calculations to Keeling's unbroken curve of rising numbers. The conclusion has been available for decades. What we do with it is a choice.
