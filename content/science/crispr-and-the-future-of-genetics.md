# CRISPR and the Future of Genetics

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Inside almost every cell of your body, coiled up in a space far smaller than the full stop at the end of this sentence, there is a molecule roughly two metres long. It is deoxyribonucleic acid — DNA — and it contains the complete set of instructions for building and running a human being. That molecule is divided into sections called genes, somewhere around twenty thousand of them, and each gene is a sequence of chemical letters — the bases adenine, thymine, cytosine, and guanine — that encodes instructions for making the proteins your body needs to function. When those instructions are followed faithfully, life works. When they carry errors — misspellings, deletions, corruptions inherited from a parent or accumulated over a lifetime — the consequences can range from the merely inconvenient to the catastrophic.

Thousands of diseases are caused, at their deepest level, by mistakes in DNA. Sickle cell disease results from a single letter change in a single gene. Cystic fibrosis, Huntington's disease, muscular dystrophy — all of them trace back to corrupted instructions in the genome. For most of human history, medicine could treat the symptoms of these conditions but could not touch the root cause. You could manage what was wrong with a patient. You could rarely fix it.

That is the problem CRISPR was born to solve. It is a technology that allows scientists — and, in principle, doctors — to open the book of a living genome and edit the text directly. To find the misspelling and correct it. To delete a harmful passage or insert a new one. The implications are so profound, and the speed of development so startling, that the scientific world has been struggling to catch up with itself ever since the technology emerged. This is the story of how we got here.

---

The idea of manipulating genes is not new. Scientists have been moving genetic material between organisms since the early nineteen seventies, when the first recombinant DNA techniques allowed researchers to splice genes from one species into another. The pharmaceutical industry was transformed by this work: human insulin, once harvested from the pancreases of pigs and cattle, has been produced by genetically engineered bacteria since the early nineteen eighties. But these early tools were blunt. They could insert new genetic material into a genome, but not with any precision. The inserted gene landed wherever it happened to land, like dropping a note into a library with no idea which shelf it would fall between.

The dream of something more precise — of editing a specific location within a genome — drove the development of increasingly sophisticated tools over the following decades. By the late nineteen nineties and early two thousands, scientists were working with molecules called zinc finger nucleases, which could be engineered to recognise and cut DNA at particular sequences. After them came TALENs — transcription activator-like effector nucleases — which offered somewhat better accuracy. Both technologies worked. Both could edit genomes with genuine precision. Both were also extraordinarily expensive and slow to design. Engineering a zinc finger nuclease to target a new sequence could take months and cost hundreds of thousands of dollars. The tools existed, but they were not yet tools that medicine could use at scale. What was needed was something faster, cheaper, and simpler. The answer, when it finally arrived, came from a completely unexpected direction: the immune system of bacteria.

---

In nineteen eighty-seven, a Japanese microbiologist named Yoshizumi Ishino and his colleagues were sequencing a gene in the gut bacterium Escherichia coli — work that had nothing to do with gene editing. The gene they were studying was called iap, and as they mapped the region of DNA surrounding it, they noticed something peculiar: a series of repeated sequences of twenty-nine base pairs, arranged in a cluster and separated from each other by unique stretches of DNA. The pattern was unlike anything they had seen before. They noted it in their paper, but had no idea what it meant, and did not pursue it further.

Over the following years, similar odd repeating sequences were spotted in other microbes by other researchers, but each discovery was isolated. No one connected them. It was not until the early nineteen nineties that a Spanish microbiologist named Francisco Mojica, working at the University of Alicante on his doctoral thesis, began to study what appeared to be the same type of structure in the salt-loving archaea Haloferax mediterranei. Mojica spent years mapping and cataloguing these peculiar repeating sequences, and in two thousand, he recognised that what different researchers had been finding in dozens of different microorganisms were all examples of the same phenomenon. He reached out to Ruud Jansen of Utrecht University, and together they coined the acronym that would eventually become one of the most significant words in modern science: CRISPR, for Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic Repeats.

But Mojica did not stop at naming the sequences. He became obsessed with understanding what they were for. In two thousand and three, while analysing CRISPR sequences from dozens of bacteria, he made a discovery that stopped him cold. The unique stretches of DNA separating the repeated sequences — the so-called spacers — were not random. They matched sequences from bacteriophages: the viruses that prey on bacteria. The implication was staggering. Bacteria appeared to be storing fragments of viral DNA inside their own genomes, like a wanted poster pinned to a wall, or a memory of a past infection. In two thousand and five, Mojica published his finding that CRISPR was a form of adaptive immune system, one that bacteria used to recognise and fend off viruses they had encountered before. When a bacterium survived a viral attack, it snipped out a fragment of the virus's DNA and tucked it into its own CRISPR array. If that virus returned, the bacterium could recognise it instantly and destroy it.

The scientific community was slow to accept this idea. Mojica's initial paper was rejected by multiple journals before finally being published in two thousand and five. But he was right. Further research by other groups through the mid-two thousands confirmed that CRISPR was indeed a bacterial immune memory system. And crucially, researchers began to understand the molecular machinery that made it work — chief among them a family of proteins called Cas, for CRISPR-associated. These proteins were the weapons of the bacterial immune system: enzymes capable of cutting DNA with extraordinary precision, guided to their target by the stored viral sequence.

---

The leap from understanding CRISPR as a bacterial defence mechanism to recognising it as a potential gene-editing tool required a particular kind of scientific imagination. It was supplied, above all, by two researchers working on opposite sides of the Atlantic.

Emmanuelle Charpentier was a French microbiologist who had spent her career moving between research institutions across Europe and North America. By two thousand and eleven she was based in Umea, Sweden, where her laboratory was studying Streptococcus pyogenes — the bacterium responsible for strep throat — and in particular its CRISPR system. Her team discovered a small RNA molecule, which they called tracrRNA, that played a critical role in helping the guide RNA find its target. That same year, Charpentier met Jennifer Doudna, a biochemist at the University of California, Berkeley, who was one of the world's leading experts in RNA. They struck up a collaboration, and within months it had produced one of the most important papers in the history of molecular biology.

Published in the journal Science on the seventeenth of August, two thousand and twelve, Doudna and Charpentier's paper demonstrated something remarkable. They had taken the CRISPR-Cas9 system from Streptococcus pyogenes, isolated it in a test tube, and shown that it could be programmed to cut any DNA sequence they chose. The key insight was that the guide RNA — the molecule that directed the Cas9 protein to the right location — could be redesigned at will. You could tell the system to find virtually any sequence of genetic letters you liked, simply by changing the guide RNA to match. The Cas9 protein would follow the guide to that precise location and make a clean cut through both strands of the DNA double helix. The cell's own repair machinery would then kick in, either disabling the cut gene or, if a template was provided, replacing the damaged sequence with a corrected version.

In eight months, Doudna and Charpentier had transformed a bacterial immune quirk into a programmable molecular scalpel. The CRISPR era had begun. In October two thousand and twenty, the Nobel Prize in Chemistry was awarded jointly to Jennifer Doudna and Emmanuelle Charpentier for the development of a method for genome editing — the first time the chemistry Nobel had been won by two women.

---

The scientific world moved with extraordinary speed. Within months of Doudna and Charpentier's paper, researchers began adapting CRISPR for use in more complex organisms. The pivotal next step came in January two thousand and thirteen, when Feng Zhang of the Broad Institute of MIT and Harvard published a paper showing that CRISPR-Cas9 could edit genes in human and mouse cells. Zhang, a Chinese-American neuroscientist and molecular biologist who had already made major contributions to other gene-editing technologies, had been working on the same problem in parallel with Doudna and Charpentier, though his focus was on making the system work inside the far more complex machinery of mammalian cells. His two thousand and thirteen paper became one of the most cited in the history of the field, and it ignited a fierce patent dispute between the Broad Institute and the University of California over who had the rights to CRISPR technology — a legal battle that would run for years and highlight just how much money was now at stake.

The potential applications multiplied rapidly. In agriculture, researchers used CRISPR to engineer disease-resistant crops, hornless cattle, and salmon that grew faster and required less feed. In the laboratory, it became the standard tool for understanding gene function: if you wanted to know what a particular gene did, you simply switched it off with CRISPR and watched what happened. In medicine, the race was on to translate the technology into treatments for the diseases that had long been considered beyond reach.

The first human clinical trials began cautiously. In two thousand and nineteen, researchers at the University of Pennsylvania launched the first United States trial using CRISPR-modified immune cells against cancer, engineering patients' own T cells to better recognise and attack tumours. In China, trials had already begun even earlier. Results from the early cancer trials suggested the approach was safe, if not yet dramatically effective. But the most anticipated application was for inherited genetic diseases, where the promise was nothing less than a cure.

Sickle cell disease was the leading test case. The condition, which causes red blood cells to collapse into a rigid sickle shape, is agonising and debilitating: patients suffer severe episodes of pain, organ damage, and shortened lives. It is caused by a single mutation in the gene that encodes haemoglobin, and it affects millions of people worldwide, with the highest burden falling on sub-Saharan Africa and African-American communities. The CRISPR-based treatment, developed by Vertex Pharmaceuticals and CRISPR Therapeutics under the name Casgevy, worked by editing a patient's own stem cells to reactivate a form of haemoglobin normally switched off after birth — essentially bypassing the faulty gene entirely. Victoria Gray, a mother of four from Mississippi, became the first sickle cell patient to receive the treatment in a clinical trial in two thousand and nineteen. Before the treatment, she had been rushed to the emergency room at least once a month. After it, her crises stopped. On the eighth of December, two thousand and twenty-three, the United States Food and Drug Administration approved Casgevy, making it the first CRISPR-based therapy ever approved for clinical use. It was, by any measure, a historic moment: a technology described in a journal paper barely eleven years earlier had become an approved medicine.

---

But the story could not remain triumphant for long. In November two thousand and eighteen, a Chinese biophysicist named He Jiankui stood before a scientific conference in Hong Kong and announced that he had done something no researcher had done before, and no responsible researcher had sanctioned: he had used CRISPR to edit the genomes of human embryos, which were then implanted and brought to term. Twin girls, whom he called Lulu and Nana, had been born with permanently altered DNA.

He had targeted the CCR5 gene — the protein that HIV uses to enter human cells — attempting to engineer a mutation that confers near-immunity to the virus. He had acted in secret without proper oversight, falsified ethical review documents, and introduced mutations that had never been seen in humans before, meaning no one could predict their long-term effects. And there was a further irony: the CCR5 mutation that confers HIV resistance also appears to increase susceptibility to certain other infections, including West Nile virus.

The outcry from the scientific community was immediate and almost universally condemnatory. He had crossed a line that the entire community had agreed must not be crossed: the editing of the human germline. When a doctor treats a patient with CRISPR by editing cells in their blood or immune system, the changes affect only that patient. When CRISPR edits an embryo, the changes are heritable — passed on to every subsequent generation, potentially propagating through the human species indefinitely. It is a change not just to one life but to all the lives that flow from it. On the thirtieth of December, two thousand and nineteen, a Chinese court sentenced He Jiankui to three years in prison for illegal medical practice.

The affair forced a reckoning. International bodies convened. Scientists and ethicists debated where the line should be drawn. A broad consensus emerged that while CRISPR had enormous promise for treating disease in individual patients — what is known as somatic editing — the deliberate heritable editing of the human germline remained unacceptable until far more was known about safety, until much stronger regulatory frameworks existed, and until society as a whole had had the chance to debate what kind of future it wanted. As recently as two thousand and twenty-five, leading scientific societies were calling for a decade-long moratorium on germline editing for reproductive purposes.

The ethical questions are not simple. CRISPR offers the prospect of eliminating inherited diseases that have caused immeasurable suffering across generations. Who would argue against curing Huntington's disease, or preventing a child from inheriting a condition that will kill them before they reach adulthood? But the same technology that could delete a deadly mutation could also, in principle, be used to select for traits — intelligence, height, athletic ability, resistance to disease — in ways that begin to shade into something that has historically been called eugenics. The worry is not merely philosophical. Once germline editing becomes accepted for clear medical purposes, the boundary between treatment and enhancement may prove difficult to maintain. And enhancements, unlike treatments, are likely to be available only to those who can afford them, raising the prospect of a genetic divide running alongside all the other divisions that already fracture human society.

Beyond medicine, CRISPR raises questions of ecological consequence. Gene drive technology could in theory spread a modification through an entire wild population — potentially eliminating malaria by making mosquitoes infertile or resistant to the parasite. But releasing a gene drive is, in the most literal sense, irreversible, and the ecological consequences of eliminating a species are unknown and may be unknowable in advance.

---

We are living in the early chapters of a story whose full implications will take generations to understand. A tool that did not exist in two thousand and eleven is already treating patients in clinics, and a therapy approved in two thousand and twenty-three may within a decade render sickle cell disease a condition of the past. Researchers are now investigating CRISPR's potential against cancers, viral infections, and inherited blindness.

The story of CRISPR is a story about the deep strangeness of science — how threads pulled from completely different directions, by researchers who had no idea where they were going, converged on a technology that may redefine what it means to be human.

CRISPR did not come from a grand plan. It came from curiosity: from scientists following strange patterns in bacterial DNA because the patterns were interesting, without any idea where the trail would lead. That is how science usually works. And the trail, it turns out, leads all the way to the human genome — to the three billion letters of code that make us who we are, now, for the first time in history, sitting within reach of the editorial pen.
