# The Enlightenment: The Age of Reason

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

Sometime in the late seventeenth century, thinking changed. The change was gradual, contested, and incomplete — it is still incomplete — but its direction was clear: away from authority and tradition as the primary sources of truth, and toward reason, evidence, and individual judgement. This shift in how educated people thought about the world, about human nature, about government and religion and justice, is what historians call the Enlightenment. It produced some of the most consequential political ideas in history — ideas that still underpin the legal and political systems of most democracies today.

The Enlightenment is usually dated from the late 1600s to the end of the eighteenth century — roughly from the Glorious Revolution in England in 1688 to the French Revolution of 1789. It was not a single movement or a school with fixed doctrine. It was a conversation, conducted across the salons of Paris, the coffee houses of London, the universities of Edinburgh and Königsberg, through books, pamphlets, and letters, among people who disagreed with each other about almost everything except the conviction that human reason was the proper guide to human affairs.

The intellectual roots of the Enlightenment lay partly in the Scientific Revolution of the previous century, in which figures like Galileo and Newton had demonstrated that the natural world operated according to laws discoverable through careful observation and mathematical reasoning. If the physical world could be understood through reason, why not human society? Why not politics, morality, and the proper organisation of government?

---

John Locke, the English philosopher who lived from 1632 to 1704, was in many ways the founding voice of Enlightenment political thought. He argued that all human beings were born with natural rights — to life, liberty, and property — that no government could legitimately violate. Government derived its authority from the consent of the governed, not from divine right or inherited status. If a government systematically violated the natural rights of its citizens, those citizens had the right — indeed, the duty — to replace it. These ideas read almost like the Declaration of Independence, which is not coincidental: Jefferson read Locke carefully.

In France, the Enlightenment produced a remarkable group of writers and thinkers known as the philosophes. Voltaire, the most brilliant and prolific, wrote with devastating wit against religious intolerance, superstition, and the arbitrary power of church and state. His most famous work, Candide, savaged the comfortable belief that all was for the best in the best of all possible worlds, by subjecting his hero to a catalogue of disasters. Voltaire's target was not religion itself but organised religion's alliance with state power to persecute those who thought differently.

Montesquieu studied the constitutions of different societies and in The Spirit of the Laws developed the concept of the separation of powers — the idea that executive, legislative, and judicial functions of government should be held in separate hands to prevent tyranny. This idea became the foundation of the American Constitution and virtually every liberal democratic constitution that followed.

---

Jean-Jacques Rousseau offered a different and more radical vision. Where Locke was primarily concerned with protecting individuals from government overreach, Rousseau argued that true freedom could only exist in a community organised around the general will of the people. His Social Contract begins with one of the most famous opening lines in philosophy: "Man is born free, and everywhere he is in chains." Rousseau's ideas were the most emotionally and politically charged of the Enlightenment, and they fed directly into the passion and violence of the French Revolution.

In Scotland, the philosopher David Hume applied the same spirit of sceptical enquiry to religion, ethics, and the limits of human knowledge itself. In Germany, Immanuel Kant offered what remains the most concise definition of what the Enlightenment was: "Dare to know," he wrote. Have the courage to use your own understanding. This was its essence — not any particular set of conclusions, but the disposition to think for oneself rather than defer to tradition or authority.

---

The political consequences were seismic. The American Declaration of Independence of 1776 is a direct application of Enlightenment ideas: that all men are created equal, that they are endowed with inalienable rights, that government derives its just powers from the consent of the governed. The French Revolution of 1789, more violent and more radical, was equally rooted in Enlightenment thought — the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen directly echoes the language and arguments of the philosophes.

The Enlightenment's legacy is complicated. Many of its ideals were proclaimed by men who enslaved other human beings and denied full rights to women. The same reasoning that declared all men equal was also deployed to justify colonial domination through the idea of civilisational hierarchy. Reason, it turned out, could be used to rationalise almost anything.

But the core insights — that government must answer to the governed, that individuals possess rights that precede and limit state power, that belief should be held and revised on the basis of evidence — became the foundations on which modern democracy and science are built. We are still, in the truest sense, children of the Enlightenment, living in the world its arguments made possible.
