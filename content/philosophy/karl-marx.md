# Karl Marx: The Man Who Changed How We See Society

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Few people in history have had their ideas more widely adopted — or more violently misapplied. Karl Marx died in 1883, largely unknown outside a small circle of European radicals, his great work incomplete, his political projects unsuccessful. By the middle of the twentieth century, roughly a third of humanity lived under governments claiming to govern in his name. Those governments built gulags and engineered famines; they also built schools and hospitals and abolished feudalism in societies that had never managed it themselves. The gap between what Marx wrote and what was done in his name is one of the great and terrible ironies of modern history — though whether the terror was inherent in the ideas or betrayed them is a question that has never been settled and probably never will be.

What is certain is that Marx's analysis of capitalism — its mechanisms, its contradictions, its human costs — remains the most penetrating and influential critique ever written. You do not have to be a Marxist to think that class matters, that economic structures shape political possibilities, that the distribution of wealth is not natural but historical and changeable. These ideas, which now seem obvious to many people across the political spectrum, became obvious largely because of Marx.

---

Karl Marx was born on the fifth of May, 1818, in Trier, in the Rhineland — then part of Prussia, later Germany. His father was a lawyer from a long line of rabbis who had converted to Christianity to practise his profession without legal obstruction. Marx grew up in a prosperous, cultured household, was educated at Bonn and Berlin, and seemed headed for an academic career in philosophy. He absorbed the philosophy of Hegel — particularly Hegel's idea that history moved through a dialectical process, in which contradictions generated their own resolution — and began applying it not to the realm of ideas but to the material world.

He became a journalist and a political radical. In 1843, his newspaper was shut down by the Prussian censors, and he moved to Paris, where he met Friedrich Engels, the son of a wealthy German textile manufacturer who was already working in his father's factory in Manchester and had seen industrial capitalism at first hand. The friendship between Marx and Engels was one of the most consequential in intellectual history. Engels provided not just intellectual companionship but financial support, without which Marx could almost certainly not have continued his work. The two men collaborated, argued, disagreed on specifics, and agreed on fundamentals for the rest of Marx's life.

It was for the Communist League, a small German radical organisation, that Marx and Engels wrote the Communist Manifesto, published on the twenty-first of February, 1848. It was a moment of extraordinary timing: within weeks of its publication, revolutionary uprisings broke out across Europe — in France, Germany, Austria, Hungary, Italy. The Manifesto did not cause these revolutions, which had their own deep economic and political roots, but its appearance in the midst of them gave it a significance it might otherwise not have had. The revolutions of 1848 all ultimately failed, suppressed by conservative forces reasserting control. Marx was expelled from Prussia, Belgium, and France in turn, and settled finally in London in 1849, where he would remain for the rest of his life.

---

London was both salvation and exile. Marx lived in poverty that was sometimes desperate — several of his children died in infancy, at least partly because of the conditions the family lived in. He worked in the reading room of the British Museum, surrounded by the accumulated knowledge of the empire that had built the world's first fully industrial economy, and he spent thirty years analysing that economy in meticulous, relentless detail. The result was Das Kapital, the first volume of which was published in 1867. It is one of the most ambitious intellectual undertakings in history: a detailed analysis of how capitalism worked, what it produced, what it cost, and why it contained within itself the seeds of its own transformation.

The central concept of Das Kapital is the labour theory of value: the idea that the value of commodities is ultimately derived from the labour required to produce them. From this, Marx developed his analysis of surplus value — the difference between what workers are paid and the value of what they actually produce, which is appropriated by the capitalist as profit. This was not just an economic observation but a moral indictment: capitalism, Marx argued, was a system in which the labour of the many was systematically extracted for the benefit of the few, with the appearance of a free market transaction concealing what was in reality a form of exploitation.

He was not merely concerned with economics. The concept of alienation — which he developed early in his career and which runs through all his later work — described the way in which workers under capitalism became estranged from the products of their labour, from the act of production itself, from other human beings, and ultimately from their own human nature. Work that should be expressive and meaningful had become a commodity — something sold in exchange for survival — and this distortion of human life was one of capitalism's deepest injuries.

---

Marx's theory of history — historical materialism — proposed that human history was fundamentally driven not by ideas or by the actions of great men but by the material conditions of production: who owned the means of production, how work was organised, and how the surplus was distributed. Each historical epoch was characterised by a particular mode of production — ancient slavery, feudalism, capitalism — and each contained contradictions that would eventually generate a new mode. Capitalism, in Marx's analysis, was characterised by the fundamental contradiction between the social nature of production — vast numbers of people working together in factories and systems of global trade — and the private ownership of the means of production. This contradiction would eventually generate a working class — the proletariat — that would organise, become conscious of its own interests, and overthrow the system.

He was wrong about the timing. The revolutions he expected in the industrialised countries of Western Europe did not come, or came too early and failed. The revolutions that did occur in his name — in Russia in 1917, in China in 1949 — happened in largely peasant societies rather than advanced industrial ones, in conditions almost exactly unlike those he had specified. What emerged from those revolutions was not the withering away of the state he had predicted but an enormously powerful state that used Marxist language to justify one-party dictatorship. This was not what Marx wrote about — but the relationship between his ideas and those outcomes is not as simple as pure betrayal, and honest Marxist scholars have wrestled with it for a century.

The enduring power of Marx's work lies less in its predictions, most of which were wrong, than in the questions it asks and the analytical tools it provides. The insight that economic structures shape political possibilities — that who owns what determines who has power — seems simply true, and it came from him. The observation that capitalism generates both enormous wealth and enormous inequality, that it is creative and destructive simultaneously, that it transforms human relationships at the most intimate level — these are observations that anyone looking at the contemporary world would struggle to dismiss.

---

Marx died on the fourteenth of March, 1883, in London. Only a handful of people attended his funeral in Highgate Cemetery. Engels spoke. He said that Marx had discovered the law of development of human history, just as Darwin had discovered the law of evolution. It was a generous comparison between two friends, and perhaps an overstated one. But the ambition behind it — to understand human society with the same scientific rigour that Darwin had brought to natural history — captures something real about what Marx attempted and, in part, achieved.

His notebooks, his letters, his drafts, and his marginalia fill many volumes and are still being studied. The second and third volumes of Das Kapital, edited by Engels from Marx's notes, were published after his death. The ideas in them have been argued over, built on, refuted in part, extended in part, and applied in wildly varying directions by every generation since. No thinker in the modern period has provoked more argument, more hope, or more horror. That alone is a measure of how much he mattered.
