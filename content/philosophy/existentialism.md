# Existentialism: Freedom, Responsibility, and the Absurd

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

Existentialism is a philosophy born of crisis. It emerged fully into the world in the rubble of the Second World War, in the cafés and lecture halls of occupied and liberated Paris, addressing questions that the catastrophe of that war had made impossible to avoid: What does it mean to live in a world without guaranteed meaning? What is a human being when stripped of all external definitions — nationality, religion, class, the roles assigned by family and society? What remains when God, tradition, and the certainties of progress have all failed to prevent Auschwitz?

The answers existentialism offered were not comforting, but they were honest. There is no pre-given meaning to human life, it said — no divine purpose written into us at creation, no natural law that tells us what to be. But this absence of given meaning is not a cause for despair; it is the ground of freedom. Human beings are radically free: free to choose, to define themselves through their choices, and — crucially — responsible for those choices in a way that cannot be evaded. "Existence precedes essence" was how Jean-Paul Sartre put it: we are not born with a nature that determines what we must become; we exist first, choose second, and only then do we have an essence — a character, a life — of our own making.

This was a philosophy that spoke directly to a generation that had watched traditional institutions — states, churches, political parties — fail catastrophically, and that had to decide, often at great personal cost, how to act in the face of that failure. Many of the existentialists had been active in the French Resistance. Their philosophy was not an academic exercise; it was the intellectual expression of choices they had already had to make.

---

The roots of existentialism run deeper than postwar Paris, however. The Danish philosopher Søren Kierkegaard, who was born in 1813 and died in 1855, is usually identified as its earliest precursor, though he would not have used the word. Writing in the mid-nineteenth century against the dominant philosophy of his time — Hegel's systematic idealism, which seemed to explain everything in terms of the march of reason through history — Kierkegaard insisted that the most important questions were not theoretical but personal: how should I live? What should I commit to? The crowd, the public, the system — all the comfortable structures that tell an individual what to think and believe — were, for Kierkegaard, enemies of genuine selfhood. He called for a "leap of faith" — a personal, passionate commitment that could not be grounded in reason alone, because the most important things in life resist rational justification.

Friedrich Nietzsche, writing in the latter half of the nineteenth century, contributed the other great provocation. In proclaiming that "God is dead" — by which he meant not a literal theological statement but a cultural diagnosis, that the belief in a divinely ordered universe had ceased to be believable for modern Europeans — Nietzsche posed the question that existentialism would spend the next century answering: if there is no divine authority, no cosmic order, no natural moral law, what is the basis for values? His answer — that we must create our own values, become the authors of our own lives — anticipates existentialism directly.

The German philosopher Martin Heidegger provided the technical philosophical framework. In Being and Time, published in 1927, he analysed human existence in terms of what he called Dasein — literally "being-there," the condition of being a creature who is always already in a world, always already thrown into a particular time, place, language, and culture, always conscious of its own mortality. The confrontation with death — what Heidegger called "being-toward-death" — was, for him, the condition that could shake Dasein loose from its everyday absorption in the public world and force it toward authentic self-understanding. Heidegger's political associations with Nazism remain a source of profound controversy; but his philosophical analysis of human existence was enormously influential.

---

It was in France, and above all in the work of Jean-Paul Sartre, that existentialism became a cultural movement as well as a philosophical school. Sartre was born in Paris in 1905 and died there in 1980; in between, he produced an extraordinary body of work in philosophy, fiction, drama, and political commentary that made him one of the most influential intellectual figures of the twentieth century.

His major philosophical treatise, Being and Nothingness, was published in 1943 — during the German occupation of France. It is a dense and difficult work that analyses human consciousness in radical terms: human beings, unlike objects, have no fixed nature; they are, in Sartre's phrase, "condemned to be free." Freedom, for Sartre, is not a blessing but a burden: we cannot escape the necessity of choosing, even refusing to choose is itself a choice, and we are fully responsible for what we become through our choices. There is no God, no human nature, no historical determinism that can take that responsibility from us. "Man is nothing else but what he makes of himself," he wrote.

"Bad faith" — mauvaise foi — was Sartre's term for the self-deception by which people try to deny their freedom: telling themselves they had no choice, that they were only following orders, that their character made them act as they did, that circumstances left them no alternative. It was, he argued, not merely a philosophical error but a moral failure — and one that the Second World War had demonstrated could have catastrophic consequences.

In October 1945, Sartre delivered a public lecture later published as Existentialism Is a Humanism, an attempt to make his ideas accessible and to defend them against the charge that existentialism was a philosophy of despair. If there is no God, no given human nature, no predetermined purpose — then everything depends on us, he argued. This is not nihilism; it is the most serious possible affirmation of human responsibility. The lecture was attended by such enormous crowds that people fainted in the crush.

---

Albert Camus — though he resisted the existentialist label — was Sartre's near-contemporary and, in some ways, the more accessible voice of this philosophical moment. Born in Algeria in 1913 to a French-Algerian working-class family, Camus was shaped by the Mediterranean poverty of his childhood and by the tuberculosis that would shadow his life. His essay The Myth of Sisyphus, published in 1942, set out the concept of the absurd — the collision between the human desire for meaning and the universe's silence on the subject. Life has no inherent meaning; the universe offers no answers to our most urgent questions. What do we do with this fact?

Camus's answer was refusal: refusal of suicide (which would be giving in to the absurd), and refusal of the "philosophical suicide" of religious faith or political ideology that paperes over the absence of meaning with false certainties. Instead: rebellion, awareness, and the deliberate choice to live fully in the face of meaninglessness. His image was Sisyphus — condemned by the gods to roll a boulder up a hill for eternity, watching it roll back down, and beginning again. "One must imagine Sisyphus happy," Camus wrote. The struggle itself is enough.

Camus and Sartre were close friends and central figures of the postwar Parisian intellectual scene — and had a spectacular public falling-out in 1952, when Camus's The Rebel, which criticised revolutionary violence and Marxist historical determinism, was attacked by Sartre's journal. Their dispute mapped onto the deepest political tensions of the Cold War era: could one support justice without endorsing revolutionary terror? Camus said no to the terror; Sartre, for most of his career, gave it more latitude.

Simone de Beauvoir, Sartre's lifelong partner and one of the most important philosophers of the twentieth century in her own right, applied existentialist ideas to the condition of women. The Second Sex, published in 1949, analysed how women had been defined historically as the "Other" — in relation to men, never as autonomous subjects in their own right — and argued for women's full realisation of their existential freedom. It became the foundational text of second-wave feminism.

---

Existentialism's influence spread far beyond philosophy in the narrow academic sense. It shaped the literature of the postwar decades: Samuel Beckett's Waiting for Godot, with its two characters in a featureless landscape waiting endlessly for an arrival that never comes, is perhaps its purest theatrical expression. It influenced psychology — the existential therapists, like Viktor Frankl and Rollo May, applied its insights to the clinical encounter, arguing that many forms of psychological suffering could be understood as responses to the fundamental anxieties of human freedom. It resonated in art, film, and music through the nineteen-fifties and sixties.

The existentialist moment in philosophy has passed — later movements, including structuralism, post-structuralism, and analytic philosophy, moved in different directions. But the questions existentialism asked have not gone away: they are permanent features of the human condition. What does it mean to live freely? What am I responsible for? How do I find meaning in a world that does not provide it? These questions arise freshly for every generation, and every generation must find its own answers. What existentialism contributed was the insistence that the search for answers is itself the most fully human thing we can do.
