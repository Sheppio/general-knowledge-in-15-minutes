# Adam Smith and the Wealth of Nations: The Birth of Modern Economics

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Adam Smith is the most influential economist who ever lived. His great work, An Inquiry into the Nature and Causes of the Wealth of Nations, published in seventeen seventy-six, founded a discipline, shaped the policies of governments for more than two centuries, and gave us concepts — the invisible hand, the division of labour, the market — that have become so embedded in the way we think about economic life that we rarely notice we are using them. Smith's ideas were themselves the product of a particular time and place: Scotland in the eighteenth century, an era of commercial expansion, intellectual ferment, and deep uncertainty about the sources of national prosperity. Understanding who Smith was, what problems he was trying to solve, and what he actually argued — as distinct from how he has been used and misused — is essential to understanding modern economics and the world it has helped create.

He was baptised on the fifth of June, seventeen twenty-three, in Kirkcaldy, a small coastal town in Fife, Scotland. His father died before he was born, and he was raised by his mother, who remained close to him throughout his life. He was sent to Glasgow University at the age of fourteen — not unusual at the time — where he studied under Francis Hutcheson, one of the great moral philosophers of the Scottish Enlightenment, who taught Smith that the purpose of moral philosophy was to understand the foundations of human happiness and social order. Smith then spent six unhappy years at Balliol College, Oxford, which he found intellectually moribund — "the greater part of the public professors have, for these many years, given up altogether even the pretence of teaching," he would later write, an observation that reads as freshly today as it did then.

---

Smith returned to Scotland, lectured in Edinburgh, and in seventeen fifty-two was appointed Professor of Moral Philosophy at Glasgow — the subject that then encompassed what we would now separate into ethics, political theory, jurisprudence, and economics. His first major work, The Theory of Moral Sentiments, published in seventeen fifty-nine, established his reputation across Europe and dealt with a question that might seem far from economics: how is it that human beings, who are self-interested creatures, are also capable of moral behaviour? How do we develop a sense of right and wrong, of sympathy for others, of the desire to be well-regarded? Smith's answer centred on the concept of the "impartial spectator" — an imagined observer whose approval or disapproval we internalise as a guide to conduct. The Theory of Moral Sentiments is a sophisticated and surprisingly modern piece of moral psychology, and it is essential context for the later work.

The conventional narrative in which Smith is the prophet of ruthless self-interest is a misreading that ignores almost everything in The Theory of Moral Sentiments. Smith believed human beings were both self-interested and sympathetic; that markets were powerful mechanisms for coordinating economic activity; and also that they operated within a framework of moral norms, legal rules, and social institutions without which they could not function well. His economics and his ethics were a single integrated project, not two separate and contradictory things.

---

In seventeen sixty-four, Smith took leave from Glasgow to travel to France as tutor to the young Duke of Buccleuch — a common arrangement for academics of his time, and an enormously intellectually enriching one. In France he met Voltaire, who was then living near Geneva, and spent extended time in Paris meeting the French economists known as the Physiocrats — in particular François Quesnay and Anne-Robert-Jacques Turgot — who had developed a systematic account of how economic activity was organised and how wealth was generated. The Physiocrats argued that agriculture was the only true source of wealth, a view Smith found stimulating but ultimately wrong. The encounter with Physiocratic economics crystallised Smith's own thinking, and he returned to Kirkcaldy in seventeen sixty-six and spent the next decade in intensive work on the book that would become The Wealth of Nations.

The book was published on the ninth of March, seventeen seventy-six — the same year as the American Declaration of Independence, with which it shares certain assumptions about rational individuals, natural rights, and the proper limits of government authority. The Wealth of Nations is a long, discursive work that ranges across history, philosophy, law, and political theory as well as what we would now call economics. Its central argument begins with a simple observation: some nations are wealthy and some are poor, and understanding why is one of the most important questions a society can ask.

---

Smith's answer begins with the division of labour — the practice of breaking down production into specialised tasks each performed by different workers. His famous illustration, which opens the book, is a pin factory. A single worker making pins from scratch might produce twenty a day. But in a factory where one worker draws out the wire, another straightens it, another cuts it, another points it, another grinds the head, and so on — with ten workers performing seventeen distinct operations — the same ten workers might produce forty-eight thousand pins a day. The source of this productivity gain is not individual talent or effort but organisation: the specialisation of tasks allows each worker to become highly skilled at a narrow operation, eliminates the time lost in switching between tasks, and creates the opportunity for machinery to substitute for human labour.

The division of labour, Smith argued, was the primary source of what he called "the wealth of nations" — the productive capacity that determined the standard of living of a country's people. And the division of labour was driven by markets: the ability to exchange what one produces for what others produce. A worker could only specialise if he could sell his specialised output and buy the things he needed that he was no longer producing for himself. The extent of the division of labour, Smith wrote in one of his most famous formulations, is limited by the extent of the market. Larger markets — created by trade, by transport improvements, by the removal of barriers — allowed finer divisions of labour and higher productivity. This was Smith's argument for free trade: not just that it was profitable for individual traders, but that it expanded markets and thereby raised the productive capacity and living standards of everyone.

---

The price mechanism — the system by which supply and demand interact to set prices, and prices signal to producers what to make and consumers what to buy — was Smith's answer to the question of how a complex economy coordinates itself without any central direction. Individuals pursuing their own interests in competitive markets are led, Smith argued, "as if by an invisible hand" to promote ends that were no part of their intention — the general welfare of society. This is not because they are altruistic, but because competition disciplines self-interest: a seller who charges too much loses customers; a producer who makes something nobody wants goes out of business. The invisible hand — a phrase Smith uses only once in The Wealth of Nations, in a context quite different from the sweeping theory it has been recruited to support — describes this coordination mechanism.

Smith's analysis was not a blanket endorsement of business or markets. He was scathing about merchants and manufacturers who lobbied for protections and privileges, noting that people of the same trade seldom meet together, even for merriment and diversion, without the conversation ending in a conspiracy against the public. He recognised that markets could be distorted by monopoly, by information asymmetries, and by the exploitation of workers. He supported public provision of education, roads, and other infrastructure that markets would undersupply. He argued for progressive taxation. The "free market" fundamentalism that has sometimes been attributed to Smith would not have been recognised by its supposed founder.

---

Smith's influence on economic thought and on policy was enormous and rapid. The Wealth of Nations was read by politicians, statesmen, and intellectuals across Europe and America; William Pitt the Younger cited it in parliamentary debates; it became the founding text of what became known as classical economics, and through classical economics the ancestor of the entire discipline of economics as it is practised today. The concepts Smith introduced — comparative advantage, the labour theory of value, the theory of prices, the analysis of wages and profits — were elaborated, refined, and in many cases overturned by the economists who followed him, from David Ricardo and John Stuart Mill in the nineteenth century to Alfred Marshall, John Maynard Keynes, and Paul Samuelson in the twentieth. But the questions Smith asked, and the methods he used to ask them — combining historical evidence, logical analysis, and attention to the actual behaviour of real people in real markets — defined the agenda of a new discipline.

Smith died on the seventeenth of July, seventeen ninety, at the age of sixty-seven, having instructed that the unpublished manuscripts he had not completed to his satisfaction be destroyed. He was by all accounts a warm, absent-minded, and exceptionally learned man, happiest in conversation, committed to his students and his mother, capable of falling into an empty vat at a tannery while deep in thought. He would probably be surprised — and possibly dismayed — by some of what has been done in his name. He would, perhaps, be less surprised by the endurance of the fundamental problems he identified: how to generate prosperity, how to ensure it is shared, and how to build the institutions that allow markets and societies to work for everyone.
