# The History of Artificial Intelligence: From Dream to Disruption

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

In 1950, a British mathematician named Alan Turing published a paper that began with a deceptively simple question: "Can machines think?" He was careful about how he answered it. Rather than trying to define what thinking is — a notoriously slippery philosophical question — he proposed a practical test. If a machine could carry on a conversation that was indistinguishable from a conversation with a human being, then it was reasonable to say the machine was thinking. The Turing Test, as it became known, set the agenda for the field that would come to be called Artificial Intelligence: can we build machines that do what we consider to require intelligence when humans do it?

Seventy-odd years later, that field has transformed the world in ways Turing could not have imagined. Machines diagnose cancer from scans, write poetry, compose music, translate languages in real time, drive cars, and hold conversations sophisticated enough to routinely pass the test Turing proposed. The journey from Turing's paper to the AI systems of today is a story of extraordinary intellectual achievement, repeated failure, inflated expectations, and sudden, sometimes shocking, breakthroughs.

---

The formal birth of Artificial Intelligence as a discipline is usually dated to the summer of 1956, when the mathematician and computer scientist John McCarthy organised a workshop at Dartmouth College in New Hampshire. McCarthy had coined the term "Artificial Intelligence" — deliberately choosing it to distinguish the field from cybernetics and to emphasise the goal: machines that could actually reason, not merely compute. The Dartmouth workshop gathered a small but remarkable group that included Marvin Minsky, Claude Shannon, and Herbert Simon. At the workshop, Allen Newell and Herbert Simon presented the Logic Theorist — a program that could prove mathematical theorems and was in that sense the first working AI program.

The optimism that surrounded the early days of AI was extraordinary. McCarthy and Minsky both predicted in the late 1950s that a machine with the general intelligence of a human being would exist within twenty years. Simon predicted in 1965 that "machines will be capable, within twenty years, of doing any work a man can do." These predictions were not merely wrong; they were spectacularly wrong. What the pioneers had underestimated was the difficulty of the problem. Many things that seem simple to humans — recognising a face, understanding a sentence spoken in a noisy room, navigating a pavement without tripping — turned out to be extraordinarily difficult to programme. And many things that seemed hard for humans — playing chess at a grandmaster level, doing calculus — turned out to be relatively straightforward.

The gap between what AI could do and what its proponents had promised led to the first "AI winter" — a period of reduced funding, declining interest, and general disillusionment that set in during the 1970s. The US and UK governments, which had poured money into AI research on the basis of the early optimism, pulled back sharply. A second AI winter followed in the late 1980s, after a wave of commercial AI products based on "expert systems" — programmes that encoded human expertise as rules — failed to live up to their billing. The field had a recurring problem: each generation of progress opened new vistas of possibility while revealing new depths of difficulty.

---

The breakthroughs that began to accumulate in the 1990s had a different character. Rather than trying to programme intelligence explicitly — telling a machine what rules to follow — researchers began focusing on machine learning: techniques that allowed machines to learn from data, improving their performance through experience rather than through programmed instruction. This was not a new idea; some of the Dartmouth pioneers had explored it. But the combination of new mathematical techniques, vastly more powerful computers, and vastly larger datasets made it newly practical.

On the eleventh of May, 1997, a supercomputer called Deep Blue, developed by IBM, defeated the reigning world chess champion Garry Kasparov in a match played in New York. It was not the first time a computer had beaten a chess grandmaster, but it was the first time a computer had beaten the very best human player alive under tournament conditions. Kasparov, who had previously beaten an earlier version of Deep Blue in 1996, was shocked and furious; he alleged that the computer's play showed signs of human-like creativity that suggested human assistance, an allegation IBM denied. The match was a global media event and a genuine milestone, though one that quickly seemed more modest than it appeared at the time. Chess, for all its complexity, turns out to be a domain in which brute computational power — the ability to evaluate millions of positions per second — can substitute for something like understanding.

---

Go was different. The ancient Chinese board game is played on a nineteen-by-nineteen grid with simple rules but an almost incomprehensible number of possible positions — more positions than there are atoms in the observable universe. For decades, Go was considered a near-impossible challenge for AI: unlike chess, it could not be approached through brute force, because the search space was simply too large. Experts predicted it would be decades before a computer could match the world's best human players.

In March 2016, AlphaGo — a programme developed by DeepMind, a British AI research company owned by Google — played the world Go champion Lee Sedol in a five-game match in Seoul. AlphaGo won four games to one. It was a different kind of achievement from Deep Blue's chess victory, because AlphaGo had not been programmed with explicit rules about how to play Go. It had learned by studying millions of human games and then by playing against itself millions of times, developing strategies and moves that no human had ever played and that Go experts initially described as strange or wrong before realising they were brilliant. The moment in Game Two when AlphaGo played Move Thirty-Seven — a move that no human player would have made, that violated conventional Go wisdom, and that turned out to be decisive — is often cited as the moment that made AI researchers and the wider world understand that something genuinely new had arrived.

The underlying technique — deep reinforcement learning, applied through neural networks capable of processing enormous amounts of data — was not just for games. The same approaches were rapidly being applied to medical image analysis, protein structure prediction, natural language processing, and drug discovery. In 2020, DeepMind's AlphaFold solved one of biology's great unsolved problems: predicting the three-dimensional structure of proteins from their amino acid sequences. It was a problem that structural biologists had worked on for fifty years; AlphaFold solved it with a speed and accuracy that left the scientific community astonished.

---

The moment that brought AI to the attention of everyone was the launch of ChatGPT on the thirtieth of November, 2022. Developed by the American company OpenAI, ChatGPT was a large language model — a system trained on enormous quantities of text to predict, word by word, what a plausible response to any given input might be. What distinguished it from earlier AI systems was not just its capability but its accessibility: anyone with an internet connection could use it, and what it could do was immediately obvious. Within two months of its launch, it had a hundred million users — the fastest-growing consumer software application in history. It could write essays, debug code, translate poetry, explain complex concepts, and hold coherent conversations on almost any subject.

The arrival of large language models did not resolve the fundamental question Turing had asked in 1950. Whether these systems are "thinking" in any meaningful sense — whether they understand anything, or whether they are very sophisticated pattern-matchers that produce the appearance of understanding without the reality — remains deeply contested among philosophers, cognitive scientists, and AI researchers themselves. What is not contested is that their practical capabilities are transforming one industry after another, and that the pace of improvement shows no sign of slowing.

The history of AI is a history of a dream that kept proving harder than expected and then, suddenly, easier. The people who built the first programmes in the 1950s would be astonished by what exists today. The people building today's systems suspect, on the basis of recent experience, that what comes next will astonish them in turn. Turing's question has not been answered. But the machines have stopped waiting for an answer to start changing everything.
