# The Green Revolution: How Science Fed the World

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

In nineteen sixty-eight, the biologist Paul Ehrlich published The Population Bomb, a book predicting that mass famine was imminent and inevitable. The world's population was growing faster than its food supply could sustain, Ehrlich argued, and hundreds of millions of people would die of starvation in the nineteen-seventies. His prediction was wrong — not because population growth slowed or because new agricultural land was found, but because the yields of the world's most important food crops increased at a pace that nobody had expected. Between roughly nineteen sixty and nineteen ninety, wheat and rice yields in developing countries roughly doubled and in some cases tripled, fed by a combination of new high-yielding crop varieties, irrigation, synthetic fertilisers, and pesticides. This transformation — the Green Revolution — saved hundreds of millions of people from starvation and reshaped the demographics and geopolitics of Asia, Latin America, and Africa. It also came with environmental costs that are still being reckoned with today.

The origins of the Green Revolution lie in Mexico in the nineteen-forties. In nineteen forty-three, the Rockefeller Foundation, in partnership with the Mexican government, launched the Mexican Agricultural Program — an initiative to improve Mexican crop yields and reduce the country's dependence on food imports. The project employed a small team of American agricultural scientists who worked alongside Mexican researchers in the fields, breeding new varieties of wheat and maize. The most significant of these scientists was Norman Borlaug, a plant pathologist from Iowa who joined the programme in nineteen forty-four and would spend the next sixteen years breeding wheat varieties adapted to Mexican conditions.

---

The central problem Borlaug confronted was one that might seem paradoxical: high-yielding wheat varieties that responded well to fertiliser grew too tall. When well-fertilised wheat plants grew heavy grain heads, their tall stems could not support the weight and collapsed — a phenomenon called "lodging" — which ruined the harvest. The solution was to breed shorter wheat plants — semi-dwarf varieties — whose stiff, compact stems could support a heavier grain head without collapsing. Borlaug crossed existing wheat varieties with a Japanese semi-dwarf wheat called Norin Ten, which had been brought to the United States after the Second World War, and over years of patient selection developed semi-dwarf high-yield varieties that could take full advantage of fertiliser application without lodging.

He also used a technique called shuttle breeding — growing two crop generations per year in different locations — to accelerate the development process. By moving plants between highland and lowland growing environments, Borlaug could test varieties under different conditions and dramatically shorten the time from breeding to release. The resulting varieties were both high-yielding and adaptable to a wide range of growing conditions — a combination that made them suitable for deployment across very different agricultural environments.

The results in Mexico were striking. Mexico, which had imported half its wheat requirements in nineteen forty-three, became self-sufficient by nineteen fifty-six and was exporting wheat by nineteen sixty-four. The demonstration that scientific plant breeding could transform the productivity of a major staple crop attracted interest from countries facing far more acute food insecurity.

---

India and Pakistan in the nineteen-sixties were among the most alarming cases. Population growth was rapid, agricultural productivity was low, and famine was a persistent threat. In nineteen sixty-one, Borlaug was invited to India by M. S. Swaminathan, an Indian geneticist who was advising the government on agricultural policy and who recognised the potential of Borlaug's wheat varieties for the Indian subcontinent. After years of negotiations and field trials, India and Pakistan imported large quantities of Borlaug's semi-dwarf wheat seed in nineteen sixty-five and sixty-six.

The results were transformative. Indian wheat production grew from twelve and a half million tonnes in nineteen sixty-five to over twenty million tonnes by nineteen seventy. By nineteen seventy-four, India was self-sufficient in the production of all cereal crops — a transformation that would have seemed impossible a decade earlier, when the country was dependent on emergency food aid from the United States. Pakistan achieved similar results. The journalists and development economists who had been writing about inevitable South Asian famine found themselves writing about a Green Revolution instead.

Meanwhile, parallel work was underway on rice — the staple crop for an even larger portion of the world's population. The International Rice Research Institute, established in nineteen sixty in the Philippines with funding from the Rockefeller and Ford Foundations, assembled the world's largest collection of rice varieties and began systematic breeding for high yields. In November nineteen sixty-six, IRRI released IR8 — a semi-dwarf, high-yielding rice variety that could produce yields several times greater than traditional varieties when well irrigated and fertilised. IR8 was quickly adopted across Asia. It was not perfect — it had a somewhat chalky texture that some consumers found undesirable, and it required careful water management — but it worked, and subsequent varieties addressed its limitations while maintaining its yield advantages.

---

Norman Borlaug was awarded the Nobel Peace Prize in nineteen seventy — the only plant breeder ever to receive it — "for having given a well-founded hope: the green revolution." In his Nobel acceptance speech, Borlaug was characteristically clear-eyed about the limits of what he had achieved: he had bought time, he said — time for the world to get its population growth under control before the food supply was once again outpaced. The temporary success of the Green Revolution, he warned, should not be mistaken for a permanent solution.

His warning was prescient. The Green Revolution's achievements were real and large, but they came with costs that were not initially well understood. The high-yielding varieties required irrigation, fertiliser, and pesticides to achieve their potential yields, and the expansion of irrigated agriculture drew down groundwater reserves in many regions — particularly in northwestern India and Pakistan — at rates that could not be sustained indefinitely. Synthetic fertiliser use increased dramatically, with consequences for water quality as nitrogen and phosphorus ran off into rivers, lakes, and coastal seas, creating oxygen-depleted dead zones. The concentration on a small number of high-yielding varieties reduced genetic diversity in the world's major staple crops, creating vulnerability to pests and diseases that the older, more diverse varieties had resisted through their variety. The social consequences were also uneven: the new varieties and the inputs they required favoured farmers with sufficient land, capital, and access to irrigation, and in many regions widened the gap between wealthier and poorer agricultural households.

---

The benefits, however, were enormous in scale. The population of the world roughly doubled between nineteen sixty and two thousand, from three billion to six billion people — a growth that, without the Green Revolution, would almost certainly have produced catastrophic famines on a scale that dwarfs any single historical famine. Instead, the calories available per person worldwide actually increased over this period. India, which had experienced repeated devastating famines through the nineteenth and early twentieth centuries, has not experienced a major famine since the Green Revolution took hold. The same is true of most of Asia and Latin America. The absolute number of people suffering from undernourishment, while still vast, has declined as a share of a much larger world population.

The challenge that faces the world in the twenty-first century is, in some ways, a continuation of the challenge Borlaug identified in his Nobel speech: how to feed a world population expected to reach nearly ten billion by mid-century, while doing so more sustainably than the original Green Revolution managed. A second Green Revolution is called for by many agricultural scientists — one based on crop varieties that can fix their own nitrogen from the air rather than requiring synthetic fertilisers, that are tolerant to heat, drought, and flooding in a changing climate, and that can maintain high yields with reduced inputs. The tools now available — including the gene-editing technologies of CRISPR and related systems — are different from those Borlaug had, and the urgency, in the context of climate change, is arguably greater. The spirit of the challenge is the same: how do we grow enough food for everyone, without destroying the soil and water that make growing food possible.
