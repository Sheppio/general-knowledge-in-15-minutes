# The History of Electricity: From Amber to the Grid

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Of all the transformations in human history, few have changed daily life as thoroughly as the harnessing of electricity. Before it, the working day ended at dusk or was prolonged by the guttering light of candles and oil lamps; food spoiled quickly; communication over distance was slow; and the energy available to power machines was limited to the burning of wood and coal in furnaces, and the strength of animal and human muscle. After it, the night was illuminated; information could travel at the speed of light; food could be refrigerated and kept; and an almost limitless supply of power became available to drive every kind of machine. The electrification of the world is one of the foundations of modern life, so thoroughly embedded in everything we do that we notice it only when it fails.

The story of electricity begins with observation — the puzzling observation, made since antiquity, that certain materials when rubbed would attract small objects. The ancient Greeks noted this property in amber — a fossilised resin they called elektron — which, when rubbed with cloth, would attract feathers and scraps of papyrus. For two thousand years this phenomenon was a curiosity rather than a subject of systematic inquiry. The first step toward a scientific understanding came in sixteen hundred, when the English physician William Gilbert, physician to Queen Elizabeth I, published De Magnete — a systematic study of magnetism and electricity. Gilbert coined the Latin term electricus to describe materials with amber's attractive property, and became the first person to distinguish clearly between magnetism and what he was beginning to identify as a separate force.

---

Over the following century, experimenters across Europe built machines for generating static electricity and observed its behaviour — the sparks it produced, its ability to travel through conducting materials, the way it built up in Leyden jars (a kind of capacitor, invented in the seventeen-forties). It was the American polymath Benjamin Franklin who made the connection between these laboratory phenomena and lightning. His famous kite experiment of seventeen fifty-two — flying a kite in a thunderstorm with a metal key attached to the string — demonstrated that lightning was electrical in nature. The experiment was genuinely dangerous (it killed at least one experimenter in Europe who tried to replicate it), and Franklin's insight led directly to the invention of the lightning rod — the first practical application of electrical knowledge, protecting buildings from lightning strikes.

Franklin also developed the concept of positive and negative electrical charge, and argued correctly that electricity was not created by friction but was a single fluid that could be transferred between objects. These ideas were important steps toward a theoretical understanding of electricity, but they could not yield practical applications without a reliable source of sustained electrical current rather than the brief discharges of static electricity available to Franklin's generation.

That source arrived in eighteen hundred, when the Italian physicist Alessandro Volta announced the invention of the voltaic pile — the first electric battery. Volta had made a crucial observation: when two different metals were separated by a moist material, a steady electrical current flowed. His pile — alternating discs of zinc and copper separated by brine-soaked cloth — produced a continuous current that could be maintained for extended periods. This was transformative. For the first time, experimenters had a reliable source of electrical current that they could use to study electricity's effects, and the following decades produced a cascade of discoveries that built the foundations of the electrical age.

---

The most important of these came from the Danish physicist Hans Christian Ørsted, who in eighteen twenty discovered that a wire carrying an electric current deflected a magnetic compass needle — establishing for the first time the crucial link between electricity and magnetism. This discovery set off a decade of intense investigation. The French physicist André-Marie Ampère showed that parallel current-carrying wires attracted or repelled each other and developed the mathematical foundations of the new science of electrodynamics. The English physicist and chemist Michael Faraday, working in London, made the discoveries that would most directly shape the electrical age.

Faraday was the son of a blacksmith, largely self-educated, and possessed of an extraordinary intuitive grasp of the physical world. In eighteen twenty-one, he built the first electric motor — a device in which electrical current produced continuous rotary motion through the interaction of a wire with a magnetic field. In eighteen thirty-one, he made his greatest discovery: electromagnetic induction. He found that a changing magnetic field in the vicinity of a conducting wire produced an electrical current in that wire. This was the principle of the electric generator — the means by which mechanical motion could be converted into electrical current. Every power station in the world still operates on this principle. Faraday could not express his discoveries in mathematical form, but James Clerk Maxwell, the Scottish physicist who followed him, did — publishing in eighteen sixty-five the equations that unified electricity and magnetism into a single framework and predicted the existence of electromagnetic waves. These equations are among the greatest achievements in the history of physics.

---

The step from the laboratory to the city was taken by Thomas Edison, an American inventor of extraordinary practical genius who understood that electricity's potential depended on creating not just individual devices but entire systems. Edison developed a practical incandescent electric light bulb — not the first, but the first that worked reliably and long enough to be commercially useful — and demonstrated it publicly in eighteen seventy-nine. But a light bulb was useless without a reliable supply of electricity, so Edison built the systems needed to supply it: the generators, the cables, the meters, and the business model.

On the fourth of September, eighteen eighty-two, Edison's Pearl Street Station in lower Manhattan began supplying electrical power to eighty-five customers in the surrounding area. This was the birth of the electrical utility industry: the first time electricity had been generated centrally and distributed to paying customers through a network. Edison's system used direct current — DC — electricity, which had the disadvantage that it could not be transmitted over long distances without losing most of its power. This meant that generating stations had to be located very close to their customers, which severely limited the scale of the system.

---

The solution to this problem was alternating current — AC — which could be stepped up to very high voltages for long-distance transmission and then stepped down again for household use through the device of the transformer. The Serbian-American inventor Nikola Tesla, who had worked briefly for Edison before the two had a falling-out, developed the AC induction motor and the polyphase AC system that made long-distance electrical transmission practical. In eighteen eighty-eight, the American entrepreneur George Westinghouse bought the patent rights to Tesla's system and began building AC networks to compete with Edison's DC networks.

The resulting "War of the Currents" — a decade of competitive and sometimes acrimonious rivalry between the Edison and Westinghouse camps — was eventually resolved in favour of alternating current on both technical and economic grounds. The decisive demonstration came with the Niagara Falls power station, built by Westinghouse and Tesla and opened in eighteen ninety-five, which transmitted electricity generated at Niagara Falls to the city of Buffalo, twenty-two miles away. It was immediately obvious that long-distance AC transmission could supply electricity to entire regions from a single generating station, and the model of large centralised power stations transmitting AC electricity through a network of cables became the standard that shaped electrical infrastructure for the next century.

---

The electrification of the world that followed was one of the great infrastructure projects in human history. City by city, country by country, national grids were built that brought electricity to homes, factories, and businesses. In the United States, the Rural Electrification Act of nineteen thirty-six brought electricity to farming communities that had been left behind by private utilities unwilling to serve dispersed rural customers. In developing countries, electrification proceeded more slowly, and more than seven hundred million people still lacked access to electricity as recently as twenty-twenty, though the figure has been falling.

Alongside the grid, electricity gave rise to an entirely new world of devices and industries. The telegraph, the telephone, the radio, the television, the computer — all are electrical technologies that transformed communication, entertainment, and information processing. Industrial motors replaced steam engines as the primary means of driving machinery, and the flexibility of electrical power — which could be delivered to any machine in a factory without the elaborate system of shafts and belts needed to distribute mechanical power from a central engine — transformed manufacturing. Refrigeration, electric lighting, electric heating, and electric cooking changed domestic life completely, and the labour-saving devices powered by electricity shifted the nature of household work, particularly for women.

Today, electricity is facing a new transformation: the shift from fossil-fuel-burning power stations to renewable sources — wind and solar — whose intermittency and distributed generation require rethinking the architecture of the grid for the first time in a century. The principle remains the same as Faraday discovered it in eighteen thirty-one: a changing magnetic field induces a current in a conductor. The scale and the stakes are different than anything Faraday could have imagined.
