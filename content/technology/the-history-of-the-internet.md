# The History of the Internet: How We Connected the World

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

The internet is the most consequential invention since the printing press, and it happened so fast that most people alive today can remember a world without it. In barely thirty years, it went from a military research project connecting a handful of university computers to an invisible infrastructure underpinning almost every aspect of modern life — communication, commerce, journalism, entertainment, politics, and the most intimate details of how we spend our time. Understanding how it came to exist is understanding one of the great transformations in human history.

The story begins not with computers but with a military problem. In the late 1950s, at the height of the Cold War, American defence planners were worried about nuclear attack. A centralised communications system had an obvious vulnerability — destroy the central hub and the entire network dies. The answer was a decentralised network with no single point of failure, where information could be automatically routed around damaged sections. The Advanced Research Projects Agency, ARPA, funded research into exactly this kind of system.

On the twenty-ninth of October, 1969, the first message was sent over ARPANET — the network that would eventually become the internet. A team at UCLA attempted to send the word "login" to a computer at Stanford Research Institute. The system crashed after the second letter. The first message ever transmitted over the internet was "lo". A few minutes later, the bug was fixed and the full word went through. The internet had begun with an accident and an anticlimax — which, in retrospect, seems about right.

---

Through the 1970s, ARPANET grew slowly. A crucial development came in 1974, when the computer scientists Vint Cerf and Bob Kahn published a paper describing TCP/IP — the Transmission Control Protocol and Internet Protocol — the common language that allowed different networks to communicate with each other. TCP/IP is the foundational architecture of the internet as it exists today. Any device connected to it, anywhere in the world, uses this protocol.

By the 1980s, the network was growing and evolving. Email had become the killer application — the thing that made connecting to the network obviously worthwhile. Universities, research institutions, and eventually businesses began connecting. But the internet was still text-based, technical, and inaccessible to ordinary people. Using it required knowing a set of arcane commands. There was no way to navigate it visually, no addresses a human being could easily remember, no pictures.

---

That changed with one invention, made by one person, at the end of the 1980s. Tim Berners-Lee, a British scientist working at the CERN particle physics laboratory in Geneva, proposed a system for sharing information over the internet that anyone could use. He called it the World Wide Web. It used hyperlinks — clickable connections between documents — and a simple addressing system to let anyone navigate from one document to another anywhere on the network. He put his proposal to CERN management in 1989. The first website went live on the sixth of August, 1991. Berners-Lee deliberately chose not to patent the Web. He gave it away, free, to the world.

The Web made the internet navigable. But it was still mostly text, displayed in simple browsers that looked like typewritten pages. The breakthrough that brought it to mass audiences came in January 1993, when a team at the University of Illinois released Mosaic — the first web browser to display images alongside text. Mosaic made the Web look like a magazine. Within months, millions of people were using it. The dot-com boom of the 1990s followed, as investors poured money into internet companies with the conviction that this technology would transform commerce and communication. Many of those companies failed spectacularly in the dot-com crash of 2000. But the technology itself continued to evolve rapidly.

Google was founded in 1998 by two Stanford doctoral students, Larry Page and Sergey Brin, who had developed a way of ranking web pages by the number and quality of other pages that linked to them. In a web growing too large and chaotic to navigate without help, a search engine that actually found what you were looking for was transformative. Google became the doorway through which most people experienced the internet — and eventually one of the most valuable companies in history.

---

The next transformation came with broadband connections, smartphones, and social media. Facebook, founded in 2004 by Mark Zuckerberg at Harvard University, grew from a campus social network to a global platform connecting billions of people. YouTube launched in 2005, making video sharing available to anyone with a camera. Twitter launched in 2006. The first iPhone, released by Apple in 2007, put a full internet connection in everyone's pocket. The internet stopped being somewhere you went — it became the environment you lived in.

These changes happened faster than society could adapt. The internet had been designed for openness, for the free flow of information, and for the good faith of its users. It was not designed for mass surveillance, for the weaponisation of misinformation, or for the concentration of enormous power in the hands of a handful of corporations. All of these things happened anyway.

The platforms that emerged — Google, Facebook, Amazon, Apple — became dominant in ways that surprised even their founders. They accumulated data about billions of people, refined their ability to predict and shape behaviour, and built business models based on capturing and holding human attention. The advertising-funded internet that resulted was extraordinarily good at delivering free services. It was also extraordinarily good at spreading outrage, division, and false information, because those things turned out to be excellent at holding attention.

---

The internet also democratised access to information and gave a voice to people who had previously been shut out of public discourse. It enabled new forms of creativity, commerce, and community. It allowed dissidents in authoritarian countries to organise and communicate. It let scientists share research instantly across the world. It built entirely new industries while destroying old ones — in journalism, music, retail, and elsewhere — in ways that are still playing out.

The internet was not built by a single genius or a single company. It emerged from decades of collaborative research, government investment, and the work of thousands of engineers and scientists who often shared their work freely. Its foundational architecture — open, decentralised, built on common protocols — was a deliberate choice. Whether that openness can survive the commercial and political pressures it now faces is one of the defining questions of our time.

We are, in a real sense, still in the early days of understanding what the internet is and what it will become. Every generation since 1991 has found that the previous generation's version of the internet was a pale preview of what came next. There is no reason to think that process has ended.
