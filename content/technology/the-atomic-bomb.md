# The Atomic Bomb: From Theory to Trinity

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

On the sixteenth of July, 1945, at five twenty-nine in the morning, in the desert of New Mexico, the world changed. A blinding flash of light expanded outward from a steel tower in the Jornada del Muerto — "Journey of the Dead Man" — desert, followed by a fireball, followed by the now-iconic cloud rising in the shape of a mushroom. The Trinity test, the first detonation of a nuclear weapon, lasted a fraction of a second. The age it inaugurated has not ended.

J. Robert Oppenheimer, the physicist who directed the laboratory that built the bomb and watched the explosion from a bunker ten kilometres away, recalled that a verse from Hindu scripture came to him: "Now I am become Death, the destroyer of worlds." It was not a boast but a recognition of what had been unleashed. Within three weeks, two atomic bombs had been dropped on Japanese cities, killing more than two hundred thousand people by the end of 1945. The Second World War ended. And the world entered a new era in which the destruction of human civilisation became, for the first time, technically feasible.

---

The physics underlying the atomic bomb was understood only in the late 1930s. The key concept was nuclear fission: the discovery that certain heavy atoms — particularly uranium-235 and plutonium-239 — could be split when struck by neutrons, releasing enormous amounts of energy and more neutrons, which in turn could split more atoms, creating a self-sustaining chain reaction. The energy released came from the conversion of a small amount of mass into energy, in accordance with Einstein's equation E equals mc squared — where c, the speed of light, is so enormous that even a tiny loss of mass produces a vast release of energy.

The discovery of fission was made in December 1938 by the German physicists Otto Hahn and Fritz Strassmann, and was correctly interpreted by Lise Meitner and her nephew Otto Frisch — Meitner, who was Jewish, had recently fled Nazi Germany for Sweden. The news spread rapidly through the international physics community, and it was immediately apparent to many of those who heard it that a bomb of unprecedented destructive power might be buildable. That knowledge was especially alarming to the European physicists — many of them Jewish refugees from Hitler's Germany — who had settled in Britain and the United States. They knew, or feared, that German scientists were working toward the same goal.

---

In the summer of 1939, the Hungarian-American physicist Leo Szilard drafted a letter to President Franklin D. Roosevelt, warning of the danger and urging that the United States begin its own nuclear research programme. He persuaded Albert Einstein to sign it. The Einstein-Szilard letter, signed on the second of August, 1939, and delivered to Roosevelt on the eleventh of October, was one of the most consequential letters in history. It set in motion a chain of events that led to the creation of the Manhattan Project.

The Manhattan Project — formally the Manhattan Engineer District — was the American-led programme to develop nuclear weapons, carried out in secret during the Second World War at a total cost of roughly two billion dollars, equivalent to over thirty billion in contemporary terms. It employed more than a hundred thousand people at sites across the United States and Canada, most of whom had no idea what the programme was for. Its three main sites were Oak Ridge, Tennessee, where uranium was enriched; Hanford, Washington, where plutonium was produced; and Los Alamos, New Mexico, where the weapons were designed and built.

Los Alamos was run by J. Robert Oppenheimer, a theoretical physicist from the University of California, Berkeley, who assembled an extraordinary collection of scientific talent — including Enrico Fermi, Hans Bethe, Richard Feynman, and Niels Bohr, the last travelling under the assumed name Nicholas Baker. Despite having signed the letter that helped launch the project, Einstein himself was not given security clearance to work on it. The scientists worked under military secrecy so absolute that Los Alamos did not officially exist; its address was a post office box in Santa Fe.

Two different bomb designs were developed in parallel. The first, known as Little Boy, was a gun-type device using uranium-235: one piece of enriched uranium was fired down a barrel into another, reaching critical mass and triggering a chain reaction. It was never tested before use — the designers were confident it would work. The second, known as Fat Man, used plutonium and an implosion design: conventional explosives arranged in a sphere detonated simultaneously, compressing a plutonium core to critical mass. This was the design tested at Trinity.

---

The Trinity test, on the morning of the sixteenth of July, 1945, was the culmination of years of work by thousands of people. The device sat atop a hundred-foot steel tower in the New Mexico desert. The explosion had a yield equivalent to roughly twenty-one kilotons of TNT — greater than the most optimistic predictions. The flash was visible from more than three hundred kilometres away. The heat at the point of detonation fused the desert sand into a glassy substance later named trinitite. Those present — scientists and military officers watching from bunkers — described a mixture of awe, relief, and dread.

The bomb had been built in the context of a war against Japan that showed no sign of ending. Allied forces had been fighting their way across the Pacific for years, suffering enormous casualties. The projected cost of an invasion of the Japanese home islands — Operation Downfall — was estimated in the hundreds of thousands of Allied lives, and potentially millions of Japanese lives. President Harry Truman, who had learned of the atomic bomb's existence only after Roosevelt's death in April 1945, authorised its use.

Little Boy was dropped on Hiroshima on the morning of the sixth of August, 1945, from a B-29 bomber called the Enola Gay, at eight fifteen in the morning. The bomb detonated approximately five hundred and eighty metres above the city centre, creating a fireball, a pressure wave, and a firestorm that destroyed roughly five square miles of the city. Estimates of immediate deaths range from sixty to eighty thousand; by the end of 1945, deaths from blast, burns, and radiation sickness had brought the total to between ninety thousand and one hundred and sixty-six thousand.

Three days later, on the ninth of August, Fat Man was dropped on Nagasaki from the bomber Bockscar, at eleven o'clock in the morning. The hilly terrain of Nagasaki confined the blast somewhat, limiting casualties compared to Hiroshima — but still killing between forty thousand and eighty thousand people by the end of 1945. Japan announced its surrender on the fifteenth of August. The formal surrender was signed on the second of September aboard the USS Missouri. The Second World War was over.

---

The debates about the bombing have never settled. Those who defend the decision argue that the alternative — an invasion of Japan — would have cost far more lives on both sides, and that the shock of the bombs brought about a surrender that months of conventional bombing had not achieved. Those who question it argue that Japan was already close to surrender, that the Soviet declaration of war against Japan on the eighth of August may have been more decisive, that targeting civilian cities was a moral line that should not have been crossed, and that the second bomb, three days after the first, gave Japan no time to respond.

What is not disputed is the effect on the world that followed. The Soviet Union detonated its first nuclear weapon in 1949, years earlier than American intelligence had expected. By the early nineteen-fifties, both superpowers had thermonuclear weapons — hydrogen bombs, which used a fission bomb as a trigger to ignite a fusion reaction, with yields measured not in kilotons but in megatons. The logic of Mutual Assured Destruction — each side knowing that any nuclear exchange would annihilate both — became the uneasy foundation of Cold War stability. The Cuban Missile Crisis of 1962, when the United States and Soviet Union came closer than at any other moment to nuclear war, demonstrated how fragile that stability could be.

Nuclear weapons spread: Britain, France, China, Israel, India, Pakistan, and North Korea all acquired them. The world built enough nuclear warheads to destroy every city on Earth many times over. Treaties like the Non-Proliferation Treaty of 1968 and successive arms control agreements reduced the numbers somewhat but did not eliminate the weapons or the danger. Today there are estimated to be approximately twelve thousand nuclear warheads in the world, and the possibility that they might one day be used — by accident, miscalculation, or intent — remains the most existential risk facing humanity.

The story that began with Einstein's equations and ended in the New Mexico desert has not finished being told. The age of the atomic bomb is still the age we live in.
