# The History of Mental Health: From Chains to Chemicals to Community

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Mental illness has existed as long as humanity has. The ancient Egyptians described what appears to be clinical depression in the Ebers Papyrus, one of the oldest surviving medical texts. Ancient Mesopotamian records describe states of despair and confusion that we would recognise as psychosis or profound depression. The suffering itself is not new. What has changed, across the centuries, is the story that different societies have told about it: whether it is a medical condition, a spiritual crisis, a moral failing, a social problem, or some combination of these; whether the people who experience it deserve treatment, containment, punishment, or compassion; and what, if anything, can be done to relieve it.

The history of mental health is not a simple story of progress from ignorance to understanding. It contains genuine advances — in the recognition of mental illness as illness rather than sin or demon possession, in the development of treatments that actually work, in the gradual extension of rights and dignity to people whose suffering had previously made them invisible or contemptible. But it also contains episodes of extraordinary cruelty: people chained in dungeons, subjected to surgical mutilation, sterilised against their will, warehoused in vast institutions that abandoned any pretence of treatment. The gap between what society said it believed about mental illness and how it treated those who suffered from it has rarely been small, and it is not small today.

---

The ancient Greeks made the first systematic attempts to understand mental disturbance in naturalistic rather than purely supernatural terms. Hippocrates, the physician who lived in the fourth and fifth centuries before the common era, proposed that all illness — including what we would call mental illness — arose from imbalances in the body's four humours: blood, phlegm, yellow bile, and black bile. Excessive black bile, in this framework, produced melancholia — what we would call depression — a term derived from the Greek words for black and bile. This was not a dismissal of mental suffering but an attempt to explain it physiologically, to bring it within the domain of medicine rather than religion. Hippocrates distinguished between epilepsy, mania, paranoia, and melancholia, and argued that the brain — not the heart or the liver — was the organ responsible for thought and feeling.

The humoral theory was wrong in its specifics but right in its general orientation: that mental illness is a matter of the body and the brain, not of demons or divine punishment. This insight had to be rediscovered repeatedly, because it was never consistently applied. The Roman physician Galen elaborated the humoral system in the second century of the common era, and his ideas dominated European medicine for over a thousand years. Throughout the medieval period, spiritual and physiological explanations for mental disturbance coexisted uneasily: the same individual might be understood as suffering from melancholia (a medical condition that could be treated with diet, rest, and herbs) or as demonically possessed (a spiritual condition requiring exorcism) or as engaging in heretical behaviour (a moral condition that might require punishment). The distinction between these categories was not sharp, and the same person could be subjected to different treatments simultaneously.

---

The treatment of the severely mentally ill — those who posed a danger to themselves or others or whose behaviour made them unable to function in their communities — was for most of history a matter of confinement. Families, where they could, kept disturbed relatives at home. Where they could not, the alternatives were grim. In England, Bethlem Royal Hospital — the institution whose name gave the English language the word "bedlam" — was founded in London in twelve forty-seven as a priory and began admitting mentally ill patients in the late fourteenth century. By the seventeenth and eighteenth centuries it had become a place of spectacular misery, where chained and distressed patients were displayed to paying visitors as a form of entertainment. The sight of the inmates was considered, by contemporary standards, instructive and amusing.

Similar institutions existed across Europe. In sixteenth- and seventeenth-century Spain, hospitals for the mentally ill were actually relatively progressive by the standards of the time, offering some degree of structured care. But the dominant model, almost everywhere, was custodial containment — locking people away to protect society from them — rather than any systematic attempt at treatment or recovery.

The intellectual framework that would eventually challenge this approach was developed during the Enlightenment, with its insistence on reason, humanity, and the reform of institutions. In France, the physician Philippe Pinel became the emblematic figure of mental health reform. In seventeen ninety-two, he was appointed chief physician at the Bicêtre, the Paris asylum for men, and he instituted a practice for which he became famous: he ordered the chains removed from patients who had been shackled, some of them for decades. In seventeen ninety-four he did the same at the Salpêtrière, the asylum for women. Pinel's argument was straightforward: the mentally ill were not criminals or monsters; they were sick people whose condition might be improved by humane treatment, fresh air, structured activity, and the elimination of unnecessary suffering. He called his approach "moral treatment" — not moral in the sense of ethical instruction, but in the older sense of psychological, as opposed to purely physical, care.

---

The moral treatment movement spread through the nineteenth century, accompanied by a building programme on an enormous scale. Across Europe and North America, governments and reformers constructed asylums — purpose-built institutions that were intended to be places of therapeutic retreat, ordered and calm, where the mentally ill could recover away from the stresses of the outside world. In the United States, the activist Dorothea Dix led a remarkable campaign in the eighteen forties and eighteen fifties to document the conditions in which mentally ill people were being held in prisons and poorhouses across the country, and to persuade state legislatures to build proper asylums. Her work led to the construction of more than thirty state mental hospitals.

The therapeutic ambitions that justified the asylum movement were not entirely dishonest. In small institutions run by committed physicians, some patients did recover, and moral treatment produced results that were often better than anything else available. But the asylum movement ran into fundamental problems as the nineteenth century progressed. The institutions grew — as more and more people were committed, often involuntarily, often for conditions that we would not now regard as mental illness — and as they grew, the ratio of patients to staff made any serious therapeutic engagement impossible. By the end of the century, the large asylum was no longer a therapeutic retreat but a warehouse: a place to keep people deemed unable to manage in society, offering little beyond basic custodial care and the opportunity to perform institutional labour.

---

The late nineteenth and early twentieth centuries brought two very different kinds of response to the failures of the asylum system. The first was the turn toward biological psychiatry — the attempt to find physical explanations for mental illness and physical treatments for it. The German psychiatrist Emil Kraepelin, working in the eighteen-eighties and eighteen-nineties, developed a systematic approach to psychiatric diagnosis that emphasised the classification of mental illnesses into discrete categories based on observable symptoms and long-term outcomes, rather than the vague constitutional theories of his predecessors. His distinction between "dementia praecox" (what we would now call schizophrenia) and manic-depressive illness laid the foundations of modern psychiatric classification.

The physical treatments of this era were often desperate and sometimes horrifying. Surgical procedures were performed on the theory that infections or physical abnormalities elsewhere in the body were causing mental illness. Fever therapy — deliberately inducing high temperatures to cure psychosis — was briefly used and actually worked for neurosyphilis, one of the few cases where there was a genuine physical cause. Insulin coma therapy, introduced in the nineteen-thirties, involved inducing comas through large doses of insulin; it was widely used for decades before being shown to be ineffective. The lobotomy — severing connections in the frontal lobe of the brain — was introduced by the Portuguese physician António Egas Moniz in the nineteen-thirties and won him the Nobel Prize in Medicine in nineteen forty-nine; it is now recognised as a procedure that caused severe permanent damage without clear therapeutic benefit.

The second response was Sigmund Freud's psychoanalysis — the theory and practice we have discussed in another script — which offered a very different model of mental illness as rooted in unconscious conflicts that could be addressed through talking. The talking therapies that developed from and alongside psychoanalysis — including cognitive-behavioural therapy, developed in the nineteen-sixties and seventies by Aaron Beck and others — have proved to be among the most effective treatments for depression and anxiety disorders, with evidence bases that now rival those for medication.

---

The real transformation of psychiatric treatment came in the nineteen-fifties, with the discovery of effective drug treatments. Chlorpromazine, the first antipsychotic medication, was synthesised in nineteen fifty and first administered to a patient on the nineteenth of January, nineteen fifty-two, at a hospital in Paris. The patient — a twenty-four-year-old man in an acute manic state — responded dramatically and was discharged after three weeks. Subsequent trials showed that chlorpromazine could substantially reduce the positive symptoms of psychosis — hallucinations, delusions, agitated behaviour — in a way that no previous treatment had reliably achieved. The first mood-stabilising drug, lithium, was shown to be effective for mania in nineteen forty-nine by the Australian psychiatrist John Cade. The first modern antidepressants — imipramine and the MAO inhibitors — were discovered in the mid-nineteen-fifties, largely by accident.

These drugs did not cure mental illness. They managed symptoms, often with significant side effects, and they worked better for some conditions than others. But they made it possible to consider something that had seemed inconceivable in the era of the great asylum: that people with serious mental illness might be able to live in the community rather than in institutions. Combined with the ideological critiques of asylum psychiatry that were developing in the same period — from the sociologist Erving Goffman, who in nineteen sixty-one published Asylums, a devastating account of what large psychiatric institutions actually did to their inhabitants, and from anti-psychiatry thinkers like R. D. Laing, who challenged the entire framework of psychiatric diagnosis — the new drugs provided the practical underpinning for deinstitutionalisation.

---

From the nineteen-sixties onward, the psychiatric hospital populations of most Western countries fell dramatically, as patients were discharged into "community care." The ambitions of deinstitutionalisation were genuine: to return people to their families and communities, to treat them as citizens rather than as inmates, to provide support in less restrictive and more humane settings. The reality, in many countries, was more troubling. The community services that were supposed to replace the hospitals were often inadequately funded, poorly organised, and incapable of providing the level of support that people with serious mental illness needed. The result, in many cities, was that people who had previously been confined in institutions ended up homeless, imprisoned, or rotating through emergency rooms in acute crisis.

The story of mental health is therefore still unfinished. Treatments have improved enormously. The stigma surrounding mental illness, while still real and damaging, has reduced significantly, particularly in younger generations. The neuroscience of mental disorders is advancing, though the complexity of the brain means that understanding remains incomplete and treatments remain largely empirical. What has not changed is the fundamental tension at the heart of the problem: between the need to provide care for people whose suffering may make them unable to care for themselves, and the imperative to respect the autonomy and dignity of people who are, above all, people.
