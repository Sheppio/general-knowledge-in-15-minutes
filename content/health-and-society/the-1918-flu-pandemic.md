# The 1918 Influenza Pandemic: The Forgotten Catastrophe

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

In the autumn of 1918, with the First World War entering its final weeks, a second catastrophe arrived. Soldiers on every front began falling ill with an influenza unlike anything anyone had seen before. Unlike ordinary flu, which kills mainly the very old and very young, this strain struck with particular savagery at healthy adults in their twenties and thirties — the very people filling the trenches. Men who seemed well in the morning were dead by nightfall. The symptoms were hideous: extreme fever, delirium, and in the worst cases lungs that filled with fluid until the patient essentially drowned. A bluish tinge spreading across the skin told doctors the blood was failing to carry enough oxygen. There was nothing they could do.

The pandemic known as the Spanish Flu was the deadliest in recorded history. Estimates of its death toll vary enormously, but most assessments place the dead at between fifty and one hundred million people worldwide — more than the entire First World War. It infected an estimated five hundred million people, roughly a third of the world's population at the time. In the space of less than two years, it killed more human beings than any other single event since the Black Death of the fourteenth century.

The disease did not originate in Spain. It earned that name because Spain, neutral in the war and free from wartime censorship, reported openly on the outbreak while the belligerent nations suppressed information to avoid damaging morale. In a grotesque irony, transparency about the epidemic made Spain appear to be its epicentre.

---

The first documented cases appeared in March 1918 at Camp Funston, a military training base in Kansas, where an army cook named Albert Gitchell reported sick on the fourth of March, quickly followed by hundreds of his fellow soldiers. Within weeks, the virus had spread along the military transport routes to Europe, travelling on the troopships and supply convoys crossing the Atlantic in enormous numbers. By April it had reached the Western Front. Through the spring of 1918, the first wave moved through the armies and the cities of the world — alarming but not catastrophic, with mortality rates not far above those of a normal flu season.

The second wave, which struck in the autumn of 1918, was entirely different. Influenza strains mutate rapidly, and the virus that returned in September was dramatically more lethal than the one that had appeared in spring. It swept through military camps and cities in a matter of weeks. Philadelphia, which had ignored warnings and allowed a large public parade in late September, saw its death toll spike so rapidly that bodies piled up faster than coffins could be made or graves could be dug. Volunteers dragged the dead from homes in wagons. Churches and schools were converted into emergency wards.

---

What made this strain so lethal was partly a phenomenon that scientists would later call a cytokine storm — the body's immune system responding to the infection with such extreme force that it destroyed the patient's own lung tissue. This explains one of the pandemic's most baffling features: why people with the strongest immune systems — young adults aged twenty to forty — were sometimes the most vulnerable. Their own defences were killing them.

The conditions of the First World War made everything catastrophically worse. Soldiers crammed into trenches, transported in crowded ships and trains, sleeping in packed barracks — all of these created ideal conditions for the virus to spread. Wartime malnutrition and physical exhaustion lowered resistance. And the censorship that prevented accurate reporting of the outbreak also prevented effective public health responses. Governments that could not acknowledge the scale of the crisis could not organise against it.

The pandemic reached every corner of the world. India suffered particularly badly, losing an estimated twelve million or more people — a catastrophe compounded by wartime disruption and colonial indifference. The Pacific Islands, Alaska, South Africa, and communities across sub-Saharan Africa were devastated. Some remote indigenous communities lost a majority of their population within weeks.

---

Medical science in 1918 could do almost nothing. Influenza was not yet understood to be caused by a virus — the concept of viruses as a distinct category of pathogen was only recently established, and the specific influenza virus would not be isolated until 1933. Doctors and nurses could offer only supportive care: rest, fluids, aspirin. Many of those who died did not succumb to the flu itself but to secondary bacterial pneumonia — pneumonia for which there was no antibiotic treatment, antibiotics not yet existing. Some historians believe that aspirin, prescribed in doses we now know were toxic, contributed to deaths.

The pandemic moved in three waves. The relatively mild first wave hit in spring 1918. The devastating second wave struck in autumn 1918. A third wave, severe but less lethal, rolled through the winter of 1918 and into 1919. Then, as mysteriously as it had arrived, the pandemic faded. Either most susceptible people had already been infected and died or developed immunity, or the virus had mutated to a less lethal form, or — most likely — some combination of both.

---

The world moved on with remarkable speed, perhaps too traumatised by the combined weight of war and pandemic to properly absorb either. The 1918 flu was largely absent from public memory for decades. Unlike the war, it left no monuments, no designated days of remembrance, no literature to rival the poetry of the trenches. It happened to so many, so fast, and with so little that anyone could do about it, that silence became the only response.

It returned to consciousness in the twenty-first century as scientists sequenced its genome from the preserved lungs of victims frozen in Alaskan permafrost, confirming it as an influenza A H1N1 virus of avian origin. The knowledge gained from studying it shaped the response to subsequent pandemics. When COVID-19 emerged in 2020, policymakers and scientists reached back explicitly to the lessons of 1918 — about the dangers of suppressing information, about the critical importance of social distancing, about the catastrophic cost of holding public gatherings during an epidemic.

The lesson the 1918 pandemic offers is stark and has not grown less relevant. A novel pathogen, combined with a densely connected world and inadequate public health infrastructure, can kill more people in months than any war. The virus does not distinguish between combatants and civilians, the powerful and the powerless. It is indifferent to borders and impervious to nationalism. The only things that work against it are knowledge, transparency, and collective action — exactly the things that the world of 1918 struggled most to provide.
