# The History of Democracy: Government by the People

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

Democracy is one of those words so familiar it can seem to require no explanation. Everyone knows what it means: the people choose their government. But this apparently simple idea has a complex and contested history stretching back two and a half thousand years, and the version we practise today — where all adult citizens vote to elect representatives — is a surprisingly recent invention. For most of human history, in most places, rule by the people was considered either impossible or dangerous. The story of democracy is the story of how that judgement was slowly overturned, not through the calm acceptance of an obviously good idea, but through revolutions, wars, and centuries of political struggle.

The word itself comes from the ancient Greeks. Demokratia — literally, "rule by the people" — was coined in Athens in the fifth century BC to describe a new system of government that was unlike anything the ancient world had seen. Its roots lay in the reforms of Solon, an Athenian statesman appointed around five hundred and ninety-four BC to resolve a crisis of debt and popular unrest. Solon cancelled the debts of the poorest citizens, abolished debt slavery, and opened the citizen assembly to all free men regardless of wealth. He did not create democracy, but he laid its foundations by establishing the principle that ordinary citizens had a legitimate role in political life.

---

It was Cleisthenes, a generation later, who is credited as the father of Athenian democracy. Around five hundred and eight BC, following a period of tyranny, Cleisthenes reorganised the Athenian citizen body into ten tribes based on geography rather than family, each tribe sending representatives to a new council. He extended the principle of equal rights — isonomia — to all male citizens. For the first time, a significant body of ordinary men had a genuine say in how their city was governed. The assembly, the Ekklesia, met on a hillside called the Pnyx and debated and voted on matters of war, law, and public policy. Not everyone in Athens was a citizen — women, enslaved people, and foreign residents were excluded — but within the citizen body, the principle of political equality was real and genuinely radical.

Under Pericles, in the golden age of the fifth century — roughly from four hundred and sixty-one to four hundred and twenty-nine BC — Athenian democracy reached its fullest development. Pericles introduced pay for jury duty and public office, which meant that poorer citizens could actually afford to participate. The Parthenon was built during his leadership, a monument in marble to a city that believed its political freedom and its cultural achievement were connected. Athenian democracy was direct rather than representative: citizens voted on laws themselves, rather than electing others to vote on their behalf. But the scale of the city made this possible in a way it could never be for a large modern state.

Athenian democracy was fragile and short-lived. The disastrous Peloponnesian War against Sparta led to its temporary suspension. The philosopher Plato, horrified by the execution of his mentor Socrates by a democratic vote, became one of democracy's most searching critics: the people, he argued, were easily manipulated by demagogues, prone to passion rather than reason, and incapable of the wise long-term governance that good government required. His pupil Aristotle was more ambivalent, seeing democracy as a corrupted form of government but acknowledging its practical merits in certain circumstances. These ancient debates — about whether the majority can be trusted, about the relationship between freedom and order, about the competence of ordinary people to govern — have never gone away.

---

The Roman Republic offered a different model: a mixed constitution in which popular assemblies, an elected senate of aristocrats, and powerful executive magistrates called consuls all played a role. It was not democracy in the Athenian sense — power was heavily weighted toward the wealthy — but it established the idea of elected government, of legal accountability, and of the republic — the res publica, the public thing — as a form of collective political life. When the republic collapsed in the first century BC and the emperors took over, the institutions survived in name but not in substance. Democracy, such as it had existed, disappeared from the Western world for more than a thousand years.

Through the medieval period, Europe was governed by a mixture of monarchy, aristocracy, and church authority. The concept of popular government survived mainly as a theoretical memory in classical texts. But within this period, the seeds of something new were being planted. In England in 1215, King John was forced by his barons to sign Magna Carta — a document that placed limits on royal power and established certain legal rights for the King's subjects. It was not democracy; it was primarily a protection for aristocratic privilege. But the principle that the ruler was bound by law rather than above it was genuinely significant, and it provided a foundation on which later reformers would build.

In 1265, the first Parliament met in England, including not just nobles and clergy but knights and burgesses from the towns. Over the following centuries, Parliament grew in power, in scope, and in the habits of representation and debate that would eventually make democracy possible. The argument between Crown and Parliament that dominated English politics through the seventeenth century — a conflict that produced a civil war, the execution of a king, a republic, and eventually the Glorious Revolution of 1688 — was in one sense a very English argument about constitutional law. But it produced ideas with universal reach: that government required the consent of the governed, that individual rights existed prior to and independent of the state, that no monarch could rule without parliamentary approval.

---

John Locke, writing in the aftermath of the Glorious Revolution, gave these ideas their most influential philosophical expression. His argument that all human beings possess natural rights to life, liberty, and property, and that government's sole legitimate purpose is to protect those rights with the consent of the governed, became the intellectual foundation of both the American and French revolutions. When Thomas Jefferson wrote that all men are created equal and endowed with inalienable rights, and that government derives its just powers from the consent of the governed, he was translating Locke into the language of a revolution.

The American Declaration of Independence of 1776 and the Constitution of 1787 created the first large-scale republic in the modern world — though it was a democracy with enormous exclusions. Enslaved people were not free. Women could not vote. The propertyless poor had limited political voice. The gap between the universal language of the founding documents and the reality of American political life was enormous, and the history of American democracy is largely the story of the long, painful effort to close it.

The French Revolution of 1789 injected a more radical energy into the democratic idea. The Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen proclaimed the sovereignty of the nation and the equality of all citizens before the law. The revolution also produced the Terror, dictatorship, and eventually Napoleon — demonstrating that the road from monarchy to democracy was neither straight nor smooth.

---

Through the nineteenth century, democracy spread slowly and unevenly across Europe and the Americas. The key battles were over who counted as a citizen entitled to vote. In Britain, the Reform Act of 1832 began a gradual process of expanding the electorate. But the most fundamental exclusion — of women — was not addressed until the twentieth century. British women over thirty gained the vote through the Representation of the People Act of 1918, with full equal suffrage coming in 1928. In the United States, women's suffrage was established nationally by the Nineteenth Amendment, ratified in August 1920.

The twentieth century put democracy under its most severe test. Fascism in Italy and Germany, and communism in the Soviet Union and China, offered alternative political visions that explicitly rejected the liberal democratic model as weak, decadent, and doomed. The Second World War was in large part a war about which of these visions would prevail. The victory of the democracies in 1945 — at enormous cost — was followed by the rapid spread of democratic institutions to many parts of the world, and by the adoption of the Universal Declaration of Human Rights in 1948, which for the first time set out a global framework of rights that democratic governance was meant to protect.

The Cold War produced a world in which democracy was claimed as a value by Western powers even as they supported authoritarian regimes when it suited their interests. The wave of democratisation that followed the collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 appeared to herald democracy's ultimate triumph. Francis Fukuyama famously declared the "end of history" — by which he meant that liberal democracy had defeated all rival political ideologies and would now spread universally.

That prediction has not been borne out. The early twenty-first century has seen democratic backsliding in many countries, the rise of authoritarian populism even within established democracies, and growing public disillusionment with democratic institutions. The challenges Plato identified — the manipulability of the public, the appeal of demagogues, the difficulty of making collective decisions wisely — have not dissolved. Democracy, it turns out, is not a destination at which societies arrive and then remain. It is a practice that must be maintained, renewed, and defended in every generation.
