# The HIV/AIDS Crisis: A Modern Plague

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,350

---

In the early nineteen-eighties, doctors at hospitals in Los Angeles, New York City, and San Francisco began seeing young men — most of them gay, most of them previously healthy — dying of diseases that should not have been able to kill them. Pneumocystis pneumonia, a lung infection rarely seen outside patients with severely compromised immune systems. Kaposi sarcoma, a cancer so rare it was known mainly in elderly Mediterranean men. These were opportunistic infections: illnesses that the healthy human immune system suppresses without difficulty, but that flourish when the immune system breaks down. Something was destroying the immune systems of these young men, and no one knew what.

The US Centers for Disease Control and Prevention published its first report on these cases in June 1981. It was a brief notice, five cases, framed as a curiosity. Within months it was a crisis. By the end of 1981, there were hundreds of cases and more than a hundred deaths. By the end of 1982, there were thousands. The disease received the formal name AIDS — Acquired Immunodeficiency Syndrome — in 1982, and it was killing people in ways that terrified those who saw it. You did not simply die of AIDS; you died piece by piece as your immune system failed, your body prey to one infection after another, each one more unusual and more difficult to treat than the last.

---

The early years of the AIDS crisis were shaped by a catastrophic failure of political will. The disease appeared first, and most visibly, among gay men in America, and in a country where homosexuality was still widely stigmatised — where sodomy laws remained on the books in many states, where evangelical Christianity had enormous political influence, and where the Reagan administration was disinclined to discuss sex publicly at all — official silence was almost complete. President Ronald Reagan did not publicly mention the word "AIDS" until 1985, by which point more than five thousand Americans had died. He did not make a major public speech on the subject until 1987, by which point the dead numbered in the tens of thousands. The failure was not Reagan's alone; it was systemic. The US government spent far less on AIDS research in the early nineteen-eighties than the scale of the epidemic warranted, and far less than would have been spent had the disease appeared first in a more politically powerful community.

In the absence of official action, affected communities organised themselves. The Gay Men's Health Crisis, founded in New York in 1982, was one of the first organisations to provide support and advocacy for people with AIDS. ACT UP — the AIDS Coalition to Unleash Power — founded in 1987, adopted a more confrontational style, staging protests at the Food and Drug Administration, the National Institutes of Health, and the New York Stock Exchange to demand faster drug approval, more research funding, and public acknowledgement of the crisis. Their tactics, which included civil disobedience and deliberate media disruption, were instrumental in changing how the US government responded to the epidemic. The activist model they developed — patients demanding a role in the research process rather than passively waiting for science to save them — changed medical research broadly, not only for AIDS.

---

The scientific response, though politically impeded, moved with remarkable speed. In 1983, researchers at the Pasteur Institute in Paris — Luc Montagnier and Françoise Barré-Sinoussi, who would share the Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine in 2008 — isolated a retrovirus they identified as the likely cause of AIDS. It was named human immunodeficiency virus, HIV. The virus specifically targeted and destroyed the CD4+ T cells that coordinate the body's immune response. As HIV replicated, the immune system progressively failed, leaving the body vulnerable to the opportunistic infections and cancers that killed AIDS patients.

The question of where HIV had come from was also eventually answered, though the answer took years to establish. HIV is a zoonosis — a disease that jumped from other animals to humans. Its closest relatives are simian immunodeficiency viruses carried by chimpanzees and other primates in central Africa; the virus crossed into humans, most likely through contact with infected blood during hunting or bushmeat preparation, probably in the early twentieth century. Genetic analysis suggests that HIV-1 Group M — the strain responsible for the global pandemic — may have originated in or around Kinshasa, in what is now the Democratic Republic of Congo, around 1920. From there it spread slowly at first, then faster as urbanisation, travel, and the use of unsterilised needles in medical programmes helped it move. By the time the first cases were identified in 1981, HIV had already been circulating globally for decades.

The scale of the epidemic was also reshaping public understanding of how it spread. HIV is transmitted through blood, breast milk, and sexual fluids — not through casual contact. It could not be spread by sharing a glass, by a handshake, or by proximity. But in the fear of the early years, rational transmission facts were frequently overwhelmed by panic and stigma, and the suffering of people with AIDS was compounded by social rejection, discrimination in employment and housing, and in some cases abandonment by their own families.

---

The first drug approved to treat HIV was AZT — zidovudine — approved by the US Food and Drug Administration on the twentieth of March, 1987. It was the fastest drug approval in FDA history to that point, the result of intense pressure from activists and the urgency of the crisis. AZT worked by inhibiting the enzyme HIV uses to copy itself, slowing the virus's replication. But used alone, it was not sufficient: HIV mutated rapidly, and drug resistance emerged quickly. Early enthusiasm for AZT gave way to a more complicated picture.

The transformation in treatment came in 1996, with the development of highly active antiretroviral therapy — HAART — which combined drugs from multiple drug classes to attack HIV at different stages of its replication cycle. A virus that was evolving resistance to one drug had to simultaneously evolve resistance to two or three others, which it could not do fast enough to survive. The results were dramatic: death rates from AIDS in wealthy countries fell sharply in the years following 1996. HIV, for people with access to treatment, was no longer a death sentence. It became a chronic, manageable condition requiring daily medication.

Those three words — "access to treatment" — concealed a global catastrophe. In sub-Saharan Africa, where the epidemic was most severe, antiretroviral drugs were priced far beyond what individuals or governments could afford through most of the nineteen-nineties and into the two-thousands. The epidemic in Africa was of a different character from the one in Europe and North America: it spread primarily through heterosexual sex, affected women as severely as men, and was devastating whole countries. By 2000, roughly twenty-five million people in sub-Saharan Africa were living with HIV. In Botswana, Swaziland, and Zimbabwe, more than a quarter of the adult population was infected. Life expectancy in some of the worst-affected countries fell by more than a decade.

---

The turning point in the global response came in 2003, when the United States launched the President's Emergency Plan for AIDS Relief — PEPFAR — a fifteen-billion-dollar commitment over five years to provide antiretroviral treatment in the most severely affected countries. It was, and remains, the largest commitment in history by any nation to address a single disease in other countries. Expanded and renewed by subsequent administrations, PEPFAR is estimated to have saved more than twenty million lives. The decision to fund it was one of the more significant — and often underappreciated — achievements of the George W. Bush administration.

The public faces of AIDS changed over the years, helping transform public understanding. Rock Hudson, one of Hollywood's most famous actors, died of AIDS-related illness in October 1985 — the first major celebrity to do so publicly. Ryan White, a teenager from Indiana who had contracted HIV through a blood transfusion and was barred from returning to school, became a symbol of the injustice of AIDS-related discrimination. Freddie Mercury, the lead singer of Queen, announced that he had AIDS on the twenty-third of November 1991 and died the next day. Each of these figures brought the epidemic into living rooms and conversations it had not previously reached, and contributed to the slow erosion of stigma.

---

As of the early twenty-first century, roughly thirty-eight to forty million people worldwide are living with HIV. The total number of deaths attributable to AIDS since 1981 exceeds forty-two million. New infections continue — about one point three million per year globally — though the number has fallen substantially from its peak. Effective pre-exposure prophylaxis, known as PrEP, has made it possible for people at high risk of HIV infection to prevent it through daily medication. Treatment has advanced to the point where people with HIV who are diagnosed and treated early have near-normal life expectancy.

What has not yet been achieved is a cure, or a vaccine. HIV persists in the body even in patients with undetectable viral loads, hidden in reservoirs of latent infection that antiretroviral drugs cannot reach. Decades of attempts to develop a vaccine have not yet produced a successful candidate. These remain the frontiers.

The AIDS crisis taught the world hard lessons about the relationship between politics and disease, about the cost of stigma and delay, and about the capacity of affected communities to drive scientific and political responses when governments fail. Those lessons were unevenly learned. The world's response to subsequent epidemics — including COVID-19 — bore the marks of both the failures and the successes of the AIDS era: the same tension between urgency and complacency, the same inequalities in who received treatment and who did not.

The distance between the desperate ignorance of 1981 and the sophisticated treatment and prevention of today is one of the most remarkable journeys in the history of medicine. It was not inevitable. It was the result of science, activism, political will, and — far too slowly, and at far too great a cost — the recognition that a virus does not discriminate, even when the people responding to it do.
