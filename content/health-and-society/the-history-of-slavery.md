# The History of Slavery: Humanity's Darkest Institution

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

Slavery is as old as human civilisation. It appears in the earliest written records — in the laws of ancient Mesopotamia, in the texts of ancient Egypt, in the Homeric epics of ancient Greece. For most of recorded history, and across most of the world, the ownership of human beings was not considered an aberration but a normal feature of economic and social life. The Athenians, who invented democracy and produced some of the most sophisticated philosophy in history, held roughly one hundred thousand slaves — a third of the population of Attica. The Roman Empire was built in large part on enslaved labour. Ancient China, India, and the pre-Columbian civilisations of the Americas all practised slavery in various forms. The institution is not the invention of any one culture; it is one of humanity's oldest and most widespread crimes against itself.

What distinguishes the transatlantic slave trade of the fifteenth through the nineteenth centuries is not that it was slavery — that was ancient — but its scale, its specific racial character, and its economic productivity. The trade that carried millions of Africans across the Atlantic to the Americas was unique in history: the largest forced migration of human beings ever recorded, systematic in its organisation, vast in its extent, and undergirded by a racial ideology that attempted to justify the unjustifiable by constructing elaborate theories about the essential inferiority of African people. The economic structures it built — the plantation economies of the Caribbean and the American South — generated enormous wealth for European and American colonisers and played a significant role in funding the industrialisation of the Western world. The human cost was almost incomprehensible.

---

The ancient world's slavery was diverse in character. Slaves in Rome were taken primarily as the spoils of war — soldiers, aristocrats, scholars, artisans, who found themselves enslaved through military defeat and might, in certain circumstances, earn or purchase their freedom. Slavery in Rome was brutal and could be deadly, but it was not defined by race; a Roman slave might be German, Greek, African, or from any of the territories Rome had conquered. Many slaves became educated members of households, tutors to the children of their owners, administrators of complex estates. Some were freed and became Roman citizens. None of this makes ancient slavery acceptable, but its character was different from what would come later.

Islam and the Arab world developed their own extensive slave trades, drawing enslaved people from sub-Saharan Africa, the Slavic regions, and the Caucasus. The word "slave" itself comes from "Slav" — a reflection of the large numbers of Eastern European people taken into slavery in the medieval period. The Ottoman Empire maintained slavery until the early twentieth century. The Arab slave trade from Africa long predated the European transatlantic trade and in some regions continued longer.

When Portuguese mariners began exploring the West African coast in the early fifteenth century, they encountered existing African slave markets and began to participate in them. The trade initially sent African captives to European households and sugar plantations on Atlantic islands such as Madeira and the Canaries. But it was the colonisation of the Americas — Brazil by Portugal, the Caribbean and mainland Americas by Spain, England, France, and the Netherlands — that transformed the scale of the trade entirely. The plantation economy demanded enormous numbers of workers for the cultivation of sugar, tobacco, cotton, and rice. The indigenous populations of the Americas had been devastated by disease to a degree that made them insufficient. The answer the colonisers found was to ship enslaved Africans in their millions.

---

The transatlantic slave trade operated for roughly four centuries, from the mid-fifteenth century to the mid-nineteenth. The numbers compiled by historians from shipping records are staggering: approximately twelve and a half million Africans were embarked at various points along the West African coast and transported west across the Atlantic; approximately ten and a half million survived to be landed in the Americas. The difference — roughly two million people — died during the crossing, known as the Middle Passage. Historians estimate that between fifteen and twenty-five percent of captives perished during the voyage, from disease, from the conditions of confinement, from violence, or from the despair that led some to refuse to eat or to throw themselves into the sea.

The conditions of the Middle Passage were deliberately engineered to maximise the number of people that could be packed into a ship's hold, with minimal regard for their survival beyond the voyage. Captives were chained together in spaces so small they could barely move, in heat and filth, for weeks or months. When disease broke out — and it always did — it could kill a majority of those on board. The trade was not only brutal; it was recognised as brutal by those who conducted it, which is why records and testimonies from the period are full of efforts to normalise it, to rationalise it, to find theological and pseudo-scientific justifications for what was being done.

Slavery in the Americas varied by region, but it was everywhere a system of extreme violence and total control. In the sugar plantations of the Caribbean — in Barbados, Jamaica, Saint-Domingue, Cuba — the conditions were so lethal that the slave population could only be maintained by constant new imports from Africa; the death rate exceeded the birth rate. In the cotton and tobacco plantations of the American South, a more stable enslaved population developed, but the system was maintained by the constant threat and regular application of brutal physical punishment, and by the legal codification of enslaved people as property with no rights under law.

---

The resistance of enslaved people to their condition was constant and took many forms. There were outright rebellions — the most consequential of which was the Haitian Revolution, which began in 1791 and in 1804 produced the world's first Black republic, the first successful slave revolt in history, after a war of extraordinary violence and determination. There were countless smaller revolts, acts of sabotage, deliberate slowdowns, and individual acts of defiance. There was the quiet resistance of maintaining culture, language, and family bonds under conditions designed to destroy all three.

The abolition movement emerged in the late eighteenth century, driven by a combination of Enlightenment philosophy, evangelical Christianity, and the self-advocacy of freed enslaved people like Olaudah Equiano, whose autobiography — published in 1789 — was a sensation in Britain and helped change public opinion. Quakers had been arguing against slavery since the seventeenth century; by the 1780s they had allies across the political spectrum.

Britain abolished the slave trade in 1807 — making it illegal for British ships to carry enslaved people — and abolished slavery itself in most of the British Empire through the Slavery Abolition Act of 1833, with the measure taking effect in 1834. Slave owners were compensated by the British government to the sum of twenty million pounds — roughly forty percent of the national budget at the time. The people who had actually been enslaved received nothing. The United States abolished slavery through the Thirteenth Amendment to the Constitution, ratified on the sixth of December, 1865, following the conclusion of the Civil War. Brazil, the largest destination for enslaved Africans in the Americas, abolished slavery last, in 1888.

---

The legacies of slavery have not dissolved. The racial hierarchies constructed to justify the transatlantic slave trade shaped the legal, social, and economic structures of the societies that practised it, and those structures did not simply disappear when slavery ended. The poverty, discrimination, and exclusion that characterised the lives of formerly enslaved people and their descendants in the United States, the Caribbean, and Brazil were not accidents but consequences of systems that continued to operate through new mechanisms: sharecropping, Jim Crow laws, mass incarceration, housing discrimination. The wealth extracted from enslaved labour funded much of the capital accumulation that drove the industrialisation of Western Europe and North America. The question of what that means — morally, politically, economically — for contemporary societies is not settled and is not going away.

Slavery also existed, and in some places continues to exist, in forms that do not involve the transatlantic trade at all. Forced labour, debt bondage, human trafficking, and chattel slavery persist in the twenty-first century, affecting an estimated tens of millions of people worldwide. The legal abolition of slavery in most countries in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries was a profound achievement. It was not the end of the story.

What the history of slavery demands of us is an unflinching acknowledgement of what human beings are capable of doing to each other when economic interest and ideology align to make it seem normal. For centuries, the most sophisticated civilisations in the world — democracies that debated the rights of man, empires that built universities and cathedrals — maintained slavery without apparent contradiction. Understanding how that was possible is not merely an exercise in historical curiosity. It is a necessary part of understanding who we are.
