# The Life of Winston Churchill

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

Winston Churchill lived one of the most extraordinary lives of the twentieth century — and the twentieth century was not short of extraordinary lives. He fought in four wars across three continents before he was thirty. He was a soldier, a journalist, a painter, a Nobel Prize-winning author, and the politician who, in the darkest moment of the Second World War, refused to accept that Britain was finished. Whether you admire him without reservation, judge him as a man of his imperial time, or both, it is impossible to understand the modern world without understanding Churchill.

He was born on the thirtieth of November, 1874, at Blenheim Palace in Oxfordshire — the ancestral home of the Dukes of Marlborough — into the highest tier of the British aristocracy. His father, Lord Randolph Churchill, was a brilliant and erratic politician who died young. His mother, Jennie, was an American heiress, energetic and glamorous. Winston adored her and barely knew his father. At school he was not a distinguished academic, but he threw himself into history and English, and even then displayed the gift for language that would define his later greatness.

He entered the army and saw action in Cuba, on the North-West Frontier of India, in Sudan at the Battle of Omdurman in 1898, and in South Africa during the Boer War, where he was captured by the Boers, escaped from prison, and returned to Britain a famous man. He was twenty-five years old. He parlayed his fame into a political career, was elected to Parliament in 1900, and served in a succession of cabinet posts over the following decades.

---

His record in those years was mixed. As First Lord of the Admiralty during the First World War, he championed the Dardanelles campaign — a naval assault on the Ottoman-held straits leading to Constantinople, intended to knock Turkey out of the war and open supply routes to Russia. The campaign became the Gallipoli disaster, badly executed from start to finish, costing around two hundred and fifty thousand Allied casualties. Churchill was widely blamed, resigned from the Admiralty, and briefly served on the Western Front. His career seemed finished.

He came back. He always came back. Through the 1920s and 1930s he held various cabinet positions and lost them, changed parties twice, and became increasingly isolated by the late 1930s — a lone voice warning that Adolf Hitler represented a genuine and mortal threat to Europe while the government pursued appeasement. When appeasement failed and war came, his judgement was suddenly, glaringly vindicated.

---

On the tenth of May, 1940 — the same day Germany launched its devastating attack on France and the Low Countries — Neville Chamberlain resigned and Churchill became Prime Minister. He was sixty-five years old. Britain stood alone. France would fall in six weeks. The United States was not yet in the war. The possibility of a negotiated peace — a surrender in all but name — was real and was being discussed in Churchill's own cabinet.

What Churchill provided in those months was something rarer than strategic genius: he provided clarity of will at a moment when clarity of will was the only thing standing between Britain and defeat. His speeches became instruments of resistance. Speaking to the House of Commons on the fourth of June, 1940, with the Dunkirk evacuation just completed and France on the verge of collapse, he declared: "We shall fight on the beaches, we shall fight on the landing grounds, we shall fight in the fields and in the streets, we shall fight in the hills; we shall never surrender." He was not making a political argument. He was creating a fact.

Churchill fought the war with tremendous energy and some significant mistakes. He was obsessive about peripheral campaigns — North Africa, the Middle East, Italy — when American generals wanted to concentrate everything on a cross-channel invasion. His personal relationships with Roosevelt and Stalin were complex navigations of competing interests. He was present at all the great conferences of the war — Casablanca, Tehran, Yalta — where the shape of the post-war world was argued over. At Yalta in February 1945, he sat at the table as one of the Big Three, but he was beginning to see that Britain's power relative to the United States and the Soviet Union had been irreversibly diminished.

---

Then, in July 1945, two months after Germany's surrender and weeks before Japan's, Britain held a general election. The country had moved on. The returning soldiers and bombed-out civilians wanted a welfare state, not a war hero. Churchill and the Conservative Party were defeated in a landslide by Clement Attlee's Labour Party. Churchill was devastated. He had won the war. He had lost his country.

He was not finished. In March 1946, now Leader of the Opposition, he visited Westminster College in Fulton, Missouri, accompanied by President Truman, and gave a speech that named the new reality: "From Stettin in the Baltic to Trieste in the Adriatic, an iron curtain has descended across the continent." The Iron Curtain speech did not invent the Cold War, but it defined it in terms that shaped how the world understood the conflict for the next forty-five years.

Churchill returned to Downing Street as Prime Minister in 1951 and won the Nobel Prize in Literature in 1953 — an unusual distinction for a sitting head of government — awarded for his historical writings and oratory. Age and ill health limited what he could accomplish in his second term, and he resigned in 1955. He died on the twenty-fourth of January, 1965, at the age of ninety. His state funeral was the largest in British history, attended by representatives of one hundred and twelve nations.

---

Churchill was a man of his empire and his class — capable of views on India, on race, and on subject peoples that cause justified discomfort today, and whose decisions, including those during the 1943 Bengal famine, remain deeply controversial. He was also the man who looked at Hitler in 1940, when almost everyone else was prepared to find accommodation, and said no. Both things are true. The question of how to weigh them is one that each generation will answer for itself.

What is not in question is the scale of the moment he inhabited, and the singular role he played in it. At the most dangerous point in the war, with the outcome genuinely uncertain and defeat a real possibility, Churchill's refusal to consider surrender changed the direction of history. That is not a small thing. It may be the largest thing a politician has ever done.
