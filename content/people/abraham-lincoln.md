# Abraham Lincoln: The President Who Held a Nation Together

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

On the twelfth of February, 1809, in a one-room log cabin in Hardin County, Kentucky, a child was born into poverty so complete that it left little trace in the historical record beyond the date and place. Abraham Lincoln would grow up to become the sixteenth president of the United States, to guide his country through its most devastating war, to issue the proclamation that began the legal abolition of slavery, and to be shot dead in a theatre before he could see the peace he had fought four years to win. His life, which began in obscurity so deep that even he knew almost nothing about his own ancestry, became one of the defining American stories: the man from nowhere who remade a nation.

Lincoln grew up on the frontier, first in Kentucky and then in Indiana. His mother died when he was nine years old, from milk sickness — a condition caused by drinking milk from cows that had eaten poisonous plants. His stepmother, Sarah Bush Lincoln, was kind to him and recognised his unusual intelligence. Education on the frontier was fragmentary — he estimated that his formal schooling added up to less than a year in total. He educated himself by reading every book he could obtain, and in a sparsely populated region where books were rare, this required real effort. He read the Bible, Aesop's Fables, Robinson Crusoe, and the few histories he could borrow. He read them multiple times, by firelight, and committed long passages to memory.

As a young man he worked with his hands: clearing forests, splitting rails, managing a flatboat on a trading voyage down the Mississippi River to New Orleans. In New Orleans, for one of the first times in his life, he saw slavery in all its reality — the slave markets, the auctions, the human beings sold and separated from their families. Whether or not the famous story of Lincoln walking away from a slave auction in disgust and saying "If I ever get a chance to hit that thing, I'll hit it hard" is literally true, it captures something about the experience. He had already developed a moral revulsion toward slavery that would never leave him, even as political realities for years forced him to be more cautious than his instincts wished.

---

Lincoln moved to Illinois, educated himself in law by reading borrowed textbooks — the only way to do it on the frontier — and was admitted to the bar in 1836. He served four terms in the Illinois state legislature and one term in the United States House of Representatives. He was not, in these years, a nationally prominent figure. He was a capable, popular local politician with a gift for storytelling, an unusual physical presence — he stood six feet four inches tall, with long arms and a face of such striking homeliness that he himself made jokes about it — and a deep, almost self-taught knowledge of the law.

The event that propelled him toward national attention was the Kansas-Nebraska Act of 1854, which opened previously free territories to the possible expansion of slavery. Lincoln found it morally intolerable. His speeches against it were among the most effective of the decade, combining moral clarity with practical political argument. He made his position unmistakable: slavery was wrong, the Founders had known it was wrong, and the republic they had created was committed — eventually, even if not immediately — to its extinction.

In 1858, he ran for the Senate against the incumbent Stephen Douglas and challenged him to a series of public debates across Illinois that became one of the great spectacles of American political life. The Lincoln-Douglas debates — seven of them, held in towns across the state before crowds of thousands — turned on a single question: could a democratic people vote to extend slavery? Lincoln's argument was that they could not, because slavery contradicted the founding principles the country had committed itself to at independence. He lost the Senate election but won a national audience.

---

In 1860, Lincoln won the Republican presidential nomination and, in November, the presidential election, receiving not a single electoral vote from the Southern states. Before he could be inaugurated, seven Southern states seceded and formed the Confederate States of America. Four more followed after the Confederacy attacked Fort Sumter on the twelfth of April, 1861, and Lincoln called for volunteers to put down the rebellion. The Civil War had begun.

For four years, Lincoln bore a weight that few presidents in any democracy have endured. He had to manage a fractious cabinet, fight a war against a determined enemy, maintain the allegiance of border states where slavery still existed, navigate the political demands of radical abolitionists and conservative Democrats, deal with catastrophic early military defeats, and hold the North's will to fight together through years of horrifying casualties. He was criticised ceaselessly — from the right for going too far against slavery, from the left for not going far enough, from every direction for the conduct of the war. He bore it all with a patience and a mordant humour that left contemporaries at once baffled and admiring.

The Emancipation Proclamation, issued on the first of January, 1863, declared enslaved people in the rebellious Confederate states to be free. It was a war measure as much as a moral statement — Lincoln could not yet abolish slavery in states that had not rebelled, because the Constitution did not give him that power — but its moral meaning was unmistakable. It transformed the war's purpose. The Union was no longer fighting merely to preserve the political structure of the republic; it was fighting for human freedom.

---

On the nineteenth of November, 1863, Lincoln traveled to Gettysburg, Pennsylvania, to dedicate a military cemetery on the ground where, five months earlier, the bloodiest battle of the war had been fought at a cost of some fifty thousand casualties. He was not the main speaker — that distinction belonged to Edward Everett, a famous orator who delivered a two-hour address — but Lincoln's few minutes of remarks became perhaps the most celebrated short speech in American history. In two hundred and seventy-two words, he reframed the meaning of the war and of the republic itself: that the men who had died there were giving their lives to prove that a nation "conceived in Liberty and dedicated to the proposition that all men are created equal" could survive, and that their deaths should propel the living to ensure that "government of the people, by the people, for the people, shall not perish from the earth."

Lincoln was re-elected in November 1864, against a Democratic opponent running on a peace platform that would have ended the war without abolishing slavery. His victory, in a genuinely contested election held in the middle of a civil war, was a demonstration of democratic endurance that he himself considered the war's most important event. In his second inaugural address, in March 1865, with victory now visible, he called for reconciliation with a generosity that his opponents would not always return: "With malice toward none, with charity for all."

---

General Robert E. Lee surrendered on the ninth of April, 1865. The war was effectively over. Five days later, on the evening of the fourteenth of April, Lincoln went to see a comedy at Ford's Theatre in Washington. An actor named John Wilkes Booth, a fanatical Confederate sympathiser, entered the presidential box and shot him in the back of the head with a small pistol. Lincoln died the following morning, the fifteenth of April, 1865, at the age of fifty-six. He did not live to see the Thirteenth Amendment abolishing slavery ratified later that year, the process of Reconstruction begin, or his vision of a reunited country take shape — imperfectly, bitterly, with enormous violence — in the decades that followed.

The grief that swept the North at his death was genuine and profound. His funeral train carried his coffin from Washington to Springfield, Illinois — a journey of nearly two thousand miles over two weeks — and hundreds of thousands of people lined the routes to watch it pass. In death he became mythic in ways that he, a man who found sentiment for its own sake slightly embarrassing, might have found excessive. But the myth was not entirely wrong. He had held a country together through its worst crisis, established the legal abolition of slavery, and done both things while maintaining the constitutional structures of democratic government. That this seems straightforward in retrospect is a measure of how effectively he made it look inevitable at the time.
