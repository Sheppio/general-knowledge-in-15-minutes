# Nelson Mandela: The Long Walk to Freedom

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,350

---

There are very few people in history whose lives became the lived embodiment of their country's transformation. Nelson Mandela is one of them. He was born into a rural village in South Africa's Eastern Cape under a system of racial segregation that would, by the time he reached adulthood, harden into one of the twentieth century's most systematic instruments of racial oppression. He spent twenty-seven years in prison for his resistance to that system. He was released to lead his country's negotiated transition to democracy, became its first democratically elected president, and handed over power peacefully and on schedule. The moral weight of what he represented — the combination of endurance, dignity, and a willingness to forgive rather than exact vengeance — made him perhaps the most admired human being on earth in the final decades of the twentieth century.

Mandela was not a simple figure. He was a trained lawyer and a skilled politician who sometimes dissembled, sometimes compromised, and made decisions that remain controversial. He was a man capable of deep personal warmth who was, by the account of those who loved him, frequently absent from the people who needed him most. He was a committed advocate for armed struggle who became the world's most famous proponent of reconciliation. To understand him as a complete human being rather than a symbol is to understand something important: that moral courage and political genius are not diminished by human complexity.

---

Nelson Rolihlahla Mandela was born on the eighteenth of July, 1918, in the village of Mvezo, in the Transkei, in what was then the Union of South Africa. His father was the chief counsellor to the acting paramount chief of the Thembu people, a sub-group of the Xhosa nation, and Mandela grew up with an awareness of chiefly tradition and communal responsibility that stayed with him throughout his life. It was his teacher at a Methodist school who gave him the English name "Nelson" — a common practice at the time. He was the first member of his family to attend school.

He came to Johannesburg in the nineteen-forties, studied law, and found himself drawn into the politics of the African National Congress. He joined the ANC in 1943, co-founded its Youth League in 1944, and became one of the most energetic advocates of a more confrontational approach to the apartheid system that the National Party, upon coming to power in 1948, was in the process of constructing. Apartheid — the Afrikaans word for "apartness" — was not merely segregation; it was a comprehensive legal architecture designed to separate South Africa's people by race in every dimension of life: where they could live, where their children could go to school, who they could marry, which beaches and buses and park benches they could use, what work they could do, and whether they had any political representation at all.

---

The ANC's initial response to apartheid was non-violent protest: campaigns, petitions, strikes. The Defiance Campaign of 1952, in which Mandela played a major organising role, mobilised thousands of South Africans to deliberately break apartheid laws and court arrest. The Congress of the People in 1955 produced the Freedom Charter, a statement of democratic principles that would remain the ANC's foundational document. None of it forced the apartheid government to move.

The turning point came at Sharpeville on the twenty-first of March, 1960. Police opened fire on a crowd of Black South Africans who had gathered to protest the pass laws — the internal passport system that controlled the movement of Black people within the country. Sixty-nine people were killed, most of them shot in the back. The government declared a state of emergency, banned the ANC, and arrested thousands. Mandela concluded that non-violence was no longer a viable strategy. With others, he co-founded Umkhonto we Sizwe — "Spear of the Nation" — the armed wing of the ANC, and began planning a campaign of sabotage against infrastructure targets. He travelled abroad to seek support, training in Algeria.

On the fifth of August, 1962, he was arrested at a roadblock in Natal — almost certainly betrayed to the South African security services by an informant. He was initially sentenced to five years for incitement and leaving the country illegally. Then the police raided the ANC's secret headquarters at Rivonia Farm near Johannesburg and found enough evidence to charge him and several colleagues with sabotage and violent conspiracy. The Rivonia Trial lasted from October 1963 to June 1964. Mandela refused to be cowed. In a statement from the dock that has entered history, he said: "I have fought against white domination, and I have fought against Black domination. I cherish the ideal of a democratic and free society in which all persons live together in harmony and with equal opportunities. It is an ideal which I hope to live for and to achieve. But if needs be, it is an ideal for which I am prepared to die."

On the twelfth of June, 1964, he was sentenced to life imprisonment, narrowly escaping the death penalty.

---

He served eighteen years on Robben Island, a windswept prison island off Cape Town where political prisoners broke rocks in a limestone quarry under a South African sun that slowly damaged the eyes of many of them, Mandela included. The conditions were harsh: the food was poor, the guards were often brutal, correspondence was limited, and prisoners were permitted one visitor and one letter every six months. But Robben Island became a strange kind of university: the prisoners taught each other, discussed politics, argued about strategy, and maintained a level of internal organisation that amounted to a shadow government in waiting. Mandela emerged from it with his convictions intact, his authority undiminished, and his reputation — outside South Africa — growing with every passing year.

After Robben Island he was moved to Pollsmoor Prison on the mainland, and later to Victor Verster Prison near Paarl, where he was given considerably more comfortable conditions as the apartheid government began, tentatively and secretly, to explore the possibility of negotiation. The political context had shifted enormously: international sanctions were biting, the South African economy was struggling, the ANC's guerrilla campaign was continuing, and the townships of South Africa were in open revolt. Pretoria needed a way out, and the only interlocutor who commanded the trust of the Black population was the man still in prison.

---

On the eleventh of February, 1990, Nelson Mandela walked out of Victor Verster Prison, his right fist raised. He had been imprisoned for twenty-seven years and eight months. He was seventy-one years old. The images were broadcast around the world. In the speech he gave that day, he made clear that he had not moderated his politics in prison: he called for international sanctions to continue, for the armed struggle to continue, and for the transfer of power to a democratic government.

What followed was four years of extraordinarily difficult negotiation, conducted between Mandela, the ANC, President F. W. de Klerk, and the other parties and armed factions contesting the country's future. It was not a smooth process: political violence killed thousands during the transition period, including organised massacres that implicated elements of the security forces. Mandela and de Klerk shared the Nobel Peace Prize in 1993 for their role in the negotiations. On the twenty-seventh of April, 1994 — a date South Africans still call Freedom Day — the country's first democratic elections took place. Mandela cast his vote for the first time in his life at the age of seventy-five. The ANC won with sixty-two percent of the vote. He was inaugurated as South Africa's first democratically elected president on the tenth of May, 1994.

His presidency was a single five-year term, which he served as he had promised. He established the Truth and Reconciliation Commission, which gave victims the opportunity to speak and perpetrators the opportunity to apply for amnesty in exchange for full disclosure — an imperfect but remarkable attempt to process the crimes of the apartheid era without descending into either blanket amnesty or retributive prosecution. He stepped down in 1999. The peaceful transfer of power was itself a kind of achievement, in a continent and a world where such transitions were rare.

---

Nelson Mandela died on the fifth of December, 2013, at his home in Johannesburg, at the age of ninety-five. The outpouring of grief around the world was extraordinary — heads of state from across the political spectrum gathered in Soweto for a memorial service. He was mourned not only as a South African leader but as one of the defining moral figures of the twentieth century.

The South Africa he left behind was not the one he had dreamed of. Inequality remained extreme; the economic promise of liberation had not reached most Black South Africans; corruption under his ANC successors would deepen. Mandela himself, in his final years, acknowledged that the struggle was not over. But what he had achieved — the end of apartheid without a racial civil war, the establishment of a constitutional democracy, the demonstration that it was possible to imprison a man for twenty-seven years and not break him — was something the world had not taken for granted and has not forgotten.
