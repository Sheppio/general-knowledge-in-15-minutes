# Nikola Tesla: The Man Who Lit the World

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Nikola Tesla is one of the strangest and most extraordinary figures in the history of science and technology. He made discoveries and inventions that were foundational to the modern world — above all, the system of alternating current electrical power that lights homes and powers machines across the globe — and he died alone and in debt, his name largely unknown to the general public, his patents having made fortunes for others while he himself had little. The story of Tesla's life is, among other things, a study in the gap between scientific genius and commercial success, and in the way that history assigns credit in a competitive and political world.

He was born on the ninth of July, eighteen fifty-six, in Smiljan, a village in what was then the Austrian Empire and is now Croatia. His father was a Serbian Orthodox priest, his mother a woman of no formal education but, by Tesla's own account, extraordinary intelligence and a remarkable memory — she could recite entire books from memory and had invented various household devices. Tesla would later attribute his own gift for visualisation to her influence. He studied physics and engineering at the Technical University in Graz and briefly at the University of Prague, developing an obsessive interest in electrical machines. Before leaving university, he had conceived the idea of an alternating current motor — a machine that would exploit the properties of alternating rather than direct current to produce rotary motion — but he had not yet worked out how to build one.

---

He worked for a few years in Budapest and then in Paris for the Continental Edison Company, which was installing electrical systems across Europe based on Thomas Edison's direct current technology. While on assignment to Strassburg in eighteen eighty-three, Tesla built his first working induction motor — the device he had been mentally constructing for years. The induction motor used a rotating magnetic field — the principle Tesla had discovered — to produce smooth, efficient rotary motion without the commutators and brushes that direct current motors required, making it simpler, more reliable, and better suited to the sustained operation that industrial machinery demanded.

In eighteen eighty-four, Tesla emigrated to the United States and went to work directly for Thomas Edison in New York. The collaboration was brief and unhappy. Edison, a brilliant and tenacious inventor in his own right, was deeply committed to his direct current system and sceptical of alternating current. He was also, by Tesla's account, careless with a promise — he had reportedly offered Tesla fifty thousand dollars if he could solve certain engineering problems, and when Tesla did so, Edison told him the offer had been a joke. Tesla left Edison's employ and spent a difficult year doing manual labour — digging ditches for the Edison company's cables, according to some accounts — before finding investors willing to back his electrical ideas.

---

The breakthrough came in eighteen eighty-eight, when George Westinghouse, an industrialist and inventor who had built a successful company around railroad air brakes and was looking for a competitive alternative to Edison's electrical system, bought the patent rights to Tesla's polyphase alternating current system. The transaction brought Tesla a lump sum payment and royalties and set in motion what became known as the "War of the Currents" — one of the most contentious commercial and technical disputes in the history of industry.

Edison's direct current system had the advantage of being already installed in parts of New York and other cities, and Edison himself had a powerful financial interest in its success. Alternating current had the decisive technical advantage: it could be stepped up to very high voltages for long-distance transmission using transformers, then stepped down again at the destination, allowing a single generating station to supply power over a wide area. Edison's DC system could not transmit power more than about a mile without unacceptable losses. As cities grew and the demand for electrical power expanded beyond the areas Edison's stations could serve, this was a fatal limitation.

Edison fought the adoption of AC with considerable ingenuity, including a campaign to associate alternating current with danger — publicly electrocuting animals with AC to demonstrate its lethality, and lobbying for the electric chair to use AC power, hoping to brand it as the "executioner's current." The campaign was not ultimately successful. In eighteen ninety-three, Westinghouse won the contract to light the World's Columbian Exposition in Chicago — a spectacular showcase that demonstrated AC's capacity to illuminate an enormous event without incident. The same year, Westinghouse and Tesla won the contract to harness Niagara Falls, installing the first large-scale hydroelectric power station using Tesla's AC system. By eighteen ninety-six, the Niagara plant was delivering power to the city of Buffalo, twenty-two miles away. The War of the Currents was effectively over, and alternating current — Tesla's invention — had won.

---

Tesla had, in the meantime, been working on other things. In eighteen ninety-one he invented the Tesla coil — a resonant transformer circuit that produces high-voltage, high-frequency alternating current and is still used in radio equipment and high-voltage applications today. He was among the first to investigate radio transmission, and his demonstrations of wireless electrical transmission — sending signals and even power through the air without wires — anticipated the radio age. The question of who truly invented radio is contested — Guglielmo Marconi, an Italian inventor, received the patent and the Nobel Prize, but the United States Supreme Court ruled in nineteen forty-three, after Tesla's death, that several of Marconi's key patents were invalid because Tesla's earlier work had priority.

Tesla's ambitions grew increasingly grand and his grip on commercial reality increasingly uncertain. He conceived a vision of wireless power transmission on a global scale — a system of towers that would deliver electrical power to any point on Earth without wires. He persuaded the financier J. P. Morgan to fund the construction of a massive wireless transmission tower at Wardenclyffe on Long Island, begun in nineteen oh one. But Morgan withdrew his funding before the project was complete, the tower was never finished, and Tesla was left with enormous debts and a project that became a symbol of his combination of visionary genius and financial recklessness. The tower was demolished in nineteen seventeen.

---

The later years of Tesla's life were marked by increasing eccentricity and poverty. He lived for decades in New York hotels, often unable to pay his bills, supported intermittently by fees from lectures and from the occasional sale of patents. He developed intense phobias — of pearls, of round objects, of human hair — and an obsessive love for pigeons, feeding them daily in the parks and squares of New York and caring for injured ones in his hotel room. He claimed to have invented a death ray, a particle beam weapon of unlimited destructive power, and went to his grave insisting it was real and that the world should take it seriously. The FBI, after his death, found the claims unprovable.

He died on the seventh of January, nineteen forty-three, in his room at the New Yorker Hotel in Manhattan. He was eighty-six years old, alone, and largely forgotten by the public. His death attracted brief attention but little sustained commemoration; it was left to later generations to reconstruct his importance.

The rehabilitation of Tesla's reputation has been one of the more striking phenomena of recent decades. From near-obscurity, he has become something of a cultural icon — the romantic, visionary inventor crushed by corporate interests and historical injustice, a symbol of the way that history gives credit to those who monetise ideas rather than those who originate them. The electric car company Tesla Motors was named after him; his image appears on T-shirts and coffee mugs; he has been the subject of novels, films, and plays. The system of alternating current electrical power that underlies the modern world — that powers the lights, the motors, the computers, the hospitals, the cities — was his invention, and the belated recognition of that fact, however mediated by myth, is not unreasonable.
