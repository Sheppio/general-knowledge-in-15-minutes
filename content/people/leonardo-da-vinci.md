# Leonardo da Vinci: The Universal Man

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Leonardo da Vinci is the supreme example of a mind that refused to stay in one place. He was a painter of two of the most famous images in the history of art. He was an anatomist who dissected more than thirty human corpses and produced drawings of the human body that were not surpassed for accuracy until the nineteenth century. He was an engineer who designed machines — flying devices, armoured vehicles, hydraulic pumps, canal locks, printing presses, a robot — centuries before the technology existed to build most of them. He was a musician, a geologist, a botanist, a cartographer, and a student of light, optics, and the movement of water. The word "Renaissance man" was coined to describe a particular ideal of the age in which he lived — the idea that a cultivated person could and should excel in multiple fields simultaneously. Leonardo is the person against whom all other claimants to that title are measured, and most are found wanting.

He was born on the fifteenth of April, fourteen fifty-two, in the hamlet of Anchiano near the town of Vinci, in what is now Tuscany. He was the illegitimate son of Ser Piero da Vinci, a Florentine notary, and a local woman named Caterina. Illegitimacy in fifteenth-century Italy was not the social catastrophe it would become in later centuries, but it did close off certain paths: Leonardo could not follow his father into the notarial profession, which required legitimate birth, and he could not attend university for the same reason. What he could do — what his father arranged for him at around the age of fifteen — was apprentice to one of the leading artist-craftsmen of Florence.

---

Andrea del Verrocchio was a sculptor, painter, goldsmith, and engineer of remarkable versatility, and his workshop in Florence was one of the great training grounds of the Italian Renaissance. Leonardo entered it around fourteen sixty-six and emerged, by his own talent and effort, as one of its most accomplished products. The story — told by the sixteenth-century art historian Giorgio Vasari and probably embellished — is that when Verrocchio saw the angel Leonardo had painted in their collaborative work The Baptism of Christ, he recognised that his pupil had surpassed him and vowed never to paint again. What is certain is that Leonardo was accepted into the painters' guild of Florence in fourteen seventy-two, marking his status as an independent professional artist.

He remained in Florence for a decade, but the city was a competitive place and the opportunities limited. In fourteen eighty-two, at the age of thirty, he left Florence for Milan, entering the service of Ludovico Sforza, the duke of Milan, as — in a remarkable document he sent listing his qualifications — primarily a military engineer who could also, he mentioned almost as an afterthought, paint. The seventeen years Leonardo spent in Milan were among the most productive of his life. He produced major paintings, including The Virgin of the Rocks, worked on a colossal bronze equestrian statue that was never cast, designed elaborate court entertainments and theatrical machinery, filled notebook after notebook with observations and designs, and — between approximately fourteen ninety-five and fourteen ninety-eight — painted the Last Supper on the wall of the refectory of the convent of Santa Maria delle Grazie.

---

The Last Supper is a painting of staggering psychological complexity. Depicting the moment at the supper table when Christ announces that one of those present will betray him, Leonardo captures the individual reactions of all twelve apostles simultaneously — shock, denial, grief, calculation, guilt — in a composition of extraordinary dramatic and spatial coherence. It was innovative in technique — Leonardo painted on dry plaster rather than wet, allowing him to rework the image, but at the cost of durability, and the painting began to deteriorate within his own lifetime. Despite centuries of damage and multiple restorations, it remains one of the most reproduced and studied images in the world.

Leonardo's notebooks are the other great monument of his genius. He filled thousands of pages with drawings, observations, calculations, and speculations, writing in his characteristic mirror script — from right to left, readable only when held up to a glass — in a hand so small and dense that its full implications are still being worked out by scholars. The notebooks reveal a mind of breathtaking scope and originality. His anatomical drawings, based on direct dissection of human and animal bodies, show the skeleton, muscles, nerves, and organs in positions and from angles that no artist had attempted before, and with an accuracy that anticipated medical illustration by centuries. His studies of water — its flow, its eddies, its behaviour around obstacles — are both scientifically sophisticated and visually magnificent. His designs for flying machines, including an ornithopter that mimicked the mechanics of bird flight and a device resembling a helicopter, were technically beyond what could be built with the materials and power sources of his day, but showed a deep understanding of aerodynamic principles.

---

When the French invaded Milan in fourteen ninety-nine and Ludovico Sforza fled, Leonardo's position evaporated. He spent the next decade moving between Florence, Venice, and various other Italian cities, working for different patrons including — in a particularly uncomfortable episode — Cesare Borgia, the ruthless son of Pope Alexander VI, whom Leonardo served as a military engineer and cartographer. He produced extraordinary maps for Borgia, including what may be the first accurate bird's-eye-view map of a city, the plan of Imola. He also, during this period, began the painting that would become the most famous image on Earth.

The Mona Lisa — known in Italy as La Gioconda — is believed to depict Lisa Gherardini, the wife of a Florentine merchant named Francesco del Giocondo. Leonardo began the portrait around fifteen oh three and continued working on it, off and on, for much of the rest of his life — it was still with him when he died. The painting is small — fifty-three by seventy-seven centimetres — and its fame rests not on its size but on qualities of execution that remain, after more than five centuries, without parallel. The sfumato technique — the blurring of contours through imperceptible gradations of tone — gives the figure a quality of life and ambiguity that no reproduction captures. The smile that seems to shift as the eye moves across it is a specific optical effect produced by the way Leonardo handles peripheral vision. The landscape behind the figure is subtly asymmetrical, giving the composition a gentle sense of unease. The painting was never delivered to its commissioner; Leonardo kept it, and it passed with him to France.

---

In fifteen sixteen, at the age of sixty-four, Leonardo accepted an invitation from King Francis I of France to join the French court, settling in the château of Clos Lucé, near Amboise in the Loire Valley, with the title of "premier painter, engineer, and architect of the King." He brought with him three paintings — the Mona Lisa, The Virgin and Child with Saint Anne, and Saint John the Baptist — and his notebooks. The French king, by all accounts, adored him and visited him regularly, reportedly spending hours in conversation. Leonardo's physical abilities were declining — his right hand appears to have been partially paralysed, possibly by a stroke — but his mind remained active, and he continued to draw and to plan.

He died on the second of May, fifteen nineteen, at the age of sixty-seven, at Clos Lucé. He left his notebooks and papers to his assistant and companion Francesco Melzi, who spent the rest of his life preserving them. After Melzi's death, the notebooks were dispersed — scattered across libraries and private collections across Europe, some lost, some still turning up in unexpected places. The Royal Library at Windsor holds hundreds of his drawings. The Codex Atlanticus in Milan is the largest single collection. The Codex Leicester — his scientific notes on water and geology — is owned by Bill Gates, who bought it at auction in nineteen ninety-four.

What Leonardo represents, beyond any individual achievement, is a particular vision of human intellectual capacity: the idea that the world is interesting, that observation yields insight, that the connections between apparently separate domains of knowledge are real and productive, and that one mind, applied with sufficient patience and curiosity, can reach across an extraordinary range of human experience and illuminate all of it. He was not modest about this vision, but he was not wrong about it either.
