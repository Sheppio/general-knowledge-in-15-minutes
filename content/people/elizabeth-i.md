# Elizabeth I: The Virgin Queen and the Making of England

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Elizabeth I reigned over England for forty-four years, from fifteen fifty-eight to sixteen oh three, and in that time transformed a small, religiously divided, financially exhausted kingdom on the northwestern fringe of Europe into a state of growing confidence and expanding ambition. She presided over the defeat of the Spanish Armada, the beginnings of English overseas exploration and expansion, and a cultural flowering — the age of Shakespeare, Marlowe, Spenser, and Sidney — that has no parallel in English literary history. She did all of this as a woman in a world built for men, navigating with extraordinary skill a political system that regarded female rule as an aberration and a foreign policy landscape dominated by powers that considered her a bastard and a heretic. She is one of the most remarkable monarchs in European history, and she remains one of the most debated.

She was born on the seventh of September, fifteen thirty-three, at Greenwich Palace, the daughter of Henry VIII and his second wife, Anne Boleyn. Her arrival was a disappointment to her father, who had torn apart the English church and his first marriage in his desperate need for a male heir and expected a son. She was less than three years old when her mother was arrested on charges of adultery and treason — almost certainly fabricated — and executed on the nineteenth of May, fifteen thirty-six. Elizabeth was declared illegitimate. Her future, in those early years, appeared to be one of marginalisation and danger rather than power.

---

The turbulent decade of the fifteen-forties and fifty-five hundred shaped Elizabeth in ways that would define her reign. She survived the reigns of her younger half-brother Edward VI — a Protestant zealot whose advisors kept her close but watched her carefully — and her elder half-sister Mary I, who restored Catholicism to England and executed nearly three hundred Protestants in the process, earning the epithet "Bloody Mary." Elizabeth, whose own religious sympathies were carefully ambiguous throughout this period, was briefly imprisoned in the Tower of London when Mary suspected her of involvement in a Protestant rebellion. She emerged from these years with a highly developed instinct for survival: an understanding of when to speak and when to stay silent, when to act and when to wait.

She became queen on the seventeenth of November, fifteen fifty-eight, when Mary I died childless. She was twenty-five years old. The kingdom she inherited was in poor shape: the treasury was depleted, Calais — England's last possession in France — had just been lost, and the country was religiously fractured after decades of swinging between Protestant and Catholic. Her first and most consequential decision was the religious settlement of fifteen fifty-nine, which restored a moderate Protestantism as the Church of England, required outward conformity without demanding the kind of examination of private belief that could have produced widespread rebellion. The settlement was imperfect by the standards of either committed Protestants or Catholics, but it was durable — and durability was what England needed.

---

The question that dominated the first thirty years of her reign — and that she managed with calculated evasiveness — was who she would marry and who would succeed her. Every monarch in Europe assumed she would marry; every government adviser urged her to produce an heir. She received numerous suitors and proposed herself as the potential bride of several foreign princes and one English earl, conducting these negotiations with tactical brilliance without ever committing. Whether she genuinely wished to remain unmarried or used the possibility of marriage as a diplomatic tool throughout — perhaps both — is a question historians have argued for centuries. Her own explanation, given in various forms at various points, was that she was married to England. The image of the Virgin Queen, wedded to her kingdom rather than to any man, became one of the great political fictions of the age — and, like many political fictions, it was partly true.

The greatest threat to her reign came not from foreign invasion but from her cousin Mary Queen of Scots, who had been forced to abdicate the Scottish throne in fifteen sixty-seven and had fled to England, where Elizabeth — against the advice of her councillors — had given her refuge. Mary was the Catholic claimant to the English throne, the focus of Catholic plots to remove Elizabeth and restore Catholicism to England, and the most dangerous person in the country so long as Elizabeth had no heir. For nineteen years Elizabeth kept Mary under comfortable captivity, refusing to execute her and refusing to release her, until the discovery of the Babington Plot — a scheme to assassinate Elizabeth and place Mary on the throne — made the situation untenable. Mary was beheaded at Fotheringhay Castle on the eighth of February, fifteen eighty-seven. Elizabeth reportedly wept at the news, and punished the secretary who had dispatched the death warrant without awaiting her final confirmation. Whether her grief was genuine or political performance is another question that has never been satisfactorily answered.

---

The Spanish Armada that sailed in the summer of fifteen eighty-eight was the culmination of decades of tension between England and Spain — Protestant England's raids on Spanish shipping, English support for the Protestant rebellion in the Spanish Netherlands, the execution of the Catholic Mary Queen of Scots. Philip II of Spain assembled a great fleet of a hundred and thirty ships and nearly twenty thousand soldiers with the ambition of escorting a Spanish army from the Netherlands across the Channel and deposing Elizabeth. The fleet sailed in July and was harassed along the Channel by the English navy under Lord Howard of Effingham and Francis Drake, more damaged by weather and its own logistical problems than by English firepower, and scattered by a celebrated fire-ship attack off Gravelines. The invasion never happened. Storms wrecked much of the returning fleet on the rocks of Scotland and Ireland.

The moment Elizabeth is most remembered for — though the Armada had already turned back by the time she delivered it — was her speech to the English land forces assembled at Tilbury on the ninth of August, fifteen eighty-eight. She arrived on horseback, and declared to her troops: "I know I have the body of a weak and feeble woman; but I have the heart and stomach of a king, and of a king of England too." The speech addressed directly the central problem of her position — that she was a woman ruling in a world that expected kings — and resolved it with bravura performance. It is one of the most celebrated pieces of rhetoric in English history.

---

The years after the Armada brought their own difficulties. The Earl of Essex, a favourite whose dashing qualities Elizabeth admired and perhaps loved, overreached himself in Ireland, made an unauthorised truce with the Irish rebels, and finally launched an abortive coup in fifteen hundred and one that ended in his arrest and execution. Elizabeth is said to have been devastated. Her final years were overshadowed by grief and by the awareness of her own mortality and the unresolved succession question. She remained, to the end, unwilling to name her successor, though it was understood that it would be James VI of Scotland, the son of Mary Queen of Scots.

She died on the twenty-fourth of March, sixteen oh three, at Richmond Palace, in her seventieth year, having been queen for forty-four years. She had outlasted most of the men who had advised her, opposed her, wooed her, and plotted against her. James VI of Scotland succeeded her as James I of England, uniting the English and Scottish crowns for the first time.

What she left behind was a country transformed. England in fifteen fifty-eight was a minor player in European affairs. England in sixteen oh three was a maritime power with the beginnings of an empire, a Protestant nation confident in its identity, and a culture that had produced Shakespeare. That transformation was not Elizabeth's work alone — it was the product of a whole society in motion. But she shaped the conditions in which it happened, and the particular combination of personal charisma, political intelligence, and strategic ambiguity with which she held the country together through four decades of religious tension and external threat was, by any measure, a remarkable achievement.
