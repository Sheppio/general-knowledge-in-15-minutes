# Marie Curie: The Woman Who Changed Science

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

There is a particular cruelty in the fact that Marie Curie was killed by the science she loved. She spent decades handling radioactive materials with her bare hands, carrying test tubes of radium in her pockets, storing radioactive isotopes in her desk drawers. She could not have known the danger — no one could, because she was the one discovering it. Her laboratory notebooks, still so radioactive that they are stored in lead-lined boxes and can only be consulted by researchers wearing protective clothing, are a measure of the price she paid for her discoveries. She paid it willingly, all the way to her death in 1934.

Marie Curie was born Maria Sklodowska on the seventh of November, 1867, in Warsaw — then under Russian occupation, in a Poland that did not officially exist on any map. Her family was educated and cultured but had lost their property through patriotic activities against the Russian Empire. Education for women in Russian-controlled Poland was limited to clandestine study. She and her sister made an arrangement: one would work to support the other through university, then they would switch. Marie worked as a governess for years to fund her sister's medical studies in Paris. Then her sister funded her in turn.

---

She arrived in Paris in 1891 and enrolled at the Sorbonne, where she was one of very few women. She lived in an unheated attic garret, sometimes too cold to study and sometimes too hungry to concentrate. She finished first in her physics degree. Then she finished first in her mathematics degree. She had come to do science, and nothing was going to stop her.

She met Pierre Curie, a brilliant French physicist, in 1894. They married the following year — a partnership of equals in love and in science, of a kind almost unheard of in the nineteenth century. Together they began investigating a phenomenon that the physicist Henri Becquerel had recently discovered: certain elements emitted rays of energy without any obvious external source. Marie coined the word "radioactivity" to describe this property. Her hypothesis — radical at the time — was that radioactivity was an atomic property, not the result of any chemical reaction between substances.

Working in a converted leaking shed with inadequate ventilation, processing tonnes of the mineral pitchblende with their own hands, the Curies discovered two new elements in 1898. They named the first polonium, after Marie's occupied homeland. The second they named radium. In 1903, Marie Curie became the first woman in history to be awarded a Nobel Prize — shared with Pierre and Becquerel — for the discovery of radioactivity.

---

Then, on the nineteenth of April, 1906, Pierre slipped in the rain on a busy Paris street and fell under the wheels of a horse-drawn cart. He was killed instantly. Marie was devastated — she had lost her husband, her closest collaborator, and the companion of her intellectual life in a single moment of ordinary misfortune. The Sorbonne offered her his professorship, making her the first woman to hold a full professorship at that institution. She accepted and continued working.

In 1911, she became the first person — man or woman — to be awarded two Nobel Prizes, this time in chemistry, for isolating pure radium and characterising its properties. The achievement was met with hostility as much as celebration. A scandal in the French press, focussing on her relationship with a colleague, led to a campaign to deny her entry to the French Academy of Sciences. She was never admitted. France denied her what her adopted country owed her.

During the First World War, she developed mobile X-ray units — nicknamed petites Curies — that she drove to field hospitals herself, training doctors to use radiation technology to locate bullets and shrapnel in wounded soldiers. She estimated that over a million soldiers were X-rayed using the equipment she deployed. She worked throughout the war at personal risk and enormous physical cost.

---

She died on the fourth of July, 1934, of aplastic anaemia — her bone marrow destroyed by decades of exposure to radiation she had discovered but whose dangers she could not have fully understood. She was sixty-six years old. Her daughter Irène would go on to win her own Nobel Prize in chemistry in 1935, making the Curies the most decorated scientific family in history.

Marie Curie remains the only person ever to win Nobel Prizes in two different sciences. She opened the atomic age, laid the foundations for nuclear medicine, and made contributions to physics and chemistry that underpinned both the development of cancer treatment and the eventual creation of nuclear weapons — a legacy as complicated as any in scientific history.

She did all of this while fighting every barrier the world of her time placed in front of women and immigrants — barriers of language, nationality, gender, and poverty. That those barriers delayed but could not stop her is the most remarkable thing about a life full of remarkable things.
