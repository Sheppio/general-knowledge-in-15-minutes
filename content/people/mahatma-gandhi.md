# Mahatma Gandhi: The Power of Nonviolence

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

On the twelfth of March, 1930, a sixty-year-old man in a white cotton loincloth walked out of his ashram near Ahmedabad in western India and began walking south toward the sea. He was accompanied by seventy-eight followers. By the time he reached the coast — twenty-four days and two hundred and forty miles later — tens of thousands of people had joined the march or lined the roads to watch. On the sixth of April, Mahatma Gandhi stooped, picked up a small handful of salt from the mudflats at Dandi, and broke the law. The British colonial government held a monopoly on salt; making your own was illegal. It was a deliberately simple act of defiance, chosen precisely because salt was something every Indian needed, and its taxation by a foreign power was an insult no one could pretend to misunderstand. Within weeks, hundreds of thousands of Indians were making salt illegally along the coastline. More than sixty thousand people were arrested. The British Empire had been given the clearest possible demonstration of the method its most dangerous opponent had spent decades refining.

That method was satyagraha — a word Gandhi coined that can be roughly translated as "truth force" or "soul force." It was not passive resistance in any simple sense. It was an active, disciplined, morally demanding practice of nonviolent confrontation — refusing to cooperate with injustice, accepting the consequences of that refusal without retaliation, and thereby placing the moral burden on the oppressor while exposing the violence inherent in their system. Gandhi had developed it not in India but in South Africa, where he had spent over twenty years, and where he had first encountered the systematic humiliation of racial discrimination in a form that could not be ignored.

---

Mohandas Karamchand Gandhi was born on the second of October, 1869, in Porbandar, a coastal town in what is now the Indian state of Gujarat. His family was from the merchant caste. He was a shy, unremarkable child, neither especially brilliant academically nor physically impressive. He went to London at eighteen to study law — a frightening adventure for a young man from a conservative Hindu family, navigating an alien city and trying to maintain his vows while surrounded by English food and manners. He passed the bar in 1891 and returned to India to practice, where he struggled. In 1893, he accepted a position with an Indian law firm in South Africa. He expected to stay a year. He stayed for twenty-one.

South Africa changed everything. On his first journey, travelling by train through Natal, Gandhi was ejected from a first-class compartment because a white passenger objected to sharing it with an Indian. He spent the night on a cold railway platform, shivering, thinking. The humiliation was not new — he had experienced discrimination before — but the cold clarity of the night forced a decision. He could return to India and forget it, or he could fight. He chose to fight, and what he discovered over the following decades was that the methods available to him were not conventional political methods but something more radical: organised, disciplined, nonviolent resistance on a mass scale.

By 1914, after years of campaigns against discriminatory legislation in South Africa — campaigns that won real, if partial, victories — Gandhi had developed satyagraha into a coherent philosophy and a practical tool. He returned to India in January 1915 at the invitation of the Congress movement, already a figure of significant reputation. The poet Rabindranath Tagore had given him the title Mahatma — Great Soul. He was forty-five years old, and his most consequential work still lay ahead of him.

---

Gandhi spent his first years back in India travelling across the subcontinent, learning its realities, and leading carefully chosen local campaigns that demonstrated his method: the indigo farmers of Champaran in 1917, the textile workers of Ahmedabad in 1918. These were his proving grounds. In 1919, the Amritsar Massacre — in which British troops under General Reginald Dyer opened fire on a large, unarmed crowd gathered in a walled garden, killing several hundred people, possibly more than a thousand — transformed the political landscape. Gandhi's response was not more violence but a non-cooperation movement: a call for Indians to boycott British goods, courts, and institutions and to refuse participation in the machinery of colonial rule. It was the largest campaign of civil disobedience the British Empire had yet faced.

The British imprisoned him. Gandhi accepted imprisonment as a normal part of the work — he spent nearly seven years in prison over the course of his life, time he used to read, to write, to spin cotton on his wheel. The spinning wheel was not just a symbol; it was a practical instrument of economic disobedience. If Indians wore homespun cloth — khadi — rather than machine-made textiles imported from English mills, they would undermine one of the central economic pillars of the colonial relationship. The sight of Gandhi at his spinning wheel became one of the most recognisable images of the twentieth century.

The Salt March of 1930 brought the independence movement to the attention of the world in a new way. Film cameras captured the images; newspapers around the globe ran the story. The moral clarity of the act — an old man making salt — against the backdrop of imperial law made the position of the British government look absurd. When police beat salt protesters at the Dharasana salt works while the protesters refused to resist or flee, the American journalist Webb Miller's dispatches were read by millions and caused outrage far beyond India.

---

Gandhi's relationship with the British was complex and constitutionally unusual. He admired aspects of British civilisation — the rule of law, certain traditions of parliamentary debate — while insisting that the British had no right to rule India. He wanted independence; he did not want revolution in the Marxist sense, class warfare, or the destruction of existing social structures. His vision was of a free India built on villages, on cottage industry, on moral self-sufficiency — a vision that put him at odds not only with the British but with many of his Congress colleagues, who wanted a modern industrial state.

His relationship with the Muslim minority of India was perhaps the most painful chapter of his political life. Gandhi worked throughout his career for Hindu-Muslim unity, seeing the division between the two communities as one of the greatest obstacles to Indian independence and one of the greatest dangers to an independent India's future. He largely failed. The partition of the subcontinent on independence in August 1947 — the creation of a Muslim-majority Pakistan and a Hindu-majority India — was, in Gandhi's view, a catastrophe, and he refused to attend the independence celebrations. The violence that accompanied partition — perhaps one million killed in communal massacres, some fifteen million displaced in one of the largest refugee movements in history — was exactly the outcome he had spent decades trying to prevent.

On the thirtieth of January, 1948, Gandhi was walking to an evening prayer meeting in the gardens of Birla House in Delhi when a young Hindu nationalist named Nathuram Godse approached him, bowed, and fired three shots into his chest. Gandhi died immediately. He was seventy-eight years old. Godse, who resented Gandhi's perceived sympathy toward Muslims during the violence of partition, was tried and hanged.

---

The influence Gandhi left behind is both enormous and complicated. His method of nonviolent civil disobedience became one of the most powerful political tools of the twentieth century. It shaped Martin Luther King Jr. directly — King read Gandhi carefully and acknowledged the debt explicitly, describing nonviolent resistance as the only morally sound and practically effective response to American segregation. It influenced Nelson Mandela, the anti-apartheid movement, the civil rights campaigns of Eastern Europe, and the Arab Spring. The idea that an unarmed population, disciplined and determined, could effectively challenge and defeat powerful state violence — that idea is Gandhi's most enduring contribution to political thought.

But the complications are real. Gandhi's views on race, expressed in his early South African writings before he developed his mature philosophy, were troubling — he distinguished between Indians and Black Africans in ways that later advocates of universal human dignity could not accept. His positions on caste, on women's roles, and on industrialisation were contested in his own lifetime and remain so. He was not a saint in the sense of being without serious flaw, and he would probably have resisted being described as one.

What he was, was something rarer: a politician who placed morality at the centre of political method, who believed that how you fought for freedom mattered as much as whether you won, and who demonstrated — in the most concrete possible terms — that this was not naivety but strategy. The British Empire left India. The nonviolent campaign did not do this alone, but it was the indispensable moral core of a movement that an empire could not simply suppress without losing the legitimacy it needed to survive.
