# Ada Lovelace: The First Programmer

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Ada Lovelace died in eighteen fifty-two at the age of thirty-six, a hundred years before the first electronic computer was built. The program she wrote — an algorithm for calculating Bernoulli numbers on a machine that was never completed — sat in the archives, occasionally cited by historians of mathematics, for over a century before the computer age arrived and people went looking for its origins. What they found was extraordinary: a nineteenth-century woman who had not only grasped the concept of a general-purpose calculating engine but had understood, more clearly than its inventor, what such a machine could ultimately do. She was, by any reasonable measure, the first computer programmer — and she saw further into the future of computing than almost anyone who came after her for decades.

She was born Ada Byron on the tenth of December, eighteen fifteen, in London, the only legitimate child of the poet George Gordon Byron — Lord Byron — and his wife Anne Isabella Milbanke. The marriage lasted only a year. Byron left England in disgrace — the rumours included affairs, debts, and accusations of a relationship with his half-sister — when Ada was five weeks old, and she never saw him again. He died in Greece in eighteen twenty-four, when Ada was eight. She grew up with her mother, who was determined that her daughter should not inherit what she regarded as her father's dangerous, irrational qualities. Ada's education was therefore steered emphatically toward mathematics and logic: cold, precise subjects that her mother hoped would counteract the Byronic temperament she feared in her daughter.

---

The strategy was successful in unexpected ways. Ada turned out to have a genuine gift for mathematics, combined with an imagination that her mother's educational programme had not suppressed but had instead given a precise and rigorous form. At the age of seventeen, she was introduced to Charles Babbage, a Cambridge mathematician and engineer who had spent years designing mechanical calculating machines, at one of his celebrated Saturday evening gatherings on the fifth of June, eighteen thirty-three. The meeting was transformative for both of them.

Babbage had already spent most of a decade designing and partially building the Difference Engine — a machine that could compute and print mathematical tables automatically, eliminating the errors that human calculators inevitably introduced. He had received substantial government funding but the project had become mired in disputes with his chief engineer. When Ada met him, he was already thinking about something more ambitious: the Analytical Engine, a vastly more powerful machine that would not be limited to a single type of calculation but would be programmable, able to perform any calculation according to instructions fed into it on punched cards — a mechanism borrowed from the Jacquard loom, which used punched cards to control the patterns woven by mechanical looms.

Ada was captivated by the Analytical Engine and by the mind behind it. She began a correspondence with Babbage that would last until her death, and studied mathematics with the logician Augustus De Morgan, one of the most distinguished mathematical minds in England. In eighteen forty, Babbage gave a lecture on the Analytical Engine in Turin, which was attended by an Italian military engineer named Luigi Menabrea, who published an account of it in French in a Swiss scientific journal in eighteen forty-two. Someone — perhaps Babbage himself, perhaps a mutual acquaintance — suggested that Ada translate the article into English.

---

She did more than translate it. The notes she added to her translation were three times as long as the article itself, and they transformed what had been a technical description of a machine into something much more significant: a conceptual account of what a general-purpose programmable computing engine could do and how it should be understood.

Note G — the seventh and final of Ada's notes, and the longest — described in precise detail an algorithm for calculating Bernoulli numbers using the Analytical Engine. The algorithm specified the sequence of operations the machine would need to perform, the variables it would need to store, the conditions it would need to evaluate. It is, by the consensus of historians, the first complete computer program ever written: the first example of someone specifying, step by step, the operations that a computing machine should perform to solve a defined mathematical problem.

But Note G, remarkable as it was, was less far-sighted than some of Ada's other observations. In Note A, she addressed the question of what distinguished the Analytical Engine from earlier calculating machines — and in doing so, she described, with astonishing prescience, the concept of a general-purpose computer. She argued that the Analytical Engine "weaves algebraical patterns just as the Jacquard-loom weaves flowers and leaves." And she went further: she speculated that such a machine might eventually be used not only for numerical calculation but for "other things besides number" — for composing music, for example, if the relations between musical notes could be expressed in terms the machine could process.

---

This was a leap that Babbage himself had not made. He thought of his machine as a calculating engine, enormously powerful but fundamentally numerical. Ada understood that if the machine could manipulate symbols according to rules, the symbols need not represent numbers — they could represent anything that could be given a formal structure. This insight, which would be central to the development of computing a hundred years later, was articulated clearly in eighteen forty-three by a woman who had never seen a working computer and was working with a machine that existed mostly on paper.

She also articulated, with equal clarity, a limitation that would still be important a century later. The Analytical Engine, she wrote, could only do what it was instructed to do: "It can do whatever we know how to order it to perform. It has no power of originating anything. It can only do what we tell it." The question of whether machines can originate — whether they can do something genuinely new and creative rather than merely execute instructions — is still actively debated in discussions of artificial intelligence, and Ada's formulation of it in eighteen forty-three is still a useful reference point.

---

The translation and notes were published in eighteen forty-three under Ada's initials, A.A.L. — Augusta Ada Lovelace, the title she had acquired through her marriage in eighteen thirty-five to William King, later created Earl of Lovelace. The article attracted some attention but did not transform her standing in the scientific world; women in nineteenth-century England were excluded from the scientific institutions in which reputation was made. Babbage never built the Analytical Engine. Funding ran out, priorities shifted, and the machine remained a set of drawings and a few partial components. Ada's notes survived in those archives.

In her later years, Ada suffered from illness — probably uterine cancer, though the diagnosis was not precise by the standards of the time — and became entangled in a gambling syndicate that left her in considerable debt. She died on the twenty-seventh of November, eighteen fifty-two, and was buried beside her father, the poet Lord Byron, in the Church of St. Mary Magdalene in Hucknall, Nottinghamshire — a pairing that seems both poetically appropriate and slightly incongruous.

Her recognition came posthumously and slowly. When the historian and mathematician B.V. Bowden edited a survey of early computing in nineteen fifty-three, he reprinted her notes on the Analytical Engine and brought her work to the attention of the computing community. In nineteen eighty, the United States Department of Defense named a newly standardised programming language Ada in her honour. Every year since two thousand and nine, Ada Lovelace Day is celebrated on the second Tuesday of October, recognising the contributions of women to science, technology, engineering, and mathematics. She has become, in the twenty-first century, a symbol of what women were capable of in an age that gave them almost no institutional recognition for it.
