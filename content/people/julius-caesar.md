# Julius Caesar: The Man Who Made Himself Rome

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Julius Caesar did not found the Roman Empire. That was the work of his adopted son Octavian, who became Augustus and ruled for more than forty years. But Caesar made the Empire inevitable. By the time he died on the fifteenth of March, 44 before the common era, stabbed twenty-three times on the floor of the Senate by men who thought they were saving the Roman Republic, the Republic was already beyond saving. Caesar had not created the instabilities that destroyed it — a century of civil strife, class conflict, military commanders whose loyalty ran to their generals rather than the state, and a constitutional order that could not cope with the scale and complexity of an empire — but he had exploited those instabilities with a genius that no one before or since has quite matched, and he had demonstrated conclusively that one man could seize control of the whole machinery of Roman power.

His name became a title. The word "Kaiser" — the German word for emperor — is Caesar. "Tsar" — the Russian word — is Caesar. The month of July is named after him. He reformed the Roman calendar in a version that, with a minor correction made in 1582, is still the basis of the calendar we use today. He is one of the most written-about figures in all of history: as soldier, politician, prose stylist, and icon of supreme ambition, he has fascinated every generation from his own to ours.

---

Gaius Julius Caesar was born on the twelfth or thirteenth of July, in the year 100 before the common era, into one of Rome's oldest and most distinguished patrician families. The Julii claimed descent from the goddess Venus — a genealogy Caesar took seriously as a political tool, if perhaps not as literal fact. His family was aristocratic in lineage but not particularly wealthy or politically powerful in his boyhood. Rome in the first century before the common era was a city convulsed by class struggle: between the optimates, the conservative senatorial aristocracy who believed power should remain with the Senate, and the populares, politicians who sought to build power through the support of the common people and the army. Caesar, despite his patrician birth, identified from early in his career with the populares.

He came of age during a period of extraordinary violence. The dictator Sulla, who had seized Rome by marching an army against it in 88 before the common era and again in 83, had carried out a systematic massacre of his political enemies. The young Caesar was connected by marriage to Sulla's opponents, and he narrowly avoided inclusion in the proscription lists. He spent time away from Rome until Sulla's death and then returned to build his career through the courts, public oratory, and military service.

An early episode reveals something essential about his character. While sailing to study rhetoric in Greece, he was captured by Cilician pirates and held for ransom. He famously told his captors that their asking price was too low and that he was worth more. He passed the weeks of his captivity joking with his kidnappers, joining their games, reading them his poetry, and informing them — with apparent good humour — that he intended to have them crucified when he was released. He was released, raised a fleet at his own expense, hunted the pirates down, and had them crucified.

---

Caesar's political career moved through the standard Roman offices — quaestor, aedile, praetor — with increasing speed and expenditure. He borrowed enormous sums to finance public spectacles, games, and construction projects that built his popular support, and by the year 60 before the common era he was one of the most prominent politicians in Rome. He needed a way to pay off his debts and build on his influence. He found it in an unlikely alliance.

The First Triumvirate — an informal, private agreement between Caesar, the military hero Pompey the Great, and the enormously wealthy Crassus — was formed in 60 before the common era. It was not a formal institution; Rome had nothing comparable. It was an agreement among three of the most powerful men in the Republic to cooperate for their mutual advantage: Pompey wanted his military settlements ratified, Crassus wanted tax farming contracts confirmed, and Caesar wanted a military command. The alliance would, over the following decade, dominate Roman politics — and ultimately destroy the Republic it was using.

Caesar secured the consulship for 59 before the common era and then, more importantly, the governorship of Gaul — the territory of modern France, Belgium, and parts of Switzerland and Germany — for an extended term. This was the platform he needed. In Gaul, he would build a personal army, accumulate vast wealth, and earn military glory. He would be out of Rome and beyond the reach of his enemies for years. The Gallic Wars would make him the most dangerous man in the world.

---

The Gallic Wars lasted from 58 to 50 before the common era. In those eight years, Caesar led his legions through what remains one of the most audacious and ruthless campaigns of conquest in ancient history. He subdued the whole of Gaul — a territory populated by dozens of independent Celtic tribes — through a combination of tactical brilliance, calculated terror, alliance, and diplomacy. He crossed the Rhine twice to intimidate the Germanic tribes beyond it. He twice crossed the Channel to Britain — the first Roman commander to do so — in what were more demonstrations of Roman reach than territorial conquests. He besieged the great Gallic chieftain Vercingetorix at Alesia in 52 before the common era in a feat of military engineering so extraordinary that it is still studied in military academies: simultaneously besieging Alesia from the inside and constructing a second ring of fortifications to hold off a massive Gallic relief army from the outside.

Caesar wrote about all of this in his Commentarii de Bello Gallico — seven books of prose in the third person, presenting his campaigns as a series of military problems solved with calm competence. They were political documents as much as military memoirs, designed to build his reputation in Rome while he was absent from it. They were also, incidentally, some of the best prose written in Latin: clear, vivid, and dispassionate in a way that somehow made the scale of the violence more disturbing rather than less. Caesar, by his own account, killed or enslaved approximately a million Gauls. Modern historians consider this figure plausible. It was the foundation on which his subsequent career was built.

---

By 49 before the common era, the alliance between Caesar, Pompey, and Crassus had collapsed. Crassus had died on campaign in the east. Pompey had drifted into alignment with the Senate and the optimates, who feared Caesar's power and popularity. The Senate ordered Caesar to disband his army before returning to Rome. Caesar knew that to comply would be to strip himself of protection and hand himself to his enemies' justice. On an early morning in January of 49 before the common era, he led his troops across the Rubicon — a small river in northern Italy that marked the legal boundary of Roman Italy, the crossing of which with an army was an act of treason. "The die is cast," he is reported to have said.

He said it was a short sentence for the longest gamble in Roman history. Pompey, unprepared and unwilling to fight in Italy, retreated to Greece with most of the Senate. Caesar swept south and took Rome without a battle. He then spent the next several years in a pursuit that took him across the Mediterranean world. He decisively defeated Pompey's forces at the Battle of Pharsalus in Greece on the ninth of August, 48 before the common era. Pompey fled to Egypt, where he was murdered by an Egyptian courtier hoping to curry favour with the victor. Caesar followed to Egypt, where he met and formed an alliance — personal as well as political — with the young queen Cleopatra, who was herself in a struggle for her throne.

He returned to Rome in 45 before the common era, having defeated the last pockets of Pompeian resistance. He was appointed dictator perpetuo — dictator for life — in February of 44 before the common era. He refused the title of king, understanding that "king" carried fatal associations in a Rome that had expelled its last king five centuries earlier and defined its identity by that expulsion. But the distinction between dictator for life and king was not one that his opponents could see.

---

A group of approximately sixty senators, led by Marcus Junius Brutus and Gaius Cassius Longinus, resolved to kill him. Their motivations were mixed: some were genuine republicans who believed the assassination would restore the Senate's authority; others were personal rivals who saw Caesar's dominance as an obstacle to their own ambitions. They called themselves the Liberatores — the liberators.

On the fifteenth of March, 44 before the common era — the Ides of March — Caesar came to a meeting of the Senate. Normally he was accompanied by a bodyguard; that day, having recently dismissed it as incompatible with the image of a man willing to live among his fellow citizens, he came alone. The conspirators surrounded him, and the attack began. He was stabbed twenty-three times. He died at the base of a statue of Pompey. Whether, as Shakespeare has it, he turned to Brutus and said "Et tu, Brute?" — "Even you, Brutus?" — no ancient source confirms. But that the moment carried a particular quality of personal betrayal is clear from the ancient accounts.

The conspirators had been confident that killing Caesar would restore the Republic. They were catastrophically wrong. They had solved nothing, because the problem was not Caesar himself but the conditions that had produced him. Rome dissolved into civil war again. The years that followed were as violent as any in Roman history. It took another round of conflict, in which Caesar's friend Mark Antony and his heir Octavian first fought together against the conspirators and then against each other, before Octavian emerged as the last man standing and became Augustus — the first Roman emperor. The Republic that Caesar's assassins had tried to save died with him on the steps of the Senate. The Empire that would rule the Mediterranean for five centuries had begun.
