# Joseph Stalin: Terror, Power, and the Making of the Soviet State

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Joseph Stalin ruled the Soviet Union for more than a quarter of a century, transforming it from a largely agricultural country into an industrial and military superpower while presiding over a terror that killed millions of his own citizens. He is one of the most consequential and most destructive leaders in modern history — a man who genuinely industrialised a vast nation and helped defeat Nazi Germany, and who simultaneously organised famines, mass executions, and a system of forced labour camps that consumed the lives of an almost incomprehensible number of people. Understanding Stalin requires holding both of these things at once: the achievement and the atrocity, the power and the paranoia, the order imposed on chaos and the chaos that order itself created.

He was born on the eighteenth of December, eighteen seventy-eight, in Gori, a small town in Georgia, then part of the Russian Empire. His father was a cobbler, his mother a domestic worker, and he grew up in poverty in a household marked by his father's violence. He was educated at a church school and then at a theological seminary in Tiflis — the future capital of Soviet Georgia — where he was exposed to revolutionary ideas and expelled before graduating. By his early twenties he had committed himself to the Bolshevik cause, becoming an organiser and agitator, funding party activities through bank robberies and extortion, repeatedly arrested by the Tsarist secret police and repeatedly escaping from Siberian exile. Lenin valued him as a practical organiser — a man who could get things done in the messy, dangerous world of underground revolutionary politics.

---

The Russian Revolution of nineteen seventeen brought the Bolsheviks to power, and Stalin rose through the party hierarchy, becoming General Secretary of the Communist Party in nineteen twenty-two. This was initially regarded as an administrative role, and Lenin's more glamorous colleagues — above all Leon Trotsky, the creator of the Red Army — did not think it particularly significant. They were wrong. Stalin used his control of the party's administrative apparatus to build a network of loyalty that would prove decisive in the power struggle that followed Lenin's death.

Lenin died on the twenty-first of January, nineteen twenty-four, without having designated a clear successor and having written a private testament that criticised Stalin and recommended his removal from the General Secretaryship. The testament was suppressed. Over the following years, Stalin outmanoeuvred each of his rivals in turn — allying with some to destroy others, then turning on his former allies — until by the late nineteen-twenties he was the undisputed master of the Soviet state. Trotsky, his most dangerous rival, was expelled from the party in nineteen twenty-seven, exiled from the Soviet Union in nineteen twenty-nine, and eventually murdered in Mexico City in nineteen forty by a Soviet agent with an ice axe.

---

Once in power, Stalin launched a programme of forced transformation more radical in its speed and violence than anything the Bolsheviks had previously attempted. The first Five Year Plan, begun in nineteen twenty-eight, set targets for industrial output that were unrealistic by any objective measure and were met, where they were met at all, through extraordinary coercion. Factories were built by forced labour; workers who failed to meet norms were punished; entire industries were created in the space of years where nothing had existed before. The industrial transformation was real — the Soviet Union did become an industrial power of the first rank during the Stalin years — but the human cost was staggering.

The agricultural policy was, if anything, more destructive. Private farming was abolished and replaced by collective farms — kolkhozy — into which peasants were compelled to pool their land, livestock, and tools. Those classified as "kulaks" — prosperous peasants, in practice often anyone who owned more than a minimum of property or who resisted collectivisation — were subjected to a campaign of terror: their property was confiscated, they were deported to remote regions of the Soviet Union, sent to labour camps, or shot. Millions died in the process of collectivisation itself, and the disruption to agriculture contributed directly to the great famine of nineteen thirty-two and nineteen thirty-three, which killed millions across the Soviet Union. In Ukraine, the famine — known as the Holodomor — was especially severe, killing an estimated three to five million people and is now recognised by many countries as a genocide: a deliberate use of starvation as a weapon against the Ukrainian people.

---

The terror that defined Stalin's rule reached its most systematic and intensive form in the Great Purge of nineteen thirty-six to nineteen thirty-eight. Stalin moved against his real and imagined enemies across every sector of Soviet society: the Old Bolsheviks who had made the revolution were tried in a series of show trials, forced through a combination of torture and psychological manipulation to confess to implausible crimes, and then shot. The army was decimated: of the Red Army's five marshals, three were shot and one died in captivity; of the army's eight highest-ranking commanders, all eight were executed. The party, the government, the diplomatic service, the arts, the sciences — no institution was spared. Official records show that at least seven hundred thousand people were executed during the Great Purge years, and the true figure was probably higher.

Running alongside the executions was the Gulag — the system of forced labour camps that stretched across the vast territories of the Soviet Union, from Siberia to the Arctic Circle to Central Asia. An estimated eighteen million people passed through the Gulag system between its expansion in the nineteen-thirties and Stalin's death. Many died: from cold, hunger, overwork, and brutality. The Gulag was not only a system of punishment; it was an economic institution, designed to extract labour from those deemed politically unreliable and to use that labour for the industrial projects the regime needed — building roads, canals, mines, and forests with human bodies where machines were unavailable or unaffordable.

---

The Second World War transformed Stalin's position from the ruthless ruler of a state perpetually at war with its own people to a wartime leader of the coalition that defeated fascism. The Molotov-Ribbentrop Pact of August twenty-third, nineteen thirty-nine — the non-aggression agreement between the Soviet Union and Nazi Germany — had shocked the world and bought Stalin time to continue his industrial buildup. But the German invasion on the twenty-second of June, nineteen forty-one — Operation Barbarossa — caught the Soviet Union catastrophically unprepared. Stalin had dismissed warnings from his own intelligence services and from the British government; his purge of the military leadership had stripped the Red Army of its most experienced commanders. In the first weeks of the invasion, the Wehrmacht advanced hundreds of miles and took millions of Soviet prisoners.

Stalin's initial response was paralysis — for several days after the invasion began, he retreated to his dacha and was unreachable. But he recovered, and the war became the defining episode of his leadership. His decision to remain in Moscow when the Germans came within a few miles of the city in October and November nineteen forty-one was of enormous symbolic importance. The Battle of Stalingrad — the massive Soviet counteroffensive that encircled and destroyed a German army of some three hundred thousand men, ending in German surrender on the second of February, nineteen forty-three — was the turning point of the war in the east, and it was fought with a ferocity that reflected the entire ethos of Stalinist rule. Soviet soldiers who retreated without orders could be shot. Order number two hundred and twenty-seven — "Not one step back" — was the principle on which the battle was won.

The Soviet Union's role in the defeat of Nazi Germany was decisive. The Eastern Front accounted for the great majority of German military losses, and the human cost to the Soviet Union was staggering: estimates of Soviet military and civilian deaths in the Second World War range from twenty-seven million upward. That victory gave Stalin enormous prestige, and in the post-war settlement at Yalta and Potsdam he secured Soviet domination of Eastern Europe — a cordon of satellite states that would define the Cold War division of the continent for four decades.

---

In his final years, Stalin's paranoia intensified. He was planning a new purge in the early nineteen-fifties, apparently targeting the Soviet Jewish community and possibly the party leadership itself, when he died. On the fifth of March, nineteen fifty-three, he suffered a stroke and died without regaining consciousness, having lain on the floor of his dacha for hours before his terrified staff dared to approach him.

His death provoked an outpouring of grief across the Soviet Union that was genuine in many cases — the cult of personality he had constructed was of such overwhelming force that millions of Soviet citizens had internalised it — and relief in others, felt but not expressible. His successor, Nikita Khrushchev, delivered a "secret speech" to the Twentieth Party Congress in nineteen fifty-six that catalogued some of Stalin's crimes and inaugurated a period of "de-Stalinisation." The secret speech was quickly not very secret at all: copies leaked and circulated, and for the first time the crimes of the Stalin era were acknowledged officially, if incompletely.

Stalin remains, in Russia and internationally, one of the most contested figures of the twentieth century. His industrial achievements, his defeat of fascism, and his transformation of the Soviet Union into a superpower are cited by those who argue that the costs, however terrible, were the price of survival and power. His terror, his famines, his labour camps, and his mass executions — the deaths of millions of people whose names were largely unknown to the world while they lived and who remain unknown now — are cited by those who argue that whatever was achieved under his rule was achieved in spite of him rather than because of him, and that no scale of achievement can justify what was done in his name.
