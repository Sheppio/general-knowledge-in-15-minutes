# Mao Zedong: Revolution, Catastrophe, and the Making of Modern China

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Mao Zedong ruled China for more than a quarter of a century, from the proclamation of the People's Republic in October 1949 until his death in September 1976. In that time, he transformed one of the world's most ancient civilisations more radically than any ruler in its four-thousand-year history. He ended a century of humiliation and civil war, expelled foreign powers, and unified a country that had been fragmented and dominated by outside forces since the mid-nineteenth century. He also presided over two catastrophes of such magnitude that they rank among the worst man-made disasters in human history. The Great Leap Forward — his programme to rapidly industrialise China through the forced collectivisation of agriculture — produced a famine that killed between fifteen and fifty-five million people in three years. The Cultural Revolution — his decade-long campaign against enemies real and imagined within Chinese society — destroyed millions of lives, purged an entire generation of intellectuals and officials, and came close to tearing apart the social fabric of the nation.

How to weigh these things is not a question with a clean answer, and the Chinese Communist Party — which continues to govern China today — has not helped clarify it. The official verdict, issued in 1981, credits Mao with having been "seventy percent correct and thirty percent wrong" — an assessment that is both mathematically precise and morally evasive. What is certain is that understanding Mao is inseparable from understanding contemporary China: the party he built, the institutions he shaped, and the trauma he inflicted are all still present in the China of today.

---

Mao Zedong was born on the twenty-sixth of December, 1893, in the village of Shaoshan in Hunan province, the son of a prosperous peasant farmer. He was a voracious reader who educated himself on Chinese classical literature and history, and who came to see himself in the mould of the great emperors and strategists of China's past. He moved to Changsha, the provincial capital, to study, and arrived there in 1911 just as the Qing dynasty — the last imperial dynasty — was collapsing in revolution. The republic that replaced it was weak and divided, prey to warlords and foreign interference. China's position in the early twentieth century — reduced from the world's largest economy to a semicolonial dependency, humiliated by the unequal treaties imposed by European powers and Japan — was the formative context of Mao's political life.

He was a founding member of the Chinese Communist Party, established in 1921. The Party's early years were shaped by its alliance with the Nationalist Party (Kuomintang), its dependence on Soviet advice, and its catastrophic defeat when Chiang Kai-shek turned on the Communists in 1927, slaughtering thousands in Shanghai and forcing the survivors underground. Mao drew his own conclusions from these defeats: instead of building a worker-based urban movement in the Soviet style, he turned to the peasantry — the vast majority of China's population — as the base for revolutionary power. This was a heterodox position, and it brought him into conflict with Party leaders who followed Moscow's line. But events would vindicate him.

---

The decade of the nineteen-thirties was one of near-continuous crisis. The Japanese invasion of Manchuria in 1931 and the subsequent invasion of northern China in 1937 added an external enemy to the civil war. In October 1934, facing annihilation by Nationalist forces, the main Communist army broke out of its encirclement in southeastern China and began what became known as the Long March — a six-thousand-mile retreat across some of the most difficult terrain in China, through mountains, swamps, and enemy territory, to a new base in the northwest. Of the roughly eighty-six thousand who began the march, perhaps twenty thousand survived to reach Yan'an. The Long March became the founding myth of Chinese communism: a demonstration of revolutionary will and endurance that validated the party's claim to power.

At Zunyi, during the Long March, Mao consolidated his leadership of the Party in a conference that sidelined his opponents. He would never again relinquish control. At Yan'an, where the Communists established their base, he developed the distinctive synthesis of Marxism and Chinese conditions that became known as Mao Zedong Thought — emphasising guerrilla warfare, the mobilisation of the peasantry, and the ongoing necessity of ideological struggle within the party itself.

The Second World War and the Japanese occupation of much of China transformed the political balance. The Communists fought a guerrilla war against the Japanese while the Nationalists fought a more conventional campaign; the Communists emerged from the war with more territory, more popular support, and a more effective army. When the civil war resumed after Japan's surrender in 1945, the outcome, though it took four years, was not ultimately in doubt. On the first of October, 1949, standing at Tiananmen — the Gate of Heavenly Peace — in Beijing, Mao proclaimed the establishment of the People's Republic of China.

---

The early years of the People's Republic brought genuine achievements. Land reform redistributed property from landlords to peasants — though it was accompanied by mass executions of landlords, estimated in the hundreds of thousands to millions. Literacy campaigns reduced illiteracy dramatically. Health campaigns extended life expectancy. Industrialisation, modelled on the Soviet Union, began to transform the urban economy. Mao's China was poor, authoritarian, and violent — but it was unified, and after a century of humiliation it was, in some respects, being rebuilt from the ground up.

The catastrophe began in 1958. The Great Leap Forward — launched in January of that year — was Mao's attempt to overtake the developed world through the sheer force of collective willpower. Agricultural communes were merged into enormous people's communes; peasants were ordered to produce steel in backyard furnaces rather than tend their fields; unrealistically high grain procurement quotas were set and rigidly enforced even as harvests failed. The combination of misguided policy, local officials falsifying production figures to please their superiors, and natural disaster produced the Great Chinese Famine of 1959 to 1961. The number of people who died remains contested and politically sensitive in China, but most serious scholarly estimates range from fifteen to fifty-five million; a range of thirty to forty-five million is common in the academic literature. By any measure, it was one of the worst famines in human history.

Mao knew of the reports of starvation; he continued the grain exports that were sustaining China's international prestige. When the scale of the disaster became undeniable, he stepped back from day-to-day governance, allowing Liu Shaoqi and Deng Xiaoping to implement pragmatic recovery policies. But he never admitted responsibility, and he never forgave those who had implicitly criticised him by repairing the damage he had done.

---

In 1966, Mao launched the Great Proletarian Cultural Revolution, a movement ostensibly aimed at purging "capitalist roaders" and reactionary elements from the Party and society, but in practice a vehicle for reasserting his personal authority against the pragmatists who had sidelined him and for attacking the educated class he had always distrusted. He mobilised millions of young people — the Red Guards — to attack their teachers, destroy cultural heritage, persecute intellectuals, close universities, and purge officials at every level of the system. Liu Shaoqi was imprisoned and died in custody; Deng Xiaoping was sent to a factory to work as a labourer. Ancient temples and cultural artefacts were destroyed. People were killed, tortured, sent to labour camps, or driven to suicide on the basis of denunciations by their colleagues, students, or neighbours.

The Cultural Revolution lasted, in its most intense form, until the early nineteen-seventies, though Mao did not formally end it before his death. Its death toll is estimated at anywhere from five hundred thousand to two million from direct violence, with far more dying in subsequent persecution and the disruption of the health and food systems. An entire generation of China's educated population was lost — killed, imprisoned, exiled to the countryside, or prevented from practising their professions.

In his final years, Mao engineered one last international surprise: the rapprochement with the United States. President Richard Nixon's visit to China in February 1972, brokered through Henry Kissinger, transformed the geopolitical landscape of the Cold War, isolating the Soviet Union and beginning China's reintegration into the international system. It was a geopolitical masterstroke, even as the Cultural Revolution was still consuming Chinese society.

Mao died on the ninth of September, 1976. He was succeeded initially by Hua Guofeng and then, in practice, by Deng Xiaoping, who dismantled much of what Mao had built and launched the economic reforms that produced China's transformation into the world's second-largest economy. The China of the twenty-first century is Deng's China as much as Mao's — but Mao's face still hangs above Tiananmen Gate, and the party he founded still rules. The weight of his legacy — achievement and catastrophe, liberation and terror — has not been lifted, and in China, it cannot be openly discussed.
