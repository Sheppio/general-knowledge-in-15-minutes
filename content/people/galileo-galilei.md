# Galileo Galilei: The Man Who Moved the Earth

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Galileo Galilei was born on the fifteenth of February, fifteen sixty-four, in Pisa — the same year that Michelangelo died and that William Shakespeare was born. He died in January sixteen forty-two, under house arrest, condemned by the Inquisition for asserting that the Earth moves around the Sun. In between, he laid the foundations of modern physics, transformed astronomy, championed a new method of understanding the natural world based on observation and mathematics, and provoked one of the most famous confrontations between scientific knowledge and religious authority in history. He is, in almost every respect, the prototype of the scientist as the modern world understands that role: curious, precise, argumentative, committed to evidence over authority, and prepared to follow an argument to its conclusions regardless of who found those conclusions uncomfortable.

He came from a cultured but not wealthy Florentine family and was sent to the University of Pisa to study medicine. Medicine quickly gave way to mathematics and natural philosophy — the umbrella subject that encompassed what we would now call physics, astronomy, and the study of the natural world. He left Pisa without a degree but had already, by his mid-twenties, begun to challenge the Aristotelian physics that dominated European universities. Aristotle had argued that heavier bodies fall faster than lighter ones. Galileo, allegedly with a famous experiment from the Leaning Tower of Pisa and certainly through experiments with inclined planes, showed this was wrong: all bodies fall at the same rate regardless of their weight. The demonstration seems simple; its implications were enormous. If Aristotle was wrong about this basic physical fact, what else might he be wrong about?

---

He taught at Padua, in the Venetian Republic, from fifteen ninety-two to sixteen ten — the period he later described as the best eighteen years of his life. The Venetian Republic was more intellectually tolerant than the Papal States that governed much of the rest of Italy, and Galileo flourished there. He worked on the mathematics of projectile motion, on the mechanics of the pendulum, on military fortifications and engineering. He corresponded with the great astronomers of the day. In fifteen ninety-nine, the German mathematician Johannes Kepler sent him a copy of the book in which Kepler had defended the heliocentric model of the solar system — the model proposed by Copernicus in fifteen forty-three, in which the Earth and planets move around the Sun rather than the other way around. Galileo wrote back agreeing with the model's merits, while admitting he had not yet published his own views for fear of ridicule.

In sixteen oh nine, word reached Venice of a new optical instrument being made in the Netherlands — a device of lenses that could make distant objects appear close. Galileo quickly built his own version, improving the magnification substantially, and in the autumn of sixteen oh nine he turned it to the sky. What he saw over the following months shattered the old astronomy. The Moon was not a perfect smooth sphere — it had mountains and craters, just like the Earth. The Milky Way was not a luminous cloud but a multitude of individual stars. Most dramatically, on the night of the seventh of January, sixteen ten, he observed three small stars close to Jupiter in a straight line, and noticed over the following nights that they changed their positions relative to Jupiter while remaining always near it. By the twenty-ninth of January he had established that there were four such objects, orbiting Jupiter in regular periods. They were moons — bodies orbiting a planet that was not the Earth — and their existence demolished one of the central arguments against the Copernican system: that if the Earth moved, the Moon would be left behind.

---

Galileo published these findings in March sixteen ten in a short book called Sidereus Nuncius — the Starry Messenger. The book was a sensation. Within weeks it was being read across Europe; within months, Galileo had been appointed Chief Mathematician and Philosopher to the Grand Duke of Tuscany, leaving Padua for Florence. He continued his telescopic observations: he discovered that Venus shows phases like the Moon — which could only be explained if Venus orbited the Sun, not the Earth — and observed that Saturn appeared to have strange protrusions on either side, which he could not explain and which turned out to be its rings, invisible as rings with the resolution his telescope afforded.

By sixteen ten, Galileo was publicly identified as a champion of Copernican astronomy, and the tensions with Church authority that would define the rest of his life were beginning to take shape. The Roman Catholic Church had not formally condemned Copernicus's heliocentric model, but many theologians were uncomfortable with it: certain passages of Scripture appeared to describe the Sun moving through the sky, and a cosmology that moved the Earth seemed to contradict the Christian account of creation. Galileo engaged these theological objections directly in a series of letters arguing that Scripture was not intended as a guide to natural philosophy and that its authority should not be invoked in matters of astronomy. This was a reasonable position — and one that subsequent Church teaching would eventually adopt — but advancing it in sixteen thirteen and sixteen fourteen was aggressive and politically unwise.

---

In sixteen fifteen and sixteen sixteen, the Inquisition investigated the Copernican system and concluded that the heliocentric model was formally heretical. Galileo was privately warned not to hold or defend the doctrine. For the next several years he kept a lower profile. Then, in sixteen twenty-three, a longtime friend and admirer became Pope Urban VIII, and Galileo, emboldened by what he took to be a more sympathetic pontificate, began work on the book that would bring his conflict with the Church to a crisis.

The Dialogue Concerning the Two Chief World Systems, published in February sixteen thirty-two, presented the arguments for and against the Copernican system in the form of a conversation among three characters. The structure was technically even-handed, but nobody who read it was in any doubt where the author's sympathies lay: the defender of the old Ptolemaic system, named Simplicio, was made to appear as a fool, and worse, his final speech was made to echo arguments that Pope Urban VIII himself had used. The Pope was furious. The Inquisition summoned Galileo to Rome.

In June sixteen thirty-three, at the age of sixty-nine and in poor health, Galileo was tried before the Inquisition, found vehemently suspect of heresy, forced to abjure his astronomical opinions, and sentenced to house arrest for the remainder of his life. The legend — almost certainly apocryphal — that he muttered "and yet it moves" as he rose from his knees is too good to abandon, even as historians have abandoned it. He was confined first at the villa of the Archbishop of Siena and then at his own house in Arcetri, near Florence, where he spent his final years nearly blind, still working.

---

The work he completed under house arrest was in some ways his greatest scientific achievement. The Two New Sciences, published in Leiden in sixteen thirty-eight — outside the reach of the Roman Inquisition — set out the foundations of modern mechanics: the mathematics of uniform acceleration, the parabolic trajectories of projectiles, the behaviour of pendulums, the strength of materials. These were the foundations on which Isaac Newton would build the edifice of classical physics a half-century later. Newton, when he described himself as standing on the shoulders of giants, had Galileo among those giants.

Galileo died on the eighth of January, sixteen forty-two. He was seventy-seven years old, blind in both eyes, and had been under house arrest for nine years. The Church refused him burial in the main body of the Basilica of Santa Croce in Florence, where he had wished to be interred; he was buried in a small room adjacent to it. In seventeen thirty-seven he was given a proper tomb in the main basilica, next to Michelangelo. In nineteen ninety-two, Pope John Paul II formally acknowledged that the Inquisition had erred in condemning him — three hundred and fifty years after the condemnation was issued.
