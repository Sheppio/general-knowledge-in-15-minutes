# Cleopatra: The Last Pharaoh

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Cleopatra VII Philopator was the last active ruler of the Ptolemaic Kingdom of Egypt, and she died in thirty BC at the age of thirty-nine. She has been dead for more than two thousand years. In that time she has become one of the most famous people who ever lived — a figure reconstructed and reinvented so many times in art, literature, and popular culture that the real woman has become almost impossible to see beneath the accretion of legend. She has been imagined as a seductress, as a temptress, as a tragic lover, and more recently as a political genius — all of these images shaped more by the agendas of the people who created them than by the historical evidence. What is certain is that she was extraordinarily capable, that she navigated a world of overwhelming male power with remarkable skill, and that she held together a kingdom for more than two decades against forces that eventually proved irresistible.

The story of Cleopatra is also the story of the end of a world. She was the last of the Ptolemies — the Macedonian Greek dynasty that had ruled Egypt since the death of Alexander the Great in three twenty-three BC, when one of Alexander's generals had seized the kingdom and founded a royal line. For nearly three centuries the Ptolemies had maintained Egypt as a wealthy and powerful state at the crossroads of the Mediterranean world. By the time Cleopatra inherited the throne in fifty-one BC, that era was ending. Rome had been expanding its power across the eastern Mediterranean for more than a century, absorbing kingdoms and client states one by one. Egypt was the last great independent power of the Hellenistic world, and holding it together required everything Cleopatra had.

---

She was born in sixty-nine BC, the daughter of Ptolemy XII, a weak and dependent ruler who maintained his throne largely through the favour of Rome — and paid heavily for that favour, in money and in dignity. Cleopatra grew up watching her father navigate the humiliations of Roman clientage, and she drew her own conclusions. She was, by all accounts, extraordinarily educated — fluent in nine languages, including Egyptian, which no previous Ptolemaic ruler had bothered to learn, as well as Greek, Hebrew, Aramaic, Ethiopian, Latin, and the languages of several neighbouring kingdoms. This was not merely an accomplishment; it was a political instrument. The ability to address her subjects, her trading partners, and her enemies in their own languages gave her a range of diplomatic reach that her predecessors had never possessed.

When Ptolemy XII died in fifty-one BC, the throne passed to Cleopatra and her younger brother Ptolemy XIII, who was perhaps ten years old. Under Ptolemaic custom, a female ruler required a male co-regent, usually a brother; in practice, power resided with whoever was stronger. The first years of her reign were marked by a power struggle between Cleopatra and the faction controlling Ptolemy XIII, and by fifty BC she had been driven out of Alexandria, gathering an army in Syria to retake her throne. The confrontation was resolved by the arrival of Julius Caesar.

---

Caesar came to Alexandria in pursuit of his defeated rival Pompey in forty-eight BC, only to discover that Pompey had already been killed by the advisors of Ptolemy XIII, who hoped to win Caesar's favour with the gift of his enemy's head. It was a catastrophic miscalculation: Caesar was not pleased. Cleopatra, understanding that the Roman general held the balance of power, had herself smuggled into the royal palace — famously, according to Plutarch, rolled in a carpet — and presented herself to him directly. Caesar was captivated. Whether their relationship was purely political or also personal is a question that ancient sources answer differently, but the outcome was clear: Caesar supported Cleopatra against her brother, and after a brief war that ended with Ptolemy XIII drowning in the Nile, she was restored to the throne.

Caesar remained in Egypt through the winter of forty-eight to forty-seven BC, and the following year Cleopatra gave birth to a son, whom she named Ptolemy Caesar — known as Caesarion, or "little Caesar." Whether Caesar acknowledged paternity is contested; he never formally did so in Roman law. He left Egypt in forty-seven BC to resume his campaigns, and Cleopatra ruled alone, maintaining Egypt's position through the turbulent years of Rome's civil wars. Caesar was assassinated on the fifteenth of March, forty-four BC, and Cleopatra's position became suddenly precarious — her protector gone, Rome in upheaval, her son's claim to any inheritance uncertain.

---

The new power that emerged from Rome's civil wars was a triumvirate: Caesar's designated heir Octavian, the general Mark Antony, and the politician Lepidus. Antony took control of the eastern Mediterranean, and in forty-one BC he summoned Cleopatra to the city of Tarsus in what is now southern Turkey to answer accusations that she had aided his enemies. She arrived on a gilded barge — or so the later accounts describe it — laden with gifts and dressed as Aphrodite. Antony was overwhelmed. He returned with her to Alexandria, where he spent the winter of forty-one to forty BC, and a relationship began that would shape the final act of both their lives.

The nature of the Antony-Cleopatra alliance was strategic as much as personal. For Cleopatra it offered the military backing she needed to maintain Egyptian independence and expand her kingdom's territory; Antony granted her control over territories in Cyprus, parts of the Levant, and Cilicia. For Antony it provided the wealth of Egypt to finance his eastern campaigns, particularly his ambitions against Parthia. They had three children together: twins, Alexander Helios and Cleopatra Selene, born in forty BC, and a son, Ptolemy Philadelphus, born in thirty-six BC.

But the alliance was also a vulnerability. In Rome, Antony's enemies — above all Octavian — used the relationship to paint him as a man who had abandoned Roman values for eastern luxury, who had fallen under the spell of a foreign queen and was plotting to move the capital of the empire to Alexandria. The propaganda was effective. In thirty-two BC, Octavian persuaded the Roman senate to declare war not on Antony — a Roman citizen — but on Cleopatra, framing the coming conflict as a war against a foreign power rather than a Roman civil war.

---

The decisive confrontation came at the Battle of Actium, on the second of September, thirty-one BC, off the western coast of Greece. The naval battle ended in defeat for Antony and Cleopatra; their fleets were routed, and they fled back to Alexandria. Antony fell on his own sword in August of thirty BC after false reports reached him that Cleopatra was already dead. She survived his death by only a few days.

The question of how Cleopatra died has fascinated the world ever since. The most famous version — that she allowed an asp, an Egyptian cobra, to bite her — is dramatic and symbolically appealing, associating her death with the sacred serpents of Egyptian royalty. Plutarch and other ancient writers mention this tradition but note that the evidence was ambiguous: the only marks found on her body were two small puncture wounds, and no snake was ever found in the room. Other accounts suggest she used a poison introduced through a hairpin or ointment. The truth is unknown. What is certain is that she died on her own terms, refusing to be taken to Rome as a captive to march in Octavian's triumph — as she told him she would, before he could arrange to prevent it.

She was thirty-nine years old. Octavian had Caesarion killed, on the principle that there can only be one Caesar. The children she had with Antony were taken to Rome and raised by Octavia, Antony's Roman wife, whom he had abandoned. Egypt became a Roman province — the most important in the empire, its grain feeding the city of Rome for centuries.

---

The mythology that formed around Cleopatra began almost immediately. Roman writers portrayed her as a dangerous enchantress who had unmanned one of Rome's greatest generals — a convenient narrative that absolved Antony of responsibility and confirmed Roman prejudices about the corrupting luxury of the East. Shakespeare built his Antony and Cleopatra on this tradition, giving it its most magnificent literary expression. In the nineteenth and twentieth centuries, as Western culture became more interested in questioning its own assumptions, Cleopatra began to be reinterpreted: as a political genius who had built and maintained a kingdom against impossible odds, as a ruler who had brilliantly used the only tools available to her in a world where women were excluded from formal power, as a figure whose intelligence and capability had been systematically reduced to her sexuality by a tradition that could not account for female authority in any other terms.

Modern historians point to facts that the myth obscures. She was not Egyptian by ethnicity — she was Macedonian Greek, a member of a dynasty that had ruled Egypt for two and a half centuries without assimilating into its culture until she chose to do so. She was fluent in Egyptian and presented herself to her subjects as a devotee of Isis, the goddess with whom Ptolemaic queens had long identified. She managed an extraordinarily complex country — a society that was simultaneously ancient Egyptian, Greek, Jewish, and increasingly Roman — with considerable skill. The famines and economic difficulties of her reign were partly the consequence of circumstances beyond her control; the stability she maintained for two decades was a genuine achievement.

What destroyed her was not her personal failings but the overwhelming force of Rome in its most expansionist period. She was the last pharaoh of Egypt not because she failed, but because no one could have prevailed against the Roman world's determination to absorb the last great independent kingdom of the Mediterranean. That she held out as long as she did, and chose the manner of her ending, are perhaps the most revealing things about her.
