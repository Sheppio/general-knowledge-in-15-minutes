# The Rise of China's Economy: From Poverty to Superpower in Four Decades

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

In nineteen seventy-eight, China was one of the poorest countries in the world. Its economy, devastated by thirty years of Maoist mismanagement — the catastrophic agricultural collectivisation of the Great Leap Forward, the decade of disruption of the Cultural Revolution — had a gross domestic product of roughly one hundred and fifty billion dollars and a per capita income of around two hundred dollars. Roughly two fifths of the population lived in absolute poverty. The country was largely closed to foreign trade and investment, its industries run by the state according to plans that bore little relationship to what the economy actually needed, its agriculture organised in collective communes that had proven incapable of feeding the population.

By twenty twenty-four, China's economy had grown to more than eighteen trillion dollars by nominal measurement, making it the world's second-largest economy. Hundreds of millions of people had been lifted out of poverty — the single largest reduction in poverty in human history. China had become the world's largest manufacturer, its largest exporter, and its largest holder of foreign exchange reserves. It had built the world's largest high-speed rail network, launched its own space station, and emerged as a leading force in technologies ranging from renewable energy to artificial intelligence. The transformation that produced all of this occurred in roughly forty years — an economic leap without precedent in the history of the world.

---

Understanding how this happened requires beginning with the man who set it in motion. Deng Xiaoping was born in eighteen ninety-four, joined the Chinese Communist Party as a young man, survived the Long March, served as a senior official in the People's Republic, was purged twice during the Cultural Revolution, and emerged in the late nineteen-seventies as the dominant figure in Chinese politics without ever holding the position of paramount leader in a formal sense. He was pragmatic in a way that Mao had never been: famously indifferent to ideological labels, he is associated with the phrase "it doesn't matter whether the cat is black or white, so long as it catches mice." What mattered, for Deng, was whether a policy worked — and he was prepared to try policies that were very far from orthodox communism to make China's economy work.

The decisive moment came on the eighteenth of December, nineteen seventy-eight, at the Third Plenary Session of the Eleventh Central Committee of the Chinese Communist Party. At this meeting, Deng consolidated his political position and launched what would become known as "Reform and Opening Up" — gaige kaifang — the programme that would transform China over the following decades. The immediate priority was agriculture. The rural communes that had replaced private farming under Mao were replaced by the household responsibility system, which returned land-use rights to individual farm families. Farmers could now keep what they grew above their quota obligations and sell it on the market. Agricultural productivity soared almost immediately.

---

The second prong of the early reforms was the creation of Special Economic Zones — designated areas, initially along China's southeastern coast, where foreign investment was permitted and market-based rules applied that were unavailable in the rest of the country. In nineteen eighty, four such zones were established: Shenzhen, adjacent to Hong Kong; Zhuhai, adjacent to Macau; and Shantou and Xiamen in Fujian province. Of these, Shenzhen became the most dramatic example of what the reforms could achieve. In nineteen eighty, Shenzhen was a small city of around three hundred thousand people. By the end of two thousand it had a population of more than four million, its skyline transformed from fishing villages into a forest of skyscrapers, its economy built on manufacturing for export that connected China to global supply chains for the first time.

The Special Economic Zones were essentially laboratories — places where the Party could test market-based policies without committing to them nationally. When they worked, the approach was gradually extended. Foreign-funded enterprises were permitted in more and more sectors. State-owned enterprises were subjected to increasing competitive pressure. Private businesses, initially tolerated, were eventually encouraged. Prices, which had been set by central planning, were gradually liberalised. The market, suppressed for thirty years, was allowed back in — carefully, partially, under political control, but unmistakably.

---

The results through the nineteen-eighties were dramatic. Growth rates of ten percent per year or above became routine. Tens of millions of rural Chinese, freed from the constraints of collective farming, migrated to the cities in what became the largest human migration in history, providing an enormous supply of cheap labour to the factories that were springing up along the coast. Foreign companies, attracted by that labour and by the prospect of access to China's vast consumer market, invested heavily. By the early nineteen-nineties, China had been transformed from an agricultural country into a manufacturing powerhouse, producing everything from toys and textiles to electronics and machinery for export to the world.

The crisis of nineteen eighty-nine — the Tiananmen Square protests and their violent suppression in June of that year — threatened to derail the process. Western governments imposed sanctions; foreign investment slowed; the political legitimacy of the reform programme was in question. Deng Xiaoping responded with what became known as the Southern Tour of nineteen ninety-two, travelling to Shenzhen and other coastal cities and making a series of speeches that emphatically re-endorsed the reform programme and opened the door to a new and faster phase of economic liberalisation. The signal was clear: the political crackdown at Tiananmen would not mean economic retrenchment. Growth resumed at an even faster pace.

---

The decisive integration of China into the global economy came with its accession to the World Trade Organisation on the eleventh of December, two thousand and one, after fifteen years of negotiations. WTO membership committed China to reducing tariffs, opening its markets to foreign competition, and operating under internationally agreed trading rules. The consequences were enormous: China's exports surged, its manufacturing sector expanded further, and its role in global supply chains deepened. Over the following decade, China became the factory of the world — the place where much of what was consumed in Europe and North America was made. In two thousand and ten, China overtook Japan as the world's second-largest economy by nominal gross domestic product.

The transformation was not painless, and not only for China's trading partners. The rapid industrialisation came with enormous environmental costs — air pollution in Chinese cities reached dangerous levels, rivers were contaminated, ecosystems were degraded. The migration of hundreds of millions of people from rural areas to cities created enormous social pressures: urban-rural inequality widened, social safety nets were strained, and the pace of change disrupted communities and families in ways that are still being processed. The workers who powered the economic miracle often worked long hours in difficult conditions for wages that remained low by Western standards even as they were transformational by the standards of what Chinese rural life had previously offered.

---

The character of the Chinese economy changed significantly in the second decade of the twenty-first century, as President Xi Jinping consolidated power and the state reasserted its role in economic life. The era of unconstrained market liberalisation gave way to a more directed model in which the Party exercised closer oversight of major corporations, private entrepreneurs were brought more firmly under political control, and national strategic priorities — in technology, renewable energy, electric vehicles, artificial intelligence — were pursued through state-guided investment and industrial policy. This model has been called "state capitalism" or "market socialism," and it defies easy categorisation in terms of the standard ideological categories of East or West.

The economic relationship between China and the rest of the world also became more complicated. The flood of Chinese exports that had benefited consumers in rich countries had also decimated manufacturing industries and the communities that depended on them — in the American Midwest, in the industrial regions of Europe, in countries across the developing world that found themselves unable to compete with Chinese manufacturers. The trade tensions that resulted, most visibly in the tariff disputes between China and the United States that escalated through the twenty-teens, reflected a fundamental question about how to manage the integration of an economy of China's scale and political character into a global trading system designed in an era when such an economy did not exist.

---

The story of China's economic rise is, in one sense, a story about what happens when a country with an enormous population, a long tradition of education and commercial acumen, and capable political leadership is allowed to participate in the global economy. The reforms launched by Deng Xiaoping in nineteen seventy-eight gave Chinese people — farmers, entrepreneurs, engineers, factory workers — the opportunity to apply their abilities to productive ends, and they did so with results that exceeded almost everyone's expectations. The lifting of hundreds of millions of people from poverty is an achievement of world-historical scale, whatever reservations one might have about the political system that produced it.

It is also, in another sense, a story with no clear ending. China is now a power of the first rank, with interests and ambitions that extend across the globe. How it navigates the transition from a middle-income country to a high-income one — a transition that has stalled or reversed for many economies — will shape the world economy for the rest of this century. The rise is complete in the sense that it has already happened; what it leads to remains, in twenty twenty-six, genuinely open.
