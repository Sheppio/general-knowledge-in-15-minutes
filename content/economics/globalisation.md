# Globalisation: Winners, Losers, and the World Remade

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Globalisation is the process by which the world's economies, societies, and cultures have become increasingly interconnected — through trade, investment, migration, communication, and the movement of ideas. It is not a new phenomenon: human beings have been trading across long distances for as long as records exist, and the ancient Silk Road connected China, Central Asia, the Middle East, and Europe in networks of commerce and cultural exchange that functioned for over a thousand years. What is new about the globalisation of the late twentieth and early twenty-first centuries is its scale, its speed, and its depth: the degree to which economic activity anywhere in the world is shaped by conditions everywhere else, the degree to which supply chains and financial markets are woven together into a single global system, and the degree to which the consequences of that integration — for good and for ill — are distributed unevenly among the people it affects.

The story of modern globalisation is inseparable from the political decisions made in its aftermath of the Second World War. In July nineteen forty-four, delegates from forty-four Allied nations gathered at a hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, to design the international economic order that would govern the post-war world. The Bretton Woods conference created the International Monetary Fund, to provide stability to exchange rates and support countries facing balance of payments crises, and the World Bank, to finance reconstruction and development. It also set up a system of fixed exchange rates pegged to the US dollar, which was in turn tied to gold — a system designed to prevent the competitive devaluations and currency chaos that had deepened the Great Depression of the nineteen-thirties.

---

The trade dimension of the post-war order was initially addressed through the General Agreement on Tariffs and Trade — GATT — signed in Geneva in October nineteen forty-seven and applied from January nineteen forty-eight. GATT was not a full international organisation but a framework of rules and a forum for negotiating reductions in tariffs and other trade barriers. It worked. In nineteen forty-seven, the average tariff among its major participants was around twenty-two percent. By the end of the Uruguay Round of trade negotiations in nineteen ninety-four, that average had fallen to around five percent. The systematic dismantling of trade barriers that GATT facilitated was one of the primary drivers of the expansion of world trade in the second half of the twentieth century: global exports roughly doubled as a share of world output between nineteen seventy and the early two-thousands.

The institutional framework was completed by the creation of the World Trade Organisation on the first of January, nineteen ninety-five, pursuant to the Marrakesh Agreement signed by a hundred and twenty-three nations the previous April. The WTO took over and extended GATT's functions, adding jurisdiction over trade in services and intellectual property rights, creating a binding dispute settlement mechanism, and establishing a forum for negotiating new trade agreements. By the time of its creation, the principles of free trade and open markets had achieved a dominance in mainstream economic thinking that was close to consensus: trade was good, barriers to trade were wasteful, and the progressive integration of national economies into a global market would produce gains for everyone.

---

The reality proved more complicated. The benefits of trade liberalisation were real — and large in aggregate. The integration of billions of workers in China, India, Southeast Asia, and elsewhere into global supply chains drove the most rapid reduction of absolute poverty in human history. In China alone, the share of the population living in absolute poverty fell from around forty-one percent in nineteen seventy-eight to five percent in two thousand and one. The consumers of rich countries benefited from access to cheaper manufactured goods — clothing, electronics, appliances — than they could have produced domestically. International trade created new industries and new opportunities across the developing world.

But the gains were not equally distributed, and the losses were real and concentrated. In the United States, Western Europe, and other developed countries, the flood of manufactured imports from lower-wage economies — particularly after China joined the WTO in December two thousand and one — devastated manufacturing industries and the communities that had grown up around them. The economist David Autor and colleagues documented what they called the "China shock": a measurable, large, and surprisingly persistent negative effect on employment and wages in American communities that competed with Chinese imports. The people who lost their factory jobs in the industrial Midwest or the English Midlands were not, in most cases, the same people who gained from cheaper consumer goods; the gains were diffuse and the losses were concentrated and severe.

The financial dimension of globalisation added another layer of complexity. The collapse of the Bretton Woods system of fixed exchange rates in the early nineteen-seventies — precipitated by the Nixon administration's decision in nineteen seventy-one to end the dollar's convertibility to gold — ushered in an era of floating exchange rates and, gradually, the liberalisation of capital flows. Money became freer to move across borders than goods or people, and the consequences were profound. Global financial markets became deeply interconnected, allowing capital to flow rapidly to wherever returns were highest. They also became deeply interconnected in ways that transmitted crises: the Asian financial crisis of nineteen ninety-seven and nineteen ninety-eight, the Russian debt default of nineteen ninety-eight, and above all the global financial crisis of two thousand and eight all demonstrated how quickly a shock in one part of the interconnected system could spread across the world.

---

The political backlash against globalisation had been visible for some time before it became decisive. The protests that disrupted the WTO Ministerial Conference in Seattle in late November and early December of nineteen ninety-nine — bringing together labour unions, environmental groups, anarchists, development advocates, and others in an unlikely coalition under the broad banner of opposition to corporate-driven trade liberalisation — were a visible early signal. The protesters argued that the WTO's rules benefited large corporations and wealthy countries at the expense of workers and the environment, that the benefits of free trade were not being shared equitably, and that the trade agreements being negotiated transferred sovereignty from democratically accountable governments to unaccountable international bodies. The Seattle conference ended without agreement, in part because of the protests, in part because of deep disagreements among member states.

The backlash intensified in the twenty-teens. The financial crisis of two thousand and eight, whose effects were felt most severely by ordinary workers and homeowners rather than the financial institutions whose risk-taking had caused it, hardened resentment of an economic system that appeared to socialise losses and privatise gains. The Brexit vote in the United Kingdom in June two thousand and sixteen, and the election of Donald Trump in the United States in November two thousand and sixteen, were the most dramatic political expressions of a widespread revolt against globalisation — or more precisely, against the version of globalisation that had been practised: one that prioritised the mobility of capital and goods over the security of workers, that generated large aggregate gains while distributing them unequally, and that created genuine hardship for communities and industries whose politicians had told them the benefits of trade would outweigh the costs.

---

The COVID-19 pandemic exposed additional vulnerabilities in the globalised supply chain model. The sudden disruption of global manufacturing and shipping in twenty twenty revealed how deeply dependent rich-country economies had become on foreign production for everything from hospital protective equipment to semiconductor chips. The "just-in-time" supply chains that had delivered efficiency and cost savings in normal times proved brittle in the face of disruption, and the resulting shortages prompted a re-evaluation of the trade-offs between efficiency and resilience.

At the same time, the pandemic demonstrated that global interconnection was not only an economic phenomenon. The spread of the virus itself — from a single city in central China to every corner of the world within a matter of weeks — was a consequence of globalisation as surely as any trade in goods. So was the speed with which scientific knowledge about the virus, the vaccine research, and the technological capacity to develop and manufacture vaccines was shared across the world.

---

The argument about globalisation's effects is, at its core, a debate about distribution — about who gets the gains and who bears the costs, and about whether the institutions that govern international economic integration are structured in ways that ensure those gains and costs are fairly shared. The economic arguments for free trade remain robust: the gains from specialisation and exchange are real, and protection is costly. But the political sustainability of an open global economy depends on whether its benefits are broadly shared and whether those who bear its costs are compensated. Where that political bargain has not been kept — where the gains were captured by the already wealthy and the costs fell on communities that had already been left behind — the backlash has been correspondingly fierce.

Globalisation is not reversible in any simple sense: the interconnections of the modern world economy are too deep, the dependencies too real, the benefits too widely distributed to be wound back to some imagined simpler time. But it is shapeable — by trade agreements, by tax policy, by investment in workers and communities, by the governance of the international institutions that set its rules. The question is not whether to globalise but how, and in whose interest.
