# The Economics of Inequality: Why the Gap Keeps Growing

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

Inequality is not new. Throughout most of human history, the distance between those at the top and those at the bottom of the economic order has been vast. Kings and peasants, landowners and serfs, slave masters and slaves — every pre-modern society was structured around hierarchies of wealth and power so entrenched that most people experienced them not as the outcomes of economic arrangements that could be otherwise, but as the natural order of things. What is distinctive about economic inequality in the modern era is that we now have the tools to measure it, the political frameworks within which to contest it, and the economic knowledge to understand at least some of what drives it. What we lack, still, is clear agreement on how much inequality is too much, what causes it, and what if anything should be done about it.

The tools for measuring inequality were developed gradually through the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. The most widely used is the Gini coefficient, developed by the Italian statistician Corrado Gini and published in nineteen twelve. The Gini coefficient is a number between zero and one: zero represents perfect equality — everyone has exactly the same income or wealth — and one represents maximal inequality, where one person has everything and everyone else has nothing. In practice, observed Gini coefficients for income tend to fall between about zero point two-five for the most equal societies and about zero point six for the most unequal. The measure is useful for comparisons within and between countries over time, though it has limitations: two societies with the same Gini coefficient can have very different distributions of who is rich and who is poor.

---

What economic research has established with reasonable confidence is that the pattern of inequality in wealthy countries over the past century has not been a steady trend in either direction, but a great arc: high inequality in the early twentieth century, compression through the middle decades, and then a sharp rise from roughly the nineteen-seventies onward that in the United States and several other countries has returned wealth concentration to levels not seen since the Gilded Age.

The compression of inequality in the mid-twentieth century — sometimes called the "Great Compression" — was driven by several forces. The two world wars and the Great Depression destroyed enormous concentrations of wealth: physical destruction, inflation, capital controls, and the collapse of asset prices all hit the wealthy hardest. The welfare states built in the post-war decades in Western Europe and, to a lesser degree, the United States redistributed income through progressive taxation, public services, and social insurance. Strong trade unions compressed wage distributions in many sectors. Full employment policies kept the labour market tight and workers' bargaining power relatively high. In the United States, the period from roughly nineteen forty-five to nineteen seventy-three is sometimes called the "Golden Age" of capitalism — not only because growth rates were high, but because the gains from growth were widely shared. Productivity rose and wages rose with it. The middle class expanded.

---

The reversal began in the nineteen-seventies and accelerated through the eighties and nineties. It is not difficult to identify the broad forces involved, though economists debate their relative importance. The shift from manufacturing to services in advanced economies destroyed many of the well-paying, unionised industrial jobs that had underpinned middle-class prosperity in the post-war decades. Globalisation, and particularly the integration of low-wage developing countries into global supply chains, put downward pressure on wages for workers in tradeable sectors of rich-country economies. Technological change — the computerisation and automation of routine tasks — displaced workers in the middle of the skill and wage distribution while increasing the productivity, and therefore the earnings, of those at the top. The deliberate political choices of the Reagan and Thatcher era — reduced top marginal tax rates, weakened labour unions, deregulation of financial markets, reduced social spending — shifted the distribution of income and wealth in favour of capital over labour and high earners over lower earners. All of these forces operated simultaneously and reinforced each other.

The numbers that resulted are stark. In the United States, the share of income going to the top one percent of earners — which had fallen from roughly eighteen percent in the nineteen twenties to about eight percent in the nineteen seventies — rose back above twenty percent by the early twenty-first century. The share of wealth held by the top one percent is even more concentrated than income: in most rich countries, the wealthiest ten percent of households own somewhere between fifty and seventy percent of total wealth, while the bottom fifty percent own very little. The very rich have grown richer faster than anyone else in large part because they earn their income primarily from capital — from the returns on financial assets and property — rather than from labour. And the return on capital has, over time, generally exceeded the rate of economic growth.

---

This last observation is the centrepiece of an argument advanced by the French economist Thomas Piketty in his book Capital in the Twenty-First Century, first published in French in twenty thirteen and translated into English in twenty fourteen. Piketty's thesis, which he expresses through the formula r greater than g, is that when the rate of return on capital exceeds the rate of economic growth — when money makes more money than the economy grows — wealth inevitably concentrates at the top. Those who already have capital accumulate it faster than those who depend on labour income can accumulate savings; inherited wealth grows faster than earned wealth; and over time the distribution becomes more and more skewed toward those who started with most. Piketty's historical data, drawn from tax records across several countries over more than a century, showed that r had exceeded g in most periods of history — and that the mid-twentieth-century compression had been an exception driven by exceptional events, not a new normal.

The book was a sensation because it provided an elegant theoretical framework for a phenomenon that many people were observing in their daily lives: that hard work and talent were no longer sufficient guarantees of economic advancement, that the deck seemed to be stacked toward those who already had wealth, and that the American Dream — the idea that anyone who worked hard enough could get ahead — was looking increasingly like a myth for the majority who did not start from a position of advantage. Piketty's proposed solution — a global progressive wealth tax — was widely regarded as politically utopian, but the diagnosis he offered has proven robust and influential.

---

The consequences of high inequality extend well beyond the economic realm. Political scientists and sociologists have documented relationships between high inequality and a range of social pathologies: lower social trust, higher rates of violent crime, worse health outcomes across the population, lower social mobility, reduced educational attainment among those from poorer backgrounds, and weaker democratic institutions. The political scientist Robert Putnam, the epidemiologists Richard Wilkinson and Kate Pickett, and others have produced evidence that more unequal societies do worse on almost every measure of social wellbeing, not just for the poor but for the population as a whole.

High inequality also affects political outcomes in ways that can entrench itself. When wealth is highly concentrated, the wealthy have enormous resources to influence political processes — funding campaigns, lobbying governments, shaping public discourse — in ways that tend to produce policies favourable to the interests of the wealthy. The political scientist Martin Gilens documented that in the United States, policy outcomes are significantly more responsive to the preferences of the affluent than to those of ordinary citizens, even controlling for other factors. If inequality shapes political power, and political power shapes the rules that determine economic outcomes, inequality can become self-reinforcing.

---

The debate about inequality is complicated by a distinction that is often blurred in public discourse: the distinction between inequality within countries and inequality between countries. On the global scale, the picture of the past several decades looks rather different than the picture within rich countries. The integration of China, India, and other large developing countries into the global economy has raised hundreds of millions of people out of extreme poverty and reduced the income gap between the richest and poorest countries, even as inequality within many of those countries has risen. Whether one concludes that globalisation has increased or decreased inequality depends crucially on which level of analysis one is using.

There is also a persistent disagreement about how much inequality matters, as distinct from the absolute levels of income and wealth. Some economists argue that what matters for human welfare is not how much better off the rich are than the poor, but how well off the poor are in absolute terms — and that growth, even inequality-increasing growth, can benefit the poor if it raises their absolute standard of living. Others argue that relative position matters enormously for human wellbeing — that living in a society where others have vastly more than you do damages health, social cohesion, and political stability regardless of your absolute income level. The evidence, on balance, supports the latter position. Inequality is not merely an aesthetic or moral concern; it is a practical one, with real consequences for the kind of societies we live in and the quality of life of everyone within them.
