# The 2008 Financial Crisis: When the Banks Broke

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

In the autumn of 2008, the global financial system came closer to complete collapse than at any point since the Great Depression. Banks that had operated for more than a century ceased to exist in days. Credit markets froze solid. Governments across the world were forced to intervene on a scale that would have seemed unthinkable weeks before. And while the acute phase of the crisis lasted only months, its consequences — in unemployment, lost savings, and political disruption — shaped the decade that followed and echoed into the one after that.

To understand how it happened, you have to go back to the early 2000s and to American housing. Interest rates were low. Banks were flush with cash. And a financial innovation — the mortgage-backed security — had made it possible to bundle thousands of individual home loans together and sell them as investment products to institutions around the world. This seemed, on the surface, like a smart way of spreading risk. In practice, it spread risk invisibly, in ways that no one fully understood, throughout the entire global financial system.

The problem was that the underlying mortgages were increasingly reckless. Banks were lending money to home buyers who had little income, little collateral, and little ability to repay if things went wrong. These were called subprime mortgages. Because house prices had been rising for years, no one worried too much — if a borrower defaulted, the bank could repossess and sell the house at a profit. And because the mortgages were bundled and sold on, the banks that made the loans often no longer held the risk. They had passed it to someone else. Or so they believed.

---

When house prices began to fall in 2006 and 2007, the carefully constructed assumptions unravelled. Borrowers began defaulting in large numbers. The mortgage-backed securities that had been sold around the world began losing value rapidly. And suddenly no one was sure which banks were holding how many bad assets. Trust between financial institutions — the invisible foundation on which all banking rests — began to collapse.

The crisis accelerated through 2007 and into 2008. Bear Stearns, one of America's largest investment banks, collapsed in March 2008 and was sold at a fire-sale price with Federal Reserve assistance. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac, the government-sponsored mortgage giants, were taken into government control in September 2008. And then, on the fifteenth of September, 2008, Lehman Brothers — a Wall Street institution that had survived for over a hundred and fifty years — filed for bankruptcy. It was the largest bankruptcy in American history, involving more than six hundred billion dollars in assets.

The Lehman collapse was the trigger for full-scale panic. Stock markets plunged around the world. Credit markets froze. Companies found they could not borrow money for ordinary daily operations. The money market funds that millions of ordinary Americans used as everyday savings accounts came close to collapse. For several weeks, the global financial system was genuinely on the verge of seizing up entirely.

---

Governments responded with interventions of extraordinary scale. In the United States, Congress passed the Troubled Asset Relief Program — TARP — authorising seven hundred billion dollars to stabilise the financial system. The Federal Reserve slashed interest rates to near zero and began purchasing assets on a massive scale. Central banks around the world did the same. Major banks were nationalised, part-nationalised, or rescued with public money. The principle that banks were private businesses that should bear their own losses was set aside in the name of preventing complete economic catastrophe.

The measures worked, in the sense that the financial system did not collapse. But the broader economic damage was severe. By October 2009, unemployment in the United States had reached around ten percent. Some eight and a half million jobs were lost between 2008 and 2010. Ordinary families who had done nothing wrong lost their homes, their savings, and their financial security.

Across Europe, the crisis merged into a sovereign debt crisis. Countries whose governments had borrowed heavily to fund bank rescues found themselves unable to meet their own debts. Greece, Ireland, Portugal, and Spain all required international bailouts. The eurozone teetered on the edge of breaking apart. In the United Kingdom, the government's share of public debt doubled in three years. Austerity programmes cut public services across a continent for most of the following decade.

---

The political consequences proved as lasting as the economic ones. In many countries, the spectacle of governments spending hundreds of billions of public money to rescue the same banks whose recklessness had caused the crisis — while ordinary people lost jobs, homes, and savings — produced a deep and lasting fury. That fury fed the political insurgencies of the following decade: the Tea Party and then Trumpism in America, the rise of anti-establishment movements across Europe, and a general collapse of faith in the institutions and elites that had allowed the crisis to happen.

The regulatory response took the form of new laws and stricter oversight. In the United States, the Dodd-Frank Act of 2010 introduced new rules on banking, required large institutions to hold more capital, and created new oversight mechanisms. In Europe, new banking regulations and stress tests were introduced. The aim was to prevent a repeat of the crisis — to ensure that financial institutions could not again accumulate risks they did not understand and could not absorb.

The 2008 crisis did not produce a Great Depression. But it changed the world in ways that are still playing out. It demonstrated that financial systems had become so complex and interconnected that their failures could not be contained within the institutions that caused them. It raised profound questions about the relationship between finance and democracy — about how much risk private institutions could accumulate and then, in crisis, transfer to the public. Those questions remain largely unanswered today, and the financial system that exists now, for all its new regulations, would still be recognisable to the traders and bankers who built the one that broke.
