# The History of Money: How Humanity Invented Value

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

Money is one of the strangest inventions in human history. It is, in its essence, an abstraction — a shared agreement about what has value. A gold coin is worth something not because gold is inherently precious but because enough people agree that it is. A banknote is worth something not because paper has any intrinsic value but because a government has declared it legal tender and the rest of us believe, on reasonable grounds, that others will accept it. Cryptocurrency is worth something because a network of users and speculators agree that it is, at least for now. Strip away the agreements and the institutions that enforce them, and money is nothing: coloured paper, inert metal, a string of digits on a server somewhere.

This strangeness is easy to overlook because money is so deeply embedded in everyday life that it seems as natural as language or shelter. But like language, it is an invention — one that evolved over thousands of years, in different forms in different places, and that continues to evolve today. Understanding the history of money is understanding one of the most fundamental technologies humans have ever developed: not a technology of things but of trust.

---

The standard story told about the origin of money goes something like this: before money, people bartered — exchanging goods directly, cow for grain, fish for pottery. Barter was cumbersome because it required a "double coincidence of wants": you had to find someone who had what you wanted and wanted what you had, at the same time, in the same place. Money solved this problem by providing a universally accepted medium of exchange.

This story has the feel of economic logic, and it has been told so often that it seems like historical fact. But anthropologists and economic historians have found very little evidence that barter economies of this kind actually existed as the norm in pre-monetary societies. What they find instead is something more complex: gift economies, communal sharing, credit arrangements, and systems of reciprocal obligation that sustained economic life without anything like coins or notes. The barter origin story appears to be less a historical account than a thought experiment — one that happens to make money seem like a natural solution to a natural problem.

What the historical and archaeological record does show is that some of the earliest monetary arrangements were in fact systems of credit and debt: records, in the ancient Middle East, of obligations owed and received, denominated in grain or silver, long before anything like a coin existed. Temples and palaces in ancient Mesopotamia functioned partly as proto-banks — recording and administering the circulation of goods and labour in ways that were, in some respects, more sophisticated than simple exchange. The earliest written documents in human history — the cuneiform tablets of Sumer — are not poems or prayers but accounting records.

---

The earliest coins in the historical record appear in the kingdom of Lydia — in what is now western Turkey — in the first half of the seventh century before the common era. These were lumps of electrum, a naturally occurring alloy of gold and silver, stamped with marks to guarantee their weight and purity. The Lydian king Alyattes is generally credited with issuing the first officially minted coins; his son Croesus, whose name became proverbial for wealth, issued the first standardised gold coins — the Croeseid — around five hundred and fifty years before the common era. From Lydia, coinage spread rapidly to the Greek city-states and across the Mediterranean world.

The advantages of coin money over earlier systems were substantial. Coins were portable, durable, divisible, and could be accepted anywhere that recognised the issuing authority. They made trade across long distances easier and commerce between strangers possible. They also made taxation more efficient, which is one reason states were so enthusiastic about them. The Roman Empire, at its height, ran a sophisticated monetary economy across dozens of languages and ethnic groups, held together partly by the legions and partly by a shared currency.

Paper money was invented in China, in forms that eventually became standard practice under the Song Dynasty from the tenth century of the common era. The Chinese had long used paper for various administrative purposes, and the step to issuing paper certificates representing stored value was a natural one in a society with sophisticated institutions and long-distance trade. The Mongol Yuan Dynasty continued and expanded paper money use; Marco Polo, visiting China in the thirteenth century, was astonished to find a system in which paper issued by the emperor circulated as money, and he described it to European readers who found it nearly incomprehensible. Europe would not develop equivalent paper money systems until the seventeenth century.

---

European banking evolved through the medieval period, driven largely by the needs of long-distance trade. Merchants travelling between cities did not want to carry large quantities of gold or silver — too heavy, too vulnerable to robbery. Italian bankers, particularly in Florence, Genoa, and Venice, developed the letter of credit: a document that could be presented at a partner bank in another city and redeemed for local currency. From the letter of credit came the bill of exchange, and from there, step by step, the modern banking system.

The Bank of England, founded in 1694, was one of the first modern central banks, created to manage government debt and issue banknotes backed by the government's credit. By the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries, paper banknotes had become the dominant medium of exchange across the industrialising world. Behind these notes was gold: the gold standard, which meant that paper money could in theory be exchanged for a fixed quantity of the metal. The international gold standard, as it existed from roughly the eighteen-seventies to the outbreak of the First World War in 1914, provided a fixed exchange rate system that facilitated global trade but removed from governments the ability to expand the money supply in response to economic crises.

The interwar period revealed the gold standard's dangers. Countries that clung to it during the Great Depression of the nineteen-thirties were unable to expand their money supplies to stimulate their economies, deepening and prolonging the crisis. Britain left the gold standard in 1931; the United States followed in stages, suspending domestic gold convertibility in 1933.

---

The postwar monetary order was established at a conference held in July 1944 at the Mount Washington Hotel in Bretton Woods, New Hampshire, where delegates from forty-four allied nations created a new international monetary system. Under the Bretton Woods agreement, currencies were pegged to the United States dollar, which was in turn convertible to gold at the fixed rate of thirty-five dollars per troy ounce. Two new international institutions — the International Monetary Fund and the World Bank — were created to manage the system and assist countries in balance of payments difficulties.

Bretton Woods held for nearly three decades, underpinning the postwar economic expansion. But it depended on the United States maintaining enough gold to back its dollars, and by the late nineteen-sixties the US was running large deficits — partly because of the costs of the Vietnam War and domestic spending programmes — and other countries were converting their dollars to gold at a rate the system could not sustain. On the fifteenth of August, 1971, President Richard Nixon announced that the United States would no longer convert dollars to gold. This "Nixon shock" effectively ended the Bretton Woods system and ushered in the era of floating exchange rates and fiat currency — money backed not by gold but solely by government decree and public trust — in which we still live.

---

The shift to fiat currency removed the external constraint that gold had imposed on governments. Central banks could now expand the money supply as economic conditions required, using monetary policy — the setting of interest rates and the buying and selling of financial assets — to manage inflation and growth. This flexibility came with its own risks: without a hard anchor, money's value depended entirely on the credibility of the institutions managing it. Where those institutions failed — in Weimar Germany in the nineteen-twenties, in Zimbabwe in the two-thousands, in Venezuela more recently — the result was hyperinflation, the destruction of monetary value, and the devastation of ordinary people's savings.

Since 1971, the history of money has accelerated dramatically. Credit cards, debit cards, and electronic banking have progressively dematerialised money — reducing it from coins and notes to numbers in databases. The global financial system has become extraordinarily complex, capable of moving vast sums across borders in seconds, and capable, as the financial crisis of 2008 demonstrated, of catastrophic failure when the trust underlying that complexity breaks down.

In 2009, a person or group using the name Satoshi Nakamoto released Bitcoin: the first widely adopted cryptocurrency, a system of digital money secured by cryptographic proof rather than government authority. The Bitcoin protocol used a distributed ledger — a blockchain — to record transactions without any central bank or institution, making it theoretically immune to government control and inflation. Whether cryptocurrencies represent the next stage in money's evolution or a speculative phenomenon of limited lasting importance remains genuinely uncertain. What is certain is that they raise, in a new form, the oldest question about money: what makes something worth something?

The answer, as it has always been, is agreement — the shared fiction, simultaneously fragile and powerful, that makes exchange possible. From the Lydian electrum coin to the digital token, that agreement is what money has always been. Change the agreement, and you change the money.
