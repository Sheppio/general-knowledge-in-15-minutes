# Capitalism: How It Works and Where It Came From

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Capitalism is the economic system that most of the world lives under, yet it is one of the least examined features of daily life. We navigate its structures constantly — buying and selling, working for wages, investing and borrowing — without typically pausing to ask how this system came about, what assumptions underlie it, or what alternatives have existed and might exist. These are important questions, because capitalism is not the natural order of things. It was invented, at a particular time and place, for particular reasons, and it has changed significantly throughout its history. Understanding capitalism — how it actually works, where it came from, and what its costs and benefits have been — is essential to understanding the modern world.

At its most basic, capitalism is an economic system characterised by the private ownership of the means of production — the factories, land, tools, and technologies used to create goods and services — and the use of those means of production to generate profit. In a capitalist economy, decisions about what to produce, how to produce it, and at what price to sell it are made primarily by private individuals and firms responding to price signals in markets, rather than by central authorities or customary arrangements. The profit motive — the desire to earn more than one spends — is the primary engine of economic activity.

This sounds simple, but the implications are profound and the reality is complex. Capitalism generates enormous wealth; it is also associated with persistent inequality, cyclical instability, and the externalisation of costs onto people and environments that have no power to resist. How to understand these features — whether they are accidental or inherent, whether they can be reformed or require replacement — has been the central question of economics and political philosophy for the past two and a half centuries.

---

The origins of capitalism are contested, but most historians locate the earliest forms in the trade networks and proto-industrial enterprises of late medieval and early modern Europe. The fourteenth and fifteenth centuries saw the development in the Italian city-states of sophisticated financial instruments — letters of credit, bills of exchange, double-entry bookkeeping — that made long-distance trade easier and capital accumulation more systematic. The Italian merchants of Florence, Venice, and Genoa were pioneering forms of economic organisation that would eventually develop into modern banking and corporate structures.

In England and the Netherlands during the sixteenth and seventeenth centuries, the development of joint-stock companies — enterprises in which ownership was divided into shares that could be bought and sold — allowed the aggregation of capital for large commercial ventures. The Dutch East India Company, founded in 1602, and the English East India Company, founded in 1600, were among the first such corporations, pooling the capital of many investors to finance the enormously expensive business of long-distance oceanic trade. These companies were the first multinational corporations — entities that operated across political boundaries, maintained their own armies, and in some cases governed territories directly. They were capitalism in early and, by modern standards, remarkably uninhibited form.

The system that emerged in England over the seventeenth century — the enclosure of common land, the dispossession of peasants, the creation of a landless labouring class dependent on wages — laid the social foundation for what would become industrial capitalism. The historian Karl Polanyi later called this a "great transformation": the embedding of economic life in the market, rather than in the social relationships of family, community, and customary obligation that had previously governed most economic activity.

---

The first systematic theoretical account of capitalism was provided by Adam Smith in The Wealth of Nations, published in 1776 — the same year as the American Declaration of Independence, and no coincidence, since both were expressions of the same Enlightenment belief in the rational individual pursuing his own interests within a framework of laws and institutions. Smith identified the division of labour — the specialisation of workers on different parts of a production process — as the primary source of economic productivity. He famously illustrated this with the example of a pin factory: where one man doing all the operations might make twenty pins a day, ten men each specialising in a different operation could make forty-eight thousand. The market, Smith argued, coordinated this specialised production through the mechanism of price: responding to supply and demand, prices signal to producers what to make and to consumers what to buy. No central authority needed to direct this process; the market's "invisible hand" led individuals, pursuing their own interests, to outcomes that benefited society as a whole.

Smith was not a naive apologist for commerce. He understood and criticised the tendency of merchants and manufacturers to collude against the public interest, to lobby for protective legislation, and to exploit workers. But his fundamental insight — that markets could coordinate economic activity more efficiently than central direction — became the theoretical foundation on which subsequent liberal economic thought was built.

---

The Industrial Revolution of the eighteenth and nineteenth centuries transformed capitalism from a system based primarily on trade and agriculture into one based on machine production in factories. The concentration of workers in large industrial establishments, the replacement of skilled craft production with mechanised processes, the burning of fossil fuels to drive engines and furnaces — all of this created extraordinary productive capacity and, simultaneously, created the conditions of working-class life that Karl Marx would diagnose with devastating clarity in Das Kapital.

Marx accepted much of Smith's analysis of how capitalism worked — the division of labour, the role of markets, the drive to accumulate — but turned it toward a different conclusion. The value produced by workers, he argued, exceeded the wages they were paid; the difference was surplus value, appropriated by the capitalist as profit. This was not an accident of particular capitalists being greedy; it was structural, built into the logic of the system. Capitalism, in Marx's analysis, was a system of exploitation, however freely the labour contract was signed, and it contained within itself the contradictions that would eventually destroy it: the tendency toward monopoly, the periodic crises of overproduction, the progressive immiseration of the working class.

Marx was wrong about much — particularly the predictions — but his analytical tools remain powerful, and the questions he raised about the relationship between economic structure and political power have never been fully answered by his opponents.

---

The twentieth century saw capitalism take multiple forms, subject to multiple crises and multiple reformations. The Great Depression of the nineteen-thirties — in which unregulated financial markets produced a speculative bubble and then a catastrophic collapse that left a third of Americans unemployed — demonstrated that markets did not always self-correct, and that the consequences of failure were borne disproportionately by those with the least. The economist John Maynard Keynes argued that governments needed to manage aggregate demand through fiscal and monetary policy — spending money to stimulate the economy in downturns and restraining it in booms. Keynesian economics provided the theoretical framework for the mixed economy that dominated the post-war Western world: private ownership, market mechanisms, but significant government intervention in the form of welfare states, public services, and demand management.

The postwar decades produced the highest sustained economic growth rates in capitalist history, accompanied by declining inequality in most Western countries. But from the nineteen-seventies onward, a counter-revolution occurred: the economists associated with the University of Chicago, particularly Milton Friedman, argued that government intervention was inefficient and that markets, left to themselves, would produce optimal outcomes. "Neoliberalism" — the reduction of taxes, deregulation of finance and industry, privatisation of public services, and weakening of trade unions — became the dominant policy framework in the United States and Britain under Reagan and Thatcher, and spread from there to much of the world.

The consequences of this policy shift are still being debated. Economic growth continued, but its fruits were distributed far less equally than in the postwar period. The deregulation of financial markets contributed to the crisis of 2008 — the worst financial collapse since 1929. Inequality, by most measures, has increased significantly in most rich countries since the nineteen-eighties.

---

Capitalism has never existed in a pure form — every actually existing capitalist economy involves some combination of markets and state intervention, private enterprise and public provision, regulation and freedom. The question has never been capitalism or not capitalism, but what kind of capitalism, and for whose benefit. The Nordic social democracies, the American model, the Chinese state capitalism that has lifted hundreds of millions of people out of poverty since the nineteen-eighties, the financial capitalism of the City of London — all are variants of a system that has no agreed definition and no stable form.

What is stable is the underlying principle: private ownership, the profit motive, and the market mechanism for allocating resources. Whether those principles can be made compatible with the requirements of ecological sustainability, genuine equality of opportunity, and democratic accountability is the central economic question of the twenty-first century — and one that capitalism's admirers and critics are equally obligated to take seriously.
