# The Great Depression: When the World's Economy Collapsed

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,200

---

On the twenty-ninth of October, 1929, the New York Stock Exchange collapsed in the most catastrophic single day in the history of American finance. Share prices plummeted. Fortunes that had taken decades to build vanished in hours. The day became known as Black Tuesday, and it triggered an economic catastrophe that would spread across the world, destroy the livelihoods of hundreds of millions of people, and last for more than a decade.

The Great Depression was not just a financial event. It was a human disaster on a vast scale — a decade of unemployment, poverty, hunger, and despair that shattered faith in capitalism, gave rise to extremist political movements, and ultimately helped create the conditions for the Second World War.

To understand how it happened, you have to understand the 1920s. After the trauma of the First World War, America experienced a decade of extraordinary prosperity. New industries boomed — automobiles, radios, electricity, film. Consumer spending soared. And the stock market went on a seemingly endless upward run. Ordinary Americans who had never invested before poured their savings into stocks, many of them borrowing money to buy more shares — a practice known as buying on margin.

But beneath the glittering surface, serious problems were building. Agricultural prices had been depressed throughout the 1920s, meaning that farmers — a huge portion of the American population — were already struggling before the crash. American banks were poorly regulated and had made enormous numbers of risky loans. And the stock market had risen far beyond the actual value of the companies it represented. It was a bubble, and in October 1929, it burst.

---

The crash alone did not cause the Depression. What turned a severe recession into a decade-long catastrophe was a cascade of policy failures and economic feedback loops. Banks began failing as borrowers defaulted on loans. As banks failed, people lost their savings. As savings disappeared, spending collapsed. As spending collapsed, businesses shut down. As businesses shut down, workers lost their jobs. And as unemployment spread, even fewer people could spend, causing more businesses to fail. By 1933, the American economy had contracted by nearly a third. Unemployment had reached approximately twenty-five percent — one in four American workers had no job.

The human cost was crushing. Families who had been comfortably middle-class found themselves unable to pay rent or buy food. Evictions were so common that shantytowns sprang up on the edges of cities, bitterly nicknamed Hoovervilles after President Herbert Hoover, who became the symbol of the government's inadequate response. Soup kitchens and bread lines stretched around city blocks. Millions of Americans simply went hungry.

In the American south and midwest, the economic collapse was compounded by environmental catastrophe. Years of intensive farming had stripped the topsoil across hundreds of thousands of square miles of plains. When drought struck in the early 1930s, the soil literally blew away. Massive dust storms — some of them black and miles high — buried farms, blanketed towns, and drove hundreds of thousands of families off their land. This region became known as the Dust Bowl. Families packed everything they owned into cars and trucks and headed west, most of them to California, only to find poverty and hostility waiting for them there. John Steinbeck immortalised their suffering in his novel The Grapes of Wrath.

---

The Depression spread far beyond America. Because the United States was the world's dominant creditor nation, when American banks began calling in international loans and American spending collapsed, the effects rippled outward. Germany, already fragile from the strains of the First World War and the Versailles settlement, was hit particularly hard. Unemployment in Germany soared above thirty percent. It was in this environment of despair and humiliation that Adolf Hitler and the Nazi Party found their mass audience, promising to restore Germany's greatness and blaming its suffering on Jews, communists, and foreign enemies.

---

In the United States, the 1932 presidential election became a referendum on the Depression. Franklin Delano Roosevelt, the Democratic candidate, won a landslide victory over the incumbent Hoover. Roosevelt had promised a new deal for the American people, and he moved with extraordinary speed once in office. In his first hundred days, his administration pushed through an unprecedented range of legislation — stabilising the banking system, creating jobs programs, regulating financial markets, providing relief to farmers, and establishing new protections for workers.

The New Deal did not end the Depression. Unemployment remained stubbornly high throughout the 1930s, and a sharp downturn in 1937 proved that the recovery was fragile. But the New Deal changed the relationship between the American government and its citizens. For the first time, the federal government accepted responsibility for the economic welfare of ordinary people. Social Security, which Roosevelt signed into law in 1935, created a safety net for the elderly and unemployed that persists to this day.

What finally ended the Great Depression was not the New Deal but the Second World War. As governments across the world began spending massively on armaments from the late 1930s onward, factories reopened, workers were rehired, and the economy roared back to life. By the time America entered the war in December 1941, unemployment had fallen sharply. By 1943, it had essentially disappeared.

The Great Depression left permanent marks on the world. In economic policy, it discredited the idea that markets are entirely self-correcting and gave rise to the theories of John Maynard Keynes, who argued that governments must actively stimulate demand during downturns. In politics, it demonstrated how economic suffering could fuel extremism and tear apart democratic societies. And in the lives of those who lived through it, it left a deep and lasting caution about money, debt, and the fragility of prosperity that would shape the behaviour of an entire generation.

The lessons of the Great Depression did not go unlearned. When the global financial system teetered on the edge of collapse in 2008, policymakers reached back explicitly to the lessons of the 1930s — cutting interest rates, injecting money into failing banks, and avoiding the catastrophic policy mistakes that had turned a crash into a decade of misery. The Depression's shadow, cast nearly a century ago, still falls across the decisions of every central bank and treasury in the world today.
