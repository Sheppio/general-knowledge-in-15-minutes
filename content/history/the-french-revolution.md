# The French Revolution: Liberty, Equality, and the Guillotine

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

In the summer of 1789, one of the most powerful monarchies in the world collapsed in a matter of weeks. What followed was a decade of upheaval that executed a king, unleashed a reign of mass political violence, and gave the world its most enduring political vocabulary — liberty, equality, fraternity. The French Revolution did not just change France. It changed the idea of what a nation could be.

To understand why it happened, you need to picture France in the 1780s. It was a country of stark and rigid inequality. At the top sat King Louis the Sixteenth and his Austrian wife Marie Antoinette, living in the gilded splendour of Versailles. Below them, the nobility and the clergy enjoyed vast privileges, including exemption from most taxes. And at the bottom was everyone else — the vast majority of the French population — crushed by taxes, bad harvests, and rising bread prices.

France was also financially ruined. Years of costly wars, including French support for the American Revolution, had left the treasury empty. The king's finance ministers tried repeatedly to reform the tax system and make the nobility pay their fair share, but the nobility blocked every attempt. By 1788, France was essentially bankrupt.

---

The crisis came to a head in 1789. A catastrophic harvest the previous year had sent bread prices soaring. Ordinary people were going hungry. King Louis, desperate for a solution, called the Estates-General — a representative body that had not met since 1614 — to discuss the financial crisis. When it met in May 1789, the representatives of the Third Estate — meaning everyone who was not clergy or nobility — found themselves sidelined and their voices ignored. They broke away and declared themselves a National Assembly, vowing to write a new constitution for France.

When Louis tried to lock them out of their meeting hall, they gathered on a nearby tennis court and took an oath not to disperse until they had given France a constitution. It was an act of extraordinary defiance. Even more extraordinary was that Louis backed down and recognized the National Assembly.

But Paris was on the edge. Rumours spread that the king was massing troops to crush the new Assembly. On the fourteenth of July, 1789, a crowd stormed the Bastille, a royal fortress and prison in Paris that had become a symbol of royal tyranny. They freed its prisoners — there were only seven held inside at the time — killed its governor, and paraded his head through the streets. The fall of the Bastille was powerful in symbolism rather than scale, but that symbolism was enormous. The revolution had begun.

---

Throughout the summer of 1789, the old order disintegrated. Peasants across France rose up against their landlords in what became known as the Great Fear. The National Assembly abolished feudalism, stripped the nobility of their privileges, and issued the Declaration of the Rights of Man and of the Citizen, a document that proclaimed liberty, equality before the law, and the sovereignty of the people. Its echoes would be heard in constitutions and revolutions around the world for centuries.

The royal family attempted to flee France in June 1791, hoping to reach allied territory and raise an army to reverse the revolution. They got as far as Varennes before being recognised and arrested. Their failed escape destroyed whatever remained of public trust in the king.

War added fuel to the fire. In April 1792, France declared war on Austria, fearing a coalition of European monarchies intent on crushing the revolution. The early campaigns went badly. Prussian forces crossed into France, and their commander issued a declaration threatening to destroy Paris if the royal family was harmed. This threat enraged Parisians, who stormed the Tuileries palace in August 1792, overthrew the monarchy, and imprisoned the royal family. The First Republic was declared.

Louis the Sixteenth was put on trial in December 1792, charged with treason. He was convicted and executed by guillotine on the twenty-first of January, 1793. Marie Antoinette was executed on the sixteenth of October that same year.

---

The revolution now entered its darkest phase. France was under threat from foreign armies on multiple borders, fighting a civil war in the Vendée region, and consumed by political paranoia within. The radical Committee of Public Safety, dominated by Maximilien Robespierre, seized effective control of the government. What followed became known as the Reign of Terror. Anyone suspected of being an enemy of the revolution could be arrested, tried before a revolutionary tribunal, and executed within days. The guillotine worked constantly. Between September 1793 and July 1794, approximately seventeen thousand people were officially executed, and tens of thousands more died in prison or without trial.

The Terror consumed its own. Former revolutionary heroes were denounced and executed. Robespierre himself, who had sent so many others to the guillotine, was arrested on the ninth of Thermidor — the twenty-seventh of July, 1794 — and executed the following day. His fall marked the end of the Terror and the beginning of a more moderate phase of the revolution.

---

But France remained unstable. Governments rose and fell. The revolution had produced brilliant military commanders, none more brilliant than a young Corsican artillery officer named Napoleon Bonaparte. He had made his name defending the revolution and winning spectacular victories in Italy and Egypt. In November 1799, he staged a coup, overthrew the government, and made himself First Consul — effectively the ruler of France. Ten years after the storming of the Bastille, the revolution had ended in a military dictatorship.

The French Revolution's contradictions are part of its legacy. It proclaimed liberty and equality while sending thousands to their deaths. It toppled one tyranny and eventually produced another. And yet its ideas proved unstoppable. The notion that governments derive their legitimacy from the people, that all citizens are equal before the law, that hereditary privilege has no natural place in a just society — these ideas, born in the chaos of revolutionary France, would go on to inspire independence movements, democratic revolutions, and constitutions around the world for the next two centuries and beyond.
