# The Scramble for Africa

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Imagine a map. A vast continent — the second largest on Earth, home to one fifth of the world's land surface — covered not in the neat colour-coded blocks that appear in colonial-era atlases, but in the territories of hundreds of kingdoms, empires, city-states, and confederacies, each with its own history stretching back centuries or even millennia. That is what Africa looked like before the eighteen eighties. And then, in the space of roughly thirty years, almost all of it was carved up and claimed by a handful of European powers who had never set foot in most of the land they were dividing.

This is the story of the Scramble for Africa — one of the most dramatic and consequential acts of territorial seizure in human history, and one whose consequences are still playing out today.

Long before the first European ship rounded the Cape of Good Hope, Africa was a continent of extraordinary political and economic complexity. In the west, the great empires of Ghana, Mali, and Songhai had risen and fallen over centuries, each one controlling the trans-Saharan trade routes that linked sub-Saharan gold and salt to the markets of North Africa and the Mediterranean. The city of Timbuktu, at its height in the fifteenth and sixteenth centuries, was not a dusty backwater but a centre of scholarship and commerce, home to one of the world's great universities and a population that scholars estimate may have reached one hundred thousand. Further south and east, the Kingdom of Kongo straddled the lower Congo River basin with a sophisticated system of governance, currency, and diplomacy. In East Africa, the Swahili city-states — Mombasa, Kilwa, Zanzibar — had been conducting ocean trade with Arabia, Persia, and India for over a thousand years. The stone-walled enclosures of Great Zimbabwe testified to a civilisation whose craftsmen worked gold and whose rulers commanded regional trade networks across southern Africa.

In the early nineteenth century, as Europeans were beginning to grow curious about the African interior, the continent was still home to powerful, functioning states. The Zulu Kingdom in the south had been forged by Shaka into a military force of formidable capability. The Ashanti Empire in the west controlled the Gold Coast and had repelled British attempts at domination on multiple occasions. The Islamic caliphates of the western Sudan were centres of learning and law. These were not primitive societies waiting to be rescued. They were polities with systems of justice, trade, diplomacy, and culture — some of them older than any European nation state.

---

Europe's relationship with Africa had, of course, begun long before the eighteen eighties. The Portuguese had established trading posts along the West African coast as early as the fourteen hundreds, building Fort Elmina on the Gold Coast in fourteen eighty-two. They were there primarily for gold, and later for enslaved people — a trade that would, over four centuries, remove somewhere between ten and twelve million Africans from their homes and scatter them across the Americas. By the early nineteenth century, European presence in Africa still consisted mainly of coastal footholds: port towns, forts, and trading stations clinging to the edge of a continent whose interior remained largely unknown to outsiders.

That began to change with the age of exploration. European governments and geographical societies sent expeditions inland, driven by a mixture of genuine scientific curiosity, commercial ambition, and a particular Victorian strain of humanitarian idealism that often masked harder imperial motives. And no figure embodied this complex mixture more completely than David Livingstone.

Livingstone was a Scottish doctor and Congregationalist missionary who first arrived in Africa in eighteen forty-one. Over the following three decades, he ranged across the continent in a series of extraordinary journeys, becoming in eighteen fifty-five the first European to see the great waterfall on the Zambezi that the local Kololo people called Mosi-oa-Tunya — the smoke that thunders — which he renamed Victoria Falls. He was driven by twin impulses: to spread Christianity and to abolish the Arab slave trade that he witnessed devastating communities across central and eastern Africa. His published accounts gripped the British public and made the African interior feel, for the first time, vivid and accessible.

When Livingstone went missing during an expedition in the late eighteen sixties, the New York Herald sent a Welsh-American journalist named Henry Morton Stanley to find him. Stanley tracked him down at the town of Ujiji on the shores of Lake Tanganyika on the tenth of November, eighteen seventy-one, greeting him with the immortal, if perhaps apocryphal, words: "Dr. Livingstone, I presume?" Livingstone died in eighteen seventy-three, still searching. But Stanley returned to Africa as an explorer in his own right, navigating the length of the Congo River in eighteen seventy-six and eighteen seventy-seven — a journey that opened the vast central African interior to European eyes and set in motion a chain of events that would reshape the continent forever.

---

The man who noticed Stanley's achievement most keenly was not a prime minister or a foreign secretary. He was a king — King Leopold II of Belgium, a man of outsized ambition and a small, prosperous country that had no history of overseas empire. Leopold had been searching for a colony for years, convinced that overseas possessions were the key to wealth and national greatness. The Congo, Stanley's reports made clear, was a territory of almost incomprehensible natural richness. Leopold moved quickly. He hired Stanley to return to the Congo and sign treaties with local chiefs — treaties the chiefs did not understand, written in legal language that ceded sovereignty to Leopold personally. He presented his enterprise to the world under the banner of the International Association of the Congo, cloaked in the language of humanitarianism and free trade. He was, he insisted, bringing civilisation to the heart of darkness.

The European powers were watching one another with growing anxiety. France, Germany, Portugal, and Britain were all making moves in Africa, staking claims and counter-claims at a pace that threatened to produce outright conflict between them. Into this diplomatic tinder box stepped the German Chancellor Otto von Bismarck, who called the major powers to a conference in Berlin in the autumn of eighteen eighty-four.

The Berlin Conference opened on the fifteenth of November, eighteen eighty-four, and ran until the twenty-sixth of February, eighteen eighty-five. Fourteen countries sent representatives: the major European powers, plus the United States and the Ottoman Empire. There was not a single African representative in the room. Not one. The fate of an entire continent — its kingdoms, its peoples, its trade routes, its sacred landscapes — was decided without any African voice being heard. The conference established the rules by which European powers could claim African territories. The key principle was "effective occupation": a European power could claim sovereignty over African land only if it could demonstrate that it actually controlled it. This turned the continent into a competition — a scramble — in which speed of conquest and occupation became the currency of empire. The conference also, crucially, recognised Leopold's personal claim to the Congo Basin, granting one man private ownership over a territory roughly the size of Western Europe.

Within a decade of the conference's conclusion, the shape of modern Africa had been roughed out in European capitals. Britain claimed a vast corridor running from Egypt in the north to the Cape Colony in the south. France controlled most of West Africa and the Sahara. Germany had staked out South West Africa (present-day Namibia), Togoland, Cameroon, and a stretch of the East African coast. Portugal kept Angola and Mozambique. Belgium — or rather, Leopold personally — held the Congo. By nineteen fourteen, when the First World War began, roughly ninety percent of Africa was under European control, up from about ten percent just forty years earlier. Only two nations remained independent: Liberia, founded by freed American slaves in eighteen forty-seven, and Ethiopia.

---

Africans did not simply submit. Across the continent, people fought back — often with extraordinary courage and, sometimes, with extraordinary success. In southern Africa, the Zulu Kingdom under King Cetshwayo resisted British encroachment with brilliant ferocity. On the twenty-second of January, eighteen seventy-nine, at the Battle of Isandlwana, a Zulu force of approximately twenty thousand warriors annihilated a British column of around one thousand eight hundred men — one of the worst defeats the British Army suffered in the Victorian era. The British eventually prevailed and broke Zulu independence later that year, but Isandlwana demonstrated that African armies, even when facing European firepower, could and did win.

In West Africa, the Mandinka warrior-king Samori Touré built an empire and then spent nearly two decades fighting the French, relocating his entire state eastward in a strategic retreat while continuing to resist. He was not captured until eighteen ninety-eight. In Sudan, a religious leader known as the Mahdi — Muhammad Ahmad — declared a holy war against Egyptian and British control, and his followers besieged Khartoum for nearly a year. On the twenty-sixth of January, eighteen eighty-five, Mahdist forces stormed the city and killed the British governor, General Charles Gordon, on the steps of the palace. It was not until eighteen ninety-eight that a British force under General Herbert Kitchener retook Sudan at the Battle of Omdurman, using machine guns and artillery to kill an estimated ten thousand Mahdist fighters in a matter of hours.

The most spectacular African victory of the entire colonial era came in Ethiopia. Emperor Menelik II had been modernising his army for years, acquiring rifles and artillery from European suppliers. When Italy attempted to impose a protectorate over Ethiopia in eighteen ninety-five, Menelik raised an army of roughly one hundred thousand men and marched north. On the first of March, eighteen ninety-six, at the Battle of Adwa, Ethiopian forces shattered the Italian army — killing, wounding, or capturing over half of its troops. It was the first time in the colonial era that an African nation had decisively defeated a European power on the battlefield. Italy was forced to recognise Ethiopian sovereignty. Menelik II became a symbol of African dignity that would inspire independence movements for generations to come.

---

But Ethiopia was the exception. For most of Africa, conquest was followed by exploitation of a kind that ranged from the brutal to the genocidal. In the Congo Free State, Leopold's personal property, the pursuit of rubber produced a system of terror without parallel. Congolese men were forced to meet rubber collection quotas under threat of extreme violence. Soldiers of Leopold's Force Publique were ordered to return a severed hand for every bullet they fired, to prove they had not wasted ammunition. Villages that failed to produce their quotas were burned. Women and children were taken hostage. The atrocities eventually came to light through the work of journalists and campaigners, most notably the British consul Roger Casement and the activist E. D. Morel, who built an international campaign that forced Belgium to take the Congo away from Leopold in nineteen oh eight. Historians estimate that between five and ten million people died in the Congo under Leopold's rule.

In German South West Africa, the colonial authorities responded to an uprising by the Herero people in nineteen oh four with systematic extermination. General Lothar von Trotha issued an order explicitly calling for the annihilation of the Herero. Those who were not killed in battle were driven into the Omaheke Desert, where their wells were poisoned or sealed. Around eighty percent of the Herero population — somewhere between forty thousand and eighty thousand people — were killed. The Nama people, who joined the uprising later, lost half their population. Historians widely recognise the Herero and Nama genocide as the first genocide of the twentieth century, and a grim precedent for what would follow in the century ahead.

Across the continent, the human cost of conquest and colonisation was immense. Land was confiscated, traditional economies were dismantled, and African men were conscripted into labour regimes that resembled slavery in everything but name. Languages were suppressed, religions were attacked, and systems of governance that had functioned for centuries were abolished and replaced with the arbitrary authority of a district commissioner appointed in London, Paris, or Brussels.

The borders those commissioners administered were perhaps the most consequential legacy of all. Drawn in European capitals by diplomats with maps that were often inaccurate and with no knowledge of the people who lived on the ground, colonial boundaries sliced straight through ethnic homelands, language communities, and ancient trade routes. The Somali people were divided among five different colonial territories. The Maasai were split between Kenya and Tanzania. Hundreds of distinct peoples who had never shared a political identity were bundled together inside the same colonial unit, while others who had always functioned as a single community were cut in two.

---

The colonial period lasted roughly seventy years for most of Africa, though some parts endured it for much longer. After the Second World War, as European powers emerged exhausted and morally diminished, and as new ideas about self-determination gained force in international politics, the tide began to turn. Ghana led the way in nineteen fifty-seven, when Kwame Nkrumah led the country to independence from Britain — the first sub-Saharan African colony to achieve it. Nineteen sixty became known as the Year of Africa, when seventeen countries gained independence in a single year, among them Nigeria, Senegal, the Ivory Coast, and the Congo. By the end of the nineteen sixties, Africa had forty-eight independent nations. The process was not complete until nineteen ninety, when Namibia finally broke free of South African rule, and nineteen ninety-four, when South Africa itself held its first fully democratic election.

But independence did not erase the colonial past. The borders drawn in Berlin in eighteen eighty-four and finalised in the years that followed are almost all still there today. The ethnic tensions created or inflamed by colonial divide-and-rule policies still fuel conflicts across the continent. The economic structures established to extract African resources for European benefit proved remarkably durable.

The Scramble for Africa was not simply a historical event with a beginning and an end. It was an act of violence — economic, political, and often literally physical — that reshaped one of the world's great continents in a generation, and whose echoes have never fully faded. Understanding it is not merely an exercise in historical curiosity. It is essential context for understanding the world we live in now: the conflicts, the borders, the inequalities, and the extraordinary resilience of the people who have lived with its consequences ever since.
