# Ancient Egypt: The Civilisation That Endured Three Thousand Years

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

There is no civilisation in human history quite like ancient Egypt. It lasted, in recognisable form, for roughly three thousand years — from its political unification around 3150 BC to the death of Cleopatra in 30 BC, when the last pharaoh was gone and Egypt had become a Roman province. Three thousand years is a span of time so vast that it dwarfs everything else in recorded history. When the great pyramid of Giza was being built, in the twenty-sixth century BC, it was already closer in time to us than to the earliest dynastic Egyptians. The Roman Empire, which feels ancient to us, was to an Egyptian of Cleopatra's day a recent development. The medieval cathedrals of Europe have barely been standing for a thousand years; ancient Egyptian civilisation had already achieved and maintained a complex, sophisticated, literate culture for twice that long before a single stone of Notre-Dame was laid.

What allowed Egypt to endure was, above all, the Nile. The river defines the country absolutely: Egypt without the Nile is desert — and it is desert everywhere except the narrow ribbon of fertile land along the river's banks and the wide triangle of the delta where it meets the Mediterranean. Each year, the Nile flooded, depositing a layer of rich black silt that made the land extraordinarily productive. The Egyptians called their country Kemet — "the black land" — to distinguish it from Deshret, "the red land," the surrounding desert. The flood was predictable, reliable, and generous. It allowed agriculture in an otherwise inhospitable region, sustained a large population, and generated the surplus that made civilisation possible.

---

The first pharaoh unified Upper and Lower Egypt — the Nile valley and the delta — around 3150 BC, inaugurating what historians call the Early Dynastic Period. It is from this moment that Egyptian history is conventionally counted, though human beings had been living and farming in the Nile valley for thousands of years before this political unification. What the unification created was not just a kingdom but a state with a distinctive ideology: the pharaoh was not merely a king but a god — the living embodiment of Horus, the falcon deity, and at death the manifestation of Osiris, ruler of the dead. The entire apparatus of Egyptian civilisation — the temples, the rituals, the monumental art, the obsessive preoccupation with death and the afterlife — flowed from this theology. Everything the pharaoh did was, in principle, a ritual act. To build a pyramid was not merely to build a tomb; it was to participate in the cosmic order, to ensure the renewal of the sun, to maintain Ma'at — the Egyptian concept of truth, justice, and cosmic harmony.

The period historians call the Old Kingdom, running roughly from 2686 to 2181 BC, was the age of the great pyramid-builders. The Third through Sixth Dynasties saw the construction of the most spectacular monuments in human history. Under the Fourth Dynasty pharaoh Khufu — known to the Greeks as Cheops — the Great Pyramid of Giza was built: a structure of more than two million stone blocks, rising to an original height of nearly one hundred and forty-seven metres, remaining the tallest man-made structure in the world for nearly four thousand years. His son Khafre and grandson Menkaure built the other two pyramids of the famous Giza complex; Khafre's is associated with the Great Sphinx, carved from the bedrock beside his pyramid. These were not slave-built monuments, as popular myth long held — archaeological evidence shows that the builders were skilled workers, housed and fed by the state, working in rotating shifts.

---

The Old Kingdom eventually collapsed, around 2181 BC, in a period of fragmentation and regional power struggles historians call the First Intermediate Period. It was followed by reunification under the Middle Kingdom, which lasted until roughly 1650 BC, and which produced some of Egypt's finest literature, including the Story of Sinuhe and the Instructions of Amenemhat. A second period of fragmentation followed, during which Egypt was ruled in part by the Hyksos — a people from the Levant who brought new technologies, including the horse-drawn chariot, and who were eventually expelled.

The expulsion of the Hyksos marked the beginning of the New Kingdom, roughly 1550 to 1070 BC, which is the period most people think of when they imagine ancient Egypt at its height. It was an era of empire: Egyptian armies pushed south into Nubia and north into Canaan and Syria, creating the most extensive Egyptian state in history. The New Kingdom produced some of the most famous names in Egyptian history. Hatshepsut, one of the few women to rule as pharaoh, oversaw extensive building programmes and trading expeditions to the land of Punt. Thutmose III, who came to the throne after her, was perhaps Egypt's greatest military commander, winning more than seventeen campaigns.

Amenhotep IV, who renamed himself Akhenaten and reigned from roughly 1353 to 1336 BC, remains one of the most enigmatic figures in all of ancient history. He abandoned Egypt's traditional polytheistic religion — a rich pantheon of gods headed by Amun — and replaced it with the exclusive worship of a single deity: the Aten, the solar disc. It was the world's first experiment with something like monotheism. He moved the capital to a new city, Akhetaten (modern Amarna), closed the temples, and had the name of Amun erased from monuments throughout Egypt. His religious revolution died with him. His successor — probably his son — restored the old religion, moved the capital back, demolished Akhenaten's city, and erased his name from the records. That successor was Tutankhamun, who died young and whose fame today rests entirely on the remarkable preservation of his tomb, discovered by Howard Carter in 1922.

Ramesses II, who ruled from 1279 to 1213 BC, was the longest-reigning pharaoh in Egyptian history and the one who most aggressively promoted his own legend. He built on a scale that dwarfed his predecessors, covered existing temples with his own reliefs, and fathered an almost legendary number of children. The Battle of Kadesh, in which he fought the Hittites to something closer to a draw than his monuments admit, resulted in what is often called the world's earliest surviving peace treaty.

---

The New Kingdom eventually weakened through a combination of external pressures — invasions by the "Sea Peoples," the loss of empire, drought, and economic decline — and internal fragmentation. The period that followed was one of division, foreign domination, and periodic revivals, as successive powers — Libyan dynasties, Nubian pharaohs from Kush, Assyrian conquerors — held Egypt in whole or in part.

In 332 BC, Alexander the Great arrived from Greece and was welcomed by many Egyptians as a liberator from Persian rule. He was recognised as pharaoh, founded the city of Alexandria at the Nile delta, and visited the oracle of Amun at Siwa, which reportedly declared him the son of the god. Alexander died before he could consolidate his empire, and Egypt passed to his general Ptolemy, who declared himself king in 305 BC, founding the Ptolemaic dynasty.

The Ptolemies were Macedonian Greeks who adopted the external forms of Egyptian religion — presenting themselves as pharaohs, building temples in the Egyptian style, maintaining the cult of the gods — while conducting their court in Greek. Their capital, Alexandria, became the greatest city in the Mediterranean world: a centre of trade, scholarship, and culture, home to the famous Library of Alexandria and the Mouseion, a kind of ancient research institute. The Ptolemaic period produced the Rosetta Stone — a decree issued in 196 BC, inscribed in three scripts: hieroglyphic, Demotic, and Greek. When it was rediscovered by French soldiers during Napoleon's Egyptian campaign in 1799, the parallel texts provided the key that allowed scholars to crack the code of hieroglyphics. Jean-François Champollion's decipherment in the 1820s opened the entire edifice of ancient Egyptian civilisation to modern understanding.

---

The last of the Ptolemies was Cleopatra VII — the Cleopatra of history, whose name means "glory of the father" in Greek. She was not Egyptian by ethnicity but she was, by all accounts, the first of the Ptolemaic rulers to bother learning the Egyptian language. She was also a formidably intelligent politician who navigated the collapsing world of the late Ptolemaic kingdom with extraordinary skill, forming alliances with the two most powerful Romans of her age — Julius Caesar and Mark Antony — in attempts to maintain Egyptian independence. She failed. After the naval Battle of Actium in 31 BC, in which her forces and Antony's were defeated by Octavian's, and after Antony's suicide, Cleopatra killed herself rather than be taken to Rome as a trophy in a triumphal procession. She died on the twelfth of August, 30 BC. Egypt became a Roman province. The three-thousand-year story of the pharaohs was over.

What Egypt left behind was not merely ruins and artefacts — though it left those in extraordinary abundance. It left a complete civilisation: a system of writing, a calendar, a body of mathematics and medicine, a tradition of monumental architecture, a theology, an art, and a way of understanding the human relationship to time and death that had no equal for the depth and persistence of its influence. The image of the pyramid is still the most universally recognised symbol of civilisation itself. That this is so is a measure of what ancient Egypt was.
