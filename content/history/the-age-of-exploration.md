# The Age of Exploration: When Europe Found the World

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

For most of human history, the world was larger than anyone knew. Civilisations rose and fell in near-isolation. The Americas were entirely unknown to Europeans. Much of Africa was a mystery beyond its northern coast. The seas beyond the horizon were full of monsters, or so the stories went. Then, in the space of barely a century — from the late 1400s to the mid-1500s — European sailors shattered all of that. They found new continents, mapped coastlines that had never been drawn, and linked the world together for the first time. The Age of Exploration was one of the most consequential periods in human history — and one of the most violent.

The story begins in Portugal. A small nation on the western edge of Europe, Portugal had powerful reasons to look seaward. Its Atlantic coastline offered natural harbours and experienced sailors. And by the mid-fifteenth century, Portuguese explorers had been methodically pushing south along the African coast, driven by a mixture of curiosity, commercial ambition, and a desire to find a sea route to the spice trade of India and the East Indies — enormously profitable and currently controlled by Arab and Italian middlemen.

The Portuguese approach was systematic and patient. Year after year, their ships pushed further south, mapping the African coast, establishing trading posts, learning the winds. In 1488, the Portuguese explorer Bartolomeu Dias became the first European to round the southern tip of Africa — the Cape of Good Hope — proving that the Indian Ocean could be reached by sea. It was a breakthrough that transformed what was possible.

---

Nine years later, Vasco da Gama set sail from Lisbon with four ships and a crew of around one hundred and seventy men. Following the route Dias had pioneered, he rounded the Cape, crossed the Indian Ocean, and on the twentieth of May, 1498, arrived at Kozhikode on the southwest coast of India. It was the first time a European had reached Asia by sea. The voyage opened a direct route to the spice trade that would make Portugal fabulously wealthy and permanently shift the balance of global commerce.

While Portugal was exploring the route around Africa, Spain was backing a different gamble. Christopher Columbus, a Genoese sailor, believed the world was small enough that sailing west from Europe would bring a ship to Asia faster than the long route around Africa. He was wrong about the size of the world, but Spain's monarchs Ferdinand and Isabella agreed to fund his voyage. On the third of August, 1492, Columbus set sail with three ships from Spain. On the twelfth of October, 1492, a lookout spotted land. Columbus had not reached Asia. He had reached the Bahamas — part of a world that Europeans had not known existed.

Columbus made four voyages across the Atlantic and never accepted that what he had found was not Asia. But others quickly understood the truth. The Italian explorer Amerigo Vespucci concluded that these lands were not Asia but an entirely new continent. When a German cartographer produced a world map in 1507, he named this new continent America — after Amerigo.

---

The most astonishing voyage of the era came between 1519 and 1522. Ferdinand Magellan, a Portuguese navigator sailing for Spain, set out with five ships and around two hundred and seventy men on the twentieth of September, 1519, intending to circumnavigate the globe. Magellan himself did not complete the journey — he was killed in a battle in the Philippines in 1521. But his lieutenant, Juan Sebastián Elcano, led the survivors home. On the sixth of September, 1522, one ship — the Victoria — limped into port in Spain carrying just eighteen surviving Europeans out of the original crew. They had sailed all the way around the world. It was one of the greatest feats of navigation in history, and it proved conclusively that the Earth was a sphere and that its oceans were connected.

The decades that followed brought a rapid expansion of European knowledge and power. Spanish conquistadors — Hernán Cortés in Mexico, Francisco Pizarro in Peru — toppled the Aztec and Inca empires with small forces of soldiers, aided by disease, indigenous allies, and military technology the Americas had never encountered. The gold and silver they looted, and later mined with forced labour, flowed back to Spain in quantities that transformed European economies. Portugal established trading posts across the Indian Ocean. The Dutch, English, and French followed, establishing their own routes, colonies, and commercial empires.

---

The Age of Exploration brought wealth, knowledge, and catastrophe in roughly equal measure. The contact between European and American civilisations was devastating for the people of the Americas. Smallpox and other European diseases, to which indigenous Americans had no immunity, swept through populations that had never encountered them. Some regions may have lost eighty percent or more of their population within a century. The Aztec and Inca empires — each vast and sophisticated — were conquered and dismantled within decades.

The trading networks established in this period transformed the global economy. Vast quantities of silver from Spanish mines flowed across the Pacific to China. Sugar, grown in the Caribbean with enslaved African labour, became one of the most profitable commodities in the world. And the plants of the Americas — potatoes, maize, tomatoes, tobacco, chocolate — transformed diets and agriculture on multiple continents. The potato alone, brought back from Peru, became a staple crop across Europe and fed population growth for centuries.

The world that emerged from the Age of Exploration was more connected than any before it. It was also more unequal and more violent. The same voyages that proved the Earth's roundness and opened new trade routes also began centuries of colonisation, enslavement, and extraction. For the peoples European sailors encountered, the age of discovery was often an age of catastrophe — a trauma whose consequences are still being reckoned with today.
