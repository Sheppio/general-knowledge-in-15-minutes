# The Boer War

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

To understand why a war broke out at the southern tip of Africa in the dying months of the nineteenth century, you have to go back more than two hundred years, to a small Dutch settlement clinging to the edge of the Cape of Good Hope. In sixteen fifty-two, an agent of the Dutch East India Company named Jan van Riebeeck arrived with a handful of settlers to establish a provisioning station for ships rounding the Cape on their way to the East Indies. Those settlers took root. Over the generations, they mixed with German immigrants, French Huguenot refugees, and the indigenous people of the region. They developed their own language — a simplified form of Dutch that would eventually become Afrikaans — and their own fierce, devout, Calvinist identity. They called themselves Boers, the Dutch word for farmers, and they regarded the wide, spare landscapes of southern Africa as land that God had given to them.

Then the British arrived. Britain seized the Cape Colony in eighteen o six, during the Napoleonic Wars, and the Boers found themselves under a colonial administration whose values clashed sharply with their own. The British abolished the slave trade, and then, in eighteen thirty-four, slavery itself. While the freeing of enslaved people was a moral good by any fair reckoning, the Boers did not see it that way. For many, it struck at both their economic livelihood and their social order. Combined with growing grievances about land policy and a legal system that treated Black Africans with more equality than the Boers thought appropriate, these tensions reached a breaking point.

The response was one of the most dramatic migrations in modern history. Beginning in eighteen thirty-five and running through the early eighteen forties, somewhere between twelve and fourteen thousand Boers — men, women, children, and their dependants — loaded their ox-wagons and trekked away from British territory into the interior of southern Africa. They called themselves Voortrekkers, meaning pioneers or those who move ahead, and what they undertook became known as the Great Trek. The Afrikaner people would eventually come to regard it as the foundational event of their nationhood, their own Exodus into the wilderness. They fought battles with Zulu and Ndebele kingdoms along the way. They suffered disease, hardship, and loss. But they pressed on, and in the end they carved out two independent republics beyond the reach of the British Crown: the Orange Free State, between the Orange and Vaal rivers, recognised by Britain in eighteen fifty-four, and the South African Republic — usually called the Transvaal — recognised in eighteen fifty-two. The Boers had, it seemed, found their promised land.

---

Then the earth gave up its secrets, and everything changed.

In eighteen sixty-seven, diamonds were found near the confluence of the Orange and Vaal rivers, in a region that would become the city of Kimberley. The discovery set off a rush of prospectors, speculators, and fortune-seekers. Britain, recognising the economic significance of the find, annexed the diamond fields in eighteen seventy-one — a move the Orange Free State bitterly resented, since the territory had arguably been theirs. The man who came to dominate the diamond industry was a young, driven, obsessive Englishman named Cecil Rhodes, who built his fortune through the De Beers mining company and developed a grand imperial vision: a British-controlled corridor running from the Cape all the way to Cairo.

But diamonds were merely the prologue. In eighteen eighty-six, the world changed far more dramatically. A prospector named George Harrison found traces of gold on a farm called Langlaagte, on a rocky ridge south of Pretoria called the Witwatersrand — the Ridge of White Waters. What lay beneath that ridge turned out to be the largest gold deposit ever discovered. Almost overnight, a tented camp on the highveld became the roaring, cosmopolitan city of Johannesburg. Miners and merchants, financiers and engineers flooded in from Britain, Australia, Germany, and beyond. The Transvaal, which had been a struggling pastoral republic of perhaps a hundred thousand farmers, found itself sitting atop the most valuable real estate on earth.

By eighteen ninety-nine, British investors owned roughly two thirds of the Witwatersrand's mines and had poured more than three hundred million pounds into the Transvaal. The influx of foreign workers — called Uitlanders, meaning outsiders — was so large that within a decade they may have outnumbered the Boers in their own republic. Paul Kruger, the deeply religious, deeply stubborn president of the Transvaal, watched all this with alarm. He knew that if the Uitlanders were granted the vote, the Boer republic would be swamped politically just as it was being swamped economically. So he kept the qualification period for citizenship at fourteen years, long enough to ensure the Uitlanders remained politically powerless. Britain, nominally protecting the rights of its subjects abroad, began pressing Kruger for reforms. The battle lines were being drawn, but it would take one act of extraordinary recklessness to make the war feel truly inevitable.

In late December of eighteen ninety-five, a British colonial administrator named Leander Starr Jameson led a force of roughly six hundred men across the border into the Transvaal. The Jameson Raid — backed, it quickly emerged, by Cecil Rhodes himself — was intended to spark an Uitlander uprising and hand the Transvaal to Britain. It was a spectacular fiasco. No uprising materialised. Jameson and his men were captured by Boer forces on the second of January, eighteen ninety-six, having barely set foot inside the republic. Rhodes was forced to resign as Prime Minister of the Cape Colony. The raid inflamed Boer opinion, confirmed every fear about British intentions, and drove the Transvaal and the Orange Free State into a defensive military alliance. It also embarrassed the British government enough that relations between the two sides entered a kind of embittered détente — unresolved, resentful, and heading toward collision.

The collision came through Lord Alfred Milner. Appointed as British High Commissioner for South Africa in eighteen ninety-seven, Milner was an imperialist of the most committed kind, firmly convinced that the Transvaal could never be allowed to remain a rival power to British interests. He saw the Uitlander franchise question not merely as a humanitarian issue but as the lever by which he could either force Kruger to submit politically or provoke a war that Britain would win. In late May and early June of eighteen ninety-nine, Milner and Kruger met at the Bloemfontein Conference. Kruger offered to reduce the Uitlander residency requirement for the vote to seven years. Milner wanted five, and insisted on other conditions that effectively made the Transvaal a British protectorate. On the fifth of June, Milner walked out. Both sides then began mobilising troops.

On the ninth of October, eighteen ninety-nine, the Transvaal issued an ultimatum: Britain must withdraw its troops from the borders within forty-eight hours, or a state of war would exist. London refused. On the eleventh of October, eighteen ninety-nine, the Second Boer War began.

---

In the opening weeks, it was the Boers who moved with speed and purpose. They crossed into British-held Natal and the Cape Colony, driving hard toward British garrisons before the British could concentrate their forces. By early November, they had placed three towns under siege. At Kimberley, the diamond capital, Cecil Rhodes himself was trapped inside with the garrison. At Mafeking, a small railway town near the Transvaal border, a British colonel named Robert Baden-Powell found himself besieged by six thousand Boer fighters. And at Ladysmith, in Natal, a substantial British force under General Sir George White was encircled and cut off. The Boers, fighting on their own terrain with an uncanny instinct for fieldcraft and an intimate knowledge of the landscape, had immediately seized the initiative.

Then came the week that broke British confidence entirely. Black Week, running from the tenth to the seventeenth of December, eighteen ninety-nine, brought three catastrophic British defeats in seven days. At Stormberg on the tenth of December, a night march went disastrously wrong under General Sir William Gatacre: more than six hundred British troops were captured, with over a hundred killed and wounded. At Magersfontein on the eleventh of December, Lord Methuen's attempt to relieve Kimberley was shattered when his Highland Brigade blundered into hidden Boer trenches in the pre-dawn darkness, suffering over a thousand casualties. And at Colenso on the fifteenth of December, General Sir Redvers Buller — the commander-in-chief of British forces in South Africa — led his army to defeat trying to cross the Tugela River and reach Ladysmith, losing eleven hundred men and seeing ten field guns abandoned on the riverbank. In total, across those seven days, two thousand seven hundred and seventy-six British soldiers were killed, wounded, or captured. The shock in Britain was profound.

The government responded with urgency. Buller was superseded as commander-in-chief on the eighteenth of December and replaced by Field Marshal Lord Frederick Roberts, the most celebrated British soldier of his generation. Roberts arrived at the Cape on the tenth of January, nineteen hundred, with General Lord Kitchener as his chief of staff. The pair would transform the war's momentum.

Roberts refused to be drawn into the Boers' preferred kind of fighting. Instead, he launched a massive flanking movement. In February, his cavalry under General John French relieved Kimberley on the fifteenth of February, nineteen hundred, after a siege of one hundred and twenty-four days. Shortly after, Boer General Piet Cronjé found himself surrounded at Paardeberg and surrendered on the twenty-seventh of February with just over four thousand men. Roberts pushed on, taking Bloemfontein in mid-March. In May, the siege of Mafeking was finally lifted after two hundred and seventeen days, and the news sparked near-hysterical celebrations across Britain — the word "mafficking" entered the language to describe the wild public rejoicing. Pretoria, the capital of the Transvaal, fell on the fifth of June, nineteen hundred. To all appearances, the war was over.

It was not over.

---

The Boer commanders — Louis Botha, Christiaan de Wet, Koos de la Rey, and Jan Smuts among them — refused to accept defeat. They sent their families away, discarded their heavy wagons, split into small, fast-moving units called commandos, and melted into the veld. What followed was two more years of some of the most exhausting guerrilla warfare the British Army had ever faced. Kitchener, who took over as commander-in-chief in November nineteen hundred, found himself chasing ghosts across a vast and hostile landscape with nearly four hundred and fifty thousand men.

Kitchener's solution was brutal and systematic. He began burning Boer farmhouses — eventually some thirty thousand of them — to deny the commandos shelter and food. He destroyed crops and killed livestock. He strung eight thousand blockhouses across the veld, connected by three thousand seven hundred miles of barbed wire, dividing the land into compartments that could be swept by columns of soldiers. And he removed the civilian population from the farms. Women, children, and the elderly were taken from their homes — or from the rubble of homes that had been burned — and herded into what the British called concentration camps.

The camps were catastrophic. They were conceived as a means of control, not extermination, but the outcome was nearly indistinguishable from deliberate mass death. The camps were overcrowded. Rations were inadequate. Sanitation barely existed. Medical care was hopelessly insufficient. Disease tore through the tents — measles, typhoid, dysentery — killing children above all. A British welfare campaigner named Emily Hobhouse visited the camps in early nineteen oh one and returned to Britain with a devastating forty-page report documenting the conditions she had witnessed. Her report caused an enormous political scandal. A subsequent official commission headed by Millicent Fawcett confirmed everything Hobhouse had described, and the authorities were forced to improve conditions.

By the time the war ended, twenty-six thousand three hundred and seventy Boer civilians — the overwhelming majority of them children — had died in the camps. But the full toll was even larger, because a parallel system of camps held Black Africans. Around one hundred and seven thousand Black people were interned in sixty-four separate camps, where they faced even worse conditions and even less official concern. At least fourteen thousand died, though the true figure is believed to be considerably higher because records were kept so poorly. These African deaths have received far less historical attention than the Boer deaths, a disproportion that itself reflects the racial politics the war helped to entrench.

---

By the spring of nineteen oh two, the guerrilla commanders could feel the net closing. The commando bands were exhausted, hungry, and shrinking. Representatives of the Boer republics and the British government met at Vereeniging, a town on the Vaal River, and on the thirty-first of May, nineteen oh two, they signed the Peace of Vereeniging. The Boer republics surrendered their independence. Their citizens became British subjects. A general amnesty was declared, and Britain promised three million pounds toward rebuilding the devastated farms and communities of the former republics.

In purely military terms, Britain had won. But almost no one who looked clearly at the aftermath could feel comfortable about what that victory had cost. Britain had mobilised nearly half a million men to defeat a combined Boer fighting force that may never have exceeded eighty-five thousand, and it had taken two and a half years to do so. More than seven thousand five hundred British soldiers had been killed in action or died of wounds. Another thirteen thousand had died of disease — typhoid above all. The financial cost ran to over two hundred million pounds.

The war also did something more lasting: it stripped away certain comfortable assumptions about the British Empire. The brutality of the camps, exposed by Hobhouse and debated in Parliament, became an international scandal. Within Britain itself, a vocal minority had opposed the war from the start, and the camps gave their arguments a terrible vindication. The image of the empire as a civilising force, an institution that stood for liberty and law, became harder to sustain.

As for South Africa, the settlement that followed the war contained the seeds of a long and terrible future. In nineteen ten, the four colonies were merged into the Union of South Africa, a self-governing dominion within the British Empire. It was, in many ways, a settlement designed to reconcile white British South Africans and white Afrikaner South Africans at the expense of everyone else. The Black Africans who had lived through the war, many of whom had hoped that a British victory might bring greater rights and protections, were excluded from the political settlement almost entirely. The groundwork for apartheid — formally introduced in nineteen forty-eight — was laid in the ashes of the Boer War.

The Afrikaners, for their part, did not forget what had been done to them. The burned farms, the concentration camps, the dead children — these became the foundational grievances of Afrikaner nationalism. Forty years after Vereeniging, that nationalism was strong enough to win a general election and place Afrikaner politicians in power over all of South Africa. They would exercise that power for nearly half a century.

The Boer War was, in the end, a conflict that settled very little and damaged almost everything it touched. It was fought for gold and for empire, and it produced a peace that benefited almost no one except, in the very short term, the shareholders of the Witwatersrand mines. The soldiers on both sides had fought with genuine courage and considerable skill. The civilians — Boer and Black alike — had suffered in ways that no military calculation had prepared anyone for. And the world that emerged from those two and a half years of fighting was one in which the British Empire would never quite look the same again, and South Africa would carry the scars for generations to come.
