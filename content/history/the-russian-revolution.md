# The Russian Revolution: The End of the Tsars

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

In the early hours of the twentieth century, Russia was a vast, struggling empire held together by autocracy, orthodoxy, and the divine right of the Tsar. One hundred and thirty million people lived under a system that had barely changed since the Middle Ages. Most were peasants — landless, illiterate, and dependent on harvests that failed with terrible regularity. A small industrial working class had begun to grow in the cities, labouring in brutal conditions for long hours and little pay. And above them all sat Nicholas II, the last of the Romanovs, a man of limited political imagination who believed sincerely in his God-given right to rule absolutely and who was temperamentally unsuited to the pressures of the modern world.

The forces that would eventually destroy him had been building for decades. The humiliating defeat in the Russo-Japanese War of 1904 to 1905 exposed the military's incompetence and shook confidence in the regime. In January 1905, a peaceful march to the Winter Palace by workers carrying a petition was met with gunfire from imperial troops — an event known as Bloody Sunday that killed hundreds of protesters and shattered the Tsar's image as a benevolent father of his people. A wave of strikes and uprisings forced Nicholas to issue the October Manifesto, promising a constitution and a parliament, the Duma. But as soon as stability returned, he steadily undermined both.

---

The disaster that finally broke the Romanov dynasty was the First World War. When Russia entered the conflict in the summer of 1914, there was a burst of patriotic fervour — the kind of mood that seemed to transcend divisions of class and ethnicity. It did not last. The Russian army, poorly equipped and badly led, suffered catastrophic losses. By some estimates, Russia suffered more military casualties than any other belligerent power — millions dead, wounded, or taken prisoner. Soldiers deserted in enormous numbers. The supply system broke down. In the cities, bread became scarce. By the winter of 1916 to 1917, the situation had become desperate.

Nicholas made the fatal error of taking personal command of the army, which meant that every defeat was now his defeat personally. In his absence from Petrograd — the capital, renamed from its German-sounding predecessor Saint Petersburg at the outbreak of war — the Empress Alexandra fell under the influence of Grigori Rasputin, a Siberian mystic who appeared to have an uncanny ability to ease the suffering of the haemophiliac Crown Prince Alexei. Rasputin's influence over the court, his advice on ministerial appointments, his drunken debauchery — all of it became the subject of outrage among the nobility and the educated classes. In December 1916, a group of aristocrats murdered him, but by then the damage was done.

---

The February Revolution came not as a planned uprising but as a spontaneous collapse. In late February 1917 — early March by the Western calendar — bread riots and workers' strikes in Petrograd escalated beyond anything the police could contain. Crucially, when Nicholas ordered the army to suppress the disturbances, the troops refused to fire on the crowds. The soldiers were themselves hungry, exhausted, and resentful. They joined the protests instead. On the fifteenth of March, 1917, Nicholas II abdicated the throne, not in the face of a revolutionary conspiracy but in the face of a system that had simply ceased to function. Three centuries of Romanov rule ended with a whimper.

A Provisional Government, composed largely of liberal politicians, took power. It made one catastrophic decision that sealed its fate: it continued the war. For the vast majority of Russians, the war was the source of every misery. Continuing it drained any legitimacy the new government might have commanded.

Into this moment stepped Vladimir Lenin. He had been living in exile in Switzerland for years, unable to return to a Russia where he would have been arrested immediately. When the Tsar fell, he negotiated passage home through Germany — the Germans were delighted to send a radical agitator into the heart of their enemy's politics — in what became famous as the sealed train. Lenin arrived at Petrograd's Finland Station on the sixteenth of April, 1917, and within hours was delivering speeches that electrified the crowd with a message the Provisional Government most feared: no support for the war, all power to the soviets.

---

The soviets — councils of workers and soldiers — had sprung up across Russia in the revolutionary atmosphere of 1917. They were chaotic and contested, but they had real power because they controlled the factories and the troops. Lenin's Bolshevik party, a tightly disciplined revolutionary organisation, set about capturing control of the soviets with a single-minded energy the liberals could not match. His message was simple: bread, peace, land. No abstractions, no constitutional niceties — just the three things Russians most desperately wanted.

Through the summer of 1917, the Provisional Government lurched from crisis to crisis. A botched military offensive led to further enormous losses. A confused coup attempt by a conservative general, Lavr Kornilov, allowed the Bolsheviks to pose as defenders of the revolution while secretly arming themselves with weapons handed out to counter the threat. By October, the Bolsheviks had majorities in the Petrograd and Moscow soviets. Lenin pressed for immediate action.

On the seventh of November, 1917 — the twenty-fifth of October by the old Russian calendar, which is why it became known as the October Revolution — Bolshevik forces seized key buildings and installations in Petrograd: the telephone exchange, railway stations, the state bank. They surrounded the Winter Palace, where the Provisional Government was meeting, and arrested its ministers. The event that had been planned as a surgical seizure of power was in reality remarkably undramatic. Kerensky, the prime minister, had already fled. There was almost no fighting. One of the most consequential revolutions in history was accomplished with less violence than an average street brawl.

---

What followed was incomparably more violent. The Bolsheviks — who renamed themselves the Communist Party — faced a brutal civil war against a coalition of opponents: former Tsarist officers, democratic socialists, foreign interventionists from Britain, France, the United States, and Japan. The fighting lasted until 1922 and killed millions — through direct combat, famine, and the devastating epidemics that follow in the wake of war. The Bolsheviks won through a combination of iron discipline, ruthless centralised control, and the Red Terror — a campaign of mass executions, hostage-taking, and summary killings directed at class enemies.

In the night of the sixteenth to seventeenth of July, 1918, at a house in Yekaterinburg in the Urals where they had been held in captivity, Nicholas II, his wife Alexandra, their five children, and four servants were shot and bayoneted to death by Bolshevik executioners. The killing was carried out in a basement, in darkness, in terrible haste, by men who were frightened that the advancing White Army might rescue the family. Russia's last Tsar died as he had lived: not in dramatic defiance but in confusion and passivity.

Lenin — the architect of Soviet power, the man who had channelled a century's worth of rage and revolutionary theory into a working state — died in January 1924, his health shattered by the pressures of revolution and civil war. He was fifty-three. The struggle to succeed him consumed the Bolshevik leadership throughout the nineteen-twenties and ended with the absolute victory of Joseph Stalin — a figure whose cruelties would make even the most violent chapters of the revolution look restrained.

The Russian Revolution remade the world. It created the Soviet Union, which lasted until 1991 and shaped every major conflict and political development of the twentieth century. It generated both the fear that drove Western democracies to desperate counter-measures and the hope that animated liberation movements on every continent. Whatever one thinks of its results — and the results included famines, gulags, and tens of millions of deaths — there is no understanding the modern world without understanding the moment in which the Romanovs fell and the Bolsheviks rose to take their place.
