# The Birth of Islam

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Imagine standing in the middle of the Arabian Peninsula sometime in the sixth century AD. The landscape is vast and unforgiving — a world of ochre dunes, black volcanic rock, and skies so wide they seem to press down on everything beneath them. There are no rivers worth speaking of, no great lakes, no fertile plains stretching to a gentle horizon. What there is, instead, is trade. Caravans threading through the desert, moving incense and leather and spices from the south toward the great empires of the north — the Byzantine Romans on one side, the Sasanian Persians on the other. Arabia sits between these two colossal powers, and somehow that precarious position is also a kind of freedom.

This is the world that later Muslim historians would call the jahiliyyah — the age of ignorance. It is a charged term, born of a religious perspective, and historians have been careful not to take it entirely at face value. Pre-Islamic Arabia was not a culturally empty place. There was rich oral poetry, a highly developed sense of tribal honour, and an elaborate network of commercial and social relationships. But it was also a world marked by deep fragmentation. Tribes formed the basic unit of society, and loyalty to one's tribe was the supreme moral obligation. Blood feuds could run for generations. The weak — slaves, women without powerful male protectors, orphans — were frequently at the mercy of whoever had the most swords.

At the centre of this world, both literally and spiritually, stood a city called Mecca, and at the centre of Mecca stood a cube-shaped stone shrine known as the Ka'ba. By the sixth century, the Ka'ba was already ancient in the minds of the people who venerated it, and Islamic tradition holds that it was originally built by the patriarch Abraham and his son Ishmael as a house of monotheistic worship. In the centuries before Muhammad, however, it had become home to a vast collection of idols — perhaps as many as three hundred and sixty of them, representing the gods of the various Arab tribes. Once every year, tribes from across the peninsula would lay down their arms and make pilgrimage to Mecca, creating a sacred truce that benefited the city enormously as a commercial hub. The Quraysh, the tribe that controlled Mecca and served as custodians of the Ka'ba, grew wealthy and powerful on the strength of this arrangement.

It was into this world, into the Quraysh tribe and specifically its Banu Hashim clan, that a boy named Muhammad ibn Abdullah was born around the year five hundred and seventy AD. His life began in hardship. His father Abdullah died before Muhammad was born, and his mother Amina died when the boy was only six years old. He was raised first by his grandfather Abd al-Muttalib, and then, after the grandfather's death, by his uncle Abu Talib, a respected but not wealthy man. As a young man Muhammad worked as a trader and acquired a reputation for honesty so reliable that people called him al-Amin — the trustworthy one. Around the year five hundred and ninety-five, when he was approximately twenty-five years old, he entered the employment of a prosperous widow and merchant named Khadija bint Khuwaylid, managing her caravans. She was so impressed by his character that she proposed marriage. He accepted, and Khadija became the great anchor of his early life — his partner, his confidante, and, as events would unfold, his first believer.

---

For years, Muhammad had been given to long periods of reflection. He was troubled by the world around him — by the poverty, the tribal violence, the moral emptiness he saw in the religious practices of his people. He had a habit of retreating to a cave on the rocky slopes of a mountain called Hira, about three miles north of Mecca, where he would spend days at a time in prayer and contemplation. It was there, in the year six hundred and ten, during the month of Ramadan, that everything changed.

Muhammad was about forty years old when, according to Islamic tradition, the angel Gabriel appeared to him in the cave and commanded him simply to read. Muhammad replied that he could not — he was, by all accounts, illiterate. The angel seized him and pressed him with tremendous force, three times, before the first words of what would become the Quran came rushing out of him: words that translate roughly as "Read! In the name of your Lord who created." Shaking and terrified, Muhammad scrambled down the mountain to his wife. Khadija wrapped him in a cloak and held him until his trembling stopped. She then took him to her elderly cousin Waraqah ibn Nawfal, a man learned in the Christian and Jewish scriptures, who listened to what had happened and declared without hesitation that the spirit who had come to Muhammad was the same one who had once come to Moses. Muhammad, Waraqah said, was a prophet.

The revelations continued, at first in a trickle and then in a flood, over the next twenty-three years. They came as experiences of overwhelming presence, sometimes accompanied by a sound like a ringing bell, and Muhammad would afterward recite what he had received to his companions, who memorised and later wrote the words down. The message was stark in its simplicity and radical in its context: there is only one God — Allah — and Muhammad is his messenger. Idolatry is an abomination. All human beings are equal before God. Generosity to the poor is a religious duty. The Day of Judgement is coming, and every soul will be held to account for its deeds.

The first converts were people close to Muhammad. Khadija became the first Muslim. His young cousin Ali ibn Abi Talib was among the first. His close friend Abu Bakr brought in several others. But as the community began to grow and Muhammad preached more openly in the streets of Mecca, the reaction of the Quraysh leadership turned from contempt to active hostility. The new religion was a direct attack on the social and economic order they depended upon. If there was only one God and the idols of the Ka'ba were worthless, then the entire pilgrimage economy — the annual gathering of tribes, the flow of wealth into Mecca — was under threat.

The persecution that followed was severe. Enslaved followers were particularly vulnerable. A woman named Sumayyah bint Khabbab and her husband Yasir were tortured to death by their master. Other Muslims were beaten, starved, and economically boycotted. In six hundred and fifteen, Muhammad sent a group of his followers across the Red Sea to Abyssinia — what is now Ethiopia — where the Christian king, the Negus, offered them protection. This first emigration bought some of the community time, but the pressure in Mecca continued to mount. When Khadija died in six hundred and nineteen, and Abu Talib died shortly after, Muhammad lost both his emotional cornerstone and his political protector. The community was dangerously exposed.

---

Salvation came from an unexpected direction: the oasis city of Yathrib, roughly four hundred kilometres to the north of Mecca. Delegates from the city's main tribes had heard Muhammad preach during the pilgrimage season and found his message compelling. Yathrib was riven by inter-tribal conflict, and they saw in Muhammad a man who might serve as a neutral mediator. They invited him to come and lead them. In six hundred and twenty-two, Muhammad and his followers made the journey northward. This migration — the Hijra — would become so foundational to Islamic history that the Muslim calendar counts its years from this event. It was not merely a physical relocation; it was the birth of a community.

Yathrib was renamed Medina, the City of the Prophet. Almost immediately upon his arrival, Muhammad negotiated a remarkable political document that scholars call the Constitution of Medina. It was an agreement between the Muslim emigrants from Mecca, the Muslim converts of Medina, and several Jewish tribes of the city. All of them, the document declared, constituted a single community — a single ummah. Each group retained its own religious practices, but they pledged mutual defence and agreed to bring disputes before Muhammad for arbitration. It was, in its way, one of the earliest experiments in multi-faith constitutional governance.

The years in Medina were not peaceful, however. The Quraysh back in Mecca were not content to let Muhammad build a power base to the north. They had confiscated the property the emigrants had left behind, and they continued to harass the new community through raids and blockades. Muhammad authorised his followers to conduct raids on Quraysh caravans — both as a source of income and as a form of political pressure. These skirmishes escalated into open warfare. In the year six hundred and twenty-four, at a place called Badr, roughly one hundred kilometres southwest of Medina, the two sides met in a pitched battle. The Muslim force numbered approximately three hundred and thirteen men; the Quraysh sent roughly one thousand. The result was a stunning Muslim victory. Seventy Quraysh warriors were killed, including several of their most prominent leaders, and another seventy were captured. The Battle of Badr sent shockwaves through Arabia. God, the Muslims believed with renewed fervour, was on their side.

The Quraysh answered at the Battle of Uhud in six hundred and twenty-five. This time the result went the other way. A disciplined Muslim archer contingent abandoned their positions at a critical moment, allowing a cavalry charge led by Khalid ibn al-Walid to shatter the Muslim lines. Seventy Muslims died, including Muhammad's beloved uncle Hamza. Muhammad himself was wounded. The defeat was deeply sobering, but the community held together. Two years later, in six hundred and twenty-seven, the Quraysh mounted their most ambitious assault yet, arriving at Medina with an alliance of ten thousand warriors. On the advice of a Persian convert named Salman, the Muslims dug a long defensive trench across the main approach to the city — a tactic entirely foreign to Arab warfare. The besieging army could not cross it, and after roughly two weeks of stalemate, they withdrew. The Battle of the Trench effectively ended the Quraysh's ability to destroy Medina by direct assault.

---

In the years that followed, the tide turned decisively. A negotiated truce with Mecca in six hundred and twenty-eight — the Treaty of Hudaybiyyah — was widely seen as a concession at the time, but it gave the Muslims two years of peace in which their numbers and influence grew rapidly across the peninsula. When the Quraysh broke the truce by attacking a tribe allied with the Muslims, Muhammad moved. In January of the year six hundred and thirty, he led an army of approximately ten thousand men toward Mecca. The city had no realistic hope of resistance. The Quraysh leadership, including their longtime chief opponent Abu Sufyan, converted to Islam on the eve of the army's arrival. Muhammad entered Mecca without significant bloodshed. He declared a general amnesty for his former persecutors — the people who had driven him from his home, who had tortured his followers, who had sought his death for twenty years. The idols in the Ka'ba were destroyed, all three hundred and sixty of them. The ancient shrine was rededicated to the one God who, Muhammad proclaimed, had always been its true owner.

The remaining two years of Muhammad's life were spent consolidating this transformation. Delegations poured in from tribes across Arabia, and the vast majority of the peninsula accepted Islam. Muhammad made one final pilgrimage to Mecca in six hundred and thirty-two, delivering what became known as the Farewell Sermon to a gathering of tens of thousands of pilgrims. He spoke of equality — of the Arab having no superiority over the non-Arab, of the white having no superiority over the black. He enjoined his followers to treat women with kindness. He reminded them that every Muslim was the brother of every other Muslim. In June of six hundred and thirty-two, approximately three months after that sermon, Muhammad fell ill with a fever in Medina. He died on the eighth of June of that year in the arms of his wife Aisha, at approximately sixty-two years of age.

---

The immediate reaction in Medina was disbelief and chaos. Many of his followers could not accept that he was gone. It was Abu Bakr who stood before the community and spoke the words that cut through the grief: "Whoever worshipped Muhammad, let him know that Muhammad is dead. But whoever worships God, let him know that God is alive and never dies." The community gathered itself. Abu Bakr was elected the first caliph — the successor — and the work of governing the sprawling new Islamic community fell to him.

What happened next was one of the most dramatic expansions in the history of human civilisation. Under Abu Bakr, who ruled from six hundred and thirty-two to six hundred and thirty-four, the tribes that had pledged loyalty to Muhammad personally and tried to withdraw after his death were brought back into the fold through a series of wars known as the Ridda, or apostasy campaigns. Arabia was unified. Then the armies turned outward. Under the second caliph, Umar ibn al-Khattab, who led from six hundred and thirty-four to six hundred and forty-four, the caliphate exploded across the known world with a speed that confounded its neighbours. Byzantine Syria fell. Byzantine Egypt fell. The Sasanian Persian Empire — which had ruled from the Mediterranean to Central Asia for centuries — was shattered at the Battle of al-Qadisiyyah in six hundred and thirty-six and effectively destroyed by six hundred and fifty-one under the third caliph, Uthman ibn Affan. Within roughly twenty years of Muhammad's death, the caliphate stretched from the borders of present-day Libya in the west to the edges of modern Afghanistan in the east.

What drove this expansion is a question historians still debate. The theological pull of Islam — a clear, egalitarian message of one God, accessible to all people without the intermediary of a priesthood or an ethnically defined covenant — clearly played a role. So did the extraordinary military energy of a community that had been forged in decades of existential conflict. The empires they conquered were exhausted from centuries of war with each other. And the early Islamic state, in many places, offered its new subjects a lighter tax burden and greater religious tolerance than they had previously experienced, which smoothed the process of integration considerably.

The fourth caliph, Ali ibn Abi Talib, who was both Muhammad's cousin and his son-in-law, came to power in six hundred and fifty-six and ruled until his assassination in six hundred and sixty-one. His reign was troubled by the first great civil war within Islam — the First Fitna — a conflict over succession that produced the enduring split between Sunni and Shia Muslims that shapes the faith to this day. But even this division did not slow Islam's spread. The Umayyad dynasty that followed carried the faith westward across North Africa and into Spain by seven hundred and eleven, and eastward into Central Asia and the Indian subcontinent in the following decades.

Today, Islam has approximately one point nine billion adherents, making it the second-largest religion on earth and, by many projections, the fastest-growing. Its sacred text, the Quran, is the most widely memorised book in human history. Its calendar, its daily prayer rhythms, its architecture and poetry and philosophy have shaped the lives of billions of people across fourteen centuries. And it all traces back, in the Islamic account, to one man trembling on a hillside in the dark outside Mecca, carrying words he could not read — words that, once loosed into the world, would never stop moving.
