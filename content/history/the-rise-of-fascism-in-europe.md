# The Rise of Fascism in Europe

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

To understand the rise of fascism, you have to begin in the mud and misery of the First World War. When the guns finally fell silent in November nineteen eighteen, Europe lay shattered. The war had killed somewhere between fifteen and twenty million people, obliterated four empires, and upended almost every assumption that educated Europeans had held about progress, civilization, and the natural order of things. What came after was not peace — not really. It was a long, unstable armistice haunted by resentment, economic ruin, and political rage. Out of that wreckage, fascism was born.

The Treaty of Versailles, signed in June nineteen nineteen, was supposed to settle the terms of the peace. Instead, it planted the seeds of the next catastrophe. Germany was forced to accept sole responsibility for the war — the notorious "war guilt" clause — and was ordered to pay reparations eventually set at one hundred and thirty-two billion gold marks, a figure so enormous that Germany would not finish paying it off until two thousand ten. The German military was gutted: the army was capped at one hundred thousand men, the navy reduced to a skeleton, and the air force abolished entirely. Entire territories were stripped away. For millions of Germans, the treaty was not justice but humiliation, and the democratic government that had signed it — the newly formed Weimar Republic — was forever tainted by association.

Italy, meanwhile, had its own grievances. The Italians had entered the war in nineteen fifteen on the promise of territorial rewards: coastlines, islands, colonies. When the peace conference convened in Paris, those promises were largely ignored. Italy received some territory, but far less than it had been led to expect. The nationalist poet and agitator Gabriele D'Annunzio coined a phrase that would echo across the decade: the "mutilated victory." Hundreds of thousands of Italians had died, the country had gone deeply into debt, and the fruits of victory had been snatched away. For nationalists, it was an outrage that demanded a response.

Across the continent, the social fabric was fraying. Veterans who had survived four years of industrialized slaughter returned home to find unemployment, inflation, and a political establishment that seemed incapable of addressing either. Communist revolutions had convulsed Russia, and socialist uprisings flickered across Germany, Hungary, and elsewhere. Middle-class families who had spent a lifetime accumulating modest savings watched inflation reduce them to nothing. Established institutions — the church, the aristocracy, the liberal parties — seemed paralyzed. The door was open for something new and violent.

---

Fascism walked through that door first in Italy, and the man who opened it was Benito Mussolini. Born in eighteen eighty-three in the Romagna region, Mussolini had been a journalist, a socialist agitator, and then — after Italy entered the war — a passionate nationalist. He was restless, theatrical, and consumed by a hunger for power. On the twenty-third of March, nineteen nineteen, he gathered a few dozen supporters in a hall in Milan's Piazza San Sepolcro and founded the Fasci Italiani di Combattimento — the Italian Fighting Leagues. The name came from the Latin word "fasces," the bundle of rods around an axe that had symbolized authority in ancient Rome. It was, from the start, a deliberate invocation of national glory and martial strength.

Mussolini's movement was not, in those early days, a coherent political philosophy. It was more of a mood — a violent, nationalist, anti-socialist mood. His black-shirted paramilitaries, known as the squadristi or Blackshirts, roamed the countryside attacking trade union offices, socialist newspapers, and left-wing organizers. They beat people with clubs, forced castor oil down the throats of opponents, and burned down the meeting halls of anyone who dared resist. This organized brutality was not merely tolerated by landowners and industrialists — it was funded by them. The Blackshirts were a weapon aimed squarely at the socialist and communist movements that had been frightening the Italian establishment since the end of the war.

Through nineteen twenty and nineteen twenty-one, as the violence intensified and the parliamentary system lurched from one ineffectual coalition to the next, Mussolini transformed his movement into a genuine political party. In November nineteen twenty-one, the Fasci became the National Fascist Party. By now, an ideology was beginning to crystallize: extreme nationalism, the glorification of the state above the individual, contempt for liberal democracy, anti-communism, the cult of the leader, and the romanticization of violence and struggle. Fascism was not a coherent economic doctrine so much as a political style — authoritarian, theatrical, and saturated with emotion. It promised to make the nation great again by sweeping away the weak and the corrupt.

By the autumn of nineteen twenty-two, Italy's liberal government was crumbling. On the twenty-eighth of October, Mussolini's Blackshirts began converging on Rome from staging points across the country in what would become known as the March on Rome. In truth, it was less of a military coup than a carefully managed act of intimidation. The marchers were poorly armed and hardly an unstoppable force. But Prime Minister Luigi Facta's request to impose martial law was refused by King Victor Emmanuel III, who feared civil war and doubted the army's reliability. Rather than resist, the king invited Mussolini — who had been sitting in Milan watching events unfold — to form a government. On the thirtieth of October, nineteen twenty-two, Mussolini arrived in Rome by sleeper train and was appointed Prime Minister. He was thirty-nine years old. Europe had its first fascist ruler.

---

Mussolini's example was closely watched across the continent, and nowhere more keenly than in Germany. Germany's situation in the nineteen twenties was dire. The Weimar Republic, established in the chaos after the war, was associated in many minds with defeat, humiliation, and weakness. In nineteen twenty-three, France and Belgium occupied the Ruhr industrial valley to enforce reparations payments, and the German government responded by printing money to pay striking workers. The result was catastrophic hyperinflation: by November of that year, a single US dollar was worth more than four trillion German marks. People carried banknotes in wheelbarrows. Life savings vanished overnight. The psychological trauma of this period never fully healed.

It was against this backdrop that a young Austrian-born agitator named Adolf Hitler made his first serious move. Hitler had joined the tiny German Workers' Party in Munich in nineteen nineteen and rapidly taken control of it, renaming it the National Socialist German Workers' Party — the Nazi Party. He was a hypnotic speaker who combined a violent anti-Semitism with furious nationalism and a visceral contempt for the Weimar system. On the eighth of November, nineteen twenty-three, Hitler launched the Beer Hall Putsch, bursting into a Munich beer hall with around six hundred armed followers and attempting to trigger a national revolution. The attempt collapsed the following day when the marchers were met by police near the Marienplatz. Sixteen Nazi Party members and four police officers were killed. Hitler fled but was arrested on the eleventh of November.

The trial that followed gave Hitler precisely what he needed: a national stage. He turned the proceedings into a platform for his ideology, and the sympathetic Bavarian court sentenced him to just five years — of which he served nine months at Landsberg Prison. There, he dictated a long autobiographical and ideological manifesto to his fellow prisoner Rudolf Hess. That book, Mein Kampf — "My Struggle" — laid out Hitler's worldview with chilling clarity: the German people were a master race besieged by Jews and Marxists; Germany's defeat in the war had been a "stab in the back" by traitors at home; the nation needed lebensraum, or "living space," to be seized in the east. It was, in hindsight, both a warning and a programme. Almost nobody took it seriously at the time.

Hitler was released in December nineteen twenty-four, and through the mid-nineteen twenties the Nazis remained a fringe movement. Germany's economy stabilized, unemployment fell, and most Germans saw little need for extremism. The Weimar Republic even enjoyed a brief golden age of culture and relative prosperity. But the foundations were fragile, and they gave way catastrophically in October nineteen twenty-nine when the New York Stock Exchange collapsed, triggering the Great Depression. American loans that had propped up the German economy dried up almost overnight. Unemployment in Germany rocketed from under one million to over six million by nineteen thirty-two — nearly a third of the workforce. Hunger, despair, and fury returned with a vengeance.

The Nazi vote reflected the desperation of the times with brutal precision. In the nineteen twenty-eight federal election, the party had won fewer than three percent of the vote. By July nineteen thirty-two, that figure had surged to thirty-eight percent — the largest vote share of any party in the Reichstag. Hitler campaigned tirelessly, flying across Germany to address mass rallies, promising economic recovery, national revival, and the destruction of the enemies he blamed for Germany's misery: the Jews, the communists, the liberals, and the signatories of Versailles. He offered a sense of belonging and purpose to people who had felt robbed of both.

---

The machinery that brought Hitler to power in the end was not the mob — it was the establishment. Conservative politicians around President Paul von Hindenburg convinced themselves that they could use Hitler, contain him, and control him while benefiting from his popular support. On the thirtieth of January, nineteen thirty-three, Hindenburg appointed Hitler as Chancellor of Germany. It was one of the most consequential miscalculations in modern history. Within weeks, the Nazis had engineered a crisis — the burning of the Reichstag parliament building on the twenty-seventh of February was blamed on a communist conspiracy — and used it to push through emergency decrees suspending civil liberties. On the twenty-third of March, the Reichstag passed the Enabling Act with four hundred and forty-four votes in favour and ninety-four against. The act gave Hitler the power to pass laws without parliamentary approval. Democracy in Germany was over.

What followed was a consolidation of total power with remarkable speed. Political parties were banned, trade unions dissolved, the press brought to heel, and the civil service purged of Jews and political opponents. The instrument of this transformation was propaganda, managed with extraordinary skill by Joseph Goebbels, whom Hitler appointed as Minister for Public Enlightenment and Propaganda in March nineteen thirty-three. Goebbels understood that fascism was as much about spectacle and feeling as it was about policy. The Nuremberg rallies, held annually from nineteen thirty-three onwards, were masterworks of political theatre: hundreds of thousands of uniformed participants, forests of flags and torchlight, Hitler descending from the clouds like a secular deity. The nineteen thirty-four rally was filmed by director Leni Riefenstahl in her landmark documentary Triumph of the Will, which turned the event into a piece of cinema designed to inspire awe and submission. These were not political gatherings in any ordinary sense — they were rituals of collective surrender to the will of the leader.

Fascism's appeal drew on several deep wells. It offered a simple explanation for complex miseries — find the enemy, the traitor, the outsider, and blame everything on them. It promised the restoration of national greatness to people who had been humiliated. It gave individuals a sense of belonging to something larger than themselves at a moment when traditional communities had been dissolved by economic catastrophe. And it channelled the very real fear of communism that haunted business owners, landowners, and the middle classes across Europe. Fascism positioned itself as the only force strong enough to hold back the Bolshevik tide, and that message resonated with frightened conservatives who might otherwise never have contemplated violence.

---

The Spanish Civil War, which began in July nineteen thirty-six, brought the clash between fascism and its enemies into the open. When General Francisco Franco launched a military uprising against Spain's elected left-wing Republican government, Mussolini and Hitler rushed to his aid. Germany sent the Condor Legion, whose bombing of the Basque town of Guernica in April nineteen thirty-seven killed hundreds of civilians and shocked the world. Italy supplied tens of thousands of troops. Franco's Nationalists, though not purely fascist, included the Spanish fascist movement known as the Falange, and the civil war became a dress rehearsal for the larger conflict that everyone could feel approaching. Franco's forces won in March nineteen thirty-nine, installing a dictatorship that would last until his death in nineteen seventy-five.

Elsewhere across Europe, fascist and proto-fascist movements rose and fell with varying degrees of success. In Britain, Oswald Mosley founded the British Union of Fascists in nineteen thirty-two, drawing tens of thousands of members before the movement was suppressed after the outbreak of war. In Hungary, Gyula Gömbös came to power as Prime Minister in nineteen thirty-two, steering the country toward an authoritarian nationalism aligned with German and Italian interests. In Romania, the Iron Guard — a mystical, ultra-nationalist, violently antisemitic movement — gained substantial political representation through the nineteen thirties. In Austria, a clerical-fascist government under Engelbert Dollfuss suspended parliament in nineteen thirty-three and crushed the political left, before Dollfuss himself was murdered in a failed Nazi coup attempt in nineteen thirty-four. Austria was ultimately absorbed into Hitler's Germany — the Anschluss — in March nineteen thirty-eight.

By the late nineteen thirties, fascism had reshaped the map and the political climate of an entire continent. Mussolini and Hitler had signed the Rome-Berlin Axis agreement in nineteen thirty-six, cementing their alliance. Hitler had remilitarized the Rhineland, annexed Austria, and dismembered Czechoslovakia — each aggression met with appeasement from Britain and France, who were desperate to avoid another catastrophic war. That desperation was understandable but catastrophic in its own right. On the first of September, nineteen thirty-nine, German forces invaded Poland. Britain and France declared war two days later. The Second World War had begun.

The war that fascism unleashed would kill between seventy and eighty-five million people. It would produce the Holocaust — the systematic murder of six million Jews and millions of others, including Roma, disabled people, political prisoners, and gay men — an atrocity made possible not just by Nazi ideology but by the administrative machinery of a modern state fully given over to evil. It would reduce entire cities to rubble, displace tens of millions from their homes, and end with the detonation of atomic bombs over two Japanese cities. Fascism was not an aberration that appeared from nowhere. It grew from conditions — economic despair, national humiliation, institutional weakness, and the seductive promise of simple answers to complex problems — that human societies have proved capable of generating more than once.

The lesson that history draws from the rise of fascism is not a comfortable one. It suggests that democracy is not self-sustaining, that its institutions can be dismantled from within, and that the most dangerous political figures are often not those who storm the barricades but those who are politely invited through the front door. It was the King of Italy, not a revolutionary mob, who handed Mussolini his power. It was the conservative establishment of the Weimar Republic, not a coup, that made Hitler Chancellor. Fascism, in its European form, was largely a legal seizure — a reminder that the forms of democracy can survive long after its substance has been hollowed out. Understanding how it happened is not merely a historical exercise. It is, perhaps, one of the most important things a citizen of any democracy can know.
