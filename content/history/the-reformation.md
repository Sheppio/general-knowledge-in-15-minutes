# The Reformation

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Imagine you are a peasant farmer in central Europe sometime around fifteen hundred. Your world is small — bounded by the fields you till, the village you were born in, and the church whose bells mark every hour of your day. The Church is not merely a building. It is the architecture of your entire universe. It tells you who you are, why you suffer, what happens when you die, and — crucially — what you must do, and pay, to be saved. And if the Church says the price of salvation has gone up? Well, you pay. You always pay.

That world — devout, hierarchical, and largely unquestioned for a thousand years — began to crack in the early sixteenth century. The crack became a fissure. The fissure became a chasm. What tumbled through it was not just a religious argument but the entire shape of Western civilization. This is the story of the Reformation.

To understand why it happened, you have to understand just how far the medieval Church had drifted from its own stated ideals. By the fifteenth and early sixteenth centuries, the papacy had become deeply entangled in European politics, war, and dynastic ambition. Popes commanded armies. They commissioned magnificent art and architecture — the rebuilding of St. Peter's Basilica in Rome being the most extravagant example — and they needed money to pay for all of it. The institution that claimed to be the gateway to heaven had, in the eyes of many observers, become indistinguishable from any other powerful and corrupt court.

The abuses were not subtle. Simony — the buying and selling of church offices — was commonplace. A wealthy family could purchase a bishopric for a son who had no theological training whatsoever. Nepotism flourished, with popes appointing relatives to lucrative positions regardless of merit. Priests in rural parishes were often poorly educated, sometimes barely literate, conducting sacraments they barely understood. And at the top, the Renaissance popes presided over a court of remarkable worldly splendour. Pope Leo the Tenth, who would later clash with Martin Luther, was a member of the Medici family and a patron of the arts who famously remarked upon his election that God had given him the papacy — so he might as well enjoy it.

But the abuse that would prove most explosive was the selling of indulgences. An indulgence was, in Catholic theology, a remission of the temporal punishment due to sins that had already been forgiven. Over centuries, the practice had evolved — and distorted — until by the sixteenth century, papal agents were selling indulgences as if they were tickets out of purgatory, not just for the living but for the dead. The most notorious of these agents was a Dominican friar named Johann Tetzel, who operated in Germany around fifteen seventeen with a rhyme attributed to him: "As soon as the coin in the coffer rings, the soul from purgatory springs." Whether he actually said it hardly matters. It perfectly captured what ordinary people believed they were being sold.

---

Into this world walked Martin Luther — born on the tenth of November, fourteen eighty-three, in Eisleben in what is now central Germany. He was the son of a copper smelter who had worked his way into modest prosperity and wanted his son to become a lawyer. Luther obliged, enrolling at the University of Erfurt. But in July of fifteen oh five, caught in a violent thunderstorm, he was thrown from his horse and cried out in terror to Saint Anne, promising to become a monk if he survived. He survived. And he kept his promise, to his father's lasting fury.

As an Augustinian friar, Luther was devout to an almost pathological degree — fasting, confessing, mortifying his flesh — and yet he could not shake a terrifying conviction: that he was damned. No amount of religious effort seemed to bridge the gap between his sinfulness and God's perfect holiness. The transformation came through his reading of Saint Paul's letter to the Romans and its line about "the righteousness of God" being revealed through faith. Luther came to understand this not as a demand God made of humans, but as a gift God gave to humans. Salvation, he concluded, came through faith alone — sola fide — not through works, rituals, or payments to the Church. It was a quietly revolutionary idea, and for a time it remained just that — quiet.

What broke the silence was Johann Tetzel and his indulgence campaign. In fifteen seventeen, Tetzel was working near Wittenberg, and Luther watched as his parishioners returned with indulgence certificates, confident their sins were paid off, with no intention of changing their lives. On the thirty-first of October, fifteen seventeen — the eve of All Saints' Day — Luther posted his Ninety-Five Theses on the door of the Castle Church in Wittenberg. In Latin. For an academic debate. He almost certainly did not expect what happened next.

The theses were a dense, scholarly attack on indulgences and, by implication, on the pope's authority to grant them. But within weeks, they had been translated into German and printed on the recently invented movable-type printing press — a technology developed by Johannes Gutenberg in the fourteen fifties. Within two months, copies had spread across Germany. Within two years, they were known across Europe. Luther himself was reportedly amazed. The printing press did not cause the Reformation, but without it, the Reformation as we know it could not have happened. What had previously taken months or years to propagate — a letter, a sermon, an argument — could now be reproduced in the thousands and distributed in weeks. Ideas, once loosed into print, could not be recalled.

Rome's response was slow and then severe. Pope Leo the Tenth issued the papal bull Exsurge Domine on the fifteenth of June, fifteen twenty, giving Luther sixty days to recant forty-one of his propositions or face excommunication. Luther's response was to burn the document publicly in Wittenberg on the tenth of December, fifteen twenty. The formal excommunication came on the third of January, fifteen twenty-one.

But Luther's secular protector, Frederick the Wise, Elector of Saxony, was not ready to hand his famous professor over to Rome. And so Luther was summoned instead to appear before the Diet of Worms — a formal assembly of the Holy Roman Empire, presided over by the young Emperor Charles the Fifth. Luther arrived in Worms on the sixteenth of April, fifteen twenty-one. Shown his writings and asked to recant, he asked for a day to consider. The next day, the eighteenth of April, he gave his answer — one of the most consequential speeches in European history. He said he could not and would not recant unless convinced by Scripture or clear reason, because to act against one's conscience was neither safe nor right. The famous ending — "Here I stand, I can do no other" — is almost certainly a later embellishment, added to the printed account to give it more dramatic force. What he actually said was remarkable enough without it.

The Emperor issued the Edict of Worms, condemning Luther as a notorious heretic. Frederick the Wise arranged for Luther to be "kidnapped" and hidden at Wartburg Castle, where he spent nearly a year in disguise. He used the time well: in roughly eleven weeks, he translated the New Testament into German. Published in September of fifteen twenty-two — and therefore known as the September Testament — it sold thousands of copies almost immediately. A second edition had to be printed by December. By the time Luther completed the full Bible in fifteen thirty-four, more than two hundred thousand copies of his New Testament had been sold. Ordinary Germans could now read the scripture themselves, in their own language, without priestly mediation. Sola scriptura — scripture alone — was no longer just a slogan. It was a physical object that could sit on your kitchen table.

---

Luther's ideas did not spread in a vacuum. Across the German-speaking lands and beyond, other reformers were reaching similar conclusions by different routes. In Zurich, a humanist priest named Huldrych Zwingli had been appointed to the Grossmünster — the Great Minster — in fifteen nineteen, and almost immediately began preaching reform. Like Luther, Zwingli challenged Church corruption, rejected clerical celibacy, attacked the use of religious images, and insisted on the primacy of scripture. Unlike Luther, Zwingli's reform was more radical in its rejection of ritual and ceremony. He stripped the Zurich churches bare, removed the organs, and reduced worship to scripture and prayer. The two men eventually met at the Marburg Colloquy in fifteen twenty-nine, arranged by the Landgrave of Hesse in hopes of uniting the Protestant movement. They agreed on nearly everything — except one thing: what happened to the bread and wine in the Eucharist. Luther insisted Christ was truly present. Zwingli said the communion was symbolic. They parted without agreement, a division that would persist for centuries. Two years later, in fifteen thirty-one, Zwingli was killed on the battlefield at Kappel, fighting alongside Zurich troops against Catholic cantons. He remains one of the few major Reformation leaders to die in combat.

The figure who would give the Reformed tradition its most systematic and durable theology was a Frenchman: John Calvin. Born in fifteen oh nine, Calvin was a generation younger than Luther and came to reform partly through humanist scholarship. Fleeing religious persecution in France, he ended up in Basel, where in fifteen thirty-six — at just twenty-seven years old — he published the first edition of the Institutes of the Christian Religion, a work of stunning intellectual architecture that laid out a comprehensive Reformed theology. Calvin's God was sovereign above all else, and his doctrine of predestination — the idea that God had elected certain souls for salvation and others for damnation, entirely by divine will rather than human merit — was the most controversial element of his theology, and the most influential.

In that same year, fifteen thirty-six, Calvin was recruited by the reformer William Farel to help reform the church in Geneva. After a rocky start — he was actually expelled from the city for several years — Calvin returned in fifteen forty-one and transformed Geneva into what admirers called a model Christian city and critics called a theocracy. He established a system of church governance through consistories and councils, reformed education, attracted Protestant refugees from across Europe, and made Geneva a publishing centre and training ground for Reformed pastors who then spread Calvinist ideas to France, Scotland, the Netherlands, England, and eventually the New World. Calvin's Geneva was, for better or worse, one of the most influential laboratories of social organisation in the sixteenth century.

---

In England, the Reformation took a peculiarly personal and dynastic shape. Henry the Eighth had been a loyal Catholic — indeed, he had written a defence of the seven sacraments against Luther, earning from Pope Leo the title Defender of the Faith, a title British monarchs still formally hold today. But Henry had a problem. His wife, Catherine of Aragon, had not produced a surviving male heir. Henry wanted the marriage annulled. Pope Clement the Seventh refused — partly because Catherine was the aunt of the Holy Roman Emperor Charles the Fifth, and Clement was in no position to offend him.

Henry grew impatient. He had also fallen deeply in love with Anne Boleyn, a sharp-minded, ambitious woman who had spent years at the French court. In January of fifteen thirty-three, Henry secretly married Anne, who was already pregnant. In May, his newly appointed Archbishop of Canterbury, Thomas Cranmer, formally annulled the marriage to Catherine. The Pope responded with the threat of excommunication. Henry responded by pushing the Act of Supremacy through Parliament in November of fifteen thirty-four, declaring himself the Supreme Head of the Church of England. With one parliamentary act, a thousand years of papal authority in England was dissolved.

What followed was less a theological reformation than an administrative and financial one — at least initially. Henry was not a Protestant in any doctrinal sense. He still believed in transubstantiation and burned Protestants who disagreed. But he needed money, and England's monasteries were extremely wealthy. Under Thomas Cromwell's direction, between fifteen thirty-six and fifteen forty, Henry dissolved more than eight hundred monasteries, priories, and convents, seizing their lands and assets. The wealth flowed into the crown. The monks and nuns were dispersed. The physical fabric of medieval religious life was stripped, sold, and in many cases demolished. England would never look the same again.

The theological character of the English church shifted back and forth under Henry's children — Protestant under Edward the Sixth, Catholic under Mary the First, and then firmly if moderately Protestant under Elizabeth the First, who sought a settlement broad enough to hold the country together. The Church of England that emerged — episcopal in structure, Reformed in theology, liturgically conservative — was a characteristically English compromise, and it endured.

---

Rome did not merely watch these developments helplessly. The Catholic response to the Reformation — sometimes called the Counter-Reformation, sometimes the Catholic Reformation — was both a genuine spiritual renewal and a systematic effort to reclaim lost ground.

The most important institutional response was the Council of Trent, which convened in fifteen forty-five and met, in three separate sessions, until fifteen sixty-three. It reaffirmed traditional Catholic doctrine on all the points Luther and Calvin had attacked — the authority of tradition alongside scripture, the importance of works alongside faith, the seven sacraments, transubstantiation, purgatory, and the veneration of saints. But it also addressed many of the abuses that had fuelled the Reformation in the first place: the sale of indulgences was reformed, seminary education was mandated for priests, and episcopal residency requirements tightened. The Council of Trent gave Catholicism a sharper, more disciplined identity — one it would carry into the modern world.

Meanwhile, in fifteen forty, Pope Paul the Third had formally approved a new religious order founded by the Spanish former soldier Ignatius of Loyola: the Society of Jesus, known as the Jesuits. Loyola's order was built on military-style discipline, intellectual rigour, and absolute obedience to the pope. The Jesuits became the shock troops of the Counter-Reformation — establishing schools and universities across Europe, conducting missions to Asia and the Americas, and working to reconvert Protestant territories in Poland, Germany, and Bohemia. When Loyola died, the Society had around a thousand members; within a decade, it had grown to thirty-five hundred.

---

Those borders, however, did not hold without bloodshed. The religious fracture ran directly through the political landscape of Europe, and the result was a series of wars whose scale and savagery had no precedent.

The Thirty Years' War began in sixteen eighteen with an event of almost comic symbolic power: the Defenestration of Prague. On the twenty-third of May, sixteen eighteen, Protestant Bohemian nobles, furious at the Catholic Habsburg Emperor's violation of their religious freedoms, threw two royal governors and their secretary out of the windows of Prague Castle. All three men survived the fall — Catholics claimed angels had caught them; Protestants noted they had landed in a rubbish heap — but the act ignited a conflict that would consume central Europe for thirty years.

The Thirty Years' War was not simply a religious war. It began as one, pitting Catholic Habsburgs against Protestant princes, but it evolved into a broader struggle over the balance of power in Europe, eventually drawing in France — Catholic France — on the Protestant side, simply to contain Habsburg power. By the time it ended, it had killed between four and twelve million people. In some regions of Germany, populations fell by more than a third.

The Peace of Westphalia, concluded on the twenty-fourth of October, sixteen forty-eight, ended the war and remade the map of Europe. Crucially, it established the principle that rulers could not impose their religion on their subjects across borders — a de facto acceptance of religious pluralism that would have been unthinkable in fifteen seventeen. Calvinism received legal recognition alongside Lutheranism and Catholicism. The age in which one universal Church could claim to govern the conscience of all Europeans was over.

---

What had the Reformation actually done? It had shattered the unity of Western Christendom permanently — there would be no reunion. It had produced a multiplicity of Christian traditions: Lutheran, Reformed, Anglican, Anabaptist, and dozens more, each claiming scriptural authority for its own interpretation. In doing so, it had — perhaps unintentionally — planted the seed of the idea that individual conscience might be a valid source of religious authority. The logic of sola scriptura, once released, was hard to contain.

The emphasis on scripture in the vernacular promoted literacy across Protestant Europe. Luther's German Bible helped standardise the German language. The printing press accelerated the circulation of ideas across every field — scientific, political, and philosophical. The Enlightenment thinkers who argued for religious toleration drew directly on the experience of a century and a half of religious warfare as their cautionary tale. The Peace of Westphalia's acceptance of pluralism contained the germ of what would eventually become the modern concept of religious freedom.

The Reformation did not create the modern world by itself — history is never that tidy. But it broke the vessel in which medieval Europe had contained its most fundamental assumptions about God, authority, and human dignity. Once broken, that vessel could not be reassembled. Everything that came out of it flows still through the world we inhabit today.
