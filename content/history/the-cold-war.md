# The Cold War: Half a Century on the Brink

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

The Second World War ended in 1945, but peace brought no relief. Within months of Germany's surrender, the two powers that had done the most to win the war — the United States and the Soviet Union — found themselves locked in a new and different kind of conflict. It would last nearly half a century, threaten the world with nuclear annihilation, and reshape every continent on Earth. It was called the Cold War because the two superpowers never fired directly at each other. But it was never cold for the millions who lived and died in its proxy conflicts.

The roots of the Cold War lay in deep ideological incompatibility. The United States was capitalist, democratic, and committed to free markets and individual liberty. The Soviet Union was communist, authoritarian, and committed to collective ownership and the eventual worldwide spread of its ideology. These two systems had cooperated out of necessity to defeat Hitler. Once that necessity was gone, their rivalry reasserted itself with powerful force.

The immediate flashpoint was Europe. As Soviet forces had swept westward to defeat Germany, they had liberated — and then occupied — a vast swathe of Eastern Europe. Poland, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, Romania, Bulgaria, and East Germany all fell under Soviet influence. Winston Churchill, speaking in 1946, described an iron curtain descending across the continent, dividing free Europe from the Soviet-controlled east.

---

The United States responded with a strategy of containment — preventing communism from spreading further rather than trying to roll it back. In 1947, President Truman announced the Truman Doctrine, pledging American support for any nation threatened by communist takeover. That same year, Secretary of State George Marshall proposed the European Recovery Program — the Marshall Plan — and in April 1948, it was signed into law, channelling billions of dollars in aid to rebuild war-devastated Western Europe, with the explicit goal of preventing the poverty and instability that communism tended to exploit. And in 1949, the United States and its Western allies formed NATO — the North Atlantic Treaty Organization — a military alliance in which an attack on one member would be treated as an attack on all.

Germany became the most visible symbol of the Cold War's division. The country had been split into four occupation zones after the war, and Berlin — deep inside the Soviet zone — was similarly divided. In 1948, the Soviets blockaded West Berlin, trying to starve it into submission. The West responded with a remarkable feat: an airlift that flew in supplies for nearly a year until the Soviets backed down. Then, in August 1961, the East German government — with Soviet backing — built a wall through the heart of Berlin, sealing off the east from the west. The Berlin Wall became the most potent symbol of the Iron Curtain, a physical monument to the division of the world.

---

In Asia, the Cold War turned hot. When communist North Korea invaded South Korea in June 1950, the United States led a United Nations force to push them back. The Korean War lasted three years and ended in an armistice that left the peninsula divided along much the same line as before — a division that persists to this day. Then, through the 1950s and 1960s, America became increasingly entangled in Vietnam, where communist forces backed by North Vietnam fought against the American-supported South. The Vietnam War became one of the defining traumas of American history. More than fifty-eight thousand Americans were killed. Vietnamese deaths — military and civilian, north and south — are estimated in the millions. The United States withdrew in 1973 and South Vietnam fell to communist forces two years later.

The most terrifying episode of the Cold War came in October 1962. American reconnaissance aircraft discovered Soviet nuclear missiles being installed in Cuba, just ninety miles from the Florida coast. For thirteen days, the world held its breath. President John F. Kennedy blockaded Cuba and demanded the missiles be removed. Soviet leader Nikita Khrushchev refused to back down immediately. Behind the scenes, frantic diplomatic back-channels worked to find a way out that neither side would have to admit was a capitulation. Finally, the Soviets agreed to remove the missiles in exchange for a secret American pledge to remove its own missiles from Turkey. The deal was known to only a handful of officials at the time and was not officially acknowledged until 1989. The world had come closer to nuclear war than most people realised — and closer than most people were told at the time.

The Space Race was another dimension of the same competition. When the Soviet Union launched Sputnik in 1957, it was not just a scientific achievement — it was a propaganda coup, demonstrating Soviet technological prowess to the world. When America put a man on the moon in 1969, it was equally a statement of national power and ideological superiority. The two superpowers poured vast resources into the race for space, and the technologies that emerged changed the modern world.

By the 1970s, a period of détente — a relaxation of tensions — brought the two sides to the negotiating table. Arms limitation agreements were signed. Nixon visited China, driving a wedge between the two great communist powers. But the Cold War reignited in 1979 when the Soviet Union invaded Afghanistan, and the United States began funding and arming the Afghan resistance fighters — a decision whose consequences would echo far into the future.

---

The beginning of the end came with Mikhail Gorbachev, who became leader of the Soviet Union in 1985. Gorbachev introduced reforms — glasnost, meaning openness, and perestroika, meaning restructuring — that he hoped would revitalise the Soviet system. Instead, they unleashed forces he could not control. Across Eastern Europe, populations who had long chafed under Soviet-aligned governments began to push back. In 1989, one by one, communist regimes fell. Poland, Hungary, Czechoslovakia, Romania — all peacefully or with little violence. And on the ninth of November, 1989, crowds in Berlin began tearing down the Wall. The most powerful symbol of the Cold War was dismantled, brick by brick, by the people it had been built to contain.

Two years later, on the twenty-fifth of December, 1991, the Soviet Union itself dissolved. Fifteen independent nations emerged from its ruins. The Cold War was over.

---

Its legacy is complex. The decades of competition produced the nuclear weapons that still threaten humanity. They fuelled proxy wars that killed millions in Korea, Vietnam, Angola, Afghanistan, and elsewhere. They propped up dictatorships on both sides that suppressed their own people in the name of ideology. But they also produced the Marshall Plan, which rebuilt Europe. They produced the Space Race, which gave us satellites and modern communications. And they ended with the largely peaceful collapse of an empire — an outcome that, at the height of the Cuban Missile Crisis, had seemed almost miraculous.

The Cold War shaped the world we live in. The borders of Eastern Europe, the tensions of the Korean Peninsula, the fractures in the Middle East, the unresolved questions about nuclear proliferation — all trace their origins to those forty-five years of superpower rivalry. Understanding the Cold War is understanding how the modern world was made.
