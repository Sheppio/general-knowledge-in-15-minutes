# World War Two: The Deadliest Conflict in History

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

World War Two was the deadliest conflict in human history, claiming the lives of between seventy and eighty-five million people between 1939 and 1945. It reshaped every continent, ended empires, gave birth to new superpowers, and left scars that the world is still reckoning with today.

To understand how it began, you need to look at the wreckage left behind by the first world war. The Treaty of Versailles in 1919 had humiliated Germany — stripping it of territory, saddling it with crippling reparations, and placing sole blame for the war on German shoulders. Through the 1920s, Germany's economy staggered under this burden. Then the Great Depression struck in 1929, and the economic misery deepened. Unemployment soared. Hunger spread. And into this desperation stepped Adolf Hitler.

Hitler joined the Nazi Party in the early 1920s and rose through it with terrifying speed. After a failed attempt to seize power in Munich in 1923, he was imprisoned, wrote his manifesto Mein Kampf, and returned to politics with renewed focus. By 1933, he was Chancellor of Germany. Within a year he had dismantled the democratic institutions of the Weimar Republic and declared himself Führer — absolute leader. He rebuilt the military in violation of the Versailles Treaty, remilitarized the Rhineland, and made clear his intention to unite all German-speaking peoples under one flag and to acquire living space in the east.

Europe's major powers, still traumatized by the carnage of the first war, tried to appease him. In September 1938, Britain and France agreed to let Hitler annex the Sudetenland, a German-speaking region of Czechoslovakia. British Prime Minister Neville Chamberlain returned to London waving a piece of paper and declaring peace in our time. Within six months, Hitler had seized the rest of Czechoslovakia.

On the first of September, 1939, Germany invaded Poland. Two days later, Britain and France declared war on Germany. The Second World War had begun.

---

Germany's early military campaigns were devastating. A new style of warfare called Blitzkrieg — lightning war — combined fast-moving tanks, motorized infantry, and dive bombers to punch through enemy lines before defenders could react. Poland fell in weeks. Denmark and Norway fell in April 1940. Then, in May and June of 1940, Germany swept through the Netherlands, Belgium, and France with astonishing speed. France, one of the great military powers of Europe, surrendered in just six weeks. The British Expeditionary Force was evacuated from the beaches of Dunkirk in a desperate armada of military ships and civilian vessels.

Britain now stood alone. Hitler expected it to seek terms. Instead, Winston Churchill — who had replaced Chamberlain as Prime Minister in May 1940 — rallied the nation with some of the most memorable speeches in the English language. What followed was the Battle of Britain, an air campaign fought through the summer and autumn of 1940 in which the German Luftwaffe tried to destroy the Royal Air Force and clear the way for an invasion. The RAF, outnumbered but fighting over home territory, held on. Hitler postponed the invasion indefinitely and turned instead to a bombing campaign against British cities — the Blitz — which killed tens of thousands of civilians but failed to break British morale.

In June 1941, Hitler made the decision that many historians regard as his fatal mistake. He invaded the Soviet Union in the largest military operation in history, codenamed Operation Barbarossa. Three million German and Axis troops swept eastward along a front stretching nearly two thousand miles. Initial gains were staggering. Entire Soviet armies were encircled and destroyed. Millions of Soviet soldiers were captured. But the Soviet Union was vast, its population enormous, and its capacity to absorb punishment and rebuild seemingly limitless. The Germans reached the outskirts of Moscow but could not take it. As the Russian winter set in, the Wehrmacht — unprepared for the cold — began to suffer terribly.

On the seventh of December 1941, Japan attacked the American naval base at Pearl Harbor in Hawaii, killing two thousand four hundred and three Americans and sinking or damaging much of the US Pacific Fleet. The following day, the United States declared war on Japan. Germany and Italy then declared war on the United States. America, with its enormous industrial capacity and population, was now fully in the war on the Allied side.

---

The conflict in North Africa, the Pacific, and the Eastern Front raged through 1942 and into 1943. The Battle of Stalingrad, fought from August 1942 to February 1943, was a turning point on the Eastern Front. The Germans encircled Stalingrad and drove into the city, but the Soviets counterattacked and surrounded the German Sixth Army. After months of brutal urban combat, the German commander surrendered. It was the first major German defeat in the east, and it marked the moment the tide began to turn.

In the west, Allied forces invaded North Africa, then Sicily, then mainland Italy. On the sixth of June, 1944 — D-Day — the largest seaborne invasion in history landed on the beaches of Normandy in northern France. More than one hundred and fifty thousand Allied troops crossed the English Channel in a single day, fighting their way ashore against fierce German resistance. Paris was liberated in August. By the end of 1944, Allied forces were pushing toward Germany from the west while Soviet forces pressed in from the east.

In the Pacific, American forces had been fighting their way across island chains toward Japan. The strategy, known as island hopping, bypassed heavily defended Japanese positions and established airfields closer and closer to the Japanese home islands. The fighting was ferocious. The battles of Iwo Jima and Okinawa in early 1945 cost enormous casualties on both sides and convinced Allied planners that an invasion of the Japanese mainland could result in millions of deaths.

That calculation shaped the decision to use a new and terrifying weapon. On the sixth of August, 1945, the United States dropped an atomic bomb on the Japanese city of Hiroshima, killing at least seventy thousand people instantly and tens of thousands more in the following weeks from injuries and radiation. Three days later, a second bomb destroyed Nagasaki. On the fifteenth of August, 1945, Japan announced its surrender. The formal surrender was signed on the second of September, 1945. The war was over.

---

But the war's darkest chapter deserves its own recognition. While the fighting raged, Nazi Germany was systematically murdering the Jewish people of Europe in what became known as the Holocaust. Jews were stripped of their rights, forced into ghettos, and eventually transported to extermination camps — Auschwitz, Treblinka, Sobibor, and others — where they were killed on an industrial scale. By the end of the war, six million Jews had been murdered, representing approximately two thirds of Europe's pre-war Jewish population. Millions of others, including Roma, disabled people, Soviet prisoners of war, and political opponents, were also killed.

The aftermath of the war remade the world. Europe lay in ruins. The United States and the Soviet Union emerged as the dominant superpowers. The United Nations was founded to prevent future conflicts. Germany was divided into east and west. The nuclear age had begun. And the revelations of the Holocaust gave rise to new frameworks of international law around crimes against humanity and genocide.

The Second World War was a catastrophe without parallel. It demonstrated both the worst and the best of what human beings are capable of — the organized murder of millions on one hand, and the extraordinary courage, sacrifice, and solidarity of those who fought to stop it on the other.
