# The Byzantine Empire

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Imagine an empire that outlasted every other state in the medieval world — one that preserved the laws, the literature, and the learning of ancient Rome and Greece for more than a thousand years, while the rest of Europe groped through the dark. Imagine a civilisation so durable that it bridged the ancient and the modern, one that shaped the Eastern Orthodox Church, informed the legal codes of dozens of nations, and whose scholars, fleeing its final collapse, helped ignite the Renaissance in Italy. That empire was Byzantium, and most people have barely heard of it.

That is partly because Byzantium never called itself Byzantium. To the people who lived in it, from its foundation in the fourth century to its destruction in the fifteenth, it was simply the Roman Empire. Its citizens were Romans. Its emperor was the heir of Augustus and Constantine. The name "Byzantine Empire" was invented centuries after the fact by Western historians who needed a label to distinguish the surviving eastern half of Rome from the fallen western half. But the label, however convenient, carries a hidden cost: it makes this extraordinary state sound like a footnote, a pale imitation, an echo. It was nothing of the sort.

To understand how Byzantium came to exist, you need to go back to the crisis that made it necessary. By the third century, the Roman Empire had grown so vast that no single man could govern it effectively. Perpetually beset by barbarian incursions, rival emperors, and a collapsing economy, it needed a new strategic centre. Rome itself, sitting in the middle of the Italian peninsula, had become an impractical capital for an empire of such geographic reach.

It was Constantine I who saw the solution most clearly. By the early fourth century, he had fought his way to sole control of the empire after a series of vicious civil wars. He was a man of extraordinary strategic intelligence, and he grasped that the real centre of imperial gravity had shifted eastward — toward the wealthier, more densely populated, more culturally sophisticated eastern provinces. On the eleventh of May, in the year three hundred and thirty, he dedicated a magnificent new capital on the site of an ancient Greek city called Byzantium, on a triangular promontory where Europe meets Asia, where the waters of the Bosphorus, the Golden Horn, and the Sea of Marmara converge. He called it New Rome. Almost immediately, everyone else called it Constantinople — the city of Constantine.

The site was a stroke of genius. Surrounded on three sides by water and protected on its landward side by a neck of peninsula narrow enough to be defended by a single great wall, Constantinople was nearly impregnable. It sat astride the most important trade routes in the known world, connecting the Black Sea to the Mediterranean, funnelling the wealth of the east through its harbours and customs houses. And Constantine decorated it with a ferocity of ambition that announced he intended it to be not just a capital but a monument to a new kind of Rome — one in which Christianity was not merely tolerated but celebrated. Constantine had issued the Edict of Milan in three hundred and thirteen, making the practice of Christianity legal throughout the empire for the first time. Now he built churches rather than temples, and placed Christian symbols where pagan ones had stood before.

For a few more decades, there was still, formally speaking, a single Roman Empire. But the strains of governing it were too great. When Emperor Theodosius I died in three hundred and ninety-five, he divided the empire between his two sons: Arcadius in the east and Honorius in the west. It was meant to be a practical administrative arrangement, not a permanent divorce. History had other ideas. The western half, battered by wave after wave of Germanic migrations and invasions, hemorrhaged territory through the fifth century. In four hundred and seventy-six, the Germanic chieftain Odoacer deposed Romulus Augustulus — a teenager who held the empty title of western emperor — and simply declined to appoint a successor. The western Roman Empire was over.

The eastern half barely paused to mourn. Its emperor still reigned in Constantinople, still commanded armies and collected taxes, still administered Roman law to millions of subjects. The empire did not fall in four hundred and seventy-six. For the eastern half, that year was not an ending at all.

---

The moment that best captures what the Byzantine Empire could be at the height of its powers came in the reign of Justinian I, who ruled from five hundred and twenty-seven to five hundred and sixty-five. Justinian was a man of relentless ambition and remarkable capacity for work, born of peasant stock in what is now Serbia, who clawed his way to the throne through the patronage of his uncle, the Emperor Justin. He spoke bluntly, slept little, fasted often, and burned with the conviction that God had placed him on the throne to restore the Roman Empire to its former glorious extent.

Standing beside him, in every sense that mattered, was his wife Theodora. She was arguably the most formidable woman in Byzantine history, and one of the most extraordinary figures in the history of any empire. Born into poverty, the daughter of a bear-keeper at the Hippodrome in Constantinople, she had worked as an actress before converting to a devout Christian life and catching the eye of the future emperor. Justinian changed the law to allow him to marry her, and she became not merely his consort but his co-ruler, his confidante, and, on at least one crucial occasion, his backbone.

That occasion came in January of five hundred and thirty-two, when the great Nika riots convulsed Constantinople. The city's chariot-racing factions had united against Justinian's government, burning half the city and leaving Justinian seriously considering flight. According to the historian Procopius, it was Theodora who stopped him — she declared that she would rather die as empress than live in exile, that the imperial purple made a fine burial shroud. Justinian stayed. His general Belisarius sealed the rioters inside the Hippodrome and killed approximately thirty thousand of them.

From the ashes of the Nika riots, Justinian built something extraordinary. The destruction of so much of Constantinople gave him a blank canvas, and he used it with breathtaking ambition. The jewel of his building programme was the Hagia Sophia — the Church of Holy Wisdom — constructed between five hundred and thirty-two and five hundred and thirty-seven by the brilliant architects Anthemius of Tralles and Isidore of Miletus. When it was completed and Justinian walked through its doors, he is said to have exclaimed: "Solomon, I have surpassed you." He was not being modest, and he was not wrong. The Hagia Sophia was the largest enclosed space in the world. Its central dome, rising fifty-five metres from the floor, seemed — and this was entirely intentional — to float in mid-air, ringed by forty windows that flooded the interior with light. The effect on visitors was one of otherworldly awe. For nearly a thousand years it remained the largest cathedral on earth.

Justinian's ambitions extended far beyond architecture. His legal scholars produced the Corpus Juris Civilis — the Body of Civil Law — a codification of Roman legal thought that distilled centuries of scholarship into a form that could actually be used, and which became the ancestor of almost every legal system in continental Europe today. He also dispatched his great general Belisarius to reconquer the lost western provinces, taking back North Africa from the Vandals and Italy from the Ostrogoths.

But the golden age was more fragile than it looked. Justinian's wars of reconquest were enormously expensive. And in five hundred and forty-one, a catastrophe struck that no emperor could have planned for: a devastating plague, almost certainly bubonic, swept through the empire, killing perhaps a quarter of its population over the following decade. Justinian himself fell ill, though he recovered. His empire did not fully recover. By the time of his death in five hundred and sixty-five, the finances were ruined, the reconquered western territories were already beginning to slip away, and the great arc of expansion had peaked.

---

The seventh century brought the most severe crisis Byzantium had yet faced — not one catastrophe, but two arriving almost simultaneously. The first was a final, exhausting war with the Sassanid Persian Empire, Rome's great eastern rival for centuries. From six hundred and two to six hundred and twenty-eight, the two empires tore at each other with extraordinary ferocity. The Persians seized Egypt, Syria, and Palestine — including Jerusalem, from which they carried off the True Cross as a trophy. Emperor Heraclius mounted a brilliant counter-offensive, driving deep into Persian territory and forcing a humiliating peace, recovering the lost provinces and the sacred relic. He had saved the empire through sheer willpower and military ingenuity.

Then, almost immediately, came the second catastrophe. The prophet Muhammad died in six hundred and thirty-two, and from the Arabian Peninsula, his followers erupted northward and westward with astonishing speed and force. Unified by a new faith and a common purpose, Arab armies defeated the Byzantines at the Battle of the Yarmuk in six hundred and thirty-six — one of the most decisive military encounters in world history. Within a generation, Syria, Palestine, Egypt, and North Africa had all fallen. The wealthy provinces that had funded much of the empire's operations were gone. Byzantium had lost perhaps two-thirds of its territory in the space of a few decades.

What remained was a harder, leaner, more Greek-speaking state, centred on Constantinople and Anatolia, with a military and administrative structure that had been entirely rebuilt to fight for survival. This was in many ways the beginning of what historians mean when they talk about Byzantium as a specifically medieval empire, rather than simply a continuation of the ancient one. Greek replaced Latin as the official language of government. The emperor's role became infused with an ever more intense theological significance — he was not just a ruler but the representative of God on earth, and the church and state were bound together in ways the western church would have found alarming.

The empire survived repeated Arab sieges of Constantinople itself, in six hundred and seventy-four and again in seven hundred and seventeen, thanks in large part to Greek fire — a mysterious incendiary weapon that burned on water and could not be extinguished. The empire stabilised, and in the tenth and eleventh centuries it pushed its frontiers back into Syria and reconquered much of the Balkans.

But cracks were forming. In ten seventy-one, the Seljuk Turks — a new and formidable Islamic power from central Asia — inflicted a catastrophic defeat on the Byzantine army at the Battle of Manzikert in eastern Anatolia. The emperor Romanos IV was captured on the battlefield, an almost unimaginable humiliation. Most of Anatolia — the heartland of what remained of the empire, the source of most of its army and much of its revenue — fell to the Turks within years. It was this crisis that prompted Emperor Alexios I to send his famous appeal to Pope Urban II in the west, asking for military assistance. The response was the First Crusade.

---

The Crusades were, for Byzantium, a deeply ambivalent experience. The first wave of crusaders who passed through Constantinople in ten ninety-six were alarming — undisciplined, hungry, contemptuous of Greek customs and Orthodox Christianity, prone to looting the countryside. But they did, against most expectations, succeed in capturing Jerusalem in ten ninety-nine, and they established a chain of crusader states along the eastern Mediterranean coast that served as a buffer between Byzantium and its Islamic neighbours.

The relationship between the empire and the crusading movement deteriorated steadily over the following century. The Byzantines regarded the crusader states as territories that rightfully belonged to Constantinople, and the crusaders regarded the Byzantines as decadent, devious, and suspiciously friendly with Muslim powers. The mutual suspicion curdled into something worse, and the consequence was the catastrophe of the Fourth Crusade.

The Fourth Crusade, launched in twelve hundred and two with the stated aim of recapturing Jerusalem, was diverted — through a tangled sequence of debt, political intrigue, and opportunism — to Constantinople instead. On the twelfth of April, twelve hundred and four, crusader armies breached the walls of the greatest Christian city in the world and subjected it to three days of savage looting. Churches were ransacked, tombs were desecrated, libraries were burned, priceless relics were carried off to western Europe. A Flemish nobleman, Baldwin of Flanders, was crowned emperor of what the crusaders called the Latin Empire of Constantinople in the Hagia Sophia, as though nothing could be more natural.

For the Byzantines, it was an incomprehensible betrayal. Fellow Christians, men who had come in the name of the cross, had done what every Muslim army had failed to do for eight hundred years: they had taken Constantinople. The wound never entirely healed. The schism between the Roman Catholic west and the Eastern Orthodox east, which had been growing since the formal break of ten fifty-four, became an unbridgeable chasm. In twelve sixty-one, the Empire of Nicaea — the Byzantine government in exile that had held out in western Anatolia — recaptured Constantinople under Emperor Michael VIII Palaiologos. The city was a shadow of its former self, half-ruined and sparsely populated. But the empire had survived.

---

The Palaiologos dynasty that Michael founded would be the last to rule Byzantium, governing from twelve hundred and sixty-one until the very end. Politically and militarily, the empire was dying by inches. The Ottoman Turks, who had emerged in northwestern Anatolia in the early fourteenth century, expanded with relentless purpose. One by one, Byzantine cities and territories fell. The empire shrank to Constantinople, a few islands, and isolated enclaves on the Greek mainland. The city itself, once home to perhaps four hundred thousand people, had fallen to somewhere between forty thousand and fifty thousand souls by the fourteen fifties — a ghost of its former magnificence, with fields and orchards growing inside the ancient walls where palaces had once stood.

When Sultan Mehmed II came to the Ottoman throne in fourteen fifty-one, he was twenty-one years old and he had one overwhelming ambition: he intended to take Constantinople. He gathered an enormous army, built a fleet, and commissioned the Hungarian engineer Urban to cast the largest cannon the world had seen — nearly eight metres long, capable of hurling a stone ball weighing over half a tonne. The siege began on the sixth of April, fourteen fifty-three. Emperor Constantine XI Palaiologos — who shared his name with the founder of the city — led the defence with courage that won him the admiration even of his enemies.

The walls that had protected Constantinople for a thousand years held for seven weeks. But they could not hold forever. In the early hours of the twenty-ninth of May, fourteen fifty-three, Ottoman forces found a small gate left unbarred, and the Janissaries — Mehmed's elite infantry — poured through. The last Byzantine emperor, Constantine XI, removed his imperial regalia, drew his sword, and fought until he was overwhelmed and killed. His body was never definitively identified.

Mehmed II entered the city that afternoon, rode to the Hagia Sophia, and ordered it converted immediately into a mosque. It was, by any measure, the end of an age. The Roman Empire — because that is what Byzantium was, the genuine continuation of the state that Augustus had built — had lasted, in one form or another, from the first century BC to the fifteenth century after it. More than fifteen hundred years. Not a footnote. Not an echo. A civilisation.

The fall of Constantinople sent a shockwave through Europe. Byzantine scholars who had already been making their way west for decades accelerated their departure, carrying with them manuscripts of Plato and Aristotle, of Homer and Thucydides, texts that western Europe had lost or never possessed. The Italian humanists who received them were electrified. The encounter between this recovered Greek learning and the intellectual culture of fifteenth-century Italy is one of the most important events in the history of thought, and it is not too much to say that Byzantium's fall helped light the Renaissance.

Byzantium's legacy is everywhere, even if its name is rarely spoken. The legal tradition of Justinian's code shaped European jurisprudence for centuries. The Orthodox Christianity that Byzantium spread and sustained still defines the spiritual lives of hundreds of millions of people across eastern Europe, Russia, and the Middle East. Byzantine art — its golden mosaics, its luminous icons, its architectural vocabulary of domes and pendentives — echoes in churches and cathedrals from Moscow to Serbia to Ethiopia. The very idea of a Christian empire, of faith and governance bound together, shaped the political imagination of medieval Europe.

One thousand one hundred and twenty-three years after Constantine dedicated his new capital on the Bosphorus, the city fell. But the civilisation that had sheltered there — Greek in language, Roman in law, Christian in faith, and entirely itself in character — had already given the world more than enough to last another thousand years, and more.
