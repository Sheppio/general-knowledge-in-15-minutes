# The Ottoman Empire: Six Centuries at the Crossroads of History

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

The Ottoman Empire lasted for more than six hundred years. From its obscure beginnings as a small principality in the borderlands of Anatolia in the late thirteenth century, it grew into one of the largest and most powerful empires the world has ever seen — stretching at its height from the gates of Vienna in the west to the Persian Gulf in the east, from the Crimea in the north to the shores of Yemen in the south. At its peak it controlled three continents, administered tens of millions of subjects, and sat at the intersection of the trade routes connecting Europe, Asia, and Africa. When it finally fell in the aftermath of the First World War, it had endured longer than the Roman Empire in the west, longer than any other Islamic empire, and long enough to shape the boundaries, identities, and conflicts of the modern Middle East in ways that persist to this day.

The story of the Ottomans is, in part, a story about location. The great empires of history tend to be built on crossroads — on places where trade routes converge, where different civilisations meet, where the control of movement confers power. Few locations in the world have been more consequential in this sense than Anatolia, the peninsula that forms the body of modern Turkey, bridging Europe and Asia across the narrow waters of the Bosphorus. It was there that the Ottoman state was born, and it was there — at the city the Ottomans would call Istanbul and the world still calls Constantinople — that its greatest achievement was won.

---

The Ottomans take their name from Osman, a Turkoman chieftain who established a small principality — a beylik — in northwestern Anatolia around the year 1299. Anatolia at the time was a contested borderland: the great Seljuk Sultanate had fragmented, the Byzantine Empire was old and weakening, and a dozen small Turkish principalities competed for control of the region. Osman's was one of the smaller ones. But it had the enormous advantage of being positioned directly on the Byzantine frontier, which meant constant opportunity for raiding, expansion, and the recruitment of warriors eager for plunder and land.

The early Ottoman state was a frontier organism: flexible, pragmatic, and militarily aggressive. Osman and his immediate successors — Orhan, Murad I, Bayezid I — expanded rapidly across Anatolia and into Europe, pushing into the Balkans and establishing a permanent Ottoman presence on European soil. They moved their capital from the Anatolian city of Bursa to Edirne, in what is now European Turkey, in 1369. Constantinople — the great Byzantine city on the Bosphorus, once the capital of half the Roman Empire — was now surrounded on three sides by Ottoman territory. Its fall was only a matter of time.

---

That fall came on the twenty-ninth of May, 1453. The Ottoman sultan Mehmed II — he was just twenty-one years old — had besieged the city for fifty-three days, deploying an enormous army and, crucially, the largest artillery pieces the world had yet seen. The city's walls, which had held for a thousand years, were breached. The last Byzantine emperor, Constantine XI Palaiologos, died fighting. Mehmed rode into the city, renamed it Constantinople, and immediately had its greatest church — the Hagia Sophia, built by the Emperor Justinian in the sixth century — converted into a mosque. He was thereafter known as "the Conqueror."

The fall of Constantinople was one of the hinge events of European history. It ended the Byzantine Empire — the direct continuation of the eastern Roman Empire — after nearly eleven centuries. It sent a shockwave through the Christian world. It displaced Greek scholars who carried classical manuscripts westward to Italy, contributing to the Italian Renaissance. And it confirmed the Ottomans as the dominant power in the eastern Mediterranean, a position they would hold for the next four centuries.

Under Mehmed and his successors, the empire continued to expand. Selim I, who ruled from 1512 to 1520, doubled its size in just eight years, conquering Egypt, Syria, and the Hejaz — the region of Arabia containing Mecca and Medina — and acquiring the title of Caliph, supreme leader of Sunni Islam. His son Suleiman, who came to the throne in 1520 and ruled until his death in 1566, is remembered in Western history as Suleiman the Magnificent and in Ottoman history as Suleiman the Lawgiver. Under his rule, the empire reached its greatest extent: Ottoman armies besieged Vienna in 1529, though they failed to take it; Ottoman fleets dominated the eastern Mediterranean; and Istanbul became one of the great cities of the world, a cosmopolitan metropolis of perhaps half a million people, home to Turks, Greeks, Armenians, Jews, Arabs, and dozens of other peoples.

---

The Ottoman system of government was sophisticated and, by the standards of its time, remarkably flexible. The empire administered its diverse subject populations through the millet system, which allowed religious communities — Greek Orthodox Christians, Jews, Armenians, and others — to govern their own internal affairs according to their own laws and traditions, in exchange for tax payments and loyalty to the sultan. This was not tolerance in the modern sense — the hierarchy was clear, and non-Muslims were subject to various legal restrictions — but it was a workable system that allowed very different communities to coexist within a single state for centuries.

The Ottoman administration also drew on a remarkable institution: the devshirme, or "collection," a system in which Christian boys from the Balkans were recruited — often forcibly — trained in the Ottoman system, converted to Islam, and educated for service as soldiers or administrators. The elite infantry corps known as the Janissaries, who were among the most effective soldiers in the medieval and early modern world, were drawn from this system. So were many of the empire's greatest administrators and some of its most important grand viziers — the chief ministers who ran much of the empire's day-to-day government. The devshirme was, by modern standards, an institution of profound injustice. By the standards of its time, it was also a pathway to power for people who would otherwise have had none.

---

The Ottoman Empire did not decline steadily. It expanded and contracted, won wars and lost them, reformed itself and resisted reform. But from the late seventeenth century onward, a persistent pattern emerged in which European military technology and organisation began to outpace Ottoman capacity. The defeat of the great Ottoman siege of Vienna in 1683 — the last time an Ottoman army would threaten Central Europe — was a turning point. In the Treaty of Karlowitz in 1699, the Ottomans surrendered Hungary and other territories, the first significant territorial losses in Europe. Over the following two centuries, the empire continued to shrink: Greece gained independence in the 1820s; Serbia, Romania, and Bulgaria followed; Egypt came under British influence; Algeria was taken by France.

European observers took to calling the empire "the Sick Man of Europe" — a phrase attributed, probably inaccurately, to Tsar Nicholas I of Russia, who had his own designs on Ottoman territory. The term captured something real: a state struggling to modernise fast enough to survive in a world where the industrial revolution had transformed the military and economic power of Western Europe. Ottoman reformers tried. The Tanzimat reforms of the nineteenth century attempted to modernise the legal system, expand education, and rationalise administration. They had real effects. They were not enough.

---

The catastrophe came with the First World War. The Ottoman Empire allied with Germany and Austria-Hungary, calculating that a victory for the Central Powers might reverse the losses of the previous century. Instead the war was lost. In its final years, the empire committed what historians recognise as one of the first genocides of the twentieth century: the systematic deportation and massacre of the Armenian population of Anatolia, which killed between six hundred thousand and one and a half million people. The Turkish state has never formally acknowledged the genocide.

With defeat came the partition of the empire. Allied forces occupied Istanbul. The Treaty of Sèvres in 1920 proposed to divide what remained among the victorious powers and their client states, leaving a rump Turkish state in central Anatolia. That outcome was reversed by the Turkish War of Independence, led by the general and nationalist politician Mustafa Kemal — later known as Atatürk, "Father of the Turks" — who defeated the Allied-backed forces that had invaded Anatolia and negotiated a new settlement at the Treaty of Lausanne in 1923. On the first of November 1922, the Grand National Assembly in Ankara formally abolished the Ottoman sultanate. The last sultan, Mehmed VI, went into exile. The Ottoman Empire — six centuries after Osman had carved out his small frontier beylik in northwestern Anatolia — was over.

---

The Turkish Republic that succeeded it under Atatürk pursued a radical programme of westernisation and secularisation: the caliphate was abolished, the fez was banned, the Latin alphabet replaced Arabic script, religious courts were replaced by civil law, and women were given the right to vote. Atatürk's project was to create a modern, secular nation-state from the ruins of an empire — a project that succeeded in its own terms but left unresolved many of the tensions that had accumulated over centuries.

The lands the Ottomans had governed for so long became a series of new states whose borders were drawn by European colonial powers with little regard for the people who lived within them. The conflicts in the modern Middle East — in Iraq, in Syria, in Lebanon, in the Palestinian territories — are in no small part the inheritance of that moment: empires ended, borders drawn, promises broken, and the Ottoman order dissolved into something more fractured and more violent than what it replaced. To understand the contemporary Middle East is, in important ways, to understand what was lost and what was left when the six-century empire finally fell.
