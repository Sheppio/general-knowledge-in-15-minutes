# The Holocaust: The Machinery of Genocide

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

The Holocaust stands apart in history not because mass murder was unprecedented — it was not — but because of the deliberate, bureaucratic, industrial scale at which it was carried out. Six million Jewish men, women, and children were murdered by the Nazi state and its collaborators between 1941 and 1945. Millions more — among them Roma, disabled people, Soviet prisoners of war, Polish civilians, gay men, Jehovah's Witnesses, and political opponents — were also killed. The total death toll of the Nazi genocide has been estimated at between eleven million and seventeen million people. It was not a byproduct of war. It was a programme, planned in offices, administered through paperwork, and carried out on an assembly-line basis by men who drove to work in the morning, performed their duties, and drove home again in the evening.

To understand how this became possible, you have to begin long before Adolf Hitler. Anti-Semitism — the hatred of Jewish people as a group — had deep roots in European culture. It was woven into Christian theology, into popular folklore, into the economic resentments of communities where Jewish traders and moneylenders occupied a visible and vulnerable niche. In the nineteenth century, as modern nationalism took hold, older religious prejudice fused with new pseudo-scientific theories of race. Jews were reimagined not merely as practitioners of a different religion but as a separate and inferior biological category — a parasitic people contaminating the nations they lived among. These ideas circulated widely across Europe. Hitler did not invent them; he weaponised them.

---

When the National Socialist German Workers' Party — the Nazis — came to power in Germany in January 1933, discrimination against Jews began almost immediately. Jews were barred from the civil service, from universities, from the professions. The Nuremberg Laws of September 1935 stripped German Jews of their citizenship and prohibited marriage between Jews and non-Jewish Germans. Jewish businesses were boycotted and then destroyed. Jews were progressively excluded from every domain of German life.

On the night of the ninth to the tenth of November, 1938, came Kristallnacht — the Night of Broken Glass. Nazi paramilitaries and mobs attacked Jewish businesses, homes, and synagogues across Germany and Austria. More than a thousand synagogues were burned. Approximately seven thousand five hundred Jewish businesses were looted and ransacked. Ninety-one Jews were killed in the immediate violence; thirty thousand were arrested and sent to concentration camps. Kristallnacht marked the shift from legal discrimination to organised physical terror.

The outbreak of war in September 1939 accelerated the process enormously. Poland, which Germany conquered in weeks, was home to the largest Jewish community in the world — around three and a half million people. The Nazis immediately subjected Polish Jews to extreme violence and humiliation, herding them into sealed urban ghettos where starvation, disease, and systematic execution killed enormous numbers. The Warsaw Ghetto alone, at its peak, held more than four hundred thousand people in an area of about one point three square miles.

---

The decision to move from mass murder by shooting to industrial killing using gas chambers evolved through 1941 and 1942. Following the invasion of the Soviet Union in June 1941, special mobile killing units called Einsatzgruppen followed the advancing German army and shot Jewish communities en masse. In the ravine at Babi Yar outside Kyiv, over two days in September 1941, the Einsatzgruppen shot thirty-three thousand, seven hundred and seventy-one Jews — men, women, and children — the single largest massacre of the Holocaust. By the end of 1941, the Einsatzgruppen had killed well over five hundred thousand people.

On the twentieth of January, 1942, senior Nazi officials and SS leaders gathered at a villa in the Berlin suburb of Wannsee for a meeting that lasted around ninety minutes over lunch. The Wannsee Conference did not order the Final Solution — that decision had already been made — but it coordinated the bureaucratic machinery to implement it across all of occupied Europe. Reinhard Heydrich, who chaired the meeting, outlined the plan to round up all Jews from every territory under German control and transport them eastward for extermination.

The death camps — Treblinka, Sobibor, Belzec, Chelmno, Majdanek, and Auschwitz-Birkenau — were built or expanded in occupied Poland. Auschwitz, established in April 1940 as a camp for Polish political prisoners, was expanded from 1942 into a vast complex combining slave labour with mass murder. Of the approximately one point three million people deported to Auschwitz, around one point one million were murdered there — the vast majority of them Jewish. Freight trains arrived from across occupied Europe: France, the Netherlands, Hungary, Greece, Italy. People were told they were being resettled. When they arrived, SS doctors performed selections on the platform, sending the majority directly to gas chambers disguised as shower rooms, and a minority into the slave labour camp.

---

The killing continued until the very last weeks of the war, even as Germany's military position became hopeless. Nazi ideology was such that the extermination of Jews was regarded not as a means to an end but as an end in itself — the war's purpose, almost, rather than something to be suspended in the face of military necessity. In January 1945, as Soviet forces advanced, the SS evacuated Auschwitz in what became known as the death marches — columns of surviving prisoners driven westward in brutal winter conditions, shot if they fell behind. The Soviet army liberated the Auschwitz complex on the twenty-seventh of January, 1945, and found around seven thousand survivors who had been too ill to march.

The full scale of the catastrophe only became clear as the war ended and the Allies overran the camps. Photographs and film footage of the liberated concentration camps — the skeletal survivors, the mountains of hair and shoes and spectacles — confronted the world with a reality almost too large to comprehend. In the Nuremberg Trials of 1945 to 1946, the leading surviving architects of the Nazi state were tried for crimes against humanity. Some were executed; others imprisoned. Many perpetrators of the Holocaust were never tried and lived out their lives in Germany and elsewhere in complete obscurity.

The Holocaust prompted an urgent international effort to establish the framework of human rights that might prevent such atrocities from recurring. The United Nations adopted the Convention on the Prevention and Punishment of the Crime of Genocide in December 1948, and the Universal Declaration of Human Rights the same year. The state of Israel was established in 1948, partly as a direct response to the Holocaust and the question of where the survivors would go.

---

The word genocide entered the language because of the Holocaust. Raphael Lemkin, a Polish-Jewish lawyer who lost forty-nine members of his family in the killing, coined the term in 1944 precisely to describe what had happened, and what existing law had no language to condemn. The Holocaust showed that modernity — bureaucracy, railways, technology, the nation-state — could be turned to mass murder at a scale previously unimaginable. The instruments of civilisation became the instruments of annihilation.

It is still the central reference point for discussions of atrocity, of collective responsibility, of what ordinary people are capable of doing under certain conditions. The Holocaust was not carried out by monsters in any simple sense. It was carried out by people — civil servants who processed the paperwork, train drivers who ran the schedules, engineers who designed the crematoria, guards who performed roll calls and then went home. The philosopher Hannah Arendt, covering the trial of Adolf Eichmann in Jerusalem in 1961, described what she saw there as the banality of evil — the extraordinary capacity of ordinary men to participate in extraordinary crimes while telling themselves they were simply doing their jobs. That observation, disturbing precisely because it resists simple explanation, remains one of the most important the twentieth century produced.
