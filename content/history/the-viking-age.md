# The Viking Age: Raiders, Traders, and Explorers

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

On a summer morning in June 793, without warning, longships appeared out of the North Sea mist off the coast of Northumbria and made for the island of Lindisfarne. The monastery there — the most sacred site of Christianity in northern England, where the relics of Saint Cuthbert were venerated — was defenceless. The Norse raiders who poured ashore on the eighth of June killed some monks, drowned others, and stripped the monastery of its treasures. When news spread across Europe, the response was one of pure horror. Who were these people, and where had they come from?

They were Vikings — Norse seafarers from Scandinavia, primarily from what is now Norway, Denmark, and Sweden. The raid on Lindisfarne is the moment that history has come to mark as the start of the Viking Age — a period of roughly two and a half centuries during which the peoples of Scandinavia would expand, trade, raid, settle, and fight across a vast arc of the world, from North America in the west to the rivers of Russia in the east.

---

The Vikings have been remembered primarily as raiders and destroyers, and that reputation is not without foundation. For much of the ninth century, monasteries and coastal settlements from Ireland to France lived in fear of the longships. The speed of the Viking attack was their defining military advantage: a longship could sail in shallow coastal waters and be beached directly on shore, allowing raiders to appear, strike, and vanish before any defensive force could be assembled. Churches and monasteries — full of precious metalwork, largely undefended, and often isolated — were perfect targets.

But the Viking Age was never only about raiding. The same qualities that made the Norse such effective raiders — superb seamanship, extraordinary navigational ability, a willingness to venture into unknown waters — also made them great traders and explorers. Viking merchants established trading networks from the Baltic to Constantinople, selling furs, amber, slaves, and walrus ivory in exchange for silver, silk, and spices. The rivers of Russia were highways for Norse traders; the word "Russia" itself is believed to derive from the Norse word for these Scandinavian river traders.

---

In 866, a great Viking army conquered much of northern and eastern England. The region that came under Norse rule became known as the Danelaw — a vast territory where Norse law, language, and customs blended with those of the existing Anglo-Saxon population. Dublin, founded by Norse settlers in the ninth century, became one of the most important trading cities in northern Europe. Normandy — the region of northern France that would eventually produce the Norman Conquest — was settled by Viking raiders and granted to their leader Rollo by the King of France in 911.

The Norse also ventured into waters no European had previously sailed. Erik the Red, exiled from Iceland for manslaughter, sailed west around 985 and discovered Greenland, establishing settlements that would endure for centuries. His son, Leif Erikson, went further still. Around the year 1000, Leif sailed to a place he called Vinland — most likely the northern tip of Newfoundland, where Norse remains have been found at the site known as L'Anse aux Meadows — becoming the first European to set foot on the American continent, nearly five centuries before Columbus.

---

The Viking Age drew to a close in the mid-eleventh century. The last great Viking invasion of England came in September 1066, when the Norwegian king Harald Hardrada landed in the north with a large fleet, won an initial battle, but was then defeated and killed by King Harold of England at the Battle of Stamford Bridge on the twenty-fifth of September. Three weeks later, Harold was himself defeated and killed at the Battle of Hastings on the fourteenth of October, 1066, by William of Normandy — himself a descendant of Viking settlers. The Norman Conquest ended the Viking Age in England.

The Viking legacy is written into the English language more deeply than most people realise. The words "sky," "window," "knife," "egg," "husband," and "law" all come from Old Norse. Hundreds of place names in northern England and Scotland preserve Norse words. The days of the week — Tuesday, Wednesday, Thursday, Friday — are named after Norse gods: Tyr, Woden, Thor, and Frigg.

The fear that the raids of 793 inspired has coloured how the Vikings are remembered. But the world they helped to create was also one of commerce, exploration, and cultural exchange on a scale that no previous northern European civilisation had attempted. They were not simply destroyers. They were also the people who first sailed to America, who founded Dublin and Kyiv, who gave Russia its name, and who stitched together the trade routes of the medieval North Atlantic.
