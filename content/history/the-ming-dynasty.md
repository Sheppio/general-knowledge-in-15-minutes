# The Ming Dynasty

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Imagine a world where China sits at the absolute centre of civilisation — a vast, teeming empire of nearly one hundred million people, producing the finest ceramics on earth, building a palace so magnificent it would awe visitors for six centuries, and sending fleets of enormous ships across the Indian Ocean decades before any European explorer had dared venture beyond the Atlantic. This was the world of the Ming Dynasty, one of the most consequential periods in Chinese and indeed world history. It lasted from thirteen sixty-eight to sixteen forty-four, just under three centuries, and in that time it gave the world the Forbidden City, the Great Wall as we know it today, the blue-and-white porcelain that collectors still covet, and a story of human ambition, brilliance, and eventual catastrophe that deserves to be far better known than it is.

To understand where the Ming came from, you have to understand what it replaced. For nearly a century before thirteen sixty-eight, China had been ruled by the Yuan Dynasty — a Mongol empire founded by Kublai Khan, the grandson of Genghis Khan. The Mongols were outsiders who never fully integrated with Chinese society. They imposed a rigid ethnic hierarchy that placed Han Chinese near the bottom, taxed the population heavily, and presided over a series of environmental disasters including catastrophic flooding of the Yellow River that devastated agricultural communities across the north. By the middle of the fourteenth century, resentment had boiled over into open rebellion. Several overlapping peasant uprisings convulsed the country, and from this chaos emerged one of the most remarkable figures in Chinese history: a man named Zhu Yuanzhang.

Zhu Yuanzhang's origins were about as humble as it is possible to imagine. He was born around thirteen twenty-eight into a family of desperately poor tenant farmers in what is now Anhui Province. When he was sixteen, famine and plague swept through his region, killing his parents and most of his siblings within a matter of weeks. Left with nothing, he became a wandering Buddhist monk, begging for food at temples. He might easily have lived and died in obscurity. Instead, he joined the Red Turban Rebellion — a broad insurgent movement that drew on Buddhist and Manichaean millennial beliefs alongside straightforward class rage — and discovered that he was a gifted military commander. Over the course of the thirteen fifties and thirteen sixties, he systematically eliminated his rivals, including defeating the warlord Chen Youliang at the Battle of Lake Poyang in thirteen sixty-three, one of the largest naval engagements in history in terms of the number of men involved. By thirteen sixty-eight, Zhu Yuanzhang had driven the last Yuan emperor north into Mongolia and declared the founding of a new dynasty: the Great Ming, meaning "Great Brightness." He took the reign name Hongwu and established his capital at Nanjing, in the fertile south.

The Hongwu Emperor was a man shaped by poverty and survival, and it showed in everything he did. He was deeply suspicious of aristocrats, merchants, and intellectuals — groups he associated with the corruption and excess that had destabilised previous dynasties. He rebuilt the Confucian civil service examination system, which meant that government positions were theoretically open to any man with the talent and discipline to pass the famously gruelling tests, regardless of birth. He restructured agricultural taxation to benefit peasant farmers, redistributed land, and undertook massive flood-control and reforestation projects. But he also displayed a brutal paranoia: he executed tens of thousands of officials and military officers, sometimes entire families, on suspicion of disloyalty or corruption. He banned eunuchs from holding political power and tried to limit the influence of court factions. In his determination to centralise authority in his own hands, he became one of the most autocratic rulers China had ever seen — and, paradoxically, one of its most effective state-builders.

---

When Hongwu died in thirteen ninety-eight, he was succeeded by his young grandson, but within four years the throne had been seized by Hongwu's fourth son, who took the reign name Yongle. The Yongle Emperor is one of the great transformative figures of the Ming, and he left marks on China that are still visible today. The most literal of those marks is Beijing.

Yongle moved the imperial capital north from Nanjing to Beijing in fourteen twenty-one, a decision with enormous strategic and symbolic weight. Beijing sat closer to the Mongol steppe, allowing for better military oversight of the northern frontier, and it was also Yongle's personal power base. To anchor this new capital, he undertook one of the most ambitious construction projects in human history: the Forbidden City. Work began around fourteen oh seven, and for fourteen years over one million labourers — soldiers, craftsmen, and conscripted workers — hauled timber from the forests of Sichuan, quarried marble from outside the capital, and fired hundreds of millions of bricks to build a palace complex of nine thousand nine hundred and ninety-nine rooms, all arranged according to cosmic principles of feng shui and imperial symbolism. The number of rooms was deliberate: ten thousand rooms would have been the domain of heaven, so the emperor chose one fewer, as a gesture of humility before the divine. The Forbidden City was completed in fourteen twenty and it remains the world's largest surviving palace complex.

Yongle also sponsored the most extraordinary maritime enterprise of the pre-modern world. Between fourteen oh five and fourteen thirty-three, he dispatched seven enormous naval expeditions under the command of a Muslim eunuch admiral named Zheng He. These were not exploratory voyages in the European sense — Zheng He was not mapping unknown territories for future colonisation. He was projecting power, collecting tribute, and establishing China's prestige across the Indian Ocean world. The scale of the enterprise was staggering. The first voyage alone comprised three hundred and seventeen ships, sixty-two of them the enormous "treasure ships" — baochuan — that measured approximately one hundred and forty-eight metres in length and sixty metres in width. Compare that with Columbus's Santa María, which was roughly twenty metres long, and you get some sense of the ambition involved. Nearly twenty-eight thousand men sailed on that first expedition. The fleets visited Southeast Asia, India, the Persian Gulf, and the east coast of Africa, bringing back exotic animals including giraffes, which were presented to the Chinese court as mythical creatures called qilin. Over thirty countries sent tribute missions to Beijing as a result of these voyages. For a brief and brilliant moment, China was the dominant naval power on earth.

---

And then it stopped. Almost entirely.

The reasons for China's retreat from the seas are complex and still debated by historians, but they were connected to both ideology and politics. The Confucian bureaucracy was deeply sceptical of maritime trade and exploration, viewing merchants with suspicion and regarding overseas ventures as wasteful of resources that would be better spent on agriculture and internal governance. The immense cost of the treasure voyages was a genuine fiscal concern. When Yongle died in fourteen twenty-four, conservative officials moved quickly to curtail the programme. Zheng He's seventh and final voyage ended in fourteen thirty-three, and after that the great ships were allowed to rot in their harbours. In a decision that still stirs debate among historians of globalisation, the world's most powerful seafaring nation voluntarily withdrew from the oceans.

This was not entirely new — the maritime ban known as the Haijin had actually been instituted as early as thirteen seventy-one by the Hongwu Emperor himself, who declared that "not a single plank is allowed out to sea" by private merchants. The policy fluctuated over the centuries, tightening and loosening in response to piracy threats and shifting political winds, but the fundamental orientation of the Ming state increasingly turned inward. Private overseas trade was criminalised for long stretches of the dynasty, though in practice smuggling was rampant and a thriving shadow economy developed along China's southern coastline.

Instead of looking outward, the Ming looked to its northern borders — and to a building project of truly epochal scale. The Great Wall of China, as most people picture it today: wide enough for several horses to walk abreast, built of stone and brick, lined with watchtowers at regular intervals, snaking over mountain ridges for thousands of kilometres — that wall is almost entirely a Ming creation. Earlier walls had existed, but they were earthworks and tamped-earth constructions that had long crumbled. The Ming rebuilt and massively extended the wall over roughly two centuries, undertaking eighteen major construction campaigns. The finished structure ran for nearly nine thousand kilometres and was designed to defend against exactly the kind of nomadic incursions from the north that had undone the dynasty's Yuan predecessors. It required soldiers, forced labourers, famine refugees, and criminals, and it consumed enormous quantities of stone, brick, and mortar. Whether it ever truly worked as a defensive barrier is questionable — the Mongols and later the Manchu often simply found ways around it. But as a statement of imperial will, as a physical expression of China's sense of itself as a civilisation apart from the steppe world to its north, it was unmatched.

While walls went up on the frontier, the Chinese economy was becoming ever more deeply integrated into global trade, even as the state tried to resist this. By the sixteenth century, enormous quantities of American silver were flowing into China through the Manila galleon trade, as Spanish merchants exchanged New World silver — mined in Bolivia and Mexico — for Chinese silk and porcelain. The Ming economy had shifted to a silver standard, and this global monetary connection meant that disruptions in faraway silver markets could send shockwaves through Chinese commerce. China became, in effect, the engine of the first truly global trading system, even if its government was theoretically committed to restricting foreign commerce.

The culture of the Ming era was rich and varied. The dynasty is perhaps most famous abroad for its ceramics: the cobalt blue-and-white porcelain produced at the great kilns of Jingdezhen in Jiangxi Province became the most coveted luxury goods in the world, sought after from Japan to Persia to the Netherlands, where potters at Delft spent generations trying to reproduce its distinctive look. But the Ming also saw a flowering of popular literature, with novels like Journey to the West and Romance of the Three Kingdoms reaching something close to their final forms during this period. Theatre thrived. Urban commercial culture expanded. The population of China roughly doubled over the course of the dynasty, reaching somewhere in the region of two hundred million people by the early seventeenth century — making it far and away the most populous political unit on earth.

---

But greatness, in dynasties as in individuals, rarely lasts forever. By the late sixteenth century the Ming was showing severe signs of strain. The reign of the Wanli Emperor, which lasted from fifteen seventy-two to sixteen twenty — the longest reign of the dynasty at forty-eight years — became a byword for administrative paralysis. Wanli famously withdrew from public life for decades, refusing to attend court or conduct routine business, paralysed by a succession dispute and increasingly alienated from his own bureaucracy. Without imperial direction, factionalism in the court intensified. Corruption spread through the administration. Eunuchs, whom the Hongwu Emperor had tried to exclude from power, had over the generations accumulated immense influence and used it for private enrichment. Under the Tianqi Emperor in the sixteen twenties, a eunuch named Wei Zhongxian effectively controlled the government, purging his opponents and placing loyalists in key positions across the empire.

Meanwhile, the fiscal situation was deteriorating rapidly. A global cooling event known as the Little Ice Age brought decades of colder and drier weather to northern China, causing widespread crop failures and famine. A series of devastating plagues, including outbreaks of what may have been bubonic plague, swept through provinces already weakened by hunger. Tax revenues fell as the agricultural base contracted, but military costs were rising sharply, because a new threat was assembling just beyond the northeastern frontier.

In the early seventeenth century, a Jurchen tribal leader named Nurhaci had united the various Jurchen clans of Manchuria into a formidable military and political confederation. In sixteen sixteen he declared himself khan, and in sixteen eighteen he issued his "Seven Grievances" against the Ming — effectively a declaration of war. At the Battle of Sarhu in sixteen nineteen, his forces annihilated four Ming armies simultaneously, a catastrophic defeat that shifted the balance of power in Manchuria irreversibly. Nurhaci's son Hong Taiji renamed his people the Manchu and his dynasty the Qing, and continued to press against the Ming's northern defences. The Great Wall, for all its grandeur, proved unable to hold them indefinitely.

The dynasty's final collapse was triggered not by the Manchu but by a domestic uprising. Li Zicheng, a former postal worker who had lost his job in government cost-cutting, became one of the most successful rebel leaders in Chinese history. By sixteen forty-four, leading an enormous peasant army, he had swept across northern China and was approaching Beijing. On the twenty-fifth of April, sixteen forty-four, as Li's forces entered the capital, the last Ming emperor — the Chongzhen Emperor, the sixteenth ruler of the dynasty — climbed to the top of Coal Hill, a small artificial mound just north of the Forbidden City, and hanged himself from a tree. Before he died, according to accounts, he wrote in his robes: "I die unable to face my ancestors in the underworld, ashamed to meet my ministers. I remove my crown and cover my face with my hair. Let the rebels tear my body apart. But harm not a single one of my people." Whether those were his exact words we cannot know. But they capture something true about the end: a man who had inherited an impossible situation, who had genuinely tried to save his dynasty, and who died alone, having failed.

Li Zicheng's triumph was brief. The Manchu under Prince Dorgon allied with a disaffected Ming general, Wu Sangui, and on the sixth of June, sixteen forty-four, they entered Beijing. Li was driven out and the Qing Dynasty was established. Pockets of Ming resistance held out in the south for decades — the so-called Southern Ming — but the dynasty as a functioning imperial institution was finished.

---

Three hundred years is a long time to rule, and the Ming left marks on China and the world that have not faded. The Forbidden City still stands in Beijing, now a museum visited by millions every year, its red walls and golden roofs as visually arresting as they were when Yongle first walked its corridors in fourteen twenty-one. The Great Wall, rebuilt and extended during the Ming, remains one of the most recognisable man-made structures on earth, a UNESCO World Heritage Site and a symbol of Chinese civilisation. Ming porcelain is among the most avidly collected antiquities in the world, with individual pieces regularly selling for tens of millions of dollars at auction.

But the Ming's legacy runs deeper than monuments and objects. The administrative and cultural framework that the dynasty established — the civil service examination system, the Confucian-inflected bureaucracy, the emphasis on agricultural self-sufficiency and territorial defence — shaped China's development for centuries after the Qing took over. The Qing largely adopted Ming institutional structures and personnel, recognising that the Ming had built something durable even as it collapsed.

There is also a more cautionary legacy. The story of the Ming is, in part, a story about what happens when a great power turns its back on the world. The decision to abandon Zheng He's voyages left the seas open for the Portuguese, the Spanish, the Dutch, and the English, whose own expansion would eventually transform the global order in ways that would come to bear heavily on China in the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. The maritime ban, the inward turn, the preference for walls over fleets — these choices made a kind of sense within their own historical logic. But they also foreclosed possibilities. A China that had continued to project naval power into the Indian Ocean might have looked very different from the China that confronted Western imperialism in the eighteen hundreds.

The Ming Dynasty ended as it began: in chaos, with a regime losing its grip and a new power rising to replace it. What it left behind was a China transformed — more populous, more commercially sophisticated, more architecturally magnificent, and more deeply shaped by a vision of itself as the centre of the civilised world. For nearly three centuries, the Great Ming made that vision real, built it in stone and brick and porcelain, and sailed it across the oceans of the world. That is an achievement worth remembering.
