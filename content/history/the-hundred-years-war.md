# The Hundred Years War

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

The Hundred Years War between England and France was not, strictly speaking, a single war at all. It was a series of conflicts, truces, diplomatic collapses, and fresh invasions that stretched from thirteen thirty-seven to fourteen fifty-three — one hundred and sixteen years in total. It reshaped two kingdoms, killed uncountable thousands, introduced weapons that made armoured knights obsolete, and produced one of the most extraordinary individuals in all of medieval history. But for the people living through it, especially in France, it often felt like one long, grinding catastrophe. To understand why it started, we need to go back to a question of bloodlines, legal theory, and royal vanity that proved, in the end, to be worth over a century of bloodshed.

When the French king Charles IV died in February of thirteen twenty-eight without a male heir, it set off a succession crisis. Two men had a claim to the throne. Philip, Count of Valois, was Charles's first cousin through the male line. Edward III of England was Charles's nephew — but through his mother Isabella, a French princess. Edward's claim was arguably closer in blood. The trouble was that French legal scholars and nobles invoked what they called Salic Law, an ancient Frankish legal code stipulating that the right to rule could not pass through a woman. Under that interpretation, Edward's claim was null, and Philip of Valois became Philip VI of France. Edward, who was only fifteen at the time and under the thumb of his mother and her lover, initially accepted this. He even paid homage to Philip as his feudal overlord for the English-held lands in southwestern France — the duchy of Gascony and Aquitaine, which the English crown had controlled since the marriage of Eleanor of Aquitaine to Henry II back in eleven fifty-two.

But feudal obligations between English kings and French kings had always been a source of friction. Edward was a duke within France, which technically made him a subject of the French crown, yet he was simultaneously a king in his own right. When political tensions rose through the thirteen thirties, Philip VI used this lever masterfully. He declared that Edward had been sheltering Robert of Artois, a French nobleman whom Philip considered an enemy, in violation of his feudal duties. In thirteen thirty-seven, Philip confiscated Gascony. Edward's response was to do something that was either spectacularly bold or diplomatically reckless, depending on your point of view. He revived his claim to the French throne itself and, in thirteen forty, formally styled himself King of France and quartered the French fleur-de-lis onto the English royal arms. The war had begun.

---

The first phase of the conflict, known by historians as the Edwardian War, ran from thirteen thirty-seven to thirteen sixty, and it belongs to England almost entirely. Edward III proved to be one of the most capable military commanders of the medieval period, and the weapon that defined his campaigns was one that the English had not invented but had perfected: the longbow.

The Welsh longbow required years of practice to master, but a trained archer could loose twelve arrows a minute, each one capable of punching through chain mail at close range. Against the Genoese crossbowmen who formed the backbone of French missile forces, they were devastating. Crossbows were powerful but slow — in the time it took a crossbowman to fire twice, a longbowman could fire twenty-four times.

The full horror of this mismatch was demonstrated on the twenty-sixth of August, thirteen forty-six, at a village in northern France called Crécy. Edward had landed an army on the Cotentin Peninsula in mid-July and marched south and east through Normandy, burning and looting as he went — a practice known as the chevauchée, designed to humiliate the French king and drain the economic resources of the countryside. Philip VI finally caught up with him near Crécy-en-Ponthieu and decided to give battle. Edward dismounted his knights, placing them beside his archers on a gentle ridge, with the setting sun at his back and shining directly into French eyes. The Genoese crossbowmen advanced first and were slaughtered by a storm of English arrows. When they broke and retreated, the French cavalry charged straight into the waiting longbowmen anyway. Charge after charge was broken. By nightfall, some fifteen hundred French knights and nobles lay dead, including the king of Bohemia — who had famously asked to be led into battle despite being blind. English losses were negligible. It was one of the most lopsided victories in medieval warfare.

Ten years later, in September of thirteen fifty-six, his son Edward — known to history as the Black Prince — replicated the formula at Poitiers. The Black Prince had been conducting a chevauchée through southern France when a large French army under King John II cornered him near the city of Poitiers. Rather than flee, Edward chose his ground carefully, positioning his archers behind hedges and on a ridge, flanking his men-at-arms in the now-familiar English style. The result was an even more spectacular outcome than Crécy. Not only were the French routed, but King John II himself was captured on the battlefield, along with his youngest son and dozens of the most powerful nobles in France. The king of France was literally a prisoner of the English crown. His ransom was set at three million gold crowns. For a generation, France was left diplomatically prostrate, politically humiliated, and financially crippled.

The crisis forced France to sign the Treaty of Brétigny in May of thirteen sixty. Edward gave up his claim to the French throne in exchange for full sovereignty over a hugely expanded duchy covering roughly a third of the country. The first phase of the war was over.

---

But then the conflict took a darker turn, and not because of anything the combatants chose. In thirteen forty-seven, a year after the victory at Crécy, the Black Death arrived in Europe. By thirteen fifty, it had killed somewhere between a third and a half of the entire population of the continent. France was devastated. Villages emptied. Fields went unplanted. The tax base that financed armies collapsed. The social order, already strained by decades of warfare, cracked under the pressure. A peasant uprising in France known as the Jacquerie broke out in thirteen fifty-eight, and urban revolts shook the cities. The English were not spared either — the plague killed indiscriminately — but France bore the worst of the damage simply because English armies had been burning and looting it for twenty years before the pestilence arrived.

The Treaty of Brétigny held for about a decade before it, too, fell apart. French resentment of English territorial gains simmered, and when Charles V came to the French throne in thirteen sixty-four, he set about rebuilding his kingdom's military with quiet determination. He avoided pitched battles — he had seen what happened at Crécy and Poitiers — and instead relied on a brilliant commander named Bertrand du Guesclin, who used guerrilla tactics, siege warfare, and patient attrition to peel back English gains territory by territory. By thirteen eighty, when Charles V died, France had recovered most of what it had lost at Brétigny. The war had ground into an exhausted stalemate, punctuated by truces and sporadic fighting, that would last for another generation.

It was not until fourteen fifteen that the war erupted again with full, terrifying force. The agent of that eruption was Henry V of England — young, ferociously ambitious, and possessed of an absolute conviction that the French crown was rightfully his. Henry invaded France in August of fourteen fifteen, besieged and captured Harfleur, and then marched his weakened, disease-thinned army toward Calais. The French army that intercepted him at a narrow field near the village of Agincourt on the twenty-fifth of October, fourteen fifteen, outnumbered his by perhaps five to one.

What happened next became one of the most famous military engagements in English history. The terrain was a freshly ploughed field made muddy by heavy rain. The French cavalry and dismounted knights had to advance across that mud in heavy plate armour, in a long column squeezed between two patches of woodland that prevented them from spreading out. Henry's archers, positioned on the flanks and protected by sharpened stakes, waited until the French were committed to the advance and then unleashed a blizzard of arrows. The mud slowed the French to a crawl. Horses fell, riders were pinned under armour they could barely move in, and the pressure of thousands of men pushing from behind caused those at the front to be crushed before the English even reached them with weapons. Around six thousand Frenchmen died. Henry lost perhaps four hundred men. Among the French dead were three dukes, five counts, and ninety lords — the political leadership of a generation, erased in an afternoon.

Henry pressed his advantage ruthlessly. He conquered Normandy castle by castle over the next three years, and by fourteen twenty he had forced Charles VI of France — a king who suffered severe bouts of mental illness and was politically helpless — to sign the Treaty of Troyes. The terms were extraordinary. Henry V was to marry Charles's daughter Catherine. More importantly, Henry was formally recognised as heir to the French throne, displacing Charles's own son, the Dauphin. Upon Charles VI's death, England and France would be united under a single crown — Henry's crown. For a brief moment, it looked as though England had actually won.

Then fate intervened with characteristic medieval cruelty. Henry V died of dysentery on the thirty-first of August, fourteen twenty-two, at the Château de Vincennes outside Paris. He was thirty-five years old. Charles VI of France died just two months later, in October. By the terms of the Treaty of Troyes, the combined crown of France and England now passed to Henry's son — an infant nine months old, who would reign as Henry VI. A nine-month-old boy was simultaneously King of England and King of France. The prospects for this settlement surviving intact were not encouraging.

---

Into this chaos stepped one of the most astonishing figures of the medieval world. Jeanne d'Arc — Joan of Arc — was a teenage peasant girl from the village of Domrémy in the Lorraine region of eastern France. Born around fourteen twelve, she had grown up in a country at war, in territory that saw the brutality of competing armies. From around the age of thirteen, she later testified, she began hearing voices — which she identified as those of Saint Michael, Saint Catherine, and Saint Margaret — telling her that she had a divine mission to drive the English from France and see the Dauphin Charles crowned as the rightful king.

By fourteen twenty-nine, the military situation for the French royalists was desperate. The English and their Burgundian allies — the powerful Duchy of Burgundy had broken with the French crown after a political murder and sided with England — controlled northern France including Paris. The city of Orléans, the last major stronghold blocking an English advance into the heart of southern France, had been under siege since October of fourteen twenty-eight. The Dauphin Charles's cause looked finished. It was at this moment that a seventeen-year-old girl in armour arrived at his court, convinced his sceptical advisors that she was genuine, and was given command of a relief force.

On the twenty-ninth of April, fourteen twenty-nine, Joan entered Orléans with supplies and soldiers. Over the following nine days, in a series of fierce assaults on English fortified positions around the city, the French broke the siege. On the eighth of May, the English withdrew. It was the turning point of the entire war. Within weeks, Joan led French forces to a string of victories along the Loire valley. By July, she had escorted the Dauphin through territory previously held by the English to the cathedral city of Reims, where French kings were traditionally crowned, and on the seventeenth of July, fourteen twenty-nine, Charles VII was anointed King of France.

The psychological transformation in France was immense. After decades of defeat and humiliation, here was proof that the tide could turn — and it had turned through what many ordinary French people genuinely believed was divine intervention. Joan herself did not live to see the final victory. She was captured by Burgundian troops during a skirmish outside Compiègne in May of fourteen thirty, sold to the English for ten thousand livres, and put on trial for heresy in the English-controlled city of Rouen. The trial was a sham — a pro-English ecclesiastical court designed to discredit her and, by extension, the legitimacy of Charles VII's coronation. She was convicted and burned at the stake on the thirtieth of May, fourteen thirty-one. She was approximately nineteen years old. Twenty-five years later, in fourteen fifty-six, the verdict was formally overturned. In nineteen twenty, she was canonised as a saint.

But Joan's death did not reverse what she had started. The momentum she gave to the French cause proved impossible to stop. Charles VII spent the following years rebuilding his kingdom's finances and, crucially, its military. He created something genuinely new in European history: a permanent, professional standing army paid by the crown rather than assembled feudally from noble retinues. He also invested heavily in artillery — the gunpowder cannon, still a relatively novel technology, which could reduce stone walls that had resisted conventional siege for months.

---

The final reconquest came swiftly by the standards of this long war. In fourteen forty-nine, Charles VII launched a massive campaign to retake Normandy. His reformed army, equipped with superior artillery, swept through the province at remarkable speed. The English were defeated at the Battle of Formigny in April of fourteen fifty, and Normandy — which England had held since the days of Henry V — fell within months. Charles then turned south to Gascony, a region that had been under English control since eleven fifty-four, nearly three hundred years. In fourteen fifty-one, he took Bordeaux, the great English port city and the heart of the Gascon wine trade.

The English made one last attempt to recover their position. A veteran commander, John Talbot, the Earl of Shrewsbury, landed in Gascony in fourteen fifty-two and briefly retook Bordeaux. But on the seventeenth of July, fourteen fifty-three, Talbot led a cavalry charge against a strongly fortified French artillery position near the town of Castillon-sur-Dordogne. The French cannon tore his men apart. Talbot was killed. The English force was destroyed at a cost of nearly four thousand men, while French losses numbered around one hundred. It was the first European battle won decisively by massed field artillery, and it was the last major engagement of the Hundred Years War. Bordeaux surrendered in October. The English held nothing in France except the port of Calais, a toehold they would not relinquish until fifteen fifty-eight.

No formal peace treaty was ever signed. The war simply sputtered out, exhausted. England was already sliding toward the Wars of the Roses — a dynastic blood feud beginning in fourteen fifty-five that made any further French adventures impossible. France, by contrast, emerged with something it had not possessed at the beginning: a genuine sense of itself as a nation.

The Hundred Years War remade France. It had begun as a feudal dispute over dynastic rights. It ended with the birth of something closer to national consciousness. French peasants who had watched English armies burn their villages, who had cheered Joan of Arc as she rode to Orléans, who had seen Charles VII crowned at Reims — these people had experienced something that turned geography and language into identity. By fourteen fifty-three, to be French meant something it had not meant in thirteen thirty-seven.

The war also modernised warfare permanently. The longbow had broken the age of the knight; the cannon broke the age of the castle. The professional armies Charles VII built were the ancestors of the standing national armies that would define European warfare for centuries. Gunpowder changed everything it touched, and the final act of this long conflict announced that change to the world with unmistakable force at Castillon.

And Joan of Arc? She became something that transcends mere history. A peasant girl who heard voices and changed the course of a continent — condemned as a heretic, declared a saint, and eventually claimed as the national symbol of France itself. During the French Revolution, during the Napoleonic Wars, during the darkest days of the Second World War, her image was invoked to summon something essential in the French sense of themselves. That a teenage girl from a border village could matter this much, across six centuries, tells you something about the power of what the Hundred Years War created: not just a modern France, but the idea that ordinary people, animated by belief and purpose, could shape the fate of nations.

One hundred and sixteen years. Two countries transformed. A world remade. Not bad for a quarrel about who had the better claim to a throne.
