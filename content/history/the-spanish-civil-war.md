# The Spanish Civil War

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

Spain in the early nineteen thirties was a country straining under the weight of its own contradictions. It was a nation of enormous landowners and landless peasants, of a powerful Catholic Church and an increasingly secular urban working class, of army officers who saw themselves as the guardians of tradition and intellectuals who dreamed of a modern republic. For centuries, these tensions had simmered. In the nineteen thirties, they boiled over into one of the most ferocious and consequential conflicts of the twentieth century.

The story begins with the fall of a monarchy. In April nineteen thirty-one, King Alfonso XIII authorised municipal elections, and the results were so overwhelmingly in favour of republican candidates that he quietly boarded a ship and sailed into exile. The Second Spanish Republic was proclaimed almost overnight. For many Spaniards, it felt like the dawn of something extraordinary. The new government launched ambitious reforms — land redistribution, secular education, limits on the military, regional autonomy for places like Catalonia and the Basque Country. But every reform also made enemies. Landowners feared expropriation. Church authorities resisted the removal of crucifixes from schoolrooms. Army generals bristled at budget cuts and the dissolution of old privileges. What one Spaniard called liberation, another called revolution.

The republic lurched between left and right throughout the early nineteen thirties. Elections in November nineteen thirty-three brought a right-wing coalition to power, and the reforms slowed or reversed. Then a left-wing uprising in Asturias in October nineteen thirty-four was crushed with considerable brutality by forces under a young general named Francisco Franco. The pendulum swung back in February nineteen thirty-six, when a coalition of left-wing parties calling itself the Popular Front won a narrow election victory and returned to government with promises to restart the reform agenda. Spain was by now split almost perfectly in two — not just politically, but psychologically, as though the country could no longer bear to share the same idea of itself.

Violence escalated through the spring of nineteen thirty-six. Political assassinations, street fights between fascists and socialists, church burnings, and strikes created an atmosphere of barely contained anarchy. In the officer corps, a conspiracy was already taking shape. The chief planner was General Emilio Mola, a meticulous and calculating man who spent months quietly networking among discontented officers. The figurehead was to be General José Sanjurjo, a veteran conspirator who had already tried and failed to stage a coup in nineteen thirty-two. And lurking in the background, weighing his options with characteristic caution, was Francisco Franco — the youngest general in the Spanish army, the man who had suppressed the Asturian miners, a figure the conspirators desperately wanted on their side.

---

On the seventeenth of July, nineteen thirty-six, the uprising began in Spanish Morocco. Garrisons across the protectorate rose against the Republic, and the following day the rebellion spread to mainland Spain. The plan had been for a swift, coordinated takeover — a surgical coup that would install a military directorate within days, before the government could respond. It did not go according to plan.

In many of Spain's major cities, the rising was defeated. Workers armed themselves from government stores, militias poured into the streets, and loyal republican officers held their ground. In Madrid, Barcelona, Valencia, and Bilbao, the rebels were suppressed. But in Seville, Zaragoza, and much of the rural north and south, the Nationalists — as the rebels came to call themselves — took control. Within forty-eight hours, Spain had been physically split in two. What was supposed to be a coup had become a war.

General Sanjurjo never got to lead the rebellion he had helped plan. On the twentieth of July, nineteen thirty-six, just three days after the uprising began, he was killed when his light aircraft crashed on takeoff from Portugal. The conspirators needed a new figurehead, and by the first of October nineteen thirty-six, Francisco Franco had manoeuvred himself into that position. He was appointed Generalissimo — supreme commander — and head of government of the Nationalist zone. It was a remarkable ascent for a man who had been studiously noncommittal until the very last moment. Franco would rule Spain for nearly four decades, but his dominance began in these chaotic first weeks of a war nobody had fully intended to start.

The Republic had one early advantage that would prove decisive for its survival in the months ahead: the ability to pay for weapons. The Republican government held Spain's gold reserves — the fourth largest in the world — and it used them to purchase arms from the Soviet Union. Stalin's decision to intervene was partly ideological and partly strategic. He wanted to prevent a fascist state on France's southern border, and he wanted to demonstrate Soviet solidarity with the international left. But Soviet support came with strings attached. Communist advisers arrived alongside the weapons, and the Spanish Communist Party, previously a minor faction, grew rapidly in influence. Soviet tanks, aircraft, and military expertise helped keep the Republic fighting, but Soviet political manipulation would also poison it from within.

On the Nationalist side, help came from the dictators of Europe. Adolf Hitler, when he received Franco's request for transport aircraft on the twenty-fifth of July, nineteen thirty-six, agreed almost immediately. He saw the conflict as both an ideological opportunity and a practical one — a chance to test weapons, tactics, and pilots under real combat conditions. Benito Mussolini, eager to establish Italian influence in the Mediterranean and present himself as the champion of European fascism, poured in resources on an even larger scale. By the war's end, Germany had contributed some nineteen thousand personnel through what became known as the Condor Legion, while Mussolini sent roughly seventy-five thousand Italian troops along with hundreds of aircraft and vast quantities of equipment. For Hitler and Mussolini, Spain was a laboratory. For the Spanish, it was their home.

---

The autumn of nineteen thirty-six brought the war to its first great crisis: the siege of Madrid. Nationalist columns advanced rapidly on the capital through October and November, and by the eighth of November, nineteen thirty-six, Nationalist troops were fighting in the suburbs. The Republic's government fled to Valencia. The city was expected to fall within days.

It did not fall. The people of Madrid responded with extraordinary defiance. The slogan that echoed through the streets — "No pasarán," they shall not pass — became the rallying cry of an entire generation of anti-fascists worldwide. Republican militias and the newly arrived International Brigades threw themselves into the defence of University City, a district on the western edge of Madrid where the front lines ran through lecture halls and library stacks. The Nationalists were held. Madrid would not fall until the very last days of the war.

The International Brigades deserve a moment of particular attention. These were volunteer fighters who came from more than fifty countries — from France and Britain, from the United States and Canada, from Germany and Italy where many were exiles fleeing fascism at home. In total, somewhere between thirty-two thousand and thirty-five thousand fighters served in the brigades, though estimates vary. They included communists, socialists, anarchists, and liberals united by the conviction that Spain was where the line against fascism had to be drawn. Among them was the writer George Orwell, who joined not the brigades but a smaller Marxist militia, the POUM, and whose experiences in the trenches around Huesca would give him the material for his memoir Homage to Catalonia and, later, the dystopian insight behind Animal Farm and Nineteen Eighty-Four. The idealism of the International Brigades was real, but so were the politics that surrounded them — Soviet-backed communist factions worked to suppress and destroy the independent left-wing groups they regarded as rivals, and in May nineteen thirty-seven Barcelona erupted into street fighting between communist and anarchist forces. The Republic was fighting fascism on the outside and consuming itself on the inside.

The military campaigns of nineteen thirty-seven unfolded with shifting fortune. In February, Nationalist forces tried to encircle Madrid by crossing the Jarama River to the south-east of the city, in what became the Battle of Jarama. The fighting was savage, the casualties enormous on both sides, and the Nationalists failed to sever Madrid's vital supply road to Valencia. A month later, in March nineteen thirty-seven, an Italian corps of roughly thirty-five thousand men advanced on Madrid from the north-east in the Battle of Guadalajara. The result was humiliating for Mussolini. Republican forces, armed with Soviet tanks and aircraft, drove the Italians back in disarray. It was one of the few clean Republican victories of the war, and it demonstrated that Mussolini's forces were not invincible.

But the most infamous event of nineteen thirty-seven was not a conventional battle. On the twenty-sixth of April, nineteen thirty-seven, German and Italian aircraft acting in support of Franco's forces bombed the Basque town of Guernica. The town had no significant military value. It was a market day. The aircraft attacked in waves over several hours, dropping high-explosive bombs and incendiaries on the civilian population. Casualty estimates have varied widely over the decades, but historians now generally accept that somewhere between one hundred fifty and three hundred civilians were killed. The attack was, by the standards of the era, a deliberate act of terror against a civilian population. Pablo Picasso, working in Paris, saw the photographs and the newspaper reports. Within weeks he had completed the massive canvas that bears the town's name — a fractured, screaming nightmare of broken bodies and anguished faces that became the most powerful anti-war painting of the twentieth century. Guernica the painting outlasted the war, outlasted Franco, and outlasted the century. It still hangs in Madrid, a permanent indictment.

---

Through nineteen thirty-seven and into nineteen thirty-eight, the Nationalists ground down the Republic's position. The industrialised north — the Basque Country and Asturias with their coal and steel — fell by the autumn of nineteen thirty-seven, a major economic blow to the Republic. Then in March nineteen thirty-eight, Franco launched a massive offensive through Aragon with a force of approximately a hundred and fifty thousand men and more than nine hundred aircraft. The front collapsed. Nationalist troops drove all the way to the Mediterranean Sea, reaching the coast on the fourteenth of April, nineteen thirty-eight. With that breakthrough, the Republican-held zone was cut in two. Catalonia in the north-east was severed from the centre and south. The Republic still controlled its heartland, but the strategic picture was now desperate.

The Republic made one last great effort in the summer of nineteen thirty-eight — the Battle of the Ebro, the largest battle of the entire war. Beginning on the twenty-fourth of July, Republican forces crossed the Ebro River in a surprise offensive, initially making significant gains. The battle raged for nearly four months. Franco chose to feed enormous resources into grinding down the Republican advance rather than simply bypassing it, and by November the last Republican troops withdrew back across the river. The human cost was catastrophic — more than a hundred thousand casualties between both sides. The Republic had spent its last reserves of men and material.

After the Ebro, the end came quickly. Nationalist forces pushed into Catalonia. On the twenty-sixth of January, nineteen thirty-nine, Barcelona fell. More than four hundred thousand civilians and soldiers fled across the Pyrenees into France — an exodus known as La Retirada, the retreat — where many were interned in hastily constructed camps on the beach, enduring cold and misery in the country that had refused to help them. The Republican government dissolved. On the twenty-eighth of March, nineteen thirty-nine, Nationalist troops entered Madrid unopposed, and on the first of April, Franco issued a brief victory communiqué: "Today, with the Red Army captive and disarmed, the Nationalist troops have achieved their final military objectives. The war is over."

---

The human cost of the Spanish Civil War has been debated and revised by historians for decades, and the full picture remains contested. Around five hundred thousand people died from all causes during the conflict itself. On top of that, one hundred thousand or more were executed by the Nationalists during the war, and a further fifty thousand after it ended. Between nineteen forty and nineteen forty-two, an estimated two hundred thousand more Spaniards died as a consequence of political repression, hunger, and disease. Some half a million refugees had fled into exile, and many never returned. Fifteen thousand Spanish Republicans eventually ended up in Nazi concentration camps after France fell to Germany in nineteen forty.

Franco's Spain was a dictatorship dressed in the language of Catholic order and national unity. Political opposition was outlawed. Trade unions were abolished. Regional languages like Catalan and Basque were suppressed. Former Republicans lived under constant surveillance, denied employment, imprisoned, or simply shot in the years following the war. Martial law remained in place until nineteen forty-eight. Franco called himself the Caudillo — the leader — and the regime he built was built on the bones of those who had opposed him.

He survived everything: the Second World War, in which he managed to stay formally neutral despite his ideological sympathies and his debts to Hitler and Mussolini; the post-war isolation of Spain by the Western allies; the Cold War, which eventually brought American support as Spain was reframed as a useful bulwark against communism. Francisco Franco died on the twentieth of November, nineteen seventy-five, still in power, in his bed. He had outlived almost everyone who had fought against him.

The transition to democracy that followed was remarkable in its own way. King Juan Carlos I, whom Franco had personally designated as his successor, steered Spain toward constitutional monarchy and free elections. In a process that has been called the Transition, Spain transformed from dictatorship to democracy with a minimum of violence — in part because of what became known as the Pacto del Olvido, the Pact of Forgetting. Political elites on all sides agreed, tacitly, not to prosecute or publicly reckon with the crimes of the Franco era. It was a necessary bargain at a fragile moment, and for a generation it held. But history does not stay buried.

From the early two thousands onward, Spain began excavating — literally. Mass graves from the war and the post-war repression were opened, families of the disappeared began demanding answers, and in two thousand and seven the government passed a Historical Memory Law formally condemning the Franco regime and establishing rights for victims. A revised and stronger Democratic Memory Act followed in twenty twenty-two, placing the obligation to search for disappeared victims on the state itself. In twenty nineteen, Franco's remains were exhumed from the grand mausoleum he had built for himself in the Valley of the Fallen and reinterred in a municipal cemetery. The argument over his legacy, and the war's, has never fully ceased.

The Spanish Civil War matters far beyond Spain's borders. It was the last major conflict where ideological volunteers from around the world travelled to fight for a cause they believed in. It was the place where fascism and its opponents first met on a conventional battlefield, where German and Italian aggression went unchecked by the democracies that stood aside under the banner of non-intervention. The Non-Intervention Committee, established in London in September nineteen thirty-six with Britain and France at its centre, was perhaps the most cynical diplomatic fiction of the era — a body that met regularly while its members Italy, Germany, and the Soviet Union openly poured weapons and soldiers into the conflict. The democracies' failure to act in Spain was a rehearsal for the failures at Munich in nineteen thirty-eight. And the military technology that the Condor Legion refined over Spanish skies — the tactics of aerial bombardment against civilian targets, the coordination of air power with ground forces — would be unleashed on the rest of Europe within months of Franco's victory.

The Spanish Civil War was many things at once: a class war, a culture war, a colonial war, a proxy war, and a tragedy. It was the moment when the ideological convulsions of the twentieth century found their first killing ground. The men and women who fought and died there — on both sides, and none — did so in the grip of ideas that felt, to them, absolutely essential and absolutely worth dying for. Whether those ideas were fascism or communism or democracy or anarchism, Spain burned for them all. And the fire, as the rest of the decade would prove, was only just beginning.
