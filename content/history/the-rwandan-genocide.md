# The Rwandan Genocide: A Hundred Days of Horror

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,250

---

In one hundred days in the spring and summer of 1994, somewhere between five hundred thousand and eight hundred thousand people were murdered in Rwanda. Most were Tutsi — killed by their Hutu neighbours, by militias who had been told the Tutsi were cockroaches who needed to be exterminated, by ordinary people who had been prepared for months by radio broadcasts and political propaganda to commit mass murder. Some were Hutu moderates who refused to participate or who tried to protect their neighbours. The genocide unfolded in full view of the international community, which watched, debated, and did nothing. United Nations peacekeeping forces already stationed in Rwanda were forbidden to intervene. The word "genocide" was deliberately avoided by governments that did not want to be obligated to act. By the time the killing stopped, Rwanda had become a charnel house: roads piled with bodies, churches filled with the corpses of those who had sought sanctuary, rivers flowing to Lake Victoria carrying the dead.

The Rwandan genocide is not just a story about Rwanda. It is a story about the capacity of human beings to commit mass murder when political and social conditions make it possible, and about the failure of international institutions to prevent atrocity even when they have the capability to do so. Understanding it requires understanding both the specific history that made Rwanda what it was in 1994 and the universal dynamics — dehumanisation, propaganda, group psychology, the bystander effect at national scale — that make genocide possible in any society.

---

Rwanda is a small, landlocked country in central Africa, densely populated and, before the genocide, often described as one of the most beautiful landscapes on the continent — a land of green hills and volcanoes, its population crowded onto every cultivable hillside. Its two main ethnic groups — the Hutu, who made up roughly eighty-five percent of the population, and the Tutsi, who made up roughly fourteen percent — had coexisted for centuries in a complex relationship that was neither simply harmonious nor simply conflictual. Traditional Rwandan society had both cooperation and hierarchy between the groups; intermarriage occurred; identity was more fluid than later accounts suggest.

Belgian colonialism, which administered Rwanda from after the First World War as a League of Nations mandate, hardened those identities into rigid racial categories. The Belgians introduced identity cards that classified every Rwandan as Hutu, Tutsi, or Twa, and promoted the Tutsi as racially superior — implementing what was known as the Hamitic hypothesis, a pseudo-scientific theory that the Tutsi were a more "civilised" African people, closer to the Hamitic peoples of North Africa, who were supposed to be naturally gifted for leadership. The Tutsi were given preferential access to education and administrative positions. The Hutu were marginalised and resentful. Colonial administration had manufactured a racial hierarchy where previously there had been a more complex social one.

In 1959, as independence approached, the political balance shifted violently. A Hutu revolution overthrew the Tutsi monarchy, and tens of thousands of Tutsi fled into exile in Uganda, Burundi, and the Congo. Rwanda became independent in 1962 under Hutu-majority rule. Periodic anti-Tutsi violence in the following decades drove further waves of refugees into exile. The children of those exiles — raised in Uganda with no right to return to Rwanda — would eventually form the nucleus of the Rwandan Patriotic Front, the Tutsi-led rebel army whose invasion of Rwanda in October 1990 triggered the civil war that set the stage for genocide.

---

The Arusha Accords of August 1993 ended the civil war and provided for a power-sharing government between the Hutu-led government and the RPF. Extremist Hutu politicians — grouped around President Juvénal Habyarimana and known as the Akazu — viewed the accords as a betrayal. Through 1993 and into 1994, they prepared for what they called the "final solution" to the Tutsi problem: radio broadcasts on Radio Mille Collines — the "Thousand Hills Radio" — incited hatred, called Tutsi "inyenzi" (cockroaches), and rehearsed the propaganda of extermination. Lists of Tutsi and moderate Hutu to be killed were compiled. The Interahamwe — "those who attack together" — a Hutu militia, was armed and trained.

The trigger came on the evening of the sixth of April, 1994, when the plane carrying President Habyarimana and Cyprien Ntaryamira, the Hutu president of Burundi, was shot down as it approached Kigali airport, killing everyone on board. Who fired the missiles — RPF forces, Hutu extremists trying to create a pretext, or others — has never been definitively established. What happened in the hours that followed suggests the killing had been planned in advance: within thirty minutes of the plane being shot down, roadblocks were being erected across Kigali, and the Interahamwe had begun killing.

The genocide began officially on the seventh of April, 1994. The first hours set the pattern: the prime minister, Agathe Uwilingiyimana — a moderate Hutu — was murdered, along with ten Belgian UN soldiers sent to protect her. The Belgians were tortured and killed; Belgium responded by withdrawing all its peacekeeping troops from Rwanda. Across the country, Tutsi were rounded up at checkpoints, dragged from their homes, hunted through fields and forests. The killings were carried out not by armies but by ordinary people — neighbours who killed neighbours, sometimes with machetes, sometimes with clubs studded with nails, sometimes by burning people alive in churches where they had sought sanctuary.

---

The international response was a catastrophic failure of will and of conscience. The United Nations had a peacekeeping force in Rwanda — the United Nations Assistance Mission for Rwanda, known as UNAMIR, commanded by the Canadian general Roméo Dallaire. In January 1994, Dallaire had sent a now-famous telegram to UN headquarters in New York warning that Hutu extremists were planning mass killings and that he had received information about weapons caches being stockpiled. The UN told him not to act on the information. When the genocide began, the Security Council not only refused to reinforce UNAMIR but actually voted to reduce its numbers. The United States, traumatised by the failure of intervention in Somalia and unwilling to risk American lives in Africa, blocked any significant action and instructed its officials not to use the word "genocide" — because the Genocide Convention of 1948 obligated signatories to act to prevent and punish genocide, and the US did not want to be so obligated.

The arguments made at the time — that the situation was too complex, that the international community lacked the intelligence to know what was happening, that intervention would be dangerous — have not withstood scrutiny. Dallaire estimated that five thousand properly equipped troops, deployed quickly, could have stopped or significantly reduced the killing. The information was available. The political will was not.

The genocide ended not through international intervention but through military victory. The Rwandan Patriotic Front resumed its advance and captured Kigali on the fourth of July, 1994, ending the genocide as their forces took control of the country. The génocidaires — the perpetrators — fled into the Democratic Republic of Congo in their hundreds of thousands, taking the weapons of genocide with them and destabilising the region for decades.

---

In the aftermath, Rwanda faced the almost impossible task of rebuilding a country whose population had either participated in mass murder, survived it, or fled. The International Criminal Tribunal for Rwanda, established in 1994 in Arusha, Tanzania, prosecuted the architects of the genocide, securing convictions including for the crime of genocide. Rwanda itself, under the RPF government of Paul Kagame — who became president in 2000 — established a system of community justice courts called Gacaca to process the vast numbers of lower-level perpetrators: roughly one hundred and twenty thousand people were eventually tried in these courts. The country rebuilt its institutions, its economy, and, in some improbable measure, its social fabric — though the wounds of 1994 will not close within any living generation's lifetime.

The Rwandan genocide forced a reckoning — incomplete and still contested — with the concept of humanitarian intervention: under what circumstances is the international community obligated to act to prevent mass atrocity within the borders of a sovereign state? The Responsibility to Protect doctrine, adopted by the United Nations in 2005, attempted to answer that question. Whether it has done so effectively is evident in subsequent failures — in Darfur, in Syria, in other places where the pattern of dehumanisation, incitement, and mass killing has repeated itself.

What Rwanda demands of us is not merely understanding but acknowledgement: that genocide is not an aberration produced by uniquely barbaric cultures or historically unique conditions. It is a human possibility — achievable wherever sufficient political will, sufficient propaganda, and sufficient dehumanisation of an identified group combine. Knowing that is the beginning of preventing it.
