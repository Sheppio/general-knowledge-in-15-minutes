# World War One: How It Started, What Happened, and How It Ended

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes

---

World War One, often called the Great War, lasted from 1914 to 1918 and remains one of the deadliest conflicts in human history. To understand how it started, you need to picture Europe in the early twentieth century as a continent sitting on a powder keg.

For decades, the major European powers had been forming alliances, building up enormous armies, and competing for colonies and influence around the world. Nationalism was surging across the continent, with ethnic groups demanding their own nations and empires struggling to hold together populations who no longer wanted to be ruled by foreign powers. Military spending had been climbing for years. Generals on all sides had detailed war plans sitting in their drawers, ready to execute at a moment's notice. On one side stood the Triple Alliance of Germany, Austria-Hungary, and Italy. On the other stood the Triple Entente of France, Russia, and Britain. These alliances meant that a conflict between any two nations could drag in all the others like a chain reaction.

The spark that lit the fuse came on the twenty-eighth of June, 1914, in Sarajevo, Bosnia. Archduke Franz Ferdinand, heir to the Austro-Hungarian throne, was assassinated by a nineteen-year-old Bosnian Serb nationalist named Gavrilo Princip. Austria-Hungary blamed Serbia for the attack and issued a set of harsh demands. Serbia accepted most of them but not all, and Austria-Hungary declared war on Serbia on the twenty-eighth of July.

From there, the alliance system pulled everyone in. Russia began mobilizing to defend Serbia. Germany declared war on Russia, then on France. When Germany invaded neutral Belgium as a shortcut to attack France, Britain declared war on Germany. Within six weeks of a single assassination, most of Europe was at war.

---

The opening months were chaotic and fast-moving. Germany's strategy, known as the Schlieffen Plan, called for a rapid sweep through Belgium and France to knock France out of the war quickly, then turn east to deal with Russia. It nearly worked. German forces swept deep into France and came within thirty miles of Paris. Then came the First Battle of the Marne in September 1914, a turning point that changed the entire nature of the war. French and British forces stopped the German advance and pushed them back. Both sides then began a desperate race northward, each trying to outflank the other, until they ran out of room at the English Channel. What followed was something no one had anticipated. Both armies dug in. They built hundreds of miles of trenches stretching from the Belgian coast all the way to the Swiss border. The Western Front was born, and it would barely move for the next four years.

---

Life in the trenches was a nightmare unlike anything soldiers had been prepared for. The front line consisted of a network of narrow ditches reinforced with timber and sandbags, often flooded with water and thick with mud. Soldiers lived in these conditions for weeks at a time, sleeping in dugouts carved into the trench walls, surrounded by the smell of rot and decay. Rats grew enormous feeding on the dead. Lice infested every uniform. A condition called trench foot, caused by standing in cold wet mud for days, could cost a man his leg. Poison gas, first used by the Germans in 1915, added a new and terrifying dimension to the horror. Soldiers learned to pull on crude gas masks the moment they heard the warning, knowing that chlorine or mustard gas could blind or kill within minutes.

Artillery was the great killer of the war. The guns never really stopped. Day and night, shells rained down on the trenches, destroying fortifications, burying men alive, and shattering nerves. Men who survived prolonged bombardment often emerged with what was then called shell shock, what we now recognize as post-traumatic stress disorder. Many were unable to speak, trembled uncontrollably, or relived the horror in waking nightmares. The military at the time largely failed to understand it as a psychological wound, and some men were court-martialed for cowardice when they were in fact suffering from severe trauma.

Despite all this misery, the front lines hardly moved. Both sides would launch massive offensives, gain a few miles at enormous cost, then watch as the enemy pushed them back. The tactical advantage had swung completely to the defender. Barbed wire, machine guns, and artillery made attacking across open ground suicidal. Tanks were introduced by the British in 1916 in an attempt to break the deadlock, lumbering across the cratered landscape and crushing barbed wire, but they were still unreliable and too few in number to be decisive in the early years.

In the air, a brand new form of warfare was taking shape. The first military aircraft were used simply for reconnaissance, with pilots spotting enemy troop movements from above. But soon both sides began arming their planes, and the era of the fighter pilot was born. Aces like Germany's Manfred von Richthofen, known as the Red Baron, and France's René Fonck became celebrated figures, their aerial duels romanticized in newspapers as a kind of chivalric combat above the mud and slaughter below. The reality was far grimmer. The average life expectancy of a new pilot on the Western Front was measured in weeks. Aircraft technology evolved at a furious pace throughout the war, and by 1918 both sides had sophisticated air forces conducting bombing raids and coordinated ground support missions.

The Battle of Verdun in 1916 stands as perhaps the most horrifying example of grinding attrition. The Germans launched a massive assault on the French fortress city of Verdun in February, intending not necessarily to capture it but to bleed the French army white. The French held on desperately, sending in reinforcements along a single road that became known as the Voie Sacrée, the Sacred Way, because if it fell, Verdun would fall with it. The battle lasted ten months. By the end, approximately three hundred thousand soldiers had been killed across both sides combined, with total casualties including the wounded reaching around seven hundred thousand. A quarter of a million soldiers simply vanished into the mud, their bodies never recovered.

Almost simultaneously, the British launched the Battle of the Somme in July 1916, partly to relieve pressure on the French at Verdun. On the very first day, the British suffered nearly sixty thousand casualties, including almost twenty thousand killed. It remains the bloodiest single day in British military history. The battle dragged on until November, resulting in over a million total casualties on all sides, with territorial gains measured in miles.

The war was not only fought in the trenches of France. In 1915, Britain and its allies attempted to open a new front by attacking the Gallipoli Peninsula in what is now Turkey, hoping to knock the Ottoman Empire out of the war and open a supply route to Russia. The campaign was a catastrophe. Allied troops landed on heavily defended beaches in April 1915 and found themselves pinned down in conditions almost as bad as the Western Front, but now under blazing heat and with inadequate water. The Anzac forces, soldiers from Australia and New Zealand, fought with extraordinary courage at a place called Anzac Cove, suffering enormous losses. After eight months of futile fighting, the Allies withdrew having achieved nothing. The Gallipoli campaign became a defining national trauma for Australia and New Zealand, and is still commemorated solemnly every year on the twenty-fifth of April, Anzac Day.

The war was also being fought at home. Women across Britain, France, Germany, and eventually America stepped into factories, farms, and offices vacated by the millions of men who had gone to fight. They manufactured shells and weapons, drove ambulances, nursed the wounded, and kept economies running. This mass entry of women into the workforce had profound consequences for society. In Britain, women over thirty were granted the right to vote in 1918, a direct recognition of their contribution to the war effort. The old world of rigid class and gender boundaries was cracking under the pressure of total war.

On the Eastern Front, Germany and Austria-Hungary battled Russia across an enormous stretch of territory. The fighting there was more fluid, and the Russian army, poorly supplied and badly led, suffered catastrophic losses. By 1917, Russia was falling apart from within. Revolution overthrew the Tsar in February, and then the Bolsheviks seized power in October. In March 1918, Russia signed the Treaty of Brest-Litovsk and withdrew from the war entirely, freeing up hundreds of thousands of German troops to transfer to the Western Front.

At sea, Britain's powerful navy blockaded Germany, slowly strangling its economy and food supply. German civilians were going hungry. Germany responded with submarine warfare, using U-boats to attack any ships supplying Britain, including those of neutral nations. This strategy would prove to be one of Germany's most significant blunders, because it kept sinking ships belonging to America, a neutral power that Germany desperately needed to stay out of the war. In May 1915, a German submarine sank the British ocean liner Lusitania, killing nearly twelve hundred people including one hundred and twenty-eight Americans. Outrage swept the United States. Then, in early 1917, the British intercepted a telegram from German Foreign Secretary Arthur Zimmermann to Mexico, proposing that if America entered the war, Germany would help Mexico recover Texas, New Mexico, and Arizona. When the Zimmermann Telegram was made public, the last threads of American patience snapped. In April 1917, the United States declared war on Germany.

---

Fresh American troops began arriving in France in large numbers through 1917 and into 1918, just as the Allied armies were exhausted and demoralized. Mutinies had broken out across the French army after yet another catastrophic failed offensive. British forces were also stretched thin.

Germany knew it had a narrow window. In the spring of 1918, it launched a series of massive offensives using new infiltration tactics that bypassed strongpoints and drove deep into Allied lines. For a terrifying few months, it looked like Germany might actually win. Paris was shelled by long-range artillery. But the offensives exhausted Germany's finest troops and failed to deliver a knockout blow.

Then the tide turned completely. In August 1918, the Allies launched the Hundred Days Offensive. With American strength growing daily and German morale collapsing, Allied armies advanced steadily and relentlessly. Germany's allies crumbled. Bulgaria surrendered in September. The Ottoman Empire surrendered in October. Austria-Hungary collapsed in early November. Revolution broke out inside Germany itself. The Kaiser abdicated on the ninth of November, 1918.

At eleven o'clock on the morning of the eleventh of November, 1918, the guns fell silent. The Armistice was signed in a railway carriage in the forest of Compiègne, France.

---

The peace settlement, finalized at the Treaty of Versailles in 1919, placed full blame for the war on Germany, stripped it of territory and colonies, forced it to pay enormous reparations, and severely limited its military. The terms humiliated Germany, destabilized its economy, and created the resentment and desperation that would eventually bring Adolf Hitler to power and ignite a second, even more devastating world war just twenty years later.

The human cost was almost incomprehensible. Roughly seventeen million people were killed, military and civilian. Another twenty million were wounded. The great empires of Europe, the Austro-Hungarian, Ottoman, Russian, and German, all collapsed. The map of the world was redrawn. New nations emerged from the wreckage.

The war also permanently changed how humanity thought about conflict itself. It shattered the romantic notion of military glory. The poets and writers who survived it wrote not of heroism but of futility and slaughter. Wilfred Owen, who was killed just one week before the Armistice, described soldiers dying in a gas attack, writing that if you could see what he had seen, you would not tell children the old lie, that it is sweet and honorable to die for your country.

The Great War was supposed to be the war to end all wars. Tragically, it was only the beginning.
