# The American Civil War: A Nation Divided

> Approximate reading time: 15 minutes | Word count: ~2,300

---

In the spring of 1861, the United States of America tore itself in two. What followed was four years of fighting on a scale no one had anticipated, using industrial-age weapons against tactical thinking that had not yet caught up — a catastrophic combination that produced casualty figures without precedent in American history. Estimates of the dead range from around 620,000 to 750,000 soldiers — more Americans than have died in all other American wars combined. And it settled, permanently and in blood, the two questions that had haunted the republic since its founding: could the union be broken, and could slavery survive?

The origins of the war lay in slavery and the question of whether it would be permitted to expand into the new territories in the west. The Southern economy was built on the labour of nearly four million enslaved Black Americans, and the Southern political class was determined to protect that system. The Northern states had moved away from slavery, and a growing abolitionist movement was demanding its end everywhere. The election of Abraham Lincoln in November 1860 — a Republican who opposed the expansion of slavery — convinced the Southern states that their way of life was under existential threat. South Carolina seceded from the union in December 1860. Ten more Southern states followed, forming the Confederate States of America.

Lincoln insisted that secession was illegal and that the union could not be broken. The war began on the twelfth of April, 1861, when Confederate forces fired on Fort Sumter, a federal garrison in the harbour of Charleston, South Carolina. No one thought it would last long. Young men on both sides volunteered enthusiastically, expecting it to be over in months.

---

They were wrong. Both sides had advantages the other could not easily overcome. The North had the factories, the railways, the population, and the navy. The South had determined defenders fighting on familiar terrain, superb military leadership, and the powerful motivation of protecting their homes. The early battles — Bull Run, Shiloh, Antietam — shocked both sides with their ferocity. Antietam in September 1862 was the bloodiest single day of the war, with nearly twenty-three thousand casualties in a single day of fighting.

Lincoln understood that to win the war he had to change what it was about. On the first of January, 1863, he issued the Emancipation Proclamation, declaring enslaved people in Confederate states to be free. The proclamation did not immediately free a single enslaved person — it applied only in Confederate territory, beyond Union control — but it transformed the moral character of the war. It made clear that a Union victory would mean the end of slavery. It prevented Britain and France, who had been sympathetic to the Confederacy, from recognising it, since neither could afford to be seen defending slavery. And it allowed Black men to enlist in the Union army — eventually more than one hundred and eighty thousand of them serving in the United States Colored Troops.

---

The turning point came in the first three days of July, 1863. At Gettysburg, Pennsylvania, Confederate General Robert E. Lee — the South's greatest commander — led his army north into Union territory, hoping a decisive victory would convince the North to negotiate peace. Instead, in three days of brutal fighting, his army was repulsed with catastrophic losses. On the third day, he sent nearly twelve thousand men in a frontal assault across open ground — Pickett's Charge — into Union artillery and rifle fire. More than half were killed or wounded. Lee retreated south and never again threatened the North. The same week, the Union captured Vicksburg on the Mississippi, splitting the Confederacy in two.

The war's industrial scale of killing was made possible by new technology. The rifled musket — accurate at four times the range of smooth-bore weapons — made massed frontal assaults catastrophically costly. Artillery had become more powerful and more mobile. Yet tactics had not adjusted; officers were still ordering the kind of set-piece assaults that had worked in the Napoleonic era. The result was slaughter on a scale that shattered assumptions about what war looked like. Soldiers learned to dig trenches. The Siege of Petersburg, in the war's final year, looked uncannily like the Western Front of the First World War, half a century later.

---

In 1864, General Ulysses S. Grant took command of all Union forces and adopted a strategy of relentless pressure — not seeking a single decisive battle but grinding down Confederate strength until it collapsed. His campaign through Virginia was brutal, with enormous Union casualties at battles like the Wilderness and Cold Harbor. At the same time, General William Sherman marched his army through Georgia and then the Carolinas, deliberately destroying the South's infrastructure and will to fight. Sherman's March to the Sea burned a wide corridor of destruction and became one of the first examples of targeting an enemy's civilian economy as a military objective.

By April 1865, the end was near. Lee's army, starving and shrinking through desertion, could no longer hold the lines around Petersburg. He retreated west, pursued by Grant. On the ninth of April, 1865, surrounded and out of supplies, Lee surrendered to Grant at Appomattox Court House in Virginia. The remaining Confederate forces surrendered over the following weeks. The war was over.

Five days after Lee's surrender, President Lincoln attended the theatre in Washington. A Confederate sympathiser named John Wilkes Booth shot him in the back of the head. Lincoln died the following morning — the fifteenth of April, 1865.

---

The aftermath of the war brought both freedom and betrayal. The Thirteenth Amendment abolished slavery throughout the United States. The Fourteenth and Fifteenth Amendments granted Black Americans citizenship and the right to vote. For a brief period during Reconstruction, Black men held political office across the South. Then, in the 1870s, white supremacist violence and the withdrawal of federal protection allowed the old power structures to reassert themselves. Jim Crow laws, sharecropping, and systematic terror kept Black Americans in a state of effective subjugation for another century.

The Civil War did not resolve the question of racial equality. But it did resolve two others. The union survived. And slavery was finished. Those facts, won at an almost incomprehensible cost, shaped everything that followed.
